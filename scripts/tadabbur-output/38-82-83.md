## Introduction

[ayah:38:82] قَالَ فَبِعِزَّتِكَ لَأُغْوِيَنَّهُمْ أَجْمَعِينَ
[ayah:38:83] إِلَّا عِبَادَكَ مِنْهُمُ ٱلْمُخْلَصِينَ

*Qala fa-bi-'izzatika la-ughwiyannahum ajma'in. Illa 'ibadaka minhum al-mukhlasin.*

He said, "By Your might, I will surely mislead them all, except Your servants among them who are made sincere."

---

There is something unnerving about an enemy who knows his limits.

An enemy who bluffs is dangerous in one way. An enemy who understands exactly where he can enter and where he cannot is dangerous in a deeper way, because his strategy is clearer, colder, and more realistic. These two ayahs preserve that kind of speech.

Iblis makes a vow. It is sweeping, emphatic, and chilling. But then, inside that vow, he names an exception. And the exception matters as much as the threat.

Because in naming the category he cannot reach, Iblis ends up teaching us where safety actually lives.

Not in self-confidence. Not in mere religious performance. Not in the fantasy that temptation disappears for serious people. Safety lives in being Allah's servant, and more than that, in being among those whom Allah has made sincere.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### Even his oath admits where real power belongs

The first striking word is the oath:

**fa-bi-'izzatika**

By Your might.

The root **'-z-z** carries the sense of might, invincibility, unassailable strength, honor that cannot be overcome. Iblis is rebelling, but he is not confused about sovereignty. He does not swear by his own force. He swears by Allah's.

That matters theologically and spiritually.

The Quran never lets the adversary become a rival god. There is no dualism here, no second throne, no opposing kingdom balancing Allah's rule. Even the vow of misguidance is uttered under acknowledged divine might. His rebellion happens inside a sovereignty he cannot break.

That means the believer should never read these verses with panic larger than tawhid. The one who threatens is real. The One by whose might he can threaten at all is greater.

[PAUSE]

### The vow is linguistically total, not casual

Then comes the promise:

**la-ughwiyannahum ajma'in**

I will surely mislead them all.

The verb from **gh-w-y** is not light. It carries the sense of leading into error, derailment, loss, ruinous wandering. This is more than offering a bad idea. It is an attempt to bend human movement off its proper road.

And look at how much force the sentence carries. The emphatic lam at the front, the heavy nun at the end, and then **ajma'in** to sweep the field wide. This is not a passing intention. The grammar itself sounds like a campaign.

One way to sit with this is to notice that the Quran does not train us to underestimate the seriousness of temptation. Iblis is not casual about his work. He is patient, committed, and totalizing in ambition.

That should sober us. But it should not leave us there, because the ayah does something beautiful immediately after making the claim as large as possible.

[PAUSE]

### The exception is where the real secret is hidden

**illa 'ibadaka minhum al-mukhlasin**

Except Your servants among them who are made sincere.

The word **illa** cracks the total claim open. The field is not absolute after all. There is a category beyond his reach.

Then comes **'ibadaka**: Your servants.

Not merely human beings, not merely believers in the abstract, but servants defined by belonging. Before the passage gives the special adjective, it gives the relationship. Their safety begins in ubudiyyah, in being Allah's.

And then the decisive word: **al-mukhlasin**.

This matters enormously.

The form here is passive participle. Not those who made themselves sincere, but those made sincere. Those purified, selected, cleared out, claimed, protected by Allah.

If the ayah had said **mukhliṣin** in the active sense, the emphasis would fall on their act of sincerity. But **mukhlaṣin** puts the decisive action on Allah's side. The protection here is not self-manufactured purity. It is divine custody.

That does not erase striving. It reorders it. We pursue sincerity, yes, but we do so knowing that the deepest sincerity is gift before it is achievement. Rescue before it is résumé.

Even Iblis knows this. That is why he excludes them.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### We often fight temptation with the wrong center of gravity

Many of us try to resist sin by strengthening the self in the wrong way.

We become more watchful, more disciplined, more organized, more informed, and these can all be real goods. But quietly, the center of gravity remains the same: I will manage this. I will hold the line. I will become the kind of person whispers cannot move.

These ayahs disturb that confidence in a merciful way.

Iblis does not say, "Except the smart ones." He does not say, "Except the intense ones." He does not say, "Except those who built the right personal system."

He says: **Your servants among them who are made sincere.**

That means our deepest refuge from him is not finally our grip on ourselves. It is Allah's grip on us.

The practical shift is profound. We still take means. We still avoid doors of sin. We still repent, learn, guard our eyes, watch our appetites, choose good company. But underneath all of that, the heart stops trusting its own management as the final answer. It begins begging for ikhlas as something only Allah can secure.

[PAUSE]

### The heart most protected from Shaytan is the heart least impressed with itself

There is an irony here that should humble us.

Iblis fell through arrogance, through the self becoming too large. The people beyond his reach are those purified by Allah, not inflated by themselves.

That suggests that one of the deepest protections against Shaytan is not spiritual self-certainty but spiritual truthfulness. A servant who knows his need, who does not romanticize his own strength, who keeps returning to Allah, who asks to be made sincere rather than assuming he already is, is standing in the right place.

This also means ikhlas is not just purity of intention in the narrow sense. It is a whole posture of dependence. A heart emptied of performance before Allah. A life increasingly uninterested in how it appears and increasingly concerned with whether it belongs.

The enemy cannot finally master a heart Allah has taken into that kind of possession.

And that is why the passage, despite coming from the mouth of Iblis, ends as hope. He tells us his ambition. Then he tells us the border he cannot cross.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayahs hold threat and mercy in a single breath.

Iblis vows universal misguidance with the strongest language he can gather. But the same sentence carries his confession of limitation.

He can work on them all.

Except.

Except the servants who belong to Allah in a way deeper than performance.

Except the ones Allah has made sincere.

So the passage does not leave us merely afraid of Shaytan. It redirects us toward the condition he himself cannot breach. Not bravado. Not spiritual vanity. Not the illusion of self-securing purity. Ubudiyyah. Ikhlas granted by Allah. A sincerity so real because it was given, not staged.

The enemy knows the line. The wise servant lives near it.

### Questions to Carry

1. Where have you been depending on self-management more than on Allah to protect your heart?
2. What would change in your worship if you treated sincerity as a gift to beg for, not a badge you already possess?
3. In what part of your life do you most need to move from trying to look sincere to asking Allah to make you sincere?

### One-Sentence Distillation

Iblis's vow teaches that the heart safest from his reach is not the heart most impressed with its own strength, but the servant whom Allah has made sincere.

### Du'a

Allahumma, make us truly Yours. Protect us from the misguidance that bends hearts away from You, and do not leave us to our own sincerity for even a moment. Purify our intentions, empty us of performance, and place us among Your servants whom the enemy cannot reach because You have made them sincere. Ameen.
