<article class="prose-blog">
  <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed">The Quran addresses <strong>Bani Isra’il</strong> with a peculiar intensity. “O Children of Israel, remember My favor.” The directness feels both intimate and judicial. A people are being spoken to as heirs of a covenant still alive in memory, even where history has frayed its terms. Sinai in the Quran is therefore not only an old mountain event. It is an ongoing address.</p>

  <p>The root <strong>ʿ-h-d</strong> behind covenant carries the sense of entrusting, committing, charging with something to be remembered and kept. Sinai belongs to that field. The mountain becomes the site where revelation, command, and communal identity are bound together. The Quran’s repeated return to the scene suggests that covenant is one of the central ways it reads the history of Bani Isra’il.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    يَٰبَنِىٓ إِسْرَٰٓءِيلَ ٱذْكُرُوا۟ نِعْمَتِىَ ٱلَّتِىٓ أَنْعَمْتُ عَلَيْكُمْ وَأَوْفُوا۟ بِعَهْدِىٓ أُوفِ بِعَهْدِكُمْ وَإِيَّٰىَ فَٱرْهَبُونِ وَءَامِنُوا۟ بِمَآ أَنزَلْتُ مُصَدِّقًا لِّمَا مَعَكُمْ وَلَا تَكُونُوٓا۟ أَوَّلَ كَافِرٍۭ بِهِۦ وَلَا تَشْتَرُوا۟ بِـَٔايَٰتِى ثَمَنًا قَلِيلًا وَإِيَّٰىَ فَٱتَّقُونِ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">O Children of Israel, remember My favor which I bestowed upon you, and fulfill My covenant and I shall fulfill your covenant.</p>
  <cite>Al-Baqarah (2:40-41)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p>The address opens with memory before law. Favor is recalled first. Covenant rests upon given grace, then answers with obligation. This order matters. The Quran does not narrate Sinai as a bare burden imposed upon a neutral people. It narrates it as a response required from a people already marked by rescue, selection, and revelation.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    وَإِذْ أَخَذْنَا مِيثَٰقَكُمْ وَرَفَعْنَا فَوْقَكُمُ ٱلطُّورَ خُذُوا۟ مَآ ءَاتَيْنَٰكُم بِقُوَّةٍ وَٱذْكُرُوا۟ مَا فِيهِ لَعَلَّكُمْ تَتَّقُونَ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">When We took your covenant and raised the mountain above you: “Hold firmly what We have given you and remember what is in it, so that you may become mindful.”</p>
  <cite>Al-Baqarah (2:63)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p>The raising of the mountain in <strong>Al-Baqarah (2:63)</strong> is one of the most arresting images in the Quranic memory of Sinai. Revelation is accompanied by overwhelming sign. Yet even here the command is to hold firmly and remember. The root <strong>dh-k-r</strong> in remembrance suggests that covenant can fail through forgetfulness as much as through open rebellion. Memory is a religious duty.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    وَلَقَدْ أَخَذَ ٱللَّهُ مِيثَٰقَ بَنِىٓ إِسْرَٰٓءِيلَ وَبَعَثْنَا مِنْهُمُ ٱثْنَىْ عَشَرَ نَقِيبًا وَقَالَ ٱللَّهُ إِنِّى مَعَكُمْ لَئِنْ أَقَمْتُمُ ٱلصَّلَوٰةَ وَءَاتَيْتُمُ ٱلزَّكَوٰةَ وَءَامَنتُم بِرُسُلِى وَعَزَّرْتُمُوهُمْ وَأَقْرَضْتُمُ ٱللَّهَ قَرْضًا حَسَنًا لَّأُكَفِّرَنَّ عَنكُمْ سَيِّـَٔاتِكُمْ وَلَأُدْخِلَنَّكُمْ جَنَّٰتٍ تَجْرِى مِن تَحْتِهَا ٱلْأَنْهَٰرُ فَمَن كَفَرَ بَعْدَ ذَٰلِكَ مِنكُمْ فَقَدْ ضَلَّ سَوَآءَ ٱلسَّبِيلِ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">Allah certainly took the covenant of the Children of Israel, and We appointed from them twelve leaders... If you establish prayer, give zakah, believe in My messengers, support them, and lend Allah a beautiful loan, I shall surely remove from you your sins and admit you into gardens.</p>
  <cite>Al-Maidah (5:12)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p><strong>Al-Maidah (5:12)</strong> gives the covenant a tribal and institutional shape. Twelve leaders are appointed. The community is organized. Prayer, almsgiving, belief in messengers, support for revelation, and generous giving form the covenantal obligations. Sinai is therefore remembered not only as awe before a mountain but as a structured communal life under divine command.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    وَإِذْ أَخَذْنَا مِيثَٰقَ بَنِىٓ إِسْرَٰٓءِيلَ لَا تَعْبُدُونَ إِلَّا ٱللَّهَ وَبِٱلْوَٰلِدَيْنِ إِحْسَانًا وَذِى ٱلْقُرْبَىٰ وَٱلْيَتَٰمَىٰ وَٱلْمَسَٰكِينِ وَقُولُوا۟ لِلنَّاسِ حُسْنًا وَأَقِيمُوا۟ ٱلصَّلَوٰةَ وَءَاتُوا۟ ٱلزَّكَوٰةَ ثُمَّ تَوَلَّيْتُمْ إِلَّا قَلِيلًا مِّنكُمْ وَأَنتُم مُّعْرِضُونَ وَإِذْ أَخَذْنَا مِيثَٰقَكُمْ لَا تَسْفِكُونَ دِمَآءَكُمْ وَلَا تُخْرِجُونَ أَنفُسَكُم مِّن دِيَٰرِكُمْ ثُمَّ أَقْرَرْتُمْ وَأَنتُمْ تَشْهَدُونَ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">When We took the covenant from the Children of Israel: you shall worship none but Allah, show excellence to parents, relatives, orphans, and the needy, speak well to people, establish prayer, and give zakah.</p>
  <cite>Al-Baqarah (2:83-84)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p>Here the covenant is ethical and social as well as ritual. Worship, family duty, care for the vulnerable, truthful speech, prayer, and almsgiving stand together. The Quran remembers Sinai as the place where divine claim upon a people reached every dimension of life. Revelation did not merely legislate altar and shrine. It formed speech, kinship, economy, and public restraint.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    وَإِذْ نَتَقْنَا ٱلْجَبَلَ فَوْقَهُمْ كَأَنَّهُۥ ظُلَّةٌ وَظَنُّوٓا۟ أَنَّهُۥ وَاقِعٌۢ بِهِمْ خُذُوا۟ مَآ ءَاتَيْنَٰكُم بِقُوَّةٍ وَٱذْكُرُوا۟ مَا فِيهِ لَعَلَّكُمْ تَتَّقُونَ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">When We shook the mountain above them as if it were a canopy and they thought it would fall upon them: “Hold firmly what We have given you and remember what is in it, so that you may become mindful.”</p>
  <cite>Al-Araf (7:171)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p><strong>Al-Araf (7:171)</strong> repeats the mountain image with intensified visual force. Sinai in the Quran is therefore remembered as both mercy and weight, gift and demand. The direct address to Bani Isra’il keeps that double truth alive. A people given prophets and scripture are addressed with seriousness because revelation has already come close to them.</p>

  <p>What does that direct address signal? It signals that the Quran is not speaking about Bani Isra’il from a cool historical distance. It is engaging them as covenantal interlocutors. The memory of Sinai remains active because the moral terms of the covenant remain legible. Direct address preserves dignity even in rebuke. One addresses those from whom one still expects answer.</p>

  <p>So the Quran remembers Sinai as a place where favor became obligation, where memory became duty, and where a people were formed by command under a mountain of undeniable sign. The covenant remains alive in the speech that recalls it. “Remember My favor.” “Fulfill My covenant.” The mountain may lie in the past, yet the address continues in the present tense of revelation.</p>
