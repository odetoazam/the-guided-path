<article class="prose-blog">
  <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed">The Quran imagines grain in motion. Provision comes down from Allah, settles for a moment in human hands, and is asked to move again. Food is eaten with gratitude. Wealth is spent from what was first given. A fertile land thrives through thankful recognition, then withers through denial and arrogance. The economic theology of the Quran is never merely about acquisition. It is about circulation under God.</p>

  <p>The root <strong>r-z-q</strong> carries the sense of provision, sustenance, something allotted for actual benefit. The root <strong>sh-k-r</strong> carries the sense of acknowledging favor, showing appreciation in a way that makes the gift visible. The root <strong>n-f-q</strong>, from which <strong>infaq</strong> comes, carries the concrete sense of passing through, of expenditure that moves outward. Together these roots form a circuit: Allah gives provision, the servant receives with gratitude, and gratitude matures into spending.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    ٱلَّذِينَ يُؤْمِنُونَ بِٱلْغَيْبِ وَيُقِيمُونَ ٱلصَّلَوٰةَ وَمِمَّا رَزَقْنَٰهُمْ يُنفِقُونَ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">Those who believe in the unseen, establish prayer, and spend from what We have provided them.</p>
  <cite>Al-Baqarah (2:3)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p><strong>Al-Baqarah (2:3)</strong> establishes the pattern at the opening of the Quran. The pious are described by prayer and by spending from what Allah has provided. The wording is careful. Wealth is never treated as a self-originating possession. It is already marked as received before it is marked as spent. That prior reception changes the ethics of the whole scene. Expenditure becomes a response, not merely a sacrifice.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    فَكُلُوا۟ مِمَّا رَزَقَكُمُ ٱللَّهُ حَلَٰلًا طَيِّبًا وَٱشْكُرُوا۟ نِعْمَتَ ٱللَّهِ إِن كُنتُمْ إِيَّاهُ تَعْبُدُونَ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">So eat from what Allah has provided you, lawful and good, and be grateful for the favor of Allah, if it is Him you worship.</p>
  <cite>An-Nahl (16:114)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p>In <strong>An-Nahl (16:114)</strong>, gratitude is joined directly to provision. Lawful food is to be eaten with <strong>shukr</strong>. The root’s concrete sense suggests more than an inward feeling. Gratitude in Quranic speech has a declarative and manifest quality. It displays the favor by recognizing the giver. A grateful person does not merely enjoy provision. He reads it correctly.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    لَقَدْ كَانَ لِسَبَإٍ فِى مَسْكَنِهِمْ ءَايَةٌ جَنَّتَانِ عَن يَمِينٍ وَشِمَالٍ كُلُوا۟ مِن رِّزْقِ رَبِّكُمْ وَٱشْكُرُوا۟ لَهُۥ بَلْدَةٌ طَيِّبَةٌ وَرَبٌّ غَفُورٌ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">Eat from the provision of your Lord and give thanks to Him. A good land and a forgiving Lord.</p>
  <cite>Saba (34:15)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p><strong>Saba (34:15)</strong> makes the link civic and agricultural. A whole people are invited to eat from their Lord’s provision and give thanks. Fertility, settlement, and abundance become theological signs. A “good land” belongs with a forgiving Lord because both material and moral order are at stake. Later in the same narrative, ingratitude disrupts that order. The land of provision becomes a lesson in reversal.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    وَإِذْ تَأَذَّنَ رَبُّكُمْ لَئِن شَكَرْتُمْ لَأَزِيدَنَّكُمْ وَلَئِن كَفَرْتُمْ إِنَّ عَذَابِى لَشَدِيدٌ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">If you are grateful, I shall surely increase you.</p>
  <cite>Ibrahim (14:7)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p>The promise of increase in <strong>Ibrahim (14:7)</strong> shows that gratitude is itself economically generative. Increase here is not merely mathematical, though it may include quantity. It also suggests extension, blessing in use, preservation from loss, and broadening of good. Gratitude keeps provision open toward more provision because it keeps the servant related rightly to the source.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    يَٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوٓا۟ أَنفِقُوا۟ مِمَّا رَزَقْنَٰكُم مِّن قَبْلِ أَن يَأْتِىَ يَوْمٌ لَّا بَيْعٌ فِيهِ وَلَا خُلَّةٌ وَلَا شَفَٰعَةٌ وَٱلْكَٰفِرُونَ هُمُ ٱلظَّٰلِمُونَ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">Spend from what We have provided you before a Day comes in which there is neither trade nor friendship nor intercession.</p>
  <cite>Al-Baqarah (2:254)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p><strong>Al-Baqarah (2:254)</strong> brings the circuit to its ethical urgency. Spend from what We have provided you. Once again, provision is prior and human use is derivative. The command to spend is framed by the coming Day when worldly forms of exchange will fall away. In that light, <strong>infaq</strong> becomes a truer kind of transaction. One moves wealth now according to divine command before the illusions of self-owned security disappear.</p>

  <p>The Quran’s economic theology is therefore structured by source, acknowledgment, and redistribution. Source: Allah provides. Acknowledgment: the servant responds with <strong>shukr</strong>, a gratitude that names the giver and receives lawfully. Redistribution: the servant spends from what was given, allowing provision to continue its moral journey. Hoarding interrupts the circuit. Forgetfulness interrupts the circuit. Arrogance interrupts the circuit. Gratitude keeps it alive.</p>

  <p>This is why the Quran so often speaks of spending immediately after mentioning provision. To be provided for is to be enrolled in an economy of trust. Wealth in the hand is not the end of the story. It is the middle of the story. What matters is whether provision becomes gratitude and whether gratitude becomes release.</p>

  <p>Read this way, <a href="/hub/rizq">rizq</a> is one of the most dynamic ideas in the Quran. It descends from Allah, nourishes the creature, trains the heart into thankfulness, and then moves back outward in charity and social repair. The circuit is theological before it is financial. The grateful servant is the one through whom provision keeps flowing in the shape of praise and generosity.</p>
