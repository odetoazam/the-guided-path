<article class="prose-blog">
  <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed">The sentence is almost too radiant for the ear at first pass: <strong>yuhibbuhum wa yuhibbūnahu</strong>. He loves them, and they love Him. The Quran does not present the relation between Creator and creature here as metaphor only, nor as a one-way projection from below. It gives the clause in active reciprocal grammar. The order is exact. Divine love comes first. Human love answers it. The sequence feels like rain before blossom.</p>

  <p>The root <strong>h-b-b</strong> carries the sense of concentrated attachment, a dense inner leaning like a seed packed with force. <a href="/hub/mahabbah">Mahabbah</a> in the Quran is therefore never sheer sentiment. It gathers loyalty, gravity, preference, and orientation into one center. When the Quran speaks of love between Allah and human beings using this root, it is naming a mutual relation at the core of religious life.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    يَٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ مَن يَرْتَدَّ مِنكُمْ عَن دِينِهِۦ فَسَوْفَ يَأْتِى ٱللَّهُ بِقَوْمٍ يُحِبُّهُمْ وَيُحِبُّونَهُۥٓ أَذِلَّةٍ عَلَى ٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ أَعِزَّةٍ عَلَى ٱلْكَٰفِرِينَ يُجَٰهِدُونَ فِى سَبِيلِ ٱللَّهِ وَلَا يَخَافُونَ لَوْمَةَ لَآئِمٍ ذَٰلِكَ فَضْلُ ٱللَّهِ يُؤْتِيهِ مَن يَشَآءُ وَٱللَّهُ وَٰسِعٌ عَلِيمٌ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">Allah will bring a people whom He loves and who love Him, gentle toward believers, mighty toward deniers, striving in the path of Allah.</p>
  <cite>Al-Maidah (5:54)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p><strong>Al-Maidah (5:54)</strong> is the verse where the mutuality stands most clearly. The grammar matters, and the portrait that follows matters with it. These beloved people are characterized by tenderness toward believers, firmness toward hostile denial, and striving in the path of Allah. Love, then, is not treated as a hidden inward sweetness detached from history. It shapes temperament, loyalty, and action. The people loved by Allah become recognizable in the moral texture of their lives.</p>

  <p>The sequence places divine love first. That priority protects the whole doctrine from sentimentality and pride. Human love is a response awakened by prior divine favor. The heart does not generate its own axis. It is turned, chosen, drawn, and then answered. The sentence therefore contains both intimacy and humility.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    قُلْ إِن كُنتُمْ تُحِبُّونَ ٱللَّهَ فَٱتَّبِعُونِى يُحْبِبْكُمُ ٱللَّهُ وَيَغْفِرْ لَكُمْ ذُنُوبَكُمْ وَٱللَّهُ غَفُورٌ رَّحِيمٌ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">Say: If you love Allah, then follow me; Allah will love you and forgive you your sins.</p>
  <cite>Aal-e-Imran (3:31)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p>In <strong>Aal-e-Imran (3:31)</strong>, love is tested by following the Messenger. This verse gives the mutual relation its prophetic pathway. One may claim love for Allah, yet the Quran binds that claim to concrete imitation. Following is the shape love takes in time. Then divine love returns again in the promise: Allah will love you and forgive you. The reciprocity is dynamic. Love leads to following, following leads to divine love and forgiveness, and the servant’s love is refined through that return.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    وَمِنَ ٱلنَّاسِ مَن يَتَّخِذُ مِن دُونِ ٱللَّهِ أَندَادًا يُحِبُّونَهُمْ كَحُبِّ ٱللَّهِ وَٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوٓا۟ أَشَدُّ حُبًّا لِّلَّهِ وَلَوْ يَرَى ٱلَّذِينَ ظَلَمُوٓا۟ إِذْ يَرَوْنَ ٱلْعَذَابَ أَنَّ ٱلْقُوَّةَ لِلَّهِ جَمِيعًا وَأَنَّ ٱللَّهَ شَدِيدُ ٱلْعَذَابِ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">Those who believe are stronger in love for Allah.</p>
  <cite>Al-Baqarah (2:165)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p><strong>Al-Baqarah (2:165)</strong> gives love a polemical setting. Rival loves crowd the human heart, and the believers are distinguished by stronger love for Allah. Strength here suggests intensity, concentration, and sovereignty of attachment. The Quran is not speaking about an occasional religious emotion. It is speaking about the ordering power of love. What occupies the core of the heart governs the whole self.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    وَٱسْتَغْفِرُوا۟ رَبَّكُمْ ثُمَّ تُوبُوٓا۟ إِلَيْهِ إِنَّ رَبِّى رَحِيمٌ وَدُودٌ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">Seek your Lord’s forgiveness, then turn to Him in repentance. Surely my Lord is Merciful, Loving.</p>
  <cite>Hud (11:90)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p>Then <strong>Hud (11:90)</strong> gives the name <strong>Wadūd</strong>, the Loving, in the context of repentance. The root here shifts to <strong>w-d-d</strong>, the field of affectionate warmth. The shift complements rather than contradicts the language of <strong>hubb</strong>. Divine love is strong enough to ground loyalty and warm enough to receive return. The repentant servant comes back under mercy, and mercy is already clothed in loving welcome.</p>

  <p>What does it mean, then, that the Quran presents love between Creator and creature as mutual? It means revelation permits a language of relation deeper than obedience alone while keeping that relation fully theocentric. Allah loves. The servant loves. Allah’s love comes first, grants possibility, and remains sovereign. The servant’s love answers through following, striving, repentance, and fidelity.</p>

  <p>It also means that Quranic theology is more intimate than many summaries allow. Lordship does not dissolve into mere power, nor servanthood into mechanical compliance. Power remains absolute, yet love is spoken without embarrassment. Servanthood remains humble, yet the servant is invited into reciprocity. The sentence in <strong>Al-Maidah</strong> preserves that whole tension in a few words.</p>

  <p>Perhaps that is why the line lingers so powerfully in the ear. It says that the center of religion is not only law, command, fear, or reward, even though all of these remain present. At the center there is also love, announced in mutual verbs, initiated by Allah, answered by hearts that have learned where to place their deepest attachment.</p>
