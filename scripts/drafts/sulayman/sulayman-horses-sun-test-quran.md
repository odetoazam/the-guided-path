  <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed">Beautiful horses are shown in the evening, the sun disappears behind the veil, and <a href="/hub/sulayman">Sulayman</a> says he loved the love of good because of the remembrance of his Lord. Then he orders them returned and passes his hand over their legs and necks. The Quran gives the scene in a handful of lines and withholds almost everything modern readers instinctively ask for. That withholding is part of the passage’s meaning. The compression is the test.</p>

  <p>The root <strong>ḥ-b-b</strong> in the language of love carries concentrated attachment, while the root <strong>dh-k-r</strong> in remembrance carries recalling, bringing present again, keeping something from slipping into forgetfulness. Between these two roots the whole scene turns. Love of splendid horses and remembrance of Allah stand in a charged relation. The Quran preserves the relation without overexplaining it.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    إِذْ عُرِضَ عَلَيْهِ بِٱلْعَشِىِّ ٱلصَّٰفِنَٰتُ ٱلْجِيَادُ فَقَالَ إِنِّىٓ أَحْبَبْتُ حُبَّ ٱلْخَيْرِ عَن ذِكْرِ رَبِّى حَتَّىٰ تَوَارَتْ بِٱلْحِجَابِ رُدُّوهَا عَلَىَّ فَطَفِقَ مَسْحًۢا بِٱلسُّوقِ وَٱلْأَعْنَاقِ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">When fine, swift horses were displayed before him at evening, he said: “I loved the love of good because of the remembrance of my Lord, until it disappeared behind the veil. Return them to me.” Then he began to pass his hand over their legs and necks.</p>
  <cite>Sad (38:31-33)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p>The horses are described with beauty and speed. The scene is visual first: display, evening light, motion, the fading sun. Then the verse suddenly enters Sulayman’s speech. His statement can be heard in more than one tonal key. It may be a confession that delight in noble horses was tied to readiness for Allah’s cause. It may carry a trace of regret at being absorbed in them until the sun passed. The Quran allows the sentence to retain that layered openness.</p>

  <p>Then comes the command: bring them back. What follows is one of the narrative’s most debated gestures. He passes his hand over their legs and necks. The Quran does not state whether the motion is affectionate, disciplinary, preparatory, or sacrificial in some fuller sense. It simply records the act. The omission forces the reader into attentiveness rather than easy closure.</p>

  <p>This deliberate incompleteness is itself a Quranic technique. Some scenes are preserved with interpretive surplus rather than tidy gloss. The point is not obscurity for its own sake. It is moral concentration. The reader must linger where love, beauty, power, remembrance, and corrective action meet without being instantly reduced to a slogan.</p>

  <p>What is preserved, above all, is Sulayman’s responsiveness. A moment involving beauty and attachment passes into a moment of recollection and decisive return. Whether the issue was slight distraction or heightened readiness, the passage values his quick reorientation toward Allah. The prophet does not drift. He gathers the moment back into obedience.</p>

  <p>The sun’s disappearance matters too. Time passes while beauty is being contemplated. Evening closes. That fading light gives the scene its moral pressure. Human beings encounter beauty inside time, and time itself can become a teacher. The vanishing sun exposes what attention has been doing.</p>

  <p>The Quran seems to preserve this moment because it belongs to the interior education of a prophet-king. Sulayman, master of wind and kingdom, is shown in a smaller theater: evening, horses, a sentence of self-reading, a gesture the text leaves mostly unexplained. The scale is intimate, and the intimacy is the point. Greatness is tested in such moments.</p>

  <p>So the compression should not be treated as a defect to solve and move past. It is the subject. The scene asks the reader to inhabit what revelation has chosen to keep and what it has chosen to omit. What remains is enough: beauty admired, remembrance asserted, time marked by the hidden sun, and a prophet who acts at once when the moment clarifies.</p>
