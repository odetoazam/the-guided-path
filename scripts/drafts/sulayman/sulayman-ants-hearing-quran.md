<article class="prose-blog">
  <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed">At the edge of an army, under the noise of movement and command, a tiny creature speaks, and the Quran gives her words room. “O ants, enter your dwellings.” The warning is communal, practical, tender. <a href="/hub/sulayman">Sulayman</a> hears it and smiles. The scene is one of the strangest acts of scale in the Quran: imperial motion and minute speech meeting in a single instant, with the smaller voice preserved verbatim.</p>

  <p>The root behind speech, <strong>n-ṭ-q</strong>, carries the sense of articulate expression. Sulayman says he was taught the <strong>manṭiq al-ṭayr</strong>, the speech of birds. Yet the Quran here extends that auditory field below the birds, into the low hidden world of ants. The point is not taxonomy. The point is a prophetic hearing granted beyond ordinary human range.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    حَتَّىٰٓ إِذَآ أَتَوْا۟ عَلَىٰ وَادِ ٱلنَّمْلِ قَالَتْ نَمْلَةٌ يَٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلنَّمْلُ ٱدْخُلُوا۟ مَسَٰكِنَكُمْ لَا يَحْطِمَنَّكُمْ سُلَيْمَٰنُ وَجُنُودُهُۥ وَهُمْ لَا يَشْعُرُونَ فَتَبَسَّمَ ضَاحِكًا مِّن قَوْلِهَا وَقَالَ رَبِّ أَوْزِعْنِىٓ أَنْ أَشْكُرَ نِعْمَتَكَ ٱلَّتِىٓ أَنْعَمْتَ عَلَىَّ وَعَلَىٰ وَٰلِدَىَّ وَأَنْ أَعْمَلَ صَٰلِحًا تَرْضَىٰهُ وَأَدْخِلْنِى بِرَحْمَتِكَ فِى عِبَادِكَ ٱلصَّٰلِحِينَ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">Until, when they came upon the valley of ants, an ant said: “O ants, enter your dwellings so that Sulayman and his armies do not crush you while they do not perceive.” So he smiled, laughing at her words, and said: “My Lord, inspire me to give thanks for Your favor...”</p>
  <cite>An-Naml (27:18-19)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p>The ant’s speech is remarkable in itself. She warns, she identifies danger, and she interprets intention charitably: “while they do not perceive.” Even at the scale of insect life, the Quran preserves a form of justice in speech. Harm may occur through unawareness rather than malice. Sulayman hears not only language but moral nuance.</p>

  <p>His response matters as much as his hearing. He smiles and turns immediately to gratitude. The hearing of hidden speech does not inflate him. It humbles him into praise. The root <strong>sh-k-r</strong> in his supplication carries the sense of grateful acknowledgment that makes favor visible. Sulayman reads the event correctly. It is not proof of his personal magnificence. It is another favor from Allah.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    وَوَرِثَ سُلَيْمَٰنُ دَاوُۥدَ وَقَالَ يَٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلنَّاسُ عُلِّمْنَا مَنطِقَ ٱلطَّيْرِ وَأُوتِينَا مِن كُلِّ شَىْءٍ إِنَّ هَٰذَا لَهُوَ ٱلْفَضْلُ ٱلْمُبِينُ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">Sulayman inherited Dawud and said: “We have been taught the speech of birds, and we have been given of every thing. Surely this is clear bounty.”</p>
  <cite>An-Naml (27:16)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p>This earlier verse prepares the ant scene by establishing hearing as gift. Sulayman does not discover a hidden natural talent. He is taught. The speech of non-human creatures enters his world by divine instruction. The ant’s warning therefore becomes one expression of a broader bounty, yet it is the most delicate expression preserved in narrative detail.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    فَفَهَّمْنَٰهَا سُلَيْمَٰنَ وَكُلًّا ءَاتَيْنَا حُكْمًا وَعِلْمًا وَسَخَّرْنَا مَعَ دَاوُۥدَ ٱلْجِبَالَ يُسَبِّحْنَ وَٱلطَّيْرَ وَكُنَّا فَٰعِلِينَ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">We made the mountains and the birds subservient, glorifying with Dawud and Sulayman was given understanding.</p>
  <cite>Al-Anbiya (21:79)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p>Understanding in the Quran is often marked by the root <strong>f-q-h</strong> or related terms of comprehension. Sulayman’s distinction includes this kind of understanding turned toward creation. The world is not mute around him. It becomes legible in registers others miss. That legibility is not exploitation. It is relational knowledge under divine favor.</p>

  <p>Why preserve the exact speech of the smallest creature? Because the Quran values scale rightly. Kingship and army are not allowed to monopolize significance. An ant’s warning can enter revelation and redirect the scene’s center of gravity. The smallest creature becomes a speaker with social concern, and the prophet-king becomes its hearer.</p>

  <p>The scene also reveals something about mercy. Sulayman’s greatness is displayed in his capacity to hear what others would miss and to answer that hearing with thankful humility. Imperial power is softened by attentiveness to fragility. The valley of ants becomes a place where strength learns gentleness through perception.</p>

  <p>The Quran therefore preserves the ant’s words not as a curious anecdote but as a theological event. Creation speaks in more ways than human beings usually grasp. A prophet is granted access to that speech. And when the speech of the smallest is truly heard, the proper response is gratitude to the One who made a world so full of voices.</p>
