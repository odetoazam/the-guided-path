<article class="prose-blog">
  <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed">A book is named and left largely unopened. The Quran speaks of the <strong>Zabūr</strong> given to <a href="/hub/dawud">Dawud</a>, honors it as revelation, and then withholds the kind of extended citation it occasionally offers when speaking of the <strong>Tawrah</strong> or <strong>Injil</strong>. Around that silence, however, another form of disclosure appears. Mountains echo with praise. Birds gather in responsive song. Iron softens in his hands. The prophet’s book is unquoted, yet his worship is sounded everywhere.</p>

  <p>The root behind <strong>Zabūr</strong>, <strong>z-b-r</strong>, carries the sense of writing, inscribing, marking with lines, recording with firmness. The word suggests something written and preserved. Its Quranic mention therefore establishes scriptural dignity at once. Yet the Quran seems more interested in the prophet who was given this book than in verbal extracts from the book itself. That asymmetry is striking.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    إِنَّآ أَوْحَيْنَآ إِلَيْكَ كَمَآ أَوْحَيْنَآ إِلَىٰ نُوحٍ وَٱلنَّبِيِّۦنَ مِنۢ بَعْدِهِۦ وَأَوْحَيْنَآ إِلَىٰٓ إِبْرَٰهِيمَ وَإِسْمَٰعِيلَ وَإِسْحَٰقَ وَيَعْقُوبَ وَٱلْأَسْبَاطِ وَعِيسَىٰ وَأَيُّوبَ وَيُونُسَ وَهَٰرُونَ وَسُلَيْمَٰنَ وَءَاتَيْنَا دَاوُۥدَ زَبُورًا
  </p>
  <p class="translation">We revealed to you as We revealed to Nuh and the prophets after him... and We gave Dawud the Zabur.</p>
  <cite>An-Nisa (4:163)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    وَرَبُّكَ أَعْلَمُ بِمَن فِى ٱلسَّمَٰوَٰتِ وَٱلْأَرْضِ وَلَقَدْ فَضَّلْنَا بَعْضَ ٱلنَّبِيِّۦنَ عَلَىٰ بَعْضٍ وَءَاتَيْنَا دَاوُۥدَ زَبُورًا
  </p>
  <p class="translation">Your Lord knows best whoever is in the heavens and the earth. We favored some prophets above others, and We gave Dawud the Zabur.</p>
  <cite>Al-Isra (17:55)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p>These two verses establish the fact of the book with elegant brevity. The Zabur is part of the chain of revelation and part of Dawud’s distinction among prophets. The Quran needs only a short clause to secure that place. Yet after naming the book, it moves on. The silence is deliberate enough to become meaningful. Dawud’s identity in the Quran is mediated less through excerpted scripture and more through praise, judgment, softened iron, and creation joining his remembrance.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    وَلَقَدْ كَتَبْنَا فِى ٱلزَّبُورِ مِنۢ بَعْدِ ٱلذِّكْرِ أَنَّ ٱلْأَرْضَ يَرِثُهَا عِبَادِىَ ٱلصَّٰلِحُونَ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">We have already written in the Zabur, after the Reminder, that My righteous servants shall inherit the earth.</p>
  <cite>Al-Anbiya (21:105)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p><strong>Al-Anbiya (21:105)</strong> gives the nearest thing to a direct citation, and even here the Quran offers a thematic summary rather than a long quoted passage. The content is covenantal and historical: the righteous inherit the earth. The verse suggests that the Zabur participates in the same moral arc that runs through the Quran itself. What matters is continuity of divine promise rather than textual display.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    وَلَقَدْ ءَاتَيْنَا دَاوُۥدَ مِنَّا فَضْلًا يَٰجِبَالُ أَوِّبِى مَعَهُۥ وَٱلطَّيْرَ وَأَلَنَّا لَهُ ٱلْحَدِيدَ أَنِ ٱعْمَلْ سَٰبِغَٰتٍ وَقَدِّرْ فِى ٱلسَّرْدِ وَٱعْمَلُوا۟ صَٰلِحًا إِنِّى بِمَا تَعْمَلُونَ بَصِيرٌ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">We certainly gave Dawud bounty from Us: “O mountains, repeat with him, and birds as well.” And We softened iron for him: “Make coats of armor and measure well in the links.”</p>
  <cite>Saba (34:10-11)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p>Here the Quran gives something like a sonic substitution for the unquoted book. Instead of long extracts from the Zabur, we receive a prophet whose praise makes mountains answer. The root <strong>s-b-ḥ</strong> in <strong>tasbih</strong> carries the sense of moving swiftly through an element, then glorifying by transcending limitation. Dawud’s relation to revelation becomes audible as praise. The mountains and birds do not quote the book. They participate in the atmosphere it has formed.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    ٱصْبِرْ عَلَىٰ مَا يَقُولُونَ وَٱذْكُرْ عَبْدَنَا دَاوُۥدَ ذَا ٱلْأَيْدِ إِنَّهُۥٓ أَوَّابٌ إِنَّا سَخَّرْنَا ٱلْجِبَالَ مَعَهُۥ يُسَبِّحْنَ بِٱلْعَشِىِّ وَٱلْإِشْرَاقِ وَٱلطَّيْرَ مَحْشُورَةً كُلٌّ لَّهُۥٓ أَوَّابٌ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">Bear patiently what they say, and remember Our servant Dawud, possessor of strength. Surely he was one who repeatedly turned back. We subjected the mountains to glorify with him at evening and sunrise, and the birds assembled, each returning to him.</p>
  <cite>Sad (38:17-19)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p><strong>Sad (38:17-19)</strong> reinforces the same picture. Dawud is a man of strength and constant return, <strong>awwāb</strong>, from the root <strong>ʾ-w-b</strong>, the sense of coming back again and again. The unquoted book stands behind this title. What the Quran emphasizes is the shape revelation took in him: return, praise, sonic bounty, responsive creation.</p>

  <p>What does the silence around the Zabur mean? At minimum, it means the Quran is free to honor a previous scripture without reproducing it extensively. More deeply, it means Dawud’s revelation is presented through its effects on the prophet’s body, voice, and world. The book’s presence is refracted through worship. The absence of quotation becomes a way of foregrounding praise as living scripture.</p>

  <p>This is why Dawud’s story feels so musical in the Quran. Revelation in his case is linked with voice, echo, rhythm, repetition, and responsive matter. Mountains become hearers. Birds become choristers. Iron itself becomes pliant under command. The prophet’s book remains mostly unwritten on the Quranic page, yet its atmosphere saturates the narrative.</p>
