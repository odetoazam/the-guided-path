<article class="prose-blog">
  <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed">The Quran twice gives <a href="/hub/dawud">Dawud</a> a gift that sounds like weather and worship at once. Mountains answer him. Birds gather into his praise. The text never pauses to describe the timbre of his voice or the mechanics of the response. It says only enough for the imagination to understand the scale of the favor. The bounty is sonic, and its content is glorification.</p>

  <p>The key word is <strong>faḍl</strong>, bounty or overflowing favor. A bounty can take the form of kingship, knowledge, victory, or wealth. In Dawud’s case one aspect of bounty is unmistakably acoustic. The world listens differently around him. The root <strong>s-b-ḥ</strong> in the language of glorification carries movement, transcendence, and praise. Dawud’s voice enters that current so fully that creation joins him in it.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    وَلَقَدْ ءَاتَيْنَا دَاوُۥدَ مِنَّا فَضْلًا يَٰجِبَالُ أَوِّبِى مَعَهُۥ وَٱلطَّيْرَ وَأَلَنَّا لَهُ ٱلْحَدِيدَ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">We certainly gave Dawud bounty from Us: “O mountains, repeat with him, and birds as well.”</p>
  <cite>Saba (34:10)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p><strong>Saba (34:10)</strong> places the command directly upon the mountains and the birds. The mountains, vast and seemingly mute, become resonant companions. The birds, creatures of air and song, gather into responsive praise. The verse does not tell us whether they mirror his words exactly or answer with their own mode of glorification. The absence of explanation leaves the event more spacious. What matters is participation.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    فَفَهَّمْنَٰهَا سُلَيْمَٰنَ وَكُلًّا ءَاتَيْنَا حُكْمًا وَعِلْمًا وَسَخَّرْنَا مَعَ دَاوُۥدَ ٱلْجِبَالَ يُسَبِّحْنَ وَٱلطَّيْرَ وَكُنَّا فَٰعِلِينَ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">We made the mountains and the birds subservient, glorifying with Dawud.</p>
  <cite>Al-Anbiya (21:79)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p>In <strong>Al-Anbiya (21:79)</strong>, the emphasis falls on subjection. Creation is made compliant in praise. The sound of Dawud’s worship becomes a point around which the world is ordered. This is not the prophet dominating nature for spectacle. It is the prophet standing in such harmony with divine glorification that the natural world is drawn into accord.</p>

  <blockquote class="ayah-quote">
  <p class="arabic" dir="rtl" style="font-family: var(--font-amiri,'Amiri'),serif; font-size: 1.5rem; line-height: 2.2; color: rgba(201,168,76,0.85);">
    ٱصْبِرْ عَلَىٰ مَا يَقُولُونَ وَٱذْكُرْ عَبْدَنَا دَاوُۥدَ ذَا ٱلْأَيْدِ إِنَّهُۥٓ أَوَّابٌ إِنَّا سَخَّرْنَا ٱلْجِبَالَ مَعَهُۥ يُسَبِّحْنَ بِٱلْعَشِىِّ وَٱلْإِشْرَاقِ وَٱلطَّيْرَ مَحْشُورَةً كُلٌّ لَّهُۥٓ أَوَّابٌ
  </p>
  <p class="translation">Remember Our servant Dawud, possessor of strength... We subjected the mountains to glorify with him at evening and sunrise, and the birds assembled, each turning back to him.</p>
  <cite>Sad (38:17-19)</cite>
</blockquote>

  <p><strong>Sad (38:17-19)</strong> adds time and recurrence. Evening and sunrise become the stations of this sonic liturgy. The birds are assembled, each one returning. The repeated return gives the scene its rhythm. Dawud’s voice is not an isolated marvel. It is a recurring pattern of worship, dawn and dusk, strength and softness together.</p>

  <p>What does it mean that creation participates in his worship? At one level, it means the cosmos is already inclined toward praise and Dawud’s gift is to stand at a point where that inclination becomes audible. The Quran often hints that all things glorify Allah, though human beings do not comprehend their glorification. With Dawud, that hidden law comes near the surface. The gap between human worship and cosmic worship narrows.</p>

  <p>It also means that voice in the Quran can be a mode of prophetic power. Some prophets are marked by staff, wind, healing, or speech to kings. Dawud is marked by sound that reorders the environment. His voice does not merely persuade listeners. It awakens an already praising creation to audible companionship.</p>

  <p>The Quran’s restraint here is wise. It says enough to disclose wonder and withholds enough to prevent the gift from collapsing into legend about vocal beauty alone. The point is not ornament. The point is a voice so aligned with remembrance that mountains and birds become its congregation. Sound becomes theology.</p>
