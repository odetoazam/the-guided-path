The Quran grants two figures sovereignty over vast domains: Sulayman and Dhul-Qarnayn. Both receive divine establishment — tamkeen — and both channel their power toward service. The Quran frames them in fundamentally different registers. Sulayman's sovereignty operates through the miraculous: he commands the wind, speaks with animals, directs the jinn as a labor force. Dhul-Qarnayn's sovereignty operates through the procedural: he travels, encounters, builds. Placed side by side, the two figures compose a complete Quranic theology of leadership — and the differences between them are as instructive as the similarities.

The Nature of the Gift

Sulayman's story begins with an inheritance that exceeds the ordinary:

وَوَرِثَ سُلَيْمَانُ دَاوُودَ ۖ وَقَالَ يَا أَيُّهَا النَّاسُ عُلِّمْنَا مَنطِقَ الطَّيْرِ وَأُوتِينَا مِن كُلِّ شَيْءٍ ۖ إِنَّ هَٰذَا لَهُوَ الْفَضْلُ الْمُبِينُ

"And Sulayman inherited Dawud. He said: O people, we have been taught the language of birds, and we have been given of everything. Indeed, this is the evident bounty."

Surah An-Naml (27:16)

Sulayman's phrase: utina min kulli shay' — "we have been given of everything." Dhul-Qarnayn's description: ataynahu min kulli shay'in sababa — "We gave him of everything a means" (18:84). The phrases run nearly parallel until the final word. Sulayman receives things — the language of birds, dominion over jinn, wind that travels at his command. Dhul-Qarnayn receives sababa — means, pathways, connective threads between intention and accomplishment. Sulayman holds the gift directly. Dhul-Qarnayn holds a rope attached to it.

The distinction separates two modes of divine provision. Sulayman's gifts are miraculous — they bypass the ordinary causal chain. A human being speaks with birds. Invisible beings submit to his authority. Wind carries his throne across distances. Dhul-Qarnayn's gifts operate within the causal chain — extraordinary capacity, enormous resources, the strategic and logistical means to reach the ends of the earth, but all of it accomplished through natural effort. Both are divine provision. The Quran does not rank them. It presents them as two channels through which Allah equips a sovereign for the work he is meant to do.

The Relationship to Power

Sulayman asks for his kingdom. After a period of testing in which his throne is briefly lost, he prays:

قَالَ رَبِّ اغْفِرْ لِي وَهَبْ لِي مُلْكًا لَّا يَنبَغِي لِأَحَدٍ مِّن بَعْدِي ۖ إِنَّكَ أَنتَ الْوَهَّابُ

"He said: My Lord, forgive me and grant me a kingdom such as will not belong to anyone after me. Indeed, You are the Bestower."

Surah Sad (38:35)

The prayer opens with ighfir li — "forgive me" — grounding the request in repentance. Then the ask: a kingdom unmatched by any that follows. The word al-Wahhab — the Bestower — closes the sentence, placing the request entirely in the hands of the one who gives. Sulayman's relationship to power is conscious and articulate. He knows what he wants, says it aloud, frames it as a gift from the divine, and receives it. His sovereignty is deliberately sought and deliberately attributed.

Dhul-Qarnayn's relationship to power contains no recorded prayer, no request, no negotiation. The Quran's grammar places him as the object of divine action: makkanna lahu — "We established him." He appears already in possession, already deployed. When the people between the mountains offer him kharj (tribute), his refusal — "that in which my Lord has established me is better" — reveals a man who thinks of his power as divine provision he administers, a trust he carries rather than a kingdom he possesses. He does not say "my kingdom is sufficient." He says "what my Lord established me in is better." The Lord remains the grammatical subject even when Dhul-Qarnayn describes his own resources.

Sulayman possesses openly; Dhul-Qarnayn stewards quietly. Both positions are righteous. The Quran validates the sovereign who asks for unparalleled dominion and the sovereign who receives dominion without asking — because both anchor their authority in the same source.

How They Build

Both sovereigns are builders, and the Quran describes their constructions with specificity that rewards comparison.

Sulayman commands the jinn to build "structures and images, basins like reservoirs, and stationary kettles" (34:13). He constructs a palace with a floor of glass so transparent that the Queen of Sheba mistakes it for water and lifts her garments to wade through it (27:44). The glass floor serves a theological purpose — it produces the moment of disorientation that leads Bilqis to recognize monotheism. Sulayman's building carries aesthetic and persuasive dimensions. The structures are spectacular because the spectacle does work: it draws a polytheistic queen toward submission to Allah.

Dhul-Qarnayn builds a barrier of iron and copper between two mountains. The materials are industrial. The function is protective. There is no ornamentation, no glass, no visual rhetoric. The barrier exists to seal a passage, and the Quran describes its construction in terms of engineering steps — bring the iron, fill the gap, stoke the fire, pour the copper. When it is finished, Dhul-Qarnayn does not display it. He calls it mercy and announces its eventual destruction.

Sulayman builds for need and for glory — and the glory serves need, because it is the vehicle through which Bilqis arrives at la ilaha illa Allah. Dhul-Qarnayn builds for need alone. The Quran presents both approaches without ranking them. What matters in each case is the builder's orientation: Sulayman's spectacular constructions point toward Allah's power; Dhul-Qarnayn's functional construction points toward Allah's mercy. The aesthetic register differs. The theological direction is the same.

The Theology of Endings

The deepest difference lies in how each narrative handles its close.

Sulayman's death is one of the most striking scenes in the Quran. He dies leaning on his staff, and the jinn — who were building under his command — continue their labor, unaware their master has passed. The body remains upright, propped by the staff. Only when a creature of the earth (dabbat al-ard) gnaws through the wood and the body falls do the jinn realize the truth. The Quran draws the lesson explicitly: fa-lamma kharra tabayyanat al-jinnu an law kanu ya'lamuna al-ghayba ma labithu fi al-'adhab al-muhin — "when he fell, it became clear to the jinn that if they had known the unseen, they would not have remained in humiliating servitude" (34:14). Sulayman's death is an argument about the limits of knowledge. Even the jinn, who served a prophet-king, possessed no access to the unseen. The body falling, the staff eaten through — these are the Quran's evidence for a theological proposition about who holds knowledge of the hidden.

Dhul-Qarnayn does not die in the narrative. His story ends with his declaration over the completed barrier: "The promise of my Lord is ever true." The Quran transitions immediately from this statement to the eschatological scene of Ya'juj and Ma'juj's eventual release and the approaching Day of Judgment (18:99). His exit is theological rather than biographical. His final words already contain the truth of mortality — everything he built will be destroyed, and divine promise is the only permanence. The narrative does not need to stage his death because his last sentence has already dissolved the distance between the present moment and the end of time.

Sulayman's death teaches through dramatic image — the body propped on the staff, the worm's slow gnawing, the moment of collapse. Dhul-Qarnayn's ending teaches through declarative faith — a sentence spoken over copper and iron that reaches forward to the Day of Judgment itself.

Two Models, One Principle

Sulayman: sovereign-prophet, miraculous gifts, kingdom explicitly requested, buildings of glass and gold, death staged as a lesson about the unseen. Dhul-Qarnayn: sovereign-servant, natural means, power received without petition, a barrier of iron and copper, ending framed as a statement of faith in divine promise.

The unifying principle is that sovereignty is a trust. Sulayman names his gifts al-fadl al-mubin — "the evident bounty." Dhul-Qarnayn names his achievement rahma min Rabbi — "mercy from my Lord." Both attribute what they have to the one who gave it. Both know it can be withdrawn. Both deploy it in service of something larger than their own reigns.

The Quran presents them as complementary rather than competing. Some people receive miraculous capacities and must hold them with gratitude and public attribution. Others receive natural means and pathways and must direct them with integrity and self-effacement. The trial is the same in both cases: will the sovereign remember the source of his sovereignty? Will he build for mercy or for monument? Will he hold his accomplishments as permanent possessions or as provisional expressions of a will beyond his own?

In validating both Sulayman and Dhul-Qarnayn, the Quran establishes that faithful leadership has no single template. The style of sovereignty varies. The orientation does not. Both faces turn toward Allah. Both hands serve. Both voices attribute. The Quran asks for nothing more.
