MORPHOLOGICAL FLAGS:
- أَحَدٌ vs. وَاحِدٌ: ahad (أ-ح-د) is NOT the same as wahid (و-ح-د). Wahid = numerical one (first of a potential sequence). Ahad = uniquely, categorically one — removes the entity from any genus or category. In negative contexts ahad means "anyone at all." The surah opens with it as divine attribute and closes with it as universal negation.
- ٱلصَّمَدُ: fa'al pattern (like حَسَد, شَرَف) indicates a permanent, intrinsic quality — not a state that comes and goes. The root ص-م-د carries: (a) the one to whom others turn (samada ilayhi = headed toward him for needs), (b) the solid, impenetrable, without internal cavity. These are complementary: everything goes TO Him because there is nothing hollow IN Him.
- يَلِدْ (active) → يُولَدْ (passive): same root و-ل-د, same verb form, opposite voice. Active negation seals the future (no offspring proceeds from Him). Passive negation seals the past (no origin precedes Him). Together: no cause before, no consequence after.
- كُفُوًا (fronted predicate): Normal word order would be لَمْ يَكُن أَحَدٌ كُفُوًا لَهُ. The fronting of kufuwan before ahad creates emphasis — the equivalence itself is what is categorically denied.

ABSENCE FLAGS:
- 112:1 — قُلْ هُوَ ٱللَّهُ أَحَدٌ: no further elaboration of WHAT ahad means. The word is dropped and left to stand alone. No simile, no "like" — because any comparison would contradict the very claim being made.
- 112:2 — ٱللَّهُ ٱلصَّمَدُ: no verb. Pure nominal sentence (jumlah ismiyyah). No action, no time frame. The absence of any verb is the grammar of permanence — this is not something Allah does, it is what He IS.
- 112:3-4 — Three consecutive لَمْ negations: the surah moves from two positive descriptions (ayahs 1-2) to three negations (ayahs 3-4). The final half of the surah is ENTIRELY removal. The description of God is completed by describing what He is NOT.

ADDRESS FLAGS:
- 112:1 — قُلْ is second person singular imperative to the Prophet ﷺ. But after "Say," the rest of the surah is third person about Allah. The Prophet is the deliverer, not the author.
- The asbab al-nuzul places this as a response to Quraysh (or, in some narrations, the Jews of Medina) asking the Prophet ﷺ to describe his Lord's lineage/ancestry (nasab). The surah answers a genealogical question with an ontological answer — the questioners asked the wrong kind of question.

SONIC FLAGS:

## INTRODUCTION

Somebody walked up to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and asked him to describe his Lord.

Not what his Lord *wants* or what his Lord *commands* — what his Lord *is*. Describe His lineage. His ancestry. What is He made of? Who came before Him? Who comes from Him?

The question wasn't hostile. It was the only framework they had. In seventh-century Arabia, you understood a person through their tribe, their father, their offspring, their alliances. Identity was relational. You are the son of so-and-so, from the clan of such-and-such, and your worth is partly measured by who you're connected to.

So they applied the same framework upward: tell us about your God the way you'd tell us about a man. Where does He come from? What has He produced? Who is He comparable to?

And the answer that came down was four ayahs.

Four ayahs that the Prophet ﷺ said equal a third of the Quran. Four ayahs that didn't answer the question asked — because the question itself was malformed. Four ayahs that do something no human theology before or since has done with such compression: they define God not by building an image of Him, but by systematically dismantling every image you could possibly build.

Here's what we're going to discover: **this surah doesn't tell you what God looks like. It tells you what to stop imagining.** And the empty space it leaves — the space where no picture can survive — is the only space large enough for the Real.

## PART 1: THE LINGUISTIC JOURNEY

### I. The Word That Takes God Off the Number Line

<!-- morphology:112:1:4 root=أحد pos=N -->

Let's start with the last word of the first ayah, because everything depends on it.

[ayah:112:1] قُلْ هُوَ ٱللَّهُ **أَحَدٌ**

*Aḥad.* One.

Except — it doesn't mean "one." Not in the way you think.

Stay with me here, because this distinction is the key that unlocks the entire surah.

Arabic has two words for "one." The word most people know is *wāḥid* — وَاحِدٌ. *Wāḥid* is a number. It sits on a number line. One, two, three — *wāḥid* is the first position in a sequence. When you say "I have one cup of coffee," that's *wāḥid*. There's one. There could have been two. The possibility of a second is built into the grammar.

But that's not the word Allah chose.

He chose **أَحَدٌ**. *Aḥad.* And *aḥad* does something *wāḥid* cannot.

[PAUSE]

Imagine a number line. Zero, one, two, three, stretching out to infinity. Now, *wāḥid* is a point on that line. It's the point marked "1." It's one — but it's one *among potential others*. The line continues past it. Two is right there, waiting.

*Aḥad* doesn't sit on the number line.

*Aḥad* **picks the entity up off the line entirely.**

It doesn't say "God is the first of a sequence." It says God is not the type of being that *belongs on a sequence*. He is not one-out-of-many. He is not one-instead-of-two. He is not the numerical beginning of a category that happens to contain only one member. He is **categorically, ontologically, uniquely singular** — singular in a way that doesn't leave room for the question "could there be a second?"

This is why the scholars said: *wāḥid* negates plurality. *Aḥad* negates the very *possibility* of plurality.

[PAUSE]

Now here's where it gets personal.

Think about how we talk about God in casual conversation. "I believe in one God." Monotheism. One God instead of many. That sounds right. But linguistically, that's *wāḥid* theology — it places God at position one on a number line where other gods *could* theoretically occupy positions two and three but happen not to exist.

*Aḥad* theology says: there is no number line. The category "gods" doesn't have multiple slots, one of which happens to be filled. There is no genus, no species, no class to which God belongs and of which He is the sole member. **He is not a member of anything.** He is not the best of His kind. He has no kind.

When someone asks "how many gods are there?" the correct *aḥad* answer is not "one." It is: **your question cannot be answered because it assumes a category that doesn't exist.**

[PAUSE]

Notice how soft the word sounds. *Aḥad.* Alif, ḥā', dāl. It doesn't thunder. It doesn't roar. The most radical claim in the history of theology — a claim that obliterates every pantheon, every trinity, every dualism, every hierarchy of divine beings that humanity has ever constructed — and it's delivered in three consonants so gentle they barely leave the mouth.

The gentleness is deliberate. This isn't an argument being won. This is a reality being disclosed. Arguments are loud. Reality just *is.*

And this word will come back. Remember it. We'll find it waiting at the very end of the surah, doing something entirely different with the same letters.

### II. The Sealed Word

<!-- morphology:112:2:2 root=صمد pos=N -->

[ayah:112:2] ٱللَّهُ **ٱلصَّمَدُ**

The second ayah is two words. No verb. No action. No time frame.

Let's talk about that absence first, because the grammar is doing theology.

In Arabic, there are two kinds of sentences. A *jumlah fi'liyyah* — verbal sentence — has a verb, and a verb lives in time. It happened, it is happening, it will happen. A *jumlah ismiyyah* — nominal sentence — has no verb. It makes a statement about what something *is*, not what it *does*. And a statement without a verb floats above the timeline. It is not past or present or future. It simply *is*.

[ayah:112:2] ٱللَّهُ ٱلصَّمَدُ is a nominal sentence. **There is no verb.** Allah is al-Ṣamad. Not "Allah was" or "Allah becomes" or "Allah acts as" — just: **is.** The grammar refuses to let this attribute be temporary, conditional, or enacted. It is His nature, not His behavior.

[PAUSE]

Now. The word itself.

**ٱلصَّمَدُ** — *al-Ṣamad.*

This is one of the most heavily discussed single words in the Quran. The classical scholars gave it layer after layer of meaning, and what's remarkable is that they weren't disagreeing — they were each seeing a different face of the same jewel. Let me build those layers for you.

**First layer — Ibn Abbas,** رضي الله عنه, the Prophet's own cousin, the one the Prophet ﷺ prayed would understand the Quran: al-Ṣamad is **the one to whom all creation turns in their needs.** The root ص-م-د carries the meaning *ṣamada ilayhi* — he headed toward him, he sought him out. Everything in existence — every atom, every organism, every galaxy — is *heading toward* al-Ṣamad. Not metaphorically. Structurally. Every need in the universe has a direction, and that direction is Him.

**Second layer — Ikrimah** and others: al-Ṣamad is **the one with no internal void.** No cavity. No emptiness inside. Think about that: every created thing has a hollow. Your body has cavities. Your heart has chambers. Your life has gaps. Even mountains have caves. Even atoms have space between the nucleus and the electrons — the vast majority of "solid" matter is actually empty space. But al-Ṣamad has no void. Nothing missing. Nothing incomplete. Nothing that could be filled by something external because there is nothing empty that needs filling.

**Third layer:** Put the first two together and feel what happens. Why does everything head toward Him? *Because everything else has a cavity and He doesn't.* The universe is full of beings with holes — holes in their knowledge, their power, their sustenance, their peace — and all of those holes are oriented toward the one Being with no hole at all. Need flows toward the needless. Emptiness flows toward the full. That is the architecture of existence, compressed into one word.

[PAUSE]

Now listen to the word.

**Ṣamad.** ص — ṣād, an emphatic sibilant that starts heavy and sealed. م — mīm, a nasal that closes the mouth. د — dāl, a dental stop.

Sealed. Closed. Impenetrable.

**Say it and pay attention to what your mouth does.** The ṣād forces air through a narrow, heavy channel. The mīm shuts the lips. The dāl taps the tongue against the teeth and stops. There is no release. No open vowel at the end. The word sounds exactly like what it means: **something nothing can get into.** The phonetics are the theology.

Compare this to *aḥad* — which opened softly, gently, with an alif and a breathy ḥā'. *Aḥad* disclosed. *Ṣamad* seals. One invites you to understand His uniqueness. The other tells you that His self-sufficiency is a closed door — not because He's withholding, but because there is nothing to withhold. There is no lack behind the door. There is no door.

[PAUSE]

And here is why this ayah matters so much architecturally: **al-Ṣamad is the reason everything that follows must be true.**

Watch what happens in ayahs 3 and 4. He doesn't beget. He wasn't begotten. No one equals Him. Those sound like three separate claims. But they're not. They are three *consequences* of al-Ṣamad.

If He is the one with no internal void — then He doesn't beget. Begetting requires an overflow, a division, a piece of the parent passing into the child. Al-Ṣamad doesn't divide.

If He is the one everything turns to — then He wasn't begotten. A begotten being would depend on the one who produced it. Al-Ṣamad depends on nothing.

If He is the ultimate terminus of all need — then nothing can equal Him. An equal would mean two beings at the terminus. But the terminus, by definition, is singular — otherwise it's not the terminus.

*Ṣamad* isn't just a divine attribute. It is the **logical engine** of the surah. Everything after it is already contained inside it. Ayahs 3 and 4 don't add new information — they unfold what was already compressed into this single, sealed word.

### III. The Gate That Slams Three Times

<!-- morphology:112:3:4 root=ولد pos=V -->
<!-- morphology:112:4:4 root=كفأ pos=N -->
<!-- morphology:112:4:5 root=أحد pos=N -->

[ayah:112:3] لَمْ يَلِدْ وَلَمْ يُولَدْ
[ayah:112:4] وَلَمْ يَكُن لَّهُۥ كُفُوًا أَحَدٌۢ

Three times: **لَمْ**. *Lam.* Never. Did. Not.

*Lam yalid.* He never begot.
*Wa-lam yūlad.* And He was never begotten.
*Wa-lam yakun.* And there has never been.

Listen to the rhythm. Each *lam* is a door shutting. Each negation closes off a corridor of thought that the human mind tries to walk down when it thinks about God. And the surah shuts them, one by one, with percussive finality.

[PAUSE]

Let's take the first two. Notice something exquisite: **يَلِدْ** and **يُولَدْ** are the same root. و-ل-د. The same word — *walada*, to give birth, to produce. But the first is active voice and the second is passive.

He doesn't beget. Active — He doesn't produce offspring. No divine son. No divine daughter. No emanation. No fragment of Himself that separates and becomes a lesser divinity. Whatever Christian theology, Greek philosophy, Hindu cosmology, or modern New Age spirituality has proposed about God producing lesser beings from His own substance — *lam yalid.* Sealed forward.

He was not begotten. Passive — He is not the product of a prior cause. No parent deity. No primordial chaos that generated Him. No cosmic process that resulted in His existence. Whatever origin story the human mind has tried to construct — a god emerging from an ocean, from an egg, from a war between older gods — *lam yūlad.* Sealed backward.

One root. Two voices. **Both ends of the timeline closed.** Nothing proceeds from Him, and nothing precedes Him. He is not a link in a chain. He is not part of a lineage. He has no genealogy — which is exactly what the questioners asked for, and exactly what the surah refuses to provide.

[PAUSE]

And then the final ayah. The third gate.

[ayah:112:4] وَلَمْ يَكُن لَّهُۥ **كُفُوًا** أَحَدٌۢ

And here the Arabic does something subtle and devastating.

Normal word order in this sentence would be: *lam yakun **aḥadun** kufuwan lahu* — "no one has ever been his equal." Subject first, then predicate. Standard.

But Allah **fronted** *kufuwan* — the word for "equal" — and pushed *aḥadun* to the end.

In Arabic rhetoric, when you front a word, you throw the spotlight on it. You're saying: **this is the thing I'm negating.** Not just "no one equals Him" but "in the matter of *equivalence itself* — no. Not anyone. The very concept of parity, applied to Him, is null."

It's the difference between saying "nobody beat him in the race" and saying "*winning against him* — nobody, ever." The fronting makes the negation absolute, categorical, final.

[PAUSE]

And now — **أَحَدٌۢ**.

The same word from ayah 1.

The surah opened: **قُلْ هُوَ ٱللَّهُ أَحَدٌ** — He is Allah, the One.

The surah closes: **وَلَمْ يَكُن لَّهُۥ كُفُوًا أَحَدٌۢ** — and there has never been equal to Him, *anyone*.

The first *aḥad* was His name. It defined Him. He is the uniquely One.

The last *aḥad* is everyone else. In negative context, *aḥad* means "anyone at all" — *no one whatsoever.*

**The word that defined His uniqueness in the first ayah returns in the last ayah to guard it.** It's a ring. The surah begins and ends with the same letters, but the first time they exalt Him and the last time they exclude everything else. *Aḥad* as identity. *Aḥad* as perimeter. He is the One — and no one is His one.

The gate is closed. The ring is sealed.

## PART 2: THE THEMATIC DEPTHS

### I. The Theology of Subtraction

We live in a culture that builds God up.

"God is like a father." "God is like a king." "God is love." "God is energy." "God is the universe experiencing itself." Every spiritual bestseller, every feel-good sermon, every Instagram post with a sunset and a caption about the divine — they all do the same thing: they take a human concept, something familiar and warm, and they project it upward. They make God *conceivable*. Graspable. Something you can hold in your mind the way you hold a mug of coffee — shaped, bounded, comfortable.

And it feels good. It feels like understanding. When someone says "God is like a loving father," you nod, because you know what a father feels like — the protection, the warmth, the arms around you. And that's not wrong, exactly. There are divine attributes that include those qualities. *Al-Wadūd,* the Loving. *Al-Ḥafīẓ,* the Protector. Those are real names, and they carry real warmth.

But here's the problem.

**A father begets.** Ayah 3 negates that.
**A king has rivals.** Ayah 4 negates that.
**Love, as humans experience it, requires an object — it needs someone to love.** Ayah 2 negates need.
**Energy is a property of the physical universe.** Ayah 1 removes Him from every category the physical universe contains.

Every analogy you build, this surah dismantles. Every image you construct, these four ayahs disassemble. Not cruelly — carefully. The way a surgeon removes something that doesn't belong. The analogies aren't evil. They're just *insufficient*. And insufficiency, when applied to Allah, becomes distortion.

[PAUSE]

This is what the scholars call *tanzīh* — transcendence. The theological discipline of affirming that God is beyond every comparison, beyond every image, beyond every concept the human mind generates. And Al-Ikhlas is the most compressed tanzīh in existence.

Here's what's beautiful about this: **the surah doesn't replace your images with better images.** It doesn't say "God is not like a father — He is like *this* instead." It just... removes. *Aḥad* — not countable. *Ṣamad* — not needy. *Lam yalid* — not generative in the way you understand generation. *Lam yūlad* — not caused. *Lam yakun lahū kufuwan aḥad* — not comparable.

And after all four negations, what image are you left with?

**None.**

[PAUSE]

This is where most people get uncomfortable. The modern spiritual seeker wants an image. Wants to *feel* God. Wants a mental picture they can carry into meditation, into prayer, into their difficult nights. And this surah takes that away.

But consider this: what if the discomfort of having no image is not a failure of the surah — but its gift?

Think about it with a simple analogy. If you've ever been to the ocean at night — not a beach with streetlights and boardwalk music, but a real coast, somewhere remote, with no light pollution — and you look out at the water, you can't see the horizon. You can't see where the ocean ends and the sky begins. You hear it. You feel the wind from it. You smell the salt. But your eyes have nothing to hold onto. The ocean is *there* — overwhelmingly, undeniably there — but it refuses to be contained by your vision.

That moment is closer to tawhid than any picture you could draw.

**The inability to picture God is not ignorance. It is the highest knowledge.** Because any picture small enough to fit in your mind is too small to be Him. Al-Ikhlas clears the gallery walls. It takes down every painting. And what's left is not an empty room — it's a room whose walls have been removed entirely, opening onto something without borders.

[PAUSE]

The Prophet ﷺ said this surah equals a third of the Quran. The scholars have offered many explanations for why. One of the most compelling is this: the Quran addresses three subjects — *aḥkām* (rulings for human life), *qiṣaṣ* (narratives of those who came before), and *tawḥīd* (the nature of God). Al-Ikhlas is the complete, undiluted statement of the third. Not one-third in length. One-third in *weight*. The entire theological project of the Quran — thousands of ayahs pointing toward the nature of the Divine — compressed into fifteen words.

Fifteen words. Four ayahs. Two affirmations and three negations.

And the negations outnumber the affirmations. Because when it comes to God, **telling you what to stop imagining is more important than telling you what to start imagining.** The subtraction is the lesson. The empty space is the cathedral.

### II. The Mirror You Didn't Know You Were Looking Into

Now let's turn the surah around.

Everything we've discussed so far has been about Allah. *Aḥad* — His uniqueness. *Ṣamad* — His self-sufficiency. *Lam yalid* — no offspring. *Lam yūlad* — no origin. *Lam yakun lahū kufuwan aḥad* — no equal.

But here's what I want you to notice: **every single attribute this surah denies to Allah is an attribute that defines you.**

[PAUSE]

He is *aḥad* — uniquely, categorically one. Not composite, not assembled from parts, not dependent on components working together.

You? You are the opposite of *aḥad*. You are a coalition. Your body is trillions of cells, any one of which can rebel. Your mind is a parliament of competing desires. Your identity is assembled from your parents' DNA, your culture's influence, your trauma's residue, your choices' consequences. You are not one thing. You are a negotiation between many things, held together by something you don't fully control.

He is *al-Ṣamad* — no internal void, no need, nothing missing.

You? You are a walking collection of needs. You need oxygen every few seconds. You need food every few hours. You need sleep every day. You need love — not as a luxury but as a biological imperative; infants who are fed but not held literally die from the absence of touch. You need meaning. You need purpose. You need someone to tell you, on the days when you can't see it yourself, that your existence matters. You are cavity after cavity after cavity, and every moment of your life is spent trying to fill them.

He was not begotten and does not beget — not part of any chain, any lineage, any before-and-after.

You? You are nothing *but* lineage. You are your mother's eyes and your father's stubbornness and your grandmother's fear of scarcity. You will, God willing, produce children who carry fragments of you forward. You are a link in a chain that stretches back to Adam and forward until the end. You cannot be understood apart from what came before you and what will come after you.

No one equals Him.

You? You are surrounded by equals. By competitors. By people who do what you do, want what you want, occupy the space you thought was yours. You are replaceable — not as a human soul, but in every functional role you've ever held. The company would survive without you. The friend group would adjust. Even your family, eventually, would learn to carry the weight differently. You are not al-Ṣamad. Things don't collapse when you're absent. The universe does not orient itself toward your doorstep.

[PAUSE]

This isn't meant to diminish you. Listen carefully, because this is the turn.

**The mirror is not there to humiliate you. It's there to liberate you.**

Because here's what happens when you *don't* see the mirror: you start trying to be *aḥad.* You start trying to be the only one. The unique one. The irreplaceable one. You build personal brands and curate identities and fight to distinguish yourself from seven billion people because somewhere, unconsciously, you believe that your worth depends on your uniqueness. Social media is an engine built on this anxiety: *be aḥad or be invisible.*

And it's exhausting. Because you're not aḥad. You never will be. And the exhaustion of pretending — of performing uniqueness, of fearing that someone will do what you do but better, of guarding your territory — that exhaustion is what happens when a being made of need tries to inhabit an attribute that belongs only to the Needless.

Al-Ikhlas says: **stop.**

There is one Being who is *aḥad*, and it is not you. There is one Being who is *al-Ṣamad*, and it is not you. And the moment you stop trying to be what only He is — the moment you accept your composite, needy, begotten, comparable nature — something extraordinary happens.

**You become free to be what you actually are: a creation. A deliberate, willed, purposeful creation — not despite your neediness but through it.**

Because your cavities — your need for air, food, love, meaning, God — are not design flaws. They are *connection points.* They are the doors through which sustenance enters. They are the architecture of *'ubūdiyyah* — servanthood. You have needs, and He is al-Ṣamad. You have voids, and He is the one without voids. Your emptiness and His fullness are not a tragedy. They are a relationship. The most fundamental relationship in existence.

Every need you have is a thread connecting you to the Needless. Every void in you is a hand extended toward the one with no void. The cavities are not the problem. Forgetting *whose* cavities they are — that's the problem.

[PAUSE]

So the surah that seems to be entirely about God is, in every line, also about you. It defines the Creator, and in the negative space, it sketches the creation. Not as an afterthought. As a mercy. Because you cannot know your place in the universe until you know who occupies the center of it — and the center is al-Ṣamad, not you.

And that is the most comforting realization a human being can have. You don't have to be the center. You don't have to be self-sufficient. You don't have to be incomparable. You don't have to be uncaused and unconditioned and complete.

You just have to know who is.

## CLOSING SYNTHESIS

### The Architecture

Step back now and look at the surah whole.

Four ayahs. The first two are **affirmations**: He is *Aḥad.* He is *al-Ṣamad.*

The last two — which contain three negations — are **subtractions**: He does not beget, He was not begotten, no one equals Him.

**The surah moves from positive to negative.** It tells you two things about what He IS, and then spends its remaining breath telling you what He is NOT. The negations get more space. The removal outweighs the installation.

And at the very center, the hinge between affirmation and negation, sits **al-Ṣamad** — the attribute that makes every negation inevitable. He doesn't beget *because* He is Ṣamad (the impenetrable doesn't divide). He wasn't begotten *because* He is Ṣamad (the self-sufficient has no prior cause). No one equals Him *because* He is Ṣamad (the one everything turns to cannot have a peer; two Ṣamads is an impossibility — if everything flows toward one, it cannot simultaneously flow toward another).

*Al-Ṣamad* is not just the second ayah. It is the **load-bearing wall.** Remove it, and the structure of the surah collapses. Leave it, and every other ayah is already contained inside it.

And then: the ring. **أَحَدٌ** — the surah's first theological word. **أَحَدٌۢ** — the surah's last word. Same three letters. The first declares His identity. The last guards it. Between them: everything the human mind might use to approach God — need, lineage, comparison — dismantled and cleared away.

The surah begins where it ends. Because with *Aḥad*, there is nowhere else to go.

### Questions to Carry

Where in your life are you exhausting yourself trying to be *aḥad* — the only, the irreplaceable, the unique — when that attribute was never meant to be yours?

What image of God are you still carrying that this surah would gently remove? Not because the image is evil — but because it's too small?

If you truly internalized al-Ṣamad — that He has no void and you have nothing but voids — how would your relationship with your own neediness change? Would you still be ashamed of needing, or would you see each need as a thread tied to the Needless?

When you recite this surah tonight — and you recite it often, perhaps without thinking — what will you hear differently?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Al-Ikhlas doesn't describe God — it removes everything you'd use to describe anyone else, and the space that remains is the only space large enough for the Real.**

---

### The Closing Invitation

This surah was given as an answer to people who wanted God to fit inside their categories. Father, son, ancestor, peer — the categories of the familiar, the manageable, the conceivable.

The answer was: no.

Not a harsh no. A liberating one. Because a God who fits inside your categories is a God who is as limited as your categories. And a limited God is no refuge at all.

Tonight, when you stand in prayer and recite *Qul huwa-llāhu Aḥad*, let it be an act of release. Release every image. Release every analogy. Release the need to *conceive* of Him in order to *connect* with Him. Let the surah do its ancient work — clearing the gallery, taking down the paintings, removing the walls — until all that's left is a you that is nothing but need, standing before a Him that is nothing but sufficiency.

That is enough. That is everything.
