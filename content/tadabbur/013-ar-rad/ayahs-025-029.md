# What They Break Corrupts Them, and What They Remember Settles the Heart

## Surah Ar-Ra'd (13:25-29)

## Introduction

Ar-Ra'd has just shown us the people of understanding in one of the Quran's most beautiful ethical portraits. They keep covenant, join what must be joined, endure for Allah's face, pray, spend, and repel evil with good. Then the angels enter upon them in Jannah and say: peace be upon you for what you patiently endured.

Now the surah turns the portrait around.

These next ayahs show us what the opposite life looks like. It is a life of breaking after binding, cutting after command, and corruption spreading outward from inner severance. Then the surah does something subtle and deeply needed: it refuses to let worldly provision be misread as proof of divine approval, refuses to let the demand for more spectacle be mistaken for sincerity, and then places the famous verse of the resting heart right here, inside this same argument.

That placement matters. The Quran does not give us tranquility as an isolated devotional slogan. It gives it after showing us broken bonds, distorted readings of worldly abundance, and endless demands for one more sign. Then it says: the heart settles by the remembrance of Allah.

The hook of the passage is this: **Ar-Ra'd teaches that the heart loses peace when it breaks what Allah bound and misreads dunya as proof, but it finds settledness again when it returns to Allah, remembers Him, and lets the truth reorder its bonds and desires.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Yanquduna ... yaqta'una ... yufsiduna**: Misguidance Appears First as Unraveling

The passage opens by naming the opposite of the people we just met.

They **break** the covenant of Allah after its binding.

They **cut** what Allah commanded to be joined.

They **spread corruption** in the earth.

[PAUSE]

The sequence matters. Corruption does not begin at its widest visible level. It begins with breach, then severance, then spread.

First comes **yanquduna** - they unravel or break what was held in pledged firmness. The image is not only legal failure. It carries the sense of undoing what had been fastened.

Then comes **yaqta'una** - they cut what Allah ordered to be joined.

And only then:

**yufsiduna fi al-ard**

they corrupt in the earth.

Ar-Ra'd is teaching us something difficult here. Public corruption often begins in private severance. A person does not usually wake up one morning and leap straight into "فساد في الأرض". They first betray a covenant, then they normalize cutting what should have remained tied, and only after that does the disorder widen outward.

This is why the surah's moral center began with covenant and joining. Those were not secondary manners. They were structural protections against corruption itself.

And then comes the judgment:

**al-la'nah**

the curse, the being driven far away.

The tragedy is fitting. They distanced themselves from what Allah had bound close, so the end named for them is distance from mercy.

### 2. **Yabsutu al-rizqa ... wa yaqdir**: Provision Is Not a Reliable Measure of Nearness

Then the surah shifts to rizq:

Allah expands provision for whom He wills and restricts it.

And what do these people do?

They rejoice in worldly life.

[PAUSE]

That does not mean every joy in dunya is condemned. The issue is not that they ate, married, built, earned, or enjoyed. The issue is interpretive. They read worldly expansion as though it settled the bigger question.

The two verbs matter:

**yabsutu**

He spreads out, extends, opens wide.

**yaqdir**

He constricts, measures tightly, restricts.

Both are in Allah's hand. So abundance and constriction are not self-explanatory signs of ultimate favor or abandonment. They may be mercy, testing, discipline, provision, exposure, or preparation. The surah refuses the shallow reading in which visible expansion automatically means divine pleasure.

Then comes the scale-correction:

**wa ma al-hayatu al-dunya fi al-akhirati illa mata'**

The worldly life, against the Hereafter, is only passing enjoyment.

Not unreal. Not nonexistent. But **mata'** - temporary use, a thing enjoyed for a while and then left behind.

This is one of the heart's major illnesses. We do not only love dunya too much. We keep reading it as though it carries final verdicts. Ar-Ra'd breaks that illusion.

### 3. **Yahdi ilayhi man anab ... tatma'innu al-qulub**: The Heart Rests Not by More Proof, But by Return and Remembrance

Then the old objection appears again:

Why was no sign sent down to him from his Lord?

The disbelievers still want one more ayah.

The answer is not: here is a new spectacle.

The answer is:

Allah leads astray whom He wills and guides to Himself whoever **anab**.

[PAUSE]

That last verb is precious.

**anab**

He turned back. He returned. He came again toward Allah.

So the surah relocates the entire discussion. Guidance is not finally blocked by lack of spectacle. It is opened by return.

Then the next ayah explains who these people are:

those who believe,

and whose hearts find rest in the remembrance of Allah.

The verb **tatma'innu** carries the sense of becoming settled, still, composed after disturbance. Classical tafsir glosses it with language of stillness and intimate ease. The heart not only ceases panic; it becomes at home.

Then the ayah repeats itself in one of the Quran's most decisive forms:

**Ala bi-dhikri Allahi tatma'innu al-qulub**

Truly, by the remembrance of Allah do hearts find rest.

The repetition matters because the surah wants this fixed beyond negotiation. Not by expanded provision. Not by public applause. Not by endless new proofs tailored to every demand of the ego. Not by getting the world arranged exactly how the self imagines peace should look. By the remembrance of Allah.

Then the movement closes:

**Tuba lahum wa husnu ma'ab**

Goodness, blessedness, a wholesome joy for them, and a beautiful return.

The return matters because this whole cluster has been about misreading where things lead. Dunya is not the final reading. The real reading is in the **ma'ab**, the place of return.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Hearts Become Unstable When They Treat Severance as Normal

We often think of restlessness as mainly emotional. We imagine the troubled heart is only the anxious heart, the overworked heart, the grieving heart, the uncertain heart.

But Ar-Ra'd shows another layer. A heart also becomes unstable when it lives in contradiction to the bonds Allah made morally real.

When a person gets used to broken promises, neglected rights, abandoned ties, and casual betrayal, the damage does not stay "relational" as though it were happening in a separate compartment. The inner world itself begins to fray. A soul cannot repeatedly normalize severance and then expect deep inward peace to remain untouched.

[PAUSE]

That is one reason 13:28 is so often quoted but so rarely left in its original neighborhood. The verse of tranquility sits between covenantal failure and covenantal restoration. It is surrounded by questions of what we keep, what we cut, what we worship, how we read rizq, and whether we return to Allah.

So when the surah says hearts rest by the remembrance of Allah, it is not speaking into a morally neutral life. It is speaking into a life that must be turned back, rejoined, and reoriented.

This means dhikr is not a decorative spiritual accessory laid on top of unrepaired living. It is the living remembrance that softens the heart enough to stop betraying, stop severing, stop spreading corruption, and begin returning. The remembrance of Allah settles the heart because it restores the heart to the One by whom every right bond becomes meaningful in the first place.

### Theme 2: We Keep Asking the World for a Type of Assurance It Cannot Give

The disbelievers ask for one more sign.

Others rejoice in expanded provision.

Underneath both responses is a shared mistake: they want worldly conditions to give them the kind of certainty, security, and final reassurance that only Allah can give.

[PAUSE]

This mistake has not gone away. We still do versions of it constantly.

We tell ourselves we will be at peace once the income reaches a certain point, once the house is secured, once the relationship stabilizes, once the body improves, once the future looks clearer, once the evidence becomes overwhelming enough, once the unanswered question is finally removed. We ask provision to function like revelation and ask circumstances to function like Lordship.

But dunya can never bear that weight. By definition it is **mata'**. Temporary use. Brief enjoyment. Passing arrangement.

Ar-Ra'd does not deny that provision matters or that signs matter. It denies their right to become the final resting place of the heart. The heart was not built to rest in passing arrangements. It was built to rest in the remembrance of the One who governs them all.

That is why the ayah pairs guidance with **inabah**, return. Rest does not come from finally controlling the outer world enough. It comes from turning inwardly and wholly back to Allah until remembrance becomes more weighty than circumstance.

Then even when rizq expands, you do not become intoxicated by it.

And when it constricts, you do not imagine the Lord has disappeared.

And when one more demanded sign does not arrive in the form your ego wanted, you do not make spectacle the condition for surrender.

[PAUSE]

## Closing Synthesis

Ar-Ra'd begins this passage with the life of breach: they break covenant after binding, cut what Allah ordered to be joined, and spread corruption in the earth. Then it corrects another illusion: rizq expands and contracts by Allah's wisdom, so dunya cannot be read as final proof of nearness. Then it answers the demand for more signs by locating guidance in Allah and in the return of the servant. And finally it gives the heart its resting place: the remembrance of Allah.

[PAUSE]

The architecture is healing. Breakage makes the world corrupt and the self restless. Dunya cannot fix that restlessness because it was never meant to carry eternal meaning. More spectacle cannot fix it either, because the deepest problem was not lack of signs but lack of return. So the surah leads us to the point of re-entry: remember Allah until the heart becomes settled enough to live truthfully again.

So the passage asks us: what have I normalized breaking? What have I allowed myself to cut that Allah called me to preserve? Where have I been reading provision as verdict? What kind of assurance am I still demanding from dunya that only Allah can provide? And when my heart feels scattered, am I merely seeking relief, or am I returning in remembrance to the One who can actually settle it?

[PAUSE]

Ar-Ra'd teaches us here: **the heart does not find peace by breaking less elegantly, possessing more abundantly, or demanding more proofs, but by returning to Allah until His remembrance becomes the place where inner life finally grows still.**
