# Lightning Comes With Fear and Hope, While Thunder Glorifies and Humans Still Argue

## Surah Ar-Ra'd (13:12-13)

[ayah:13:12] هُوَ ٱلَّذِى يُرِيكُمُ ٱلْبَرْقَ خَوْفًا وَطَمَعًا وَيُنشِئُ ٱلسَّحَابَ ٱلثِّقَالَ

[ayah:13:13] وَيُسَبِّحُ ٱلرَّعْدُ بِحَمْدِهِۦ وَٱلْمَلَـٰٓئِكَةُ مِنْ خِيفَتِهِۦ وَيُرْسِلُ ٱلصَّوَٰعِقَ فَيُصِيبُ بِهَا مَن يَشَآءُ وَهُمْ يُجَـٰدِلُونَ فِى ٱللَّهِ وَهُوَ شَدِيدُ ٱلْمِحَالِ

*Huwa alladhi yurikumu al-barqa khawfan wa tama'an wa yunshi'u al-sahaba al-thiqal.*

*Wa yusabbihu al-ra'du bi-hamdihi wa al-mala'ikatu min khifatihi wa yursilu al-sawa'iqa fa-yusibu biha man yasha'u wa hum yujadiluna fi Allahi wa Huwa shadidu al-mihal.*

**He is the One who shows you lightning, bringing fear and hope, and He produces the heavy clouds. And the thunder glorifies His praise, and the angels as well, out of awe of Him. And He sends the thunderbolts and strikes with them whom He wills, while they argue about Allah, and He is severe in might.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

Ar-Ra'd now reaches the sky from which it takes its name.

But the surah does not present the storm as scenery. It presents it as worship, warning, and spiritual instruction all at once. Lightning is shown to the human being in a condition that cannot be reduced to one feeling. It produces fear and hope together. Then the heavy clouds gather. Then thunder itself is named as making tasbih. Then the angels appear in awe. Then thunderbolts strike while some human beings are still busy arguing about Allah.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the Quran's most breathtaking reversals of perspective. Human beings often stand under the storm as though they are the central interpreters of reality. But these ayahs say: look again. The sky is already occupied with praise. The angels are already responding correctly. The created world is already in its proper posture. The question is whether the human being will read the scene or keep disputing beneath it.

The hook of the passage is this: **Ar-Ra'd teaches that some of the most powerful scenes in creation are not merely weather events but lessons in how reality itself holds fear and hope together, glorifies Allah, and exposes the absurdity of human argument beneath an already-worshipping sky.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Al-barqa khawfan wa tama'an**: One Sign Produces Two Honest Reactions at Once

The first image is lightning.

Allah says He **shows** it to you:

**yurikumu al-barq**

Even that verb matters. The scene is not accidental exposure. It is shown. A sign being presented.

And what does it produce?

**khawfan wa tama'an**

fear and hope.

[PAUSE]

This pairing is spiritually rich. The ayah does not ask us to choose one register and ignore the other. Lightning can frighten because it carries danger, force, vulnerability, suddenness. The same lightning can also awaken longing because it signals rain, mercy, release, provision, relief after dryness.

That means the sign is truthful in both directions. It is not emotionally flat. Creation here trains the soul away from simplistic feeling. Some of Allah's signs are meant to hold you in trembling expectancy, where fear does not cancel hope and hope does not erase seriousness.

That is deeply Quranic. Much of faith is corrupted when we try to live on one half of the pair. Pure fear can harden into panic or despair. Pure hope can dissolve into carelessness. But the storm teaches a more mature posture: the servant stands between vulnerability and expectancy.

### 2. **Yunshi'u al-sahaba al-thiqal**: The Clouds Are Heavy Before They Ever Break

Then the ayah says:

**"And He produces the heavy clouds."**

The word **yunshi'u** suggests bringing forth, producing, raising into being. These clouds are not treated as random atmospheric accumulation. They are made, formed, brought forth under divine act.

[PAUSE]

And they are **al-thiqal** - heavy. Weighted. Laden.

The image is beautiful because it teaches us to look at the sky not only at the moment rain falls, but earlier, when the sign is still gathering. Before the mercy pours, the heaviness arrives. The cloud becomes dense first.

That is spiritually suggestive. Sometimes the sign of coming relief does not first appear as lightness. It appears as thickening pressure. Something gathers overhead before it opens. Ar-Ra'd teaches us not to read heaviness too quickly as absence of mercy. In the sky, heaviness may be the form mercy takes just before descent.

### 3. **Yusabbihu al-ra'du bi-hamdihi**: Thunder Is Not Merely Sound; It Is Praise

Then comes the line that gives the surah its name:

**"And the thunder glorifies His praise."**

Not that thunder vaguely points toward praise. Not that it can be poetically compared to praise. The ayah names thunder itself in the grammar of tasbih.

[PAUSE]

This should stop us. The thing we often hear as raw force, noise, disruption, atmospheric violence - the ayah teaches us to hear as glorification.

And notice the expression:

**bi-hamdihi**

with His praise. The tasbih is not bare negation alone; it is joined to praise. The created world is not merely declaring that Allah is beyond flaw. It is also situated inside acknowledgment of His perfection and worthiness.

Then the ayah widens:

**"and the angels, out of awe of Him."**

The scene is now vertically layered. Thunder in tasbih. Angels in khifah. The storm is not empty space. It is a realm of ordered response.

### 4. **Wa hum yujadiluna fi Allah**: Human Argument Is Exposed by Its Surroundings

After all of that, the ayah says:

**"And He sends the thunderbolts and strikes with them whom He wills, while they argue about Allah."**

The juxtaposition is devastating. Not after the storm has ended. Not in another scene. Right there. Thunder praising, angels fearing, bolts striking, and humans still disputing.

[PAUSE]

The verb **yujadilun** suggests contesting, wrangling, arguing back and forth. The problem is not careful inquiry. The problem is a mode of resistant disputation that keeps trying to speak over what the world is already saying.

Then the verse closes:

**"And He is severe in al-mihal."**

The classical explanations take this toward severe might, force, overpowering capacity, the irresistible strength by which Allah deals with those who resist Him. The point in the passage is clear even if translations vary at the edges: the One beneath whose sky they are arguing is not weak, absent, or unable to answer their defiance.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Mature Faith Learns to Live Between Fear and Hope

These ayahs teach emotional tawhid.

Many of us try to simplify the spiritual life into one dominant mood. We want either soothing reassurance all the time or stern warning all the time. But Allah points us to lightning and says: learn from this. One sign can honestly produce fear and hope together.

[PAUSE]

That is how much of real life before Allah actually feels. You fear your sins and hope in His mercy. You fear losing what steadies you and hope for openings still not visible. You fear a trial and hope it may become purification. You fear your own weakness and hope in the One who can hold you through it.

The servant who loses either half becomes spiritually distorted. Without fear, religion becomes soft with the self. Without hope, religion becomes unbearable. The sky trains us in both at once.

### Theme 2: The World May Already Be Responding Better Than We Are

The second theme is even more humbling.

Thunder glorifies. Angels respond in awe. The storm is not spiritually neutral. The universe around us is not waiting to decide whether Allah is worthy. Much of creation is already in correct relation to Him.

[PAUSE]

This means human rebellion is not a brave intellectual independence set against an undecided cosmos. It is dissonance inside a world already ordered toward Allah. When the ayah says **wa hum yujadiluna fi Allah**, the force comes partly from everything surrounding that phrase. They are arguing under a praising sky.

That should sober us about our own inner lives too. There are moments when the fitrah already knows, the heart has already been shown, the signs have already gathered, and what remains is not lack of evidence but the ego's insistence on keeping the dispute alive.

Ar-Ra'd does not flatter that condition. It exposes it.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayahs turn the storm into revelation. Lightning teaches the servant to live between fear and hope. Heavy clouds teach that mercy may gather in weighted form before it falls. Thunder itself is named in tasbih. The angels stand in awe. And the deniers are exposed as still disputing about Allah under a sky that is already glorifying Him.

[PAUSE]

That is the real reversal in the passage: the storm is not chaos, but the human argument is. Creation is already ordered. Praise is already underway. What feels most overwhelming to us may still be carrying a form of worship more coherent than our speech.

[PAUSE]

So the passage leaves us with difficult questions. When fear and hope come together in my life, do I know how to remain before Allah without fleeing into one extreme? When the sky grows heavy, do I read that heaviness only as threat, or can I imagine mercy gathering inside it? And when the world around me is already thick with signs, am I listening - or am I still trying to argue beneath the thunder?

[PAUSE]

Ar-Ra'd teaches us here: **the storm above you is not spiritually empty, because lightning trains the heart, thunder glorifies, angels tremble, and only the unseeing human being keeps arguing under a sky already busy praising Allah.**
