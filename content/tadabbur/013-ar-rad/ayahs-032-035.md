# Mockery Was Never New, Allah Stands Over Every Soul, and the Garden Does Not Fade

## Surah Ar-Ra'd (13:32-35)

[ayah:13:32] وَلَقَدِ ٱسْتُهْزِئَ بِرُسُلٍ مِّن قَبْلِكَ فَأَمْلَيْتُ لِلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ ثُمَّ أَخَذْتُهُمْ ۖ فَكَيْفَ كَانَ عِقَابِ

[ayah:13:33] أَفَمَنْ هُوَ قَآئِمٌ عَلَىٰ كُلِّ نَفْسٍۭ بِمَا كَسَبَتْ ۗ وَجَعَلُوا۟ لِلَّهِ شُرَكَآءَ قُلْ سَمُّوهُمْ ۚ أَمْ تُنَبِّـُٔونَهُۥ بِمَا لَا يَعْلَمُ فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ أَم بِظَـٰهِرٍ مِّنَ ٱلْقَوْلِ ۗ بَلْ زُيِّنَ لِلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ مَكْرُهُمْ وَصُدُّوا۟ عَنِ ٱلسَّبِيلِ ۗ وَمَن يُضْلِلِ ٱللَّهُ فَمَا لَهُۥ مِنْ هَادٍ

[ayah:13:34] لَّهُمْ عَذَابٌ فِى ٱلْحَيَوٰةِ ٱلدُّنْيَا ۖ وَلَعَذَابُ ٱلْـَٔاخِرَةِ أَشَقُّ ۖ وَمَا لَهُم مِّنَ ٱللَّهِ مِن وَاقٍ

[ayah:13:35] ۞ مَّثَلُ ٱلْجَنَّةِ ٱلَّتِى وُعِدَ ٱلْمُتَّقُونَ ۖ تَجْرِى مِن تَحْتِهَا ٱلْأَنْهَـٰرُ ۖ أُكُلُهَا دَآئِمٌ وَظِلُّهَا ۚ تِلْكَ عُقْبَى ٱلَّذِينَ ٱتَّقَوا۟ ۖ وَّعُقْبَى ٱلْكَـٰفِرِينَ ٱلنَّارُ

*Wa la-qadi ustuhzi'a bi-rusulin min qablika fa-amlaytu li-alladhina kafaru thumma akhadhtuhum fa-kayfa kana 'iqab.*

*Afaman huwa qa'imun 'ala kulli nafsin bima kasabat. Wa ja'alu li-Llahi shuraka'a qul sammuuhum. Am tunabbi'unahu bima la ya'lamu fi al-ardi am bi-zahirin mina al-qawl. Bal zuyyina li-alladhina kafaru makruhum wa suddu 'ani al-sabil. Wa man yudlili Allahu fa-ma lahu min had.*

*Lahum 'adhabun fi al-hayati al-dunya wa la-'adhabu al-akhirati ashaqqu wa ma lahum mina Allahi min waq.*

*Mathalu al-jannati allati wu'ida al-muttaqun tajri min tahtiha al-anhar. Ukuluha da'imun wa zilluha. Tilka 'uqba alladhina ittaqaw wa 'uqba al-kafirina al-nar.*

**And indeed, messengers before you were mocked, so I gave respite to those who disbelieved; then I seized them. So how was My punishment? Is then He who stands over every soul for what it has earned like others? Yet they have assigned partners to Allah. Say, "Name them. The likeness of the Garden promised to the God-fearing: rivers flow beneath it, its fruit is lasting and so is its shade. That is the final outcome of those who were mindful of Allah, while the final outcome of the disbelievers is the Fire.**

## Introduction

These ayahs arrive as strength for the Prophet and exposure for the deniers at the same time.

Ar-Ra'd has just reminded us that even if mountains moved and the dead spoke, the command would still belong entirely to Allah. Now the surah makes that lesson more personal. If your people mock you, this is not new. Messengers before you were mocked too. Allah gave the deniers room. Then He seized them.

From there the surah opens a devastating question: is the One who stands over every soul for what it has earned to be treated like fabricated partners? If these alleged rivals are real, then name them. Tell us what they are. Tell us what they know. Tell us what they do. But the challenge collapses immediately, because the whole thing turns out to be only outward speech beautified by delusion.

Then, after warning of punishment in this life and something even harsher in the Hereafter, the surah gives one of its most gentle turns: the likeness of the Garden promised to the God-fearing. Rivers, lasting fruit, enduring shade. What the deniers pursue is unstable. What Allah promises does not decay.

The hook of the passage is this: **Ar-Ra'd teaches that mockery does not threaten revelation, because Allah still stands over every soul and every false claim collapses before His knowledge, while the final contrast is between a fading worldly security and a Garden whose provision and shelter do not end.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Amlaytu ... thumma akhadhtuhum**: Delay Is Not Neglect

The first ayah says:

Messengers before you were mocked.

Then:

**fa-amlaytu**

So I gave respite.

And after that:

**thumma akhadhtuhum**

Then I seized them.

[PAUSE]

That sequence matters deeply. The mockers often misread delay as safety. The wounded messenger can be tempted to misread delay as abandonment. The ayah heals both misunderstandings at once.

**Imla'** is extension of time, lengthened room, a drawn-out stretch in which the denier may think he has outlasted the warning. But the extension is not indifference. It is part of the divine handling.

Then comes **thumma** - not simply "and," but "then," with real sequence and interval. There is a span between mockery and seizure. A span between rebellion and reckoning. But the span is not a cancellation of the end.

That is why the ayah closes:

**fa-kayfa kana 'iqab**

So how was My punishment?

Not as a request for information, but as a forced remembering. Read history by its endings, not by the length of the interval before them.

### 2. **Afaman huwa qa'imun 'ala kulli nafs**: Allah Is Described Here as Standing Over Every Soul

Then the passage turns to one of its greatest lines:

**afaman huwa qa'imun 'ala kulli nafsin bima kasabat**

Is then He who stands over every soul for what it has earned...

[PAUSE]

The answer is left unstated because it is too obvious to need completion.

The word **qa'im** here is powerful. It gives a sense of standing over, attending to, overseeing, maintaining, watching with active charge. This is not remote awareness. It is living supervision and sustaining authority over every soul and everything it earns.

Then against that reality the ayah places shirk:

**wa ja'alu li-Llahi shuraka'**

They assigned partners to Allah.

So the surah tells the Prophet:

**Qul sammuuhum**

Say: name them.

Name them how? As what? With what actual qualities? The challenge is almost surgical. If these partners are real in the way worship assumes they are real, then identify them in meaningful terms.

Then the ayah exposes the emptiness from two sides:

Are you informing Allah of something He does not know in the earth?

Or is it only an outward form of speech?

That last phrase is devastating:

**bi-zahirin mina al-qawl**

a surface statement, an outward verbal shell.

The whole structure of shirk is reduced to ungrounded language. It is speech wearing the costume of reality without its substance.

Then the ayah tells us why this still feels convincing to the deniers:

their plotting was beautified to them.

They were turned away from the path.

Delusion is rarely ugly to itself. It usually arrives cosmetically arranged.

### 3. **Ukuluha da'imun wa zilluha**: The Garden Is Described Through What Does Not End

After the warning of worldly punishment and a harsher Hereafter, the surah says:

**mathalu al-jannati allati wu'ida al-muttaqun**

The likeness, or description, of the Garden promised to the God-fearing.

[PAUSE]

Then it gives a few features, but look closely at what kind of features they are.

Rivers flow beneath it.

Its fruit is lasting.

Its shade is lasting.

This is striking because the description does not only emphasize beauty. It emphasizes endurance.

**ukuluha da'im**

Its provision does not run out.

**wa zilluha**

and its shade.

Shade in this world shifts, shortens, disappears, or becomes unreachable. Fruit spoils, finishes, or is withheld by season. But the Garden is marked here by non-expiring nourishment and non-withdrawing shelter.

That is exactly the contrast this whole stretch of the surah has been building toward. The deniers rejoice in a dunya that is only temporary enjoyment. The Garden is described through what remains.

Then the closing pair:

**tilka 'uqba alladhina ittaqaw**

That is the final outcome of those who were mindful of Allah.

**wa 'uqba al-kafirina al-nar**

And the final outcome of the disbelievers is the Fire.

The surah keeps teaching us to judge by **'uqba**, by ending, by where a path finally lands.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Falsehood Often Survives for a While by Looking Plausible

One of the hardest experiences for a believer is to watch mockery, distortion, or confident falsehood seem socially stable for long enough that it begins to look permanent.

People laugh at truth, build systems on unreality, speak with certainty from empty foundations, and still appear to move through the world with momentum. That can shake the heart if it reads time badly.

Ar-Ra'd teaches us to read time differently.

[PAUSE]

The mockers of earlier messengers were given respite. The deniers in this surah have their plots beautified to them. Their language sounds substantial from the outside. They can demand names, slogans, claims, and rival objects of devotion. They can even feel intellectually secure.

But revelation keeps exposing the same secret: much of falsehood survives not because it has reality, but because it has surface. It keeps going for a while because it looks arranged, persuasive, repeatable, or culturally normal. It becomes **zahir min al-qawl** - outward speech without rooted truth.

That is why believers have to learn not to panic during the interval. Delay is real, but so is seizure. Beautification is real, but so is exposure. The path to Allah is not judged by who seems most socially confident in the middle of the story.

### Theme 2: What We Really Long For Is Lasting Nourishment and Lasting Shelter

The parable of the Garden is brief, but it reaches deeply into the human condition.

What do people actually want from the things they chase in this world? Beneath all the forms - money, reputation, security, ownership, relationships, power - there is usually some hunger for two things:

something that will keep nourishing us,

and somewhere under which we can finally rest.

[PAUSE]

But dunya keeps failing both tests. Its fruit is not permanent. Its shade is not permanent. Even its best moments come with expiry built into them.

So Ar-Ra'd answers not by condemning the desire for nourishment or shelter, but by relocating their fulfillment. The Garden promised to the God-fearing is described through enduring provision and enduring shade. The soul was not foolish for wanting permanence. It was only mistaken when it tried to extract permanence from what Allah made temporary.

That is why the contrast with Fire at the end is so severe. Fire is the opposite of sheltering shade. It is the opposite of restful provision. It is exposure rather than protection, deprivation rather than settled nourishment.

The passage therefore asks us not merely what we are pursuing, but what kind of durability we are asking from it. If we keep demanding eternal functions from temporary things, disappointment is guaranteed. But if the heart reorders itself toward Allah's promised outcome, then even temporary worldly shade stops deceiving us.

[PAUSE]

## Closing Synthesis

Ar-Ra'd first comforts the Prophet with history: messengers before you were mocked too, and Allah gave the deniers respite before seizing them. Then it exposes shirk by placing it beside the One who stands over every soul for what it has earned, challenging the deniers to name their alleged partners and reducing the whole claim to beautified delusion and surface speech. After that it warns of punishment in this life and something harsher in the next, then opens the description of the Garden through rivers, lasting fruit, and lasting shade before sealing the whole scene with the final contrast: the God-fearing have the Garden as ending, while the disbelievers have the Fire.

[PAUSE]

The architecture is steadying. Mockery is temporary. Surface speech is temporary. Beautified plotting is temporary. What matters is who stands over every soul now, and what the final outcome will be when the surface is gone.

So the passage asks us: where has delay tempted me to misread Allah's judgment? What false language have I allowed to sound weightier than it really is? What worldly fruit or shade am I still asking to do the job of paradise? And when I think about my own life, am I judging it by today's appearances or by the **'uqba** toward which it is actually moving?

[PAUSE]

Ar-Ra'd teaches us here: **the One who stands over every soul is not challenged by mockery or beautified falsehood, and the real division at the end is between a fire from which nothing protects and a garden whose nourishment and shelter do not fade.**
