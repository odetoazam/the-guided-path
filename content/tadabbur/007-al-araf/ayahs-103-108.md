# I Say Nothing About Allah But the Truth

## Surah Al-Araf (7:103-108)

[ayah:7:103] ثُمَّ بَعَثْنَا مِنۢ بَعْدِهِم مُّوسَىٰ بِـَٔايَـٰتِنَآ إِلَىٰ فِرْعَوْنَ وَمَلَإِي۟هِۦ فَظَلَمُوا۟ بِهَا ۖ فَٱنظُرْ كَيْفَ كَانَ عَـٰقِبَةُ ٱلْمُفْسِدِينَ

## Introduction

Tyranny does not only control bodies. It tries to control the terms on which reality may be named.

A ruler like Fir'awn builds a world in which speech must either flatter power, fear power, or ask permission from power. So when Musa enters his court, the first battle is not yet over sorcery, spectacle, or even political negotiation. The first battle is over who has the right to say what is true about Allah, and who actually governs the world in which Fir'awn sits on a throne.

[PAUSE]

That is why these opening ayahs are so concentrated. Musa is sent with signs. Fir'awn and his elite wrong those signs. Musa then speaks with extraordinary clarity: I am a messenger from the Lord of the worlds, and it is only proper for me to say nothing about Allah except the truth. Then he immediately joins that truth-claim to a concrete demand: send with me the Children of Israel. Fir'awn answers with challenge. Musa answers with signs visible to the watching court.

[PAUSE]

The hook of the passage is this: **prophetic truth in the face of power is not vague spirituality; it is truthful speech about Allah, public evidence, and a demand that oppression release the people it has enslaved.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. Fir'awn and His Chiefs "Wronged" the Signs

The opening summary is striking:

*Fa-zalamu biha.*

They wronged them.

[PAUSE]

The surah does not merely say they denied the signs. It says they committed *zulm* through them. That language matters because *zulm* in the Qur'an is not only disbelief as private opinion. It is misplacement, moral distortion, putting things where they do not belong. So to wrong the signs is to receive evidence from Allah and treat it as material for arrogance, rejection, manipulation, or theatrical dismissal.

Then the ayah closes:

*Fa-unzur kayfa kana aqibatu al-mufsidin.*

So look at how the end of the corrupters was.

[PAUSE]

The word *mufsidin* ties Fir'awn's world back to the towns just summarized. This is a new episode, but not a new moral species. The same corruption that ruined earlier peoples is alive again inside empire.

### 2. Musa Grounds His Speech in the Lordship of All Worlds

Musa opens:

*Inni rasulun min rabbi al-alamin.*

I am a messenger from the Lord of the worlds.

[PAUSE]

Before he says anything about Bani Isra'il, before he presents a sign, before he enters argument, he relocates the court. Fir'awn is not being addressed as the highest reference point in the room. Musa names a Lord above all worlds. The whole confrontation is reframed from the first sentence.

Then comes one of the most beautiful lines of prophetic adab:

*Haqiqun ala an la aqula ala Allahi illa al-haqq.*

It is binding on me, proper for me, fitting for me, that I say nothing about Allah except the truth.

[PAUSE]

The tafsir tradition treats *haqiq* here in the register of worthiness, obligation, fitness, and binding propriety. Musa is not improvising religious language in front of power. He is defining himself as a speaker who is under obligation to truth about Allah. The message cannot be bent to survive the court because the messenger is already bound elsewhere.

This is a profound correction for us. We often imagine courage as saying hard things loudly. The Qur'an first presents courage as disciplined truthfulness: saying about Allah only what is true, neither adding nor trimming to suit fear.

### 3. Truth Immediately Becomes Liberation, and Signs Become Public

After affirming truthful speech, Musa says:

*Fa-arsil ma'iya bani Isra'il.*

So send with me the Children of Israel.

[PAUSE]

This is crucial. The theology does not remain abstract. To speak truth about Allah in Fir'awn's court immediately means confronting oppression. The رب العالمين is not merely a doctrine to be admired while the enslaved remain where they are. Once Allah's lordship is named truly, false lordship becomes exposed.

Fir'awn answers with conditional challenge: if you have come with a sign, bring it. Then the surah gives two signs in quick succession. The staff becomes *thu'ban mubin* - a manifest serpent. The hand becomes white *lil-nazirin* - for the onlookers. Both descriptions emphasize visibility. These are not private mystical states. They are public, undeniable disruptions of the court's claimed control over reality.

[PAUSE]

Even the wording matters. *Mubin* carries the sense of manifest, clear, openly evident. *Lil-nazirin* places the sign before watchers. Allah is not leaving the matter at rumor. The proof enters the public field where power thought it controlled interpretation.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Revelation Begins by Reassigning Authority

At the heart of the Fir'awn story is a battle over who gets to define the real.

Fir'awn's world is built on the performance of unquestionable authority. He commands labor, bodies, law, and public narrative. When someone speaks before such power, there is enormous pressure to speak inside the ruler's frame. Make the appeal strategic. Phrase it carefully enough to remain useful. Keep transcendence safely symbolic. Do not say anything that relocates actual sovereignty.

[PAUSE]

Musa does the opposite. He says, in effect: I am not speaking from your permission, your order, or your scale. I am a messenger from the Lord of all worlds. That sentence does not only introduce Musa. It dethrones the court's imagination of itself.

This matters for us because Fir'awn is not only an ancient tyrant. He is also a recurring pattern in human life: any system, person, institution, or appetite that trains us to act as though it were the highest thing we must answer to. The first step of freedom is often not escape of the body but reassignment of ultimate authority in the heart.

### Theme 2: Truthful Speech About Allah Must Touch the Wounded of the World

One of the most moving features of this opening is how quickly Musa's theological claim becomes a liberation claim.

He does not say, "I am a messenger from the Lord of the worlds," and then leave the enslaved where they are while the court enjoys a refined religious debate. He says it, then asks for Bani Isra'il to be released. The sequence teaches us that truth about Allah is not indifferent to what false gods do to people.

[PAUSE]

That is an important spiritual correction. Many of us are comfortable with a form of religiosity that keeps creed elevated and oppression practical, as though one belongs to heaven and the other to policy. But the Qur'an refuses that split. The same tongue that says *illa al-haqq* also says *fa-arsil ma'iya bani Isra'il.* Truth about Allah exposes every unauthorized claim to ownership over human beings.

And when Fir'awn asks for proof, Allah provides signs that the public can see. This too is mercy. The oppressed are not asked to build their hope on hidden comfort alone. The court is confronted. Onlookers are given something to see. Tyranny is made to face evidence in the open.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs open the Musa narrative by gathering creed, courage, signs, and liberation into one movement.

Musa is sent with Allah's signs to Fir'awn and his chiefs, and they wrong those signs rather than receiving them. He introduces himself not through biography or diplomacy but as messenger from the Lord of all worlds. He binds himself to saying nothing about Allah except the truth. Then he ties that truth to the release of Bani Isra'il. Fir'awn demands a sign, and Allah answers with visible, manifest proof before the watching court.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this opening is to ask: what power in my life trains me to edit the truth before I speak it, and what would it mean to stand under the discipline of saying about Allah only what is true, even when that truth destabilizes a false order?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the unit lands here: **Musa confronts tyranny by naming Allah's universal lordship, binding himself to truthful speech, and demanding the release of the oppressed, while the public signs show that revelation does not enter the court as rumor but as manifest evidence that power cannot domesticate.**
