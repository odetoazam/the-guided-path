# Hold Fast to the Book

## Surah Al-Araf (7:170-171)

[ayah:7:170] وَٱلَّذِينَ يُمَسِّكُونَ بِٱلْكِتَـٰبِ وَأَقَامُوا۟ ٱلصَّلَوٰةَ إِنَّا لَا نُضِيعُ أَجْرَ ٱلْمُصْلِحِينَ

[ayah:7:171] ۞ وَإِذْ نَتَقْنَا ٱلْجَبَلَ فَوْقَهُمْ كَأَنَّهُۥ ظُلَّةٌ وَظَنُّوٓا۟ أَنَّهُۥ وَاقِعٌۢ بِهِمْ خُذُوا۟ مَآ ءَاتَيْنَـٰكُم بِقُوَّةٍ وَٱذْكُرُوا۟ مَا فِيهِ لَعَلَّكُمْ تَتَّقُونَ

*Wa alladhina yumassikuna bi-al-kitabi wa aqamu al-salata inna la nudi'u ajra al-muslihin.*

*Wa idh nataqna al-jabala fawqahum ka-annahu zullah wa zannu annahu waqi'un bihim. Khudhu ma ataynakum bi-quwwatin wa udhkuru ma fihi la'allakum tattaqun.*

**And those who hold fast to the Book and establish prayer - indeed, We do not let the reward of the reformers go to waste. And when We raised the mountain above them as though it were a canopy, and they thought it would fall upon them: 'Take what We have given you with strength, and remember what is in it so that you may become mindful.'**

## Introduction

After describing heirs of the Book who keep reaching for low worldly gain and soothing themselves with cheap hopes of forgiveness, the surah does not leave revelation under the shadow of betrayal alone. It immediately names another kind of people: those who hold fast to the Book and establish prayer.

[PAUSE]

That shift matters. The Qur'an never lets corruption have the last descriptive word when a truthful path still exists. There are still people who grip revelation instead of using it, who stand in salah instead of hiding behind knowledge, and whose relationship to scripture is therefore counted as *islah* rather than mere inheritance.

Then the next ayah throws us back into the overwhelming mountain scene. The Book is not being handed to a distracted people in a soft atmosphere. The mountain is raised above them like a canopy, and they think it may fall. In that scene Allah commands them to take what has been given with strength and to remember what is in it. So these two ayahs together teach us that gripping the Book is not sentimental attachment. It is a covenant lived under weight.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Qur'an Praises Not Mere Possession of the Book but Gripping It

Ayah 170 says:

*Wa alladhina yumassikuna bi-al-kitab...*

Those who hold fast to the Book.

[PAUSE]

The verb is stronger than simply "have." *Yumassikuna* carries holding tightly, gripping, refusing to let slip. That is a very different posture from merely inheriting scripture, studying it at a distance, or invoking it when convenient. The Book here is something the servant clings to because he knows he will drift without it.

Then the verse joins that grip to:

*Wa aqamu al-salah.*

They establish prayer.

This is crucial. The Book and the prayer are not left apart. Revelation held in the hand must become standing before Allah in the body. Otherwise a person can speak endlessly of text while remaining unshaped by worship.

Then comes the divine promise:

*Inna la nudi'u ajra al-muslihin.*

We do not let the reward of the reformers go to waste.

[PAUSE]

Notice the word *al-muslihin*. They are not called merely readers or knowers. Holding to the Book and establishing prayer becomes *islah* - a reforming, mending relation to life. Revelation truly held does not stay theoretical. It repairs.

### 2. The Mountain Scene Makes Accountability Visible

Ayah 171 says:

*Wa idh nataqna al-jabala fawqahum ka-annahu zullah.*

When We raised the mountain above them as though it were a canopy.

[PAUSE]

The image is staggering. The mountain is not simply nearby. It is above them, suspended as though it were a shadowing cover. But unlike a comforting shade, this one terrifies:

*Wa zannu annahu waqi'un bihim.*

They thought it was about to fall upon them.

This is revelation arriving with visible weight. The people are not allowed to imagine the covenant as something light, decorative, or negotiable. The physical world itself becomes a sign of what divine command weighs.

Then the command comes:

*Khudhu ma ataynakum bi-quwwah.*

Take what We have given you with strength.

The same language of *quwwah* we heard earlier around the tablets returns here, now addressed to the people under the looming mountain. Revelation is not to be handled weakly, lazily, or selectively.

### 3. Remembering What Is in the Book Is Part of Taking It

The verse continues:

*Wa udhkuru ma fihi la'allakum tattaqun.*

Remember what is in it so that you may become mindful.

[PAUSE]

That means taking the Book with strength is not only outward conformity. It requires *dhikr* of what is in it. Keeping its contents present. Carrying its commands, warnings, and promises in active remembrance rather than archived familiarity.

This is deeply important. Many people "have" revelation in the sense of access, literacy, or even memorized language, but the Book is not alive in their practical memory. It does not rise quickly enough to meet the next temptation, next decision, next compromise. The ayah says: remember what is in it. Let the contents stay near enough to shape taqwa.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Alternative to Corrupt Inheritance Is Not More Information but Stronger Holding

The previous ayah-cluster criticized people who inherited the Book yet sold themselves cheaply to lower gain. This verse answers that disease not by praising cleverer study alone, but by praising those who *yumassikuna* - hold fast.

[PAUSE]

That is revealing. Our problem with revelation is often not that we lack contact with it in principle. It is that our hold is weak. We let go too quickly under pressure, under desire, under fatigue, under social cost, under the offer of immediate advantage. The Qur'an therefore does not only call us to learn the Book. It calls us to cling to it.

And the immediate companion to that grip is salah. Because prayer rehearses dependence. It prevents the Book from becoming merely intellectual property. Five times a day the body is returned to the posture that says: I am not above this guidance. I stand beneath it.

### Theme 2: We Need to Recover the Weight of Revelation Without Waiting for a Mountain Above Our Heads

The mountain scene can feel distant because we do not live with a literal cliff hanging above us. But the ayah is teaching a spiritual fact: revelation has weight whether we feel it or not.

[PAUSE]

Modern religious life often suffers from weightlessness. The Book is everywhere and nowhere. It is quoted, clipped, posted, archived, discussed, stylized, and admired, yet often encountered without trembling. The mountain in this passage exposes what casual religion tries to hide: when Allah speaks, the appropriate human posture is not bored familiarity. It is serious receiving.

But the mercy of the verse is that we are told how to respond even without the visible mountain. Take it with strength. Remember what is in it. Build taqwa through active remembrance before catastrophe is required to wake the heart.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayahs rescue revelation from both corruption and weightlessness.

The first names the people who truly hold to the Book and establish prayer, then promises that Allah does not waste the reward of such reformers. The second recalls the mountain raised above a people who thought it might fall on them, and under that visible weight Allah commands them to take what they have been given with strength and to keep what is in it alive in remembrance so taqwa can emerge.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this unit is to ask: do I merely possess access to the Book, or do I actually grip it? Has prayer become the embodied proof that revelation still has weight in my life? What would it look like to remember what is in the Book before the next test arrives, instead of only reaching for it after I have already slipped?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the unit lands here: **the Book reforms only those who grip it, pray it into their bodies, and remember its contents under the real weight of accountability, so Allah promises not to lose the reward of those who hold fast instead of letting revelation slip into weak inheritance.**
