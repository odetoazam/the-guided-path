# You Will Not See Me, But Look at the Mountain

## Surah Al-Araf (7:141-143)

[ayah:7:141] وَإِذْ أَنجَيْنَـٰكُم مِّنْ ءَالِ فِرْعَوْنَ يَسُومُونَكُمْ سُوٓءَ ٱلْعَذَابِ ۖ يُقَتِّلُونَ أَبْنَآءَكُمْ وَيَسْتَحْيُونَ نِسَآءَكُمْ ۚ وَفِى ذَٰلِكُم بَلَآءٌ مِّن رَّبِّكُمْ عَظِيمٌ

## Introduction

These ayahs move from rescue to rendezvous to collapse.

First the people are told to remember the violence from which Allah saved them. Then Musa is drawn into a measured appointment of nights, charged to leave behind repair rather than chaos, and brought into the rare honor of direct address from his Lord. Then, at the height of that intimacy, he asks for more than a creature can bear in this life. The answer comes not by argument but by an event: a mountain crushed flat, a prophet falling unconscious, and a recovery that ends in repentance and praise.

[PAUSE]

The sequence matters. The surah does not move from Fir'awn to Sinai as though one were merely historical background for the other. It teaches that being saved is itself a trial, that nearness is preceded by discipline, and that even the most sincere longing must still learn its limits before Allah. In three ayahs, we are shown that divine generosity does not remove creatureliness. It exposes it.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. Rescue Itself Is Named as Trial

The first surprise is in the end of ayah 141:

*Wa fi dhalikum bala'un min rabbikum 'azim.*

In that was a tremendous trial from your Lord.

[PAUSE]

We often hear the word *bala'* and instinctively think of obvious hardship. That is certainly present here: the verse has just named slaughtered sons, spared daughters for humiliation, and the worst kind of social torment. But the ayah does not say that Fir'awn alone tested you. It says that in all of that was a great *bala'* from your Lord.

That is not because oppression is good. It is because revelation refuses to let history become godless. The believer is taught to see even the most brutal worldly events within a higher address from Allah. Rescue from tyranny is mercy, but the years inside tyranny were also part of a divine schooling. The memory is therefore not merely political memory. It is theological memory.

And that changes the way deliverance is read. If liberation came from Allah, then liberation cannot be treated as the end of the story. It becomes the beginning of a new accountability. The people who were saved are now answerable for what they do with the salvation they were given.

### 2. Nearness Is Scheduled, Structured, and Surrounded by Responsibility

Ayah 142 does not say Musa wandered into mystical nearness. It says:

*Wa wa'adna Musa thalathina laylatan wa atmamnaha bi-'ashr.*

We appointed for Musa thirty nights and completed them with ten.

[PAUSE]

The movement is deliberate. Thirty, then completion with ten, then the full *miqat* of forty. Nearness here is not spontaneous emotional overflow. It is measured. Timed. Brought to fullness by Allah Himself. Even the prophet who will speak directly with Allah does not set the terms of access. The appointment is given.

Then look at what Musa says before he goes:

*Ukhlufni fi qawmi wa aslih wa la tattabi' sabila al-mufsidin.*

Take my place among my people, do right, and do not follow the path of the corrupters.

This is beautiful in its order. Before Musa climbs the mountain, he leaves behind instructions of governance: succession, reform, and distance from corruption. The road to intimacy with Allah is not detached from the moral order below. Spiritual ascent does not excuse neglect of the people. The one going up the mountain still has to secure justice in the camp.

### 3. The Longing Is Real, the Limit Is Real, and the Return Is Through Tawbah

Then comes one of the most awe-striking exchanges in the Qur'an:

*Rabbi arini anzur ilayk.*

My Lord, show Yourself to me that I may look at You.

[PAUSE]

This is not the request of doubt. It is the request of longing. Musa has just been honored with divine speech, and love naturally wants more. The request shows us something noble: closeness to Allah increases desire for Allah. Real nearness does not produce boredom. It intensifies yearning.

But the answer is equally decisive:

*Lan tarani.*

You will not see Me.

The negation is direct, and the ayah immediately places a creaturely measure beside it:

*Wa lakini unzur ila al-jabal fa-in istaqarra makanahu fa-sawfa tarani.*

But look at the mountain. If it remains in place, then you will see Me.

The mountain is the test object. The most solid thing in sight is asked to stand where Musa cannot. Then the ayah says:

*Fa-lamma tajalla rabbuhu lil-jabali ja'alahu dakkan wa kharra Musa sa'iqan.*

When his Lord manifested to the mountain, He made it level, and Musa fell unconscious.

[PAUSE]

The word *tajalla* carries manifestation, an appearing of divine disclosure. The ayah does not let us imagine how. It only lets us witness the effect. And the effect is devastating. *Ja'alahu dakkan* reduces the mountain to collapse; *kharra Musa sa'iqan* shows Musa dropping under the weight of what the event reveals.

Then comes the healing movement:

*Subhanaka tubtu ilayka wa ana awwalu al-mu'minin.*

Glory be to You. I repent to You. I am the first of the believers.

Tasbih comes first, then tawbah, then renewed iman. He does not cling to the request. He returns from the limit by glorifying Allah above every imagined grasp.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: We Often Mistake Spiritual Hunger for Entitlement

There is a subtle danger in religious longing. Sometimes the heart tastes something real from Allah, and instead of becoming more humble, it becomes impatient. It begins to think that because the hunger is sincere, every requested unveiling must now be owed.

That is not how this passage teaches us to love.

[PAUSE]

Musa's request is sincere, but sincerity does not cancel creaturely limit. In fact, one of the deepest forms of worship is to desire Allah intensely while still accepting the boundary He places. The modern self struggles with this. We are trained to think every authentic desire should culminate in immediate access. If you feel it deeply, you should have it now. If your longing is pure, the door should open the way you imagined.

But the path to Allah often works differently. Sometimes the answer to love is not possession but purification. Not "here is what you asked for," but "here is the truth about who you are, and here is the adab by which you keep loving Me without demanding what this world cannot hold."

This is liberating. It means spiritual life is not proven only by dramatic experiences. Sometimes it is proven by the ability to keep seeking, keep praising, and keep repenting even after a door closes in the form we had hoped.

### Theme 2: The Strongest Encounters with Allah Usually Break Illusion Before They Build Confidence

The mountain scene is not gentle. It is not a warm mystical image. It is shattering.

Why? Because one of the great illusions of the self is that nearness to Allah will make us feel bigger. The Qur'an repeatedly teaches the opposite. True nearness makes us truer, which also means smaller in our own eyes. It collapses fantasy before it gives stability.

[PAUSE]

Many people want an experience that will finally settle them: one prayer, one night, one retreat, one opening that will make them permanently certain and internally impressive. But in the Qur'anic pattern, the authentic meeting with Allah does not inflate the servant. It humbles the servant into *subhanaka* and *tubtu ilayk*. The fruit of real contact is not spiritual self-importance. It is deeper surrender.

That is why the ayah begins with the memory of oppression and ends with tawbah. The servant who remembers he was saved from Fir'awn, appointed by Allah, and then stopped at the limit of creaturehood becomes capable of carrying revelation without self-delusion.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs teach a single movement through three scenes.

The people are reminded that even rescue from Fir'awn was a great *bala'*. Musa is then given a structured *miqat*, and before climbing he leaves behind the commands of repair and anti-corruption. At the summit of direct address, his sincere longing to see Allah meets a boundary enforced by the mountain's destruction and his own collapse. When he rises, he rises with tasbih, tawbah, and renewed belief.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask: have I learned to read both hardship and rescue as tests from Allah? Do I seek nearness while neglecting the responsibilities He already placed in my hands? When Allah denies me a form of access I deeply wanted, do I turn bitter, or do I turn into *subhanaka tubtu ilayk*?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the unit lands here: **Allah saves, appoints, and draws near, but every stage uncovers the servant's limits, so the right response to divine closeness is not spiritual entitlement but praise, repentance, and a truer form of faith.**
