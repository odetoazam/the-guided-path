# Hearts, Eyes, and Ears

## Surah Al-Araf (7:177-179)

[ayah:7:177] سَآءَ مَثَلًا ٱلْقَوْمُ ٱلَّذِينَ كَذَّبُوا۟ بِـَٔايَـٰتِنَا وَأَنفُسَهُمْ كَانُوا۟ يَظْلِمُونَ

[ayah:7:178] مَن يَهْدِ ٱللَّهُ فَهُوَ ٱلْمُهْتَدِى ۖ وَمَن يُضْلِلْ فَأُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ هُمُ ٱلْخَـٰسِرُونَ

[ayah:7:179] وَلَقَدْ ذَرَأْنَا لِجَهَنَّمَ كَثِيرًا مِّنَ ٱلْجِنِّ وَٱلْإِنسِ ۖ لَهُمْ قُلُوبٌ لَّا يَفْقَهُونَ بِهَا وَلَهُمْ أَعْيُنٌ لَّا يُبْصِرُونَ بِهَا وَلَهُمْ ءَاذَانٌ لَّا يَسْمَعُونَ بِهَآ ۚ أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ كَٱلْأَنْعَـٰمِ بَلْ هُمْ أَضَلُّ ۚ أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ هُمُ ٱلْغَـٰفِلُونَ

## Introduction

After the humiliation of the man who shed Allah's signs, the surah does not let us leave the warning as an isolated tragedy.

It presses further. First it says the people who deny the signs are an evil likeness. Then it interrupts the flow with a stark theological sentence about who is truly guided and who is truly lost. Then it opens the inside of ruin itself: hearts that do not understand, eyes that do not really see, ears that do not truly hear.

[PAUSE]

This is one of those passages where the Qur'an refuses our favorite excuse. We like to imagine that if someone falls far enough, it must be because the truth was not available, the evidence was weak, the reminder never came, or the environment was too dark to do anything but drift. But the previous ayahs already showed a man given signs who still slipped out of them. Now these ayahs explain what that collapse becomes at scale: not lack of faculties, but misuse of faculties; not absence of self, but self-destruction; not innocence, but heedlessness.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Evil Likeness Lands Back on the Self

Ayah 177 says:

*Sa'a mathalan al-qawmu alladhina kadhdhabu bi-ayatina.*

Evil as an example are the people who denied Our signs.

[PAUSE]

The word *sa'a* is severe. It is not merely saying their condition is unfortunate. It is pronouncing it ugly, evil, wretched as a likeness. The surah has just given us the dog simile and the warning that the story should be told so people may reflect. Now it names the verdict: this is a miserable likeness to inhabit.

Then the ayah turns sharply:

*Wa anfusahum kanu yazlimun.*

And it was themselves they used to wrong.

That matters. The object of the ظلم is fronted as the self. The real disaster of denying Allah's signs is not that the signs have been injured. It is not that Allah's dominion has been reduced. It is that the denier keeps tearing into his own soul. Sin often arrives feeling like self-protection, self-expression, or self-assertion. The Qur'an names it more truthfully: self-wronging.

### 2. Guidance Is Not Owned by the Servant

Then comes ayah 178:

*Man yahdi Allahu fahuwa al-muhtadi wa man yudlil fa-ula'ika humu al-khasirun.*

Whoever Allah guides - he is the truly guided. And whoever He lets go astray - those are the losers.

[PAUSE]

The sentence is short, but it lands like a reset. After the previous case study and the verdict of self-wronging, the surah returns us to the deepest reality underneath every spiritual state: guidance is not self-manufactured.

Notice the phrasing. It does not merely say, "whoever is guided." It says, *whoever Allah guides.* And then: *fahuwa al-muhtadi* - then he, truly, is the guided one. The grammar concentrates real guidance into the one whom Allah guides.

On the other side, the ayah does not merely call the astray person confused. It calls him *al-khasir* - the loser, the one who has undergone loss. So straying here is not framed as intellectual novelty or independent freedom. It is loss. Something of immense worth has been squandered.

This ayah does not cancel human responsibility. The previous ayah already told us these people wrong themselves. But it does tear away spiritual arrogance. No one sees clearly by personal brilliance. If Allah does not open the road, no amount of self-confidence can manufacture sight.

### 3. Hearts, Eyes, and Ears Become Evidence Against Their Owners

Then ayah 179 gives the anatomy of ruin:

*Lahum qulubun la yafqahuna biha wa lahum a'yunun la yubsiruna biha wa lahum adhanun la yasma'una biha.*

They have hearts with which they do not understand, eyes with which they do not see, and ears with which they do not hear.

[PAUSE]

The repetition is deliberate. *Lahum... lahum... lahum.* They have. They possess the instruments. The problem is not non-possession. It is non-use in the direction for which they were given.

And notice the first faculty named is not the eye or the ear but the heart. In Qur'anic language, the heart is not merely the seat of sentiment. It is the organ of grasping, turning, receiving, and understanding. So the first failure is not visual but inward. They do not *fiqh* with the heart. They do not penetrate to meaning.

Then come eyes that do not truly see and ears that do not truly hear. Of course such people can physically look and physically listen. The ayah is not denying sensory function. It is exposing moral perception. There is a kind of seeing that never reaches recognition and a kind of hearing that never becomes obedience.

Then the simile comes:

*Ula'ika ka-al-an'am bal hum adall.*

Those are like cattle. Rather, they are further astray.

This is devastating. The ayah first lowers them to livestock, creatures governed by appetite and immediate stimulus. Then it says: *bal hum adall* - rather, they are even more astray. Why more astray? Because animals are not given the same covenantal burden, the same capacity for moral hearing, the same revealed summons. The animal follows its design. The heedless human abandons his.

And then the final diagnosis:

*Ula'ika humu al-ghafilun.*

Those are the heedless.

So the ending does not merely say they are mistaken. It says they are *ghafilun* - people covered over by heedlessness, living as though the deepest realities never pressed upon them.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Greatest Human Damage Is Often Self-Inflicted Blindness

These ayahs expose a hard truth: the soul can become its own saboteur.

When the Qur'an says, *wa anfusahum kanu yazlimun*, it removes the fantasy that rebellion is a form of liberation. We often frame disobedience as relief, autonomy, or realism. But the ayah says the hand landing the blow is turning back onto the self. Every time a person turns from the signs, deadens the heart, excuses what he knows, or trains his senses not to receive truth, he is not protecting himself from Allah. He is diminishing himself before Allah.

[PAUSE]

This is why heedlessness is so dangerous. It rarely feels dramatic while it is forming. It feels like postponement. It feels like selective attention. It feels like surviving the moment by refusing to look too deeply. But over time the refusal hardens. The heart stops understanding. The eyes stop taking lessons. The ears stop receiving warning. And then what once felt like choice starts to resemble captivity inside one's own narrowed self.

### Theme 2: Human Faculties Were Given for More Than Survival

The comparison to cattle is not insulting for the sake of insult. It is revelatory. Animals eat, move, mate, avoid pain, and seek what preserves them. There is nothing shameful in that for them. That is how they were made.

Human beings, however, were not given hearts, sight, and hearing only to navigate the ground-level business of survival. We were given them to recognize signs, receive reminder, and move toward Allah consciously. So when a human being lives only at the level of appetite, reaction, and immediate material orientation, he is not merely living simply. He is living beneath his own design.

[PAUSE]

That is why the ayah says, *bal hum adall.* More astray. The problem is not that they are creaturely. The problem is that they betray the extra trust built into their creatureliness. They have what the grazing animal does not have, yet they do not rise with it.

And placed beside ayah 178, this should produce both fear and du'a. Fear, because losing guidance is real loss. Du'a, because no one keeps his faculties alive by pride alone. The heart stays awake only by Allah's mercy.

## Closing Synthesis

These three ayahs take the previous warning about one man and turn it into a universal anatomy of heedless collapse.

The people who deny Allah's signs are declared a wretched likeness, and the surah clarifies that the real wrong of their denial lands back upon their own selves. Then it states the governing truth beneath every spiritual condition: whoever Allah guides is truly guided, and whoever is left astray is among the losers. Finally, the passage opens the interior of that loss - hearts that do not understand, eyes that do not truly see, and ears that do not truly hear - before pronouncing that such people have dropped below their own design into heedlessness more astray than cattle.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask: what are my heart, eyes, and ears being trained to do every day? Have I started using them only for worldly navigation while leaving them underused for remembrance, warning, and recognition? And when I feel secure in my own clarity, do I still tremble enough to ask Allah for guidance as a gift rather than assuming it as a possession?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the unit lands here: **after exposing the man who shed the signs, the surah shows that denial becomes communal self-wronging, that true guidance remains entirely Allah's gift, and that the deepest form of loss is to possess the human faculties meant for recognizing truth while living in a heedlessness that drags the soul beneath its own created rank.**
