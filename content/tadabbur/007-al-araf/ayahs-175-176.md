# He Shed the Signs

## Surah Al-Araf (7:175-176)

[ayah:7:175] وَٱتْلُ عَلَيْهِمْ نَبَأَ ٱلَّذِىٓ ءَاتَيْنَـٰهُ ءَايَـٰتِنَا فَٱنسَلَخَ مِنْهَا فَأَتْبَعَهُ ٱلشَّيْطَـٰنُ فَكَانَ مِنَ ٱلْغَاوِينَ

[ayah:7:176] وَلَوْ شِئْنَا لَرَفَعْنَـٰهُ بِهَا وَلَـٰكِنَّهُۥٓ أَخْلَدَ إِلَى ٱلْأَرْضِ وَٱتَّبَعَ هَوَىٰهُ ۚ فَمَثَلُهُۥ كَمَثَلِ ٱلْكَلْبِ إِن تَحْمِلْ عَلَيْهِ يَلْهَثْ أَوْ تَتْرُكْهُ يَلْهَث ۚ ذَّٰلِكَ مَثَلُ ٱلْقَوْمِ ٱلَّذِينَ كَذَّبُوا۟ بِـَٔايَـٰتِنَا ۚ فَٱقْصُصِ ٱلْقَصَصَ لَعَلَّهُمْ يَتَفَكَّرُونَ

*Wa utlu 'alayhim naba'a alladhi ataynahu ayatina fa-insalakha minha fa-atba'ahu al-shaytanu fa-kana mina al-ghawin.*

*Wa law shi'na la-rafa'nahu biha wa lakinnahu akhlada ila al-ard wa-ittaba'a hawahu. Fa-mathaluhu ka-mathali al-kalbi in tahmil 'alayhi yalhath aw tatrukhu yalhath. Dhalika mathalu al-qawmi alladhina kadhdhabu bi-ayatina. Faqsusi al-qasasa la'allahum yatafakkarun.*

**And recite to them the news of the one to whom We gave Our signs, but he detached himself from them; so Satan pursued him, and he became one of the deviators. And if We had willed, We could have raised him by them, but he clung to the earth and followed his own desire. So his likeness is that of a dog: if you drive it, it pants, and if you leave it, it pants. That is the likeness of the people who denied Our signs. So relate the stories, so that perhaps they may reflect.**

## Introduction

These ayahs are terrifying precisely because the man in them is left unnamed.

The surah has just taken us beneath history itself, into the primordial testimony of every child of Adam before excuses about heedlessness and inherited shirk can be raised. Then, instead of staying in the wide frame of humanity, it narrows suddenly to one human being: someone who was given Allah's signs and still fell.

[PAUSE]

That movement matters. It tells us that the deepest covenant in the soul does not remove the possibility of deliberate collapse. A person can be near to signs, touched by signs, even entrusted with signs, and still choose a downward path. So this is not a story told to satisfy curiosity about who he was. The Qur'an is not interested in protecting us from this ayah by letting us keep it trapped inside somebody else's biography. It keeps the figure open so the warning can remain alive.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. He Was Given the Signs, Then Slipped Out of Them

The passage opens with a command:

*Wa utlu 'alayhim naba'a alladhi ataynahu ayatina.*

Recite to them the news of the one to whom We gave Our signs.

[PAUSE]

The ayah does not begin with denial. It begins with gift. Allah says, *ataynahu ayatina* - We gave him Our signs. He was not starved of evidence. He was not described as distant from revelation. He was given something real from Allah.

Then comes the devastating verb: *fa-insalakha minha.*

He slipped out of them. He peeled away from them.

The root behind *insalakha* carries the sense of skin being stripped away. The word does not picture the signs being snatched from him against his will. It pictures him emerging from them as though something that should have remained intimately attached had become, for him, only an outer layer. That is one of the most frightening possibilities in religious life: that revelation stays close enough to cover the surface without sinking deeply enough to transform the core.

Then immediately:

*Fa-atba'ahu al-shaytan.*

So Satan pursued him.

Notice the sequence. First he slips out of the signs. Then Satan catches up to him. The ayah does not present satanic pursuit as the first event. The first breach is that the servant loosens his hold on what Allah gave him. Once the protective nearness is shed, the predator is no longer at a distance.

### 2. The Signs Could Have Raised Him, But He Chose Downwardness

Ayah 176 begins with one of the most painful counterfactuals in the surah:

*Wa law shi'na la-rafa'nahu biha.*

If We had willed, We could have raised him by them.

[PAUSE]

That means the signs were not ornamental. They contained elevation. The very thing he abandoned was the means by which he could have been lifted.

But the ayah immediately names the direction he chose instead:

*Wa lakinnahu akhlada ila al-ard.*

But he clung to the earth.

This is more than saying he liked worldly things. The language is heavier. *Akhlada* carries the feel of settling down into something, inclining toward staying, seeking permanence there. And what does he incline toward? *Ila al-ard* - toward the earth. Downward. Low. Immediate. The surah is not merely telling us that he sinned. It is telling us the direction of his love.

Then:

*Wa-ittaba'a hawahu.*

He followed his own desire.

There is a sharp contrast between the two verbs in the unit. In ayah 175, Satan pursued him. In ayah 176, he followed his own desire. So the passage does not let him disappear into victim language. Yes, Satan overtook him after he slipped out of the signs. But the ayah also says he actively followed something within himself. The collapse is not described as pure external attack. It becomes inward consent.

### 3. The Dog Simile Exposes an Appetite That No Circumstance Heals

Then the Qur'an seals the warning with one of its harshest images:

His likeness is like that of a dog. If you drive it, it pants, and if you leave it, it pants.

[PAUSE]

The force of the image lies in its sameness under opposite conditions. Pressure does not change the state. Ease does not change the state. Whether pushed or left alone, it pants.

That is the point. The ayah is describing a self that has become governed by craving at such a deep level that circumstance no longer explains it. We often imagine that our restlessness comes from external stress. If things calm down, then the appetite will calm down too. If the environment changes, then the soul will recover on its own. But this simile tells us there is a lower kind of life in which the engine of desire keeps running in both conditions. Confronted, it pants. Unbothered, it pants.

Then the surah widens the lesson:

*Dhalika mathalu al-qawmi alladhina kadhdhabu bi-ayatina.*

That is the likeness of the people who denied Our signs.

So the anonymous man was never meant to remain only an individual case. He opens into a type. The story moves from a single one who slipped out of the signs to a whole people who deny them, and then the Prophet is told:

*Faqsusi al-qasasa la'allahum yatafakkarun.*

Relate the stories, so that perhaps they may reflect.

The story is therefore medicine, not spectacle. It is told so thought can happen before ruin hardens.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Nearness to Revelation Is Not the Same as Surrender to Revelation

These ayahs are especially severe for anyone who lives near religious language.

It is possible to learn, teach, quote, inherit, discuss, and even carry signs in a real sense, yet still remain capable of slipping out of them when desire becomes more beloved than elevation. That is why this passage comes after the primordial covenant. The soul has already testified. The signs have already been given. The danger now is not lack of access. The danger is betrayal from within nearness.

[PAUSE]

Many of us fear disbelief only in its loud forms. We imagine collapse as a dramatic public rejection. But this ayah shows another kind of disaster: a person can remain adjacent to truth long enough for it to sit on the skin while the heart quietly migrates elsewhere. The most frightening losses are often not sudden arguments against Allah. They are slow accommodations to the lower pull of the earth until the signs no longer feel like home.

### Theme 2: The Soul That Follows Desire Becomes Restless in Every Weather

The dog simile is humiliating by design. The Qur'an is not softening what unchecked appetite does to a human being.

When a person follows *hawah*, desire stops being one impulse among others and becomes an atmosphere. He cannot imagine that obedience might satisfy him more deeply than indulgence, because he now reads the whole world through hunger. So even opposite conditions do not free him. Pressure does not purify him. Freedom does not calm him. He remains winded by wanting.

[PAUSE]

We know this condition. A person thinks the next promotion, the next purchase, the next indulgence, the next private permission, the next round of attention will finally quiet the inside. But once the heart has clung downward, the issue is no longer the object being chased. The issue is the self that has trained itself to pant.

That is why the ayah's counterfactual hurts so much: *la-rafa'nahu biha.* He could have been raised by the very signs he shed. This means the opposite of restless appetite was available. Elevation was near. But the heart preferred attachment to what is low and immediate. The tragedy is not that guidance was absent. The tragedy is that it was refused in favor of gravity.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayahs compress an entire theology of spiritual collapse into a few brutal movements.

Allah commands His Messenger to recite the story of a man who was given the signs, then slipped out of them, after which Satan pursued him and he became lost. The signs could have raised him, but he chose to cling to the earth and follow his own desire. So the Qur'an gives him a likeness of humiliating restlessness: like a dog that pants whether driven or left alone. Then the warning widens from the unnamed man to any people who deny Allah's signs, and the Prophet is told to keep telling such stories so reflection might still begin.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask: am I treating revelation as something that has entered the heart, or only as something still resting on the surface? Where have I started calling downward attachment "realism" while the Qur'an would call it clinging to the earth? And when I feel my soul panting after the next thing, do I pause long enough to ask whether the problem is really the object in front of me, or the hunger I have taught myself to obey?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the unit lands here: **the servant who is given Allah's signs is not made safe by access alone, because if he slips out of those signs and consents to the downward pull of earth and desire, the very guidance that could have raised him becomes a witness against a restless self that no circumstance can soothe.**
