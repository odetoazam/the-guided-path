# We Removed What Was in Their Chests

## Surah Al-Araf (7:42-43)

[ayah:7:42] وَٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ وَعَمِلُوا۟ ٱلصَّـٰلِحَـٰتِ لَا نُكَلِّفُ نَفْسًا إِلَّا وُسْعَهَآ أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ أَصْحَـٰبُ ٱلْجَنَّةِ ۖ هُمْ فِيهَا خَـٰلِدُونَ

[ayah:7:43] وَنَزَعْنَا مَا فِى صُدُورِهِم مِّنْ غِلٍّ تَجْرِى مِن تَحْتِهِمُ ٱلْأَنْهَـٰرُ ۖ وَقَالُوا۟ ٱلْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ ٱلَّذِى هَدَىٰنَا لِهَـٰذَا وَمَا كُنَّا لِنَهْتَدِىَ لَوْلَآ أَنْ هَدَىٰنَا ٱللَّهُ ۖ لَقَدْ جَآءَتْ رُسُلُ رَبِّنَا بِٱلْحَقِّ ۖ وَنُودُوٓا۟ أَن تِلْكُمُ ٱلْجَنَّةُ أُورِثْتُمُوهَا بِمَا كُنتُمْ تَعْمَلُونَ

*Wa alladhina amanu wa 'amilu al-salihat la nukallifu nafsan illa wus'aha ula'ika ashabu al-jannati hum fiha khalidun.*

*Wa naza'na ma fi sudurihim min ghillin tajri min tahtihimu al-anhar. Wa qalu al-hamdu lillahi alladhi hadana li-hadha wa ma kunna li-nahtadiya lawla an hadana Allah. La-qad ja'at rusulu rabbina bi al-haqq. Wa nudu an tilkumu al-jannatu urithtumuhha bima kuntum ta'malun.*

**But those who believed and did righteous deeds - We do not burden any soul beyond its capacity - those are the companions of Paradise; they will abide therein. And We will remove whatever is in their chests of rancor. Rivers will flow beneath them, and they will say, "All praise is for Allah, who guided us to this; and we would never have been guided had Allah not guided us. The messengers of our Lord had certainly come with the truth." And it will be called out to them, "That is Paradise, which you have been made to inherit for what you used to do."**

## Introduction

After the suffocating images of barred heaven, impossible entry, and fire as bedding and covering, these ayahs open a different world.

The opening itself is merciful: *We do not burden any soul beyond its capacity.* Before Paradise is described, the believer is reminded that the whole path was already under divine calibration. Allah never asked the soul for what lay outside the soul's real scope. Then, once the people of faith enter the Garden, the healing is not only around them in rivers. It is within them. What is pulled out of their chests is *ghill* - rancor, residue, inward tightness toward one another.

[PAUSE]

And what do they say when all that is complete? Not "we managed," not "we proved ourselves," but *al-hamdu lillah* and *we would never have been guided had Allah not guided us.* So Paradise is shown here as mercy in command, mercy in purification, mercy in guidance, and mercy in inheritance.

[PAUSE]

The hook of this passage is this: **Allah's salvation is not only entry into a beautiful place but release from burdens beyond capacity, cleansing of the chest, and the final unveiling that all guidance leading there was from Him.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Promise Is Introduced With Capacity

The passage begins:

*La nukallifu nafsan illa wus'aha.*

We do not burden a soul except by its capacity.

[PAUSE]

That line is a deep mercy. The path of iman and righteous action is not built on divine disregard for creaturely limitation. *Wus'* names the space a soul can truly bear. So before the promise of Paradise, the Qur'an reassures the struggling servant that the command was never outside the reach Allah Himself knew for him.

### 2. Paradise Includes the Removal of Ghill

Then:

*Wa naza'na ma fi sudurihim min ghill.*

We removed what was in their chests of rancor.

[PAUSE]

The verb *naza'na* is strong. To remove, pull out, draw away. The point is not mere suppression. The corruption is extracted.

And the location matters: *sudurihim* - their chests. Paradise is not portrayed as a place where people merely keep smiling while inward knots remain. The inward knots are gone.

### 3. Their First Theology in Paradise Is Praise and Confessed Dependence

Then they say:

*Al-hamdu lillahi alladhi hadana li-hadha.*

All praise is for Allah who guided us to this.

[PAUSE]

Then the confession becomes even more explicit:

*Wa ma kunna li-nahtadiya lawla an hadana Allah.*

We would never have been guided had Allah not guided us.

This is one of the most beautiful sentences in the Qur'an because it captures the believer's final clarity. The people of Paradise are not confused about causality. They know their deeds mattered, but they also know that without Allah's prior guidance they would never have found the road.

### 4. Paradise Is Received as Inheritance

Then:

*An tilkumu al-jannatu urithtumuhha bima kuntum ta'malun.*

That is the Garden; you were made to inherit it for what you used to do.

[PAUSE]

The language of inheritance is tender. An inheritance is received as gift, though there is also a rightful relation to it. So the ayah keeps together two truths believers must never separate: Paradise is connected to deeds, yet it is still received with the feel of bestowed inheritance.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: One of the Greatest Pleasures of Paradise Is Inward Peace

People often picture Paradise first through landscape, food, companionship, and beauty. All of that is real. But this ayah points to something equally precious: no more *ghill*. No more residues of envy, resentment, rivalry, or wounded comparison lodged in the chest.

[PAUSE]

That means one of Jannah's great delights is not only what we get, but what we are freed from. Some of the hardest pain in dunya is not external poverty but interior corrosion. Paradise is a place where that corrosion is pulled out.

### Theme 2: The Saved Are the Least Self-Congratulatory

The people of Paradise do not narrate their arrival in the language of self-manufacture. Their first speech is praise. Their theology of arrival is dependence on divine hidayah. That should teach the believer how to think about every step of guidance already now.

[PAUSE]

The more a person is truly guided, the less he becomes intoxicated with himself as the source of that guidance. Real hidayah produces gratitude, not spiritual vanity.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs give us one of the Qur'an's most healing pictures of salvation.

Allah did not burden beyond capacity. Allah removed the rancor from the chest. Allah guided to this. Allah confirmed the messengers came with the truth. Then Paradise is announced as an inheritance connected to what the believers used to do. The entire scene is saturated with mercy before, during, and after arrival.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask: when I think about guidance now, do I experience it more as a private accomplishment or as something for which my deepest honest response should already be *al-hamdu lillah*?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the passage lands here: **the people of Paradise finally see that the path was never sustained by their strength alone, because the God who measured the burden also guided the road, purified the chest, and gave the Garden as an inheritance.**
