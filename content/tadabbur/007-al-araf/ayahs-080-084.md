# They Are People Who Keep Themselves Pure

## Surah Al-Araf (7:80-84)

[ayah:7:80] وَلُوطًا إِذْ قَالَ لِقَوْمِهِۦٓ أَتَأْتُونَ ٱلْفَـٰحِشَةَ مَا سَبَقَكُم بِهَا مِنْ أَحَدٍ مِّنَ ٱلْعَـٰلَمِينَ

So We saved him and his family except his wife; she was of those who remained behind. And We rained upon them a rain, so look how was the end of the criminals.**

## Introduction

The Lut passage is short, but it is among the most morally revealing sections in the surah. It names the indecency directly, specifies the desire-pattern directly, and then moves quickly to the social consequence of a people normalizing corruption: eventually the very presence of people who still care about purity feels intolerable.

[PAUSE]

That is what makes this passage so piercing. The people of Lut are not merely doing wrong in private. Their moral world has become inverted enough that when confronted, their instinct is exile. Remove the people who remind us of what purity is. Get them out of the town. Their claim to cleanliness has become offensive to us.

[PAUSE]

So the core warning here is not only about one condemned act. It is about what sustained rebellion does to public moral perception. **When transgression becomes communal culture, purity itself begins to look like provocation.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Crime Is Named as *Al-Fahishah*

Lut says:

*A-ta'tuna al-fahishata ma sabaqakum biha min ahadin mina al-'alamin.*

Do you commit the outrageous indecency such as no one in the worlds has preceded you in?

[PAUSE]

The word *al-fahishah* carries the sense of something grossly obscene, morally outrageous, beyond the bounds of what shame and fitrah should tolerate. The phrasing is not casual. The Qur'an wants the act to be morally named before it is sociologically explained.

Then comes:

*Ma sabaqakum biha min ahadin mina al-'alamin.*

No one from the worlds preceded you in it.

The point is not novelty as a compliment. It is innovation in corruption. They have stepped into a form of shamelessness that is being highlighted for its extremity.

### 2. The Desire Pattern Is Specified

Then the next ayah becomes more explicit:

*Innakum la-ta'tuna al-rijala shahwatan min duni al-nisa'.*

You indeed approach men with desire instead of women.

[PAUSE]

The word *shahwatan* matters because it exposes the motivational center: appetite, not right order; craving, not covenant; desire unruled by revelation.

And then:

*Bal antum qawmun musrifun.*

Rather, you are a people of excess.

*Israf* is going beyond due bounds. The ayah is not portraying desire as morally self-justifying. It is portraying desire that overran its boundary and then demanded public legitimacy.

### 3. Their "Answer" Is Not an Argument but an Expulsion

Then the surah says:

*Wa ma kana jawaba qawmihi illa an qalu akhrijuhum min qaryatikum.*

The answer of his people was only that they said: expel them from your town.

[PAUSE]

This is extraordinarily revealing. The Qur'an calls it their *jawab* - their answer - and then shows that it is not really an answer at all. They do not refute Lut morally. They do not contest the description meaningfully. They move to social force. If we cannot silence the truth by reason, we will silence the people who carry it by removal.

### 4. Purity Is Recast as the Problem

And why expel them?

*Innahum unasun yatatahharun.*

They are people who keep themselves pure.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the sharpest lines in the story. The verb *yatatahharun* is Form V of ط-ه-ر — *tafa''ala*, the reflexive-effortful pattern. It does not say "they are pure" (that would be a passive or a simple active participle). It says they are **working at purity**, actively making themselves pure. The form itself carries the image of sustained effort — someone bending their life toward cleanliness as a daily practice. That is what the society of Lut finds intolerable. Not pure people. *People who are trying.* What should have been praise becomes accusation. In a diseased moral environment, visible effort at purity begins to feel like judgment on those who have stopped trying. So rather than repent, the society tries to eliminate the contrast.

### 5. The Ending Is Brief Because the Judgment Is Clear

Then:

*Fa-anjaynahu wa ahlahu illa imra'atahu kanat mina al-ghabirin.*

We saved him and his family except his wife; she was among those left behind.

[PAUSE]

Even proximity to the household of a prophet is not salvific if inward allegiance is elsewhere. Then the end:

*Wa amtarna 'alayhim mataran.*

We rained upon them a rain.

The repetition of the rain-root in verb and noun gives the punishment a stark bluntness. It came down over them in a fitting finality.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Public Sin Eventually Demands Public Moral Inversion

The people of Lut show that open corruption does not remain content with private permission. Over time it wants moral reversal. It wants language changed, shame displaced, standards mocked, and purity treated as aggression.

[PAUSE]

That is why their answer is exile. When a society grows attached to its corruption, righteous presence itself becomes unbearable. The problem is no longer simply what people are doing. The problem becomes what they must do to keep doing it without interruption from conscience.

### Theme 2: Desire Is Not Neutral When It Defies the Limits of Allah

The ayah's naming of *shahwah* is deeply important. The Qur'an does not allow desire to sit above judgment as though wanting were enough to justify acting. Desire is part of the test. It can be ordered or disordered, disciplined or excessive, surrendered or enthroned.

[PAUSE]

So when the ayah closes with *musrifun*, it teaches that sin is not only about the object chosen but about the transgressive relation to desire itself. Excess means wanting past the line Allah drew, then defending that excess as if restraint were the true problem.

## Closing Synthesis

Lut's people were asked a piercing question: do you really commit this outrageous indecency, something no one preceded you in? The Qur'an names the act, names the appetite, names the excess, and then names the social consequence of normalized corruption.

[PAUSE]

Their answer was not repentance. It was, "Expel them." Why? "They are people who keep themselves pure."

[PAUSE]

That sentence should make us tremble. There is a point of moral decline at which people stop merely resisting truth and begin resenting those who still wish to live by it.

[PAUSE]

May Allah protect us from excess that distorts perception, keep purity beloved in our hearts, and save us from ever becoming people to whom sincere moral clarity feels like something to exile.
