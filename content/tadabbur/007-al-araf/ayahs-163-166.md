# Why Do You Warn a People Allah Will Destroy?

## Surah Al-Araf (7:163-166)

[ayah:7:163] وَسْـَٔلْهُمْ عَنِ ٱلْقَرْيَةِ ٱلَّتِى كَانَتْ حَاضِرَةَ ٱلْبَحْرِ إِذْ يَعْدُونَ فِى ٱلسَّبْتِ إِذْ تَأْتِيهِمْ حِيتَانُهُمْ يَوْمَ سَبْتِهِمْ شُرَّعًا وَيَوْمَ لَا يَسْبِتُونَ ۙ لَا تَأْتِيهِمْ ۚ كَذَٰلِكَ نَبْلُوهُم بِمَا كَانُوا۟ يَفْسُقُونَ

[ayah:7:164] وَإِذْ قَالَتْ أُمَّةٌ مِّنْهُمْ لِمَ تَعِظُونَ قَوْمًا ۙ ٱللَّهُ مُهْلِكُهُمْ أَوْ مُعَذِّبُهُمْ عَذَابًا شَدِيدًا ۖ قَالُوا۟ مَعْذِرَةً إِلَىٰ رَبِّكُمْ وَلَعَلَّهُمْ يَتَّقُونَ

[ayah:7:165] فَلَمَّا نَسُوا۟ مَا ذُكِّرُوا۟ بِهِۦٓ أَنجَيْنَا ٱلَّذِينَ يَنْهَوْنَ عَنِ ٱلسُّوٓءِ وَأَخَذْنَا ٱلَّذِينَ ظَلَمُوا۟ بِعَذَابٍۭ بَـِٔيسٍۭ بِمَا كَانُوا۟ يَفْسُقُونَ

[ayah:7:166] فَلَمَّا عَتَوْا۟ عَن مَّا نُهُوا۟ عَنْهُ قُلْنَا لَهُمْ كُونُوا۟ قِرَدَةً خَـٰسِـِٔينَ

*Wa s'alhum 'ani al-qaryati allati kanat hadirata al-bahr, idh ya'duna fi al-sabt. Idh ta'tihim hitanuhum yawma sabtihim shurra'an wa yawma la yasbitun la ta'tihim. Kadhalika nabluhum bima kanu yafsuqun.*

*Wa idh qalat ummatun minhum lima ta'izuna qawman Allahu muhlikuhum aw mu'adhdhibuhum 'adhaban shadidan. Qalu ma'dhiratan ila Rabbikum wa la'allahum yattaqun.*

*Fa-lamma nasu ma dhukkiru bihi anjayna alladhina yanhawna 'ani al-su'i wa akhadhna alladhina zalamu bi-'adhabin ba'isin bima kanu yafsuqun.*

*Fa-lamma 'ataw 'an ma nuhu 'anhu qulna lahum kunu qiradatan khasi'in.*

**And ask them about the town that was by the sea, when they were transgressing concerning the Sabbath, when their fish came to them openly on the day of their Sabbath, and on the day they did not keep Sabbath they did not come. Thus did We test them because they used to defiantly disobey. And when a community among them said, 'Why do you admonish a people whom Allah is going to destroy or punish with a severe punishment?' They said, 'As an excuse before your Lord, and so that perhaps they may become mindful.' Then when they forgot what they had been reminded of, We saved those who forbade evil and seized those who wronged with a miserable punishment because they used to defiantly disobey. So when they insolently persisted in what they had been forbidden from, We said to them, 'Be apes, despised.'**

## Introduction

Some tests do not hide the temptation. They place it directly in front of the eyes on the very day obedience is hardest.

That is the shape of the Sabbath scene. The fish do not become scarce in general. They become strangely available at the exact point they are not to be taken, then absent when the restriction is lifted. The trial is almost surgical. It reaches straight into appetite and timing. It asks whether the servant can obey Allah not only when obedience is broadly reasonable, but when disobedience appears vividly easy and loss feels immediate.

[PAUSE]

But the passage does not only test the violators. It also tests the community watching them. Why keep admonishing a people already headed for punishment? Why spend words where change looks unlikely? The Qur'an answers by narrating salvation around those who kept forbidding evil. That means the story is not only about private temptation. It is about whether a believing community remains morally awake when corruption becomes normalized.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Trial Is Timed With Painful Precision

Ayah 163 says:

*Idh ta'tihim hitanuhum yawma sabtihim shurra'an wa yawma la yasbitun la ta'tihim.*

Their fish came to them on the day of their Sabbath *shurra'an*, and on the day they did not keep Sabbath they did not come.

[PAUSE]

The structure itself is the test. Presence on the forbidden day, absence on the permitted day. The verse wants us to feel the precision.

The word *shurra'an* intensifies the image. The fish come exposed, near, surfacing openly. The temptation is not buried deep under complicated circumstances. It is spread out in front of them.

Then the ayah says:

*Kadhalika nabluhum.*

Thus We test them.

This is important. The Qur'an does not narrate the pattern as bad luck or ecological oddity. It is *bala'*. Allah is arranging the moral scene. But the verse also tells us why:

*Bima kanu yafsuqun.*

Because they used to defiantly disobey.

The test is not disconnected from character. Persistent fisq prepares the soul for a test in which hidden rebellion gets drawn into public exposure.

### 2. One Group Asks Whether Warning Still Matters

Ayah 164 introduces another internal fracture:

*Lima ta'izuna qawman Allahu muhlikuhum aw mu'adhdhibuhum 'adhaban shadidan?*

Why do you admonish a people Allah will destroy or punish severely?

[PAUSE]

That is a devastating question because it sounds practical. The wrongdoers look finished. Their outcome looks settled. So what is the point of continued admonition?

The answer comes from those who keep speaking:

*Ma'dhiratan ila Rabbikum wa la'allahum yattaqun.*

As an excuse before your Lord, and so that perhaps they may become mindful.

Two reasons are given. One concerns the speaker before Allah: we must not be silent without excuse before our Lord. The other concerns the sinner: perhaps there is still taqwa to be awakened. The people of warning therefore are not naïve. They know punishment is possible. Yet they also know that obedience to Allah includes speaking before certainty hardens into silence.

### 3. Salvation Is Explicitly Attached to Forbidding Evil

Ayah 165 says:

*Anjayna alladhina yanhawna 'ani al-su'.*

We saved those who forbade evil.

[PAUSE]

That is one of the sharpest moral lines in the passage. The Qur'an does not describe them merely as privately decent. It names them through action: they forbade the evil. Their salvation is narrated in relation to that moral stance.

Then the violators are seized:

*Wa akhadhna alladhina zalamu bi-'adhabin ba'is.*

We seized those who wronged with a miserable punishment.

Then the passage intensifies further in ayah 166:

*Fa-lamma 'ataw 'an ma nuhu 'anhu...*

When they insolently persisted regarding what they had been forbidden from...

The verb *'ataw* is stronger than ordinary slipping. It carries haughty overstepping, stubborn insolence. The community has moved from temptation to transgression to arrogant persistence. At that point the command comes:

*Kunu qiradatan khasi'in.*

Be apes, despised.

The form of abasement matches the inner degradation that open insolence had already prepared.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: A Test Often Comes Exactly at the Point Where Desire Thinks It Has the Best Argument

Why did the fish come on that day and not the other?

Because tests are not always generic. They arrive at the seam where the soul feels the most pressure to rationalize. If the fish had come equally every day, the command would still matter, but the inner exposure would be less sharp. Here the command collides with the timing of appetite. Obedience begins to look costly in a very visible way.

[PAUSE]

That is how many of our own tests work. The thing you must leave often appears easiest when leaving it feels most painful. The compromise opens exactly when integrity looks most inconvenient. The nafs says: why would Allah place it right in front of me unless I were meant to take it? But the Qur'an teaches the opposite. Sometimes Allah places the thing directly before you so the hidden loyalties of the heart become unmistakable.

The real issue is not the fish. It is whether the servant loves Allah's boundary more than the arrangement of immediate gain.

### Theme 2: Moral Speech Is Part of Faith Even When Outcomes Look Closed

The second group in the story asks a question many people still ask in softer language: what is the point of advising people who probably will not change?

The Qur'an's answer is profound. We speak as *ma'dhiratan ila Rabbikum* - to stand before Allah without having normalized evil through silence. And we speak because *la'allahum yattaqun* - perhaps hearts can still turn in ways we are not qualified to pronounce impossible.

[PAUSE]

This does not mean constant noisy condemnation. It means a believing community must not let repeated wrongdoing train it into moral resignation. Once people become comfortable saying, "they are probably doomed anyway, so leave them," a vital nerve of the ummah has started to die.

The passage also warns us not to confuse private righteousness with complete innocence. The saved are identified here not only by abstaining from sin, but by opposing it. Their refusal to surrender the language of warning becomes part of the story of their rescue.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs gather temptation, communal responsibility, and divine judgment into one unforgettable sequence.

The fish appear openly on the day of prohibition and vanish on the day of permission, exposing the soul at the point of appetite. Within the town, some wonder why admonition should continue at all, while others keep speaking for the sake of standing before Allah and for the chance that hearts may still recover taqwa. Then the outcome comes: those forbidding evil are explicitly saved, the wrongdoers are seized with harsh punishment, and when defiance hardens into insolent overstepping, abasement descends.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask: where in my life does temptation become strongest exactly when obedience feels most costly? And when wrongdoing becomes common around me, have I drifted into the cynicism that says warning no longer matters, or do I still speak truth as an offering before Allah and a mercy toward people?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the unit lands here: **Allah may place temptation at the very edge of the forbidden to expose what the heart truly loves, and the believers who refuse both the sin and the silence around it are the ones the Qur'an remembers on the side of rescue.**
