# My Mercy Encompasses All Things

## Surah Al-Araf (7:153-156)

## Introduction

After the calf, the surah could have ended the scene with humiliation. Instead it opens a door, then deepens the test, then opens the door wider than we knew it could open.

First comes a general law: people who did evil and then repented and believed still stand beneath divine forgiveness. Then comes one of the strangest lines in the Qur'an: the anger fell silent from Musa. Then the tablets are taken again, now described not only as command but as guidance and mercy. Then the selected seventy are seized by the quake, and Musa's prayer begins to gather guilt, fitnah, protection, forgiveness, mercy, dunya, akhirah, and return. The answer comes with one of the greatest declarations in revelation: *wa rahmati wasi'at kulla shay'.*

[PAUSE]

This unit teaches us that mercy in the Qur'an is never sentimental. It is spoken in the middle of fallout. It does not erase seriousness, but it refuses despair. Punishment remains real, fitnah remains real, foolishness inside the community remains real, and yet the surah insists that beyond all of that there is still a divine writing of good in this world and the next for those who truly return.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. Repentance Comes After Evil, and the Door Is Still Open

Ayah 153 says:

*Wa alladhina 'amilu al-sayyi'ati thumma tabu min ba'diha wa amanu...*

Those who did evil deeds, then repented after them, and believed.

[PAUSE]

The order is mercifully realistic. The Qur'an does not pretend some people return to Allah without first actually having done *sayyi'at*. It names the evil, then names the turning after it. That matters. Many people think their sin disqualifies them from meaningful return because it was too conscious, too ugly, too recent. This ayah speaks directly into that lie.

Then it says:

*Inna Rabbaka min ba'diha la-ghafurun rahim.*

Indeed your Lord, after that, is surely forgiving and merciful.

The phrase *min ba'diha* is repeated. After it. After the evil. After the collapse. After the thing that now fills you with shame. Mercy is not located before the failure only. The ayah places mercy after it as well.

### 2. The Anger Does Not Disappear; It Falls Silent

Ayah 154 opens:

*Wa lamma sakata 'an Musa al-ghadabu...*

When the anger fell silent from Musa.

[PAUSE]

This is an extraordinary choice. The verse does not simply say, "when Musa calmed down." It makes the anger almost like an occupying force that has now become quiet. *Sakata* is silence, cessation of speech, stilling. The anger had a presence in the scene, and then it went silent.

That wording honors emotional reality. Sometimes anger does not vanish instantly because we decided to be better. It settles. It quiets. It loosens its grip. The Qur'an names that process with startling tenderness.

Then Musa takes the tablets again, and the verse says:

*Wa fi nuskhatiha hudan wa rahmatun...*

In their copy was guidance and mercy.

The tablets return not merely as law but as healing direction. After the rage, revelation is re-received under the sign of mercy. That is beautiful. The same tablets that were thrown in anguish are now taken again as something the fearful can live by.

### 3. Musa's Prayer Expands Until It Meets "My Mercy Encompasses All Things"

Ayah 155 gathers a remarkable du'a. It includes communal fear, divine decree, fitnah, guidance, forgiveness, mercy, and Allah's protecting nearness:

*Anta waliyyuna fa-ghfir lana wa irhamna...*

You are our Protector, so forgive us and have mercy on us.

[PAUSE]

Then ayah 156 adds:

*Wa uktub lana fi hadhihi al-dunya hasanatan wa fi al-akhirah inna hudna ilayk.*

Write for us good in this world and in the Hereafter; indeed, we have turned to You.

The verb *hudna* is powerful. It is not simply "we repented" in the most familiar lexical field. It carries returning, inclining back, coming home toward Allah. The prayer is not only for pardon. It is for a rewritten future in both worlds.

Then comes the answer:

*Wa rahmati wasi'at kulla shay'.*

My mercy encompasses all things.

[PAUSE]

That is one of the widest statements in the Qur'an. Yet the verse does not stop there. It immediately moves from breadth to inscription:

*Fa-sa-aktubuha lilladhina yattaquna...*

So I will write it for those who have taqwa...

Mercy is universal in reach and particular in reception. It surrounds everything, but it is specially written for those who answer it with taqwa, zakah, and belief in the signs.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: One of Allah's Mercies Is That He Lets Us Return After We Have Already Seen the Worst of Ourselves

There is a kind of shame that whispers: now that you know what you are capable of, you should stop pretending you belong near Allah.

This passage breaks that whisper.

The people have already fallen into calf-worship. Musa has already overflowed in anger. The community has already become the site of foolishness and quake. None of that is minimized. Yet the unit begins by reopening the door for those who did evil and then turned, and it ends with Allah answering a prayer for written good in both worlds.

[PAUSE]

That means one of the most crippling spiritual lies is the belief that the honest sight of our failure should push us away from Allah rather than toward Him. The Qur'an repeatedly teaches the opposite. The right reading of failure is not self-expulsion. It is return. *Inna hudna ilayk.* We have come back toward You.

This does not flatten accountability. The return is real only if the turning is real. But it means despair is itself a false reading of Allah.

### Theme 2: Mercy Is Vast, but It Is Not Vague

The phrase *wa rahmati wasi'at kulla shay'* is often heard in isolation, and rightly so - it deserves to stop us. But the surah does not leave it floating. It attaches it to criteria.

That is essential.

[PAUSE]

Modern people often want mercy as atmosphere without transformation. We want a divine kindness that comforts but does not command, soothes but does not reorder. The Qur'an will not let us have that version. Allah's mercy truly encompasses all things, but the mercy that is written in a saving, shaping way is tied to taqwa, zakah, and iman in the signs.

That means mercy is not opposed to moral seriousness. Mercy is what makes moral seriousness meaningful rather than hopeless. It is the reason taqwa is possible, the reason zakah matters, the reason belief in the signs can still be lived after collapse. Mercy is not the suspension of response. It is the divine generosity that makes response worth attempting.

This is why the unit holds punishment and mercy together. *'Adhabi usibu bihi man asha'.* My punishment, I afflict with it whom I will. The verse does not deny that. But it refuses to let punishment become the final horizon. Beyond it, wider than it, stands a mercy that has already encompassed all things.

## Closing Synthesis

These four ayahs turn the aftermath of catastrophe into one of the surah's greatest disclosures of mercy.

Those who did evil and then repented are told that forgiveness still stands after the sin. Musa's anger is not denied, but it falls silent, and he receives the tablets again under the sign of guidance and mercy. The seventy are chosen, the quake falls, and Musa's prayer stretches from communal guilt to divine guardianship and a request for written good in both worlds. Allah answers by keeping punishment in His hand while declaring that His mercy encompasses everything before being specially written for the people of taqwa, zakah, and belief.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask: do I believe Allah is merciful only before I fail, or also after I fail? When anger quiets in me, do I pick up revelation again as guidance and mercy, or stay estranged from it? Have I reduced divine mercy to a vague feeling, or do I let it actually push me toward taqwa, zakah, and deeper belief in the signs?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the unit lands here: **after evil, anger, and communal shaking, Allah reopens the road of return, and the servant's truest hope is to come back under a mercy that is wider than everything yet written for those who answer it with lived faith.**
