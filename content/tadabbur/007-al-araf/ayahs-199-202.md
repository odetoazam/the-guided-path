# Remember, and Suddenly You See

## Surah Al-Araf (7:199-202)

## Introduction

Some of the hardest spiritual failures do not begin in grand moments. They begin in irritation.

A person is provoked, misunderstood, insulted, baited, or worn down. A comment lands. A tone stings. A wound opens. The heart tightens. And before any major outward sin appears, something smaller enters first: agitation, resentment, inner spinning, the wish to answer jahalah with our own jahalah.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs arrive exactly there. They do not speak in abstractions. They give a sequence for what to do when dealing with people becomes morally expensive. First, they teach posture. Then they name the satanic interruption that tries to rupture that posture. Then they show what taqwa looks like in real time: not never being touched, but remembering fast enough that sight returns. And finally they show the opposite pattern, people continually extended deeper into error with no braking mechanism.

This is one of the Qur'an's most practical maps of spiritual recovery.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. Three Commands for the Human World

Ayah 199 comes as a chain of imperatives:

*Khudhi al-'afwa wa'mur bil-'urfi wa a'rid 'ani al-jahilin.*

Take *al-'afw*, command *al-'urf*, and turn away from the ignorant.

[PAUSE]

The opening verb, *khudh*, is direct: take, receive, adopt. And what is to be taken is *al-'afw*. The word carries the sense of pardon, gentleness, and what comes readily without harsh extraction. It teaches a mode of dealing with people that does not demand the impossible from them at every turn.

Then: *wa'mur bil-'urf*. *'Urf* comes from what is recognized, known, acknowledged as sound and upright. The command is not to obsess over the bizarre or the fringe, but to keep calling people toward what sound fitrah already knows as good.

Then comes the third command: *wa a'rid 'ani al-jahilin* - turn away from the ignorant.

This is important. The ayah does not say become like them, outshout them, or let them set the terms of the encounter. It teaches dignified non-participation. Not every provocation deserves an argument. Some things are defeated by refusing to descend into them.

### 2. The Qur'an Anticipates the Inner Ambush

But the passage is psychologically exact. It knows that even when a person is told to pardon, command good, and turn away, an inner surge can still come.

So the next ayah says:

*Wa imma yanzaghannaka mina al-shaytani nazghun fa-sta'idh billah.*

If a *nazgh* from Shaytan should incite you, seek refuge in Allah.

[PAUSE]

The root *nazgh* carries the sense of a prick, a jab, a disturbing provocation. The Qur'an is not pretending that a faithful person never feels the intrusion. It is naming the moment where irritation is no longer merely social. Something darker is trying to push the heart off balance.

And the response is immediate: *fa-sta'idh billah*. Seek refuge in Allah.

Not first "win the argument." Not first "explain yourself." Not first "prove that you were right to be angry." The first response is refuge. This is profound because it teaches that some states of agitation should not be trusted as neutral. They should be interrupted.

Then the ayah closes:

*Innahu sami'un 'alim.*

Indeed, He is Hearing and Knowing.

Allah hears what was said to you, what you were about to say back, and what the whisper sounded like inside you. He knows the wound, the trigger, the injustice, and the hidden ego that can attach itself to real injury. Refuge is meaningful because the One in whom we seek refuge actually hears and knows the whole interior event.

### 3. Taqwa Does Not Mean Never Being Touched

Ayah 201 is one of the most hopeful descriptions of the people of taqwa:

*Inna alladhina ittaqaw idha massahum ta'ifun mina al-shaytani tadhakkaru fa-idha hum mubsirun.*

Indeed, those who have taqwa, when a *ta'if* from Shaytan touches them, they remember, and suddenly they are seeing.

[PAUSE]

Notice the mercy here. The ayah does not define the muttaqun as people whom Shaytan never approaches. It says *idha massahum* - when it touches them. Contact happens.

Then it describes that contact as *ta'if*. The word evokes something circling, passing by, making a round. It is like a visitation that comes over the heart. The believer is not praised for being made of stone. The believer is praised for what happens next.

*Tadhakkaru* - they remember.

They remember Allah. They remember truth. They remember the larger frame. They remember who the whisper is serving and where the path leads. And the result comes with startling suddenness:

*Fa-idha hum mubsirun* - and then, suddenly, they are seeing clearly.

The passage does not merely say they calm down. It says sight returns. Shaytan's work often depends on narrowing perception. Remembrance reopens it.

### 4. The Opposite Pattern Is Continuous Extension

Then ayah 202 gives the contrast:

*Wa ikhwanuhum yamuddunahum fi al-ghayyi thumma la yuqsirun.*

Their brothers keep extending them in error; then they do not stop short.

[PAUSE]

This is a frightening sentence because it describes a life without interruption. The verb *yamuddunahum* suggests extension, drawing out, supplying more length to the movement. The person is not merely falling once. The path is being prolonged.

And it happens *fi al-ghayy* - in ruinous deviation, not simple mistake.

Then comes the grim ending: *thumma la yuqsirun*. They do not slacken. They do not pull back. They do not shorten the motion.

So the passage gives us two models. In one, the whisper touches, remembrance intervenes, and sight returns. In the other, the pull is extended, reinforced, and never interrupted.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Qur'an Gives a Full Recovery Sequence

These ayahs are powerful because they do not offer isolated advice. They give an ordered sequence.

With people: take the path of pardon and workable dealing, keep calling to recognized good, and do not let ignorance define the terms. With the whisper: seek refuge immediately. With the inner state: remember until sight returns. With the warning case: learn to fear the condition in which the slide continues unchecked.

[PAUSE]

This means Islamic character is not just about public manners. It includes an inward emergency protocol. A person may know how to speak gently and still be internally hijacked. The Qur'an therefore links adab with isti'adhah, and isti'adhah with tadhakkur, and tadhakkur with restored perception.

That is one reason these verses feel so alive. They are not describing an idealized soul. They are teaching a recoverable one.

### Theme 2: Remembrance Is Not Decorative. It Restores Sight.

We often speak of dhikr as though it were a pious supplement, something beautiful but secondary. This passage treats remembrance as diagnostic and corrective.

Shaytan's touch distorts vision. Everything suddenly feels immediate, personal, absolute, and worthy of reaction. The offended self fills the frame. The possible consequences disappear. The unseen fades. The nafs becomes persuasive.

[PAUSE]

Then the muttaqi remembers. And that remembrance is not sentimental. It changes what can be seen.

This has enormous relevance for how we live now. Many environments train us into constant agitation. We are baited into reaction, sped up into opinion, nudged toward outrage, and rewarded for escalation. In that atmosphere, one of the clearest signs of taqwa is not that you never feel the hook. It is that you know what to do when you feel it: seek refuge, remember, and wait for sight to come back before you move.

The opposite condition is also familiar. There are cycles where error keeps getting more fuel. A grievance is fed. A lust is fed. An ideology is fed. An insecurity is fed. The soul is continually extended in *ghayy* and no braking force enters. That is ayah 202 in human experience.

## Closing Synthesis

This passage teaches us that the moral life is often decided in the few seconds between provocation and response.

The Qur'an begins by training our posture toward people: accept the workable path, keep calling to recognized good, and refuse to descend into ignorance. Then it names the invisible escalation beneath many outward conflicts: a satanic jab that tries to move the heart off its axis. Then it gives the cure: seek refuge, remember, and let sight return. Finally, it shows the disaster case, a life where the pull into error is simply extended and never interrupted.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with these ayahs is to ask: when I am touched by irritation, what happens next inside me? Do I continue the chain automatically, or do I know how to break it? Do I have a practiced road back to refuge and remembrance, or does my agitation keep receiving reinforcement until I no longer see clearly?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of this unit lands here: **the Qur'an teaches that taqwa is not the absence of satanic touch but the presence of a rapid return, where refuge and remembrance break the provocation, restore sight, and keep the soul from being endlessly extended in error.**
