# Little Is It That You Give Thanks

## Surah Al-Araf (7:10)

[ayah:7:10] وَلَقَدْ مَكَّنَّـٰكُمْ فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ وَجَعَلْنَا لَكُمْ فِيهَا مَعَـٰيِشَ ۗ قَلِيلًا مَّا تَشْكُرُونَ

*Wa la-qad makkannakum fi al-ardi wa ja'alna lakum fiha ma'ayish. Qalilan ma tashkurun.*

**And We have certainly established you upon the earth and made for you therein means of living. Little is it that you give thanks.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

After the scales, the surah returns us to the world we are living in now.

That is important. The Qur'an does not speak about final judgment and then leave worldly life undefined. It reminds us what this life already is: placement, provision, and therefore responsibility. You are on the earth because Allah established you there. You survive there because Allah made livelihoods for you inside it. The question is not whether you are gifted. The question is whether the gift becomes gratitude.

[PAUSE]

And the answer the verse gives is painful in its brevity: *qalilan ma tashkurun* - little is it that you give thanks.

[PAUSE]

The hook of this verse is this: **human life on earth begins not from self-made achievement but from divine placing and provision, and ingratitude is essentially a failure to read our own existence correctly.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. Human Beings Are Not Merely Present on Earth; They Are Established There

The verse says:

*Wa la-qad makkannakum fi al-ard.*

We certainly established you in the earth.

[PAUSE]

The root of *makkannakum* is not simple placement only. It carries settling, enabling, giving footing, making room to act. The verse is describing the earth not as an accidental habitat we happened to master, but as a place where Allah Himself gave humanity standing.

### 2. Livelihood Is Made, Not Self-Originated

Then:

*Wa ja'alna lakum fiha ma'ayish.*

And We made for you therein livelihoods.

The word *ma'ayish* comes from life and living. The verse is not narrowly about salary or market exchange. It is about the means by which life is sustained.

[PAUSE]

That means the believer is being taught to look at the whole fabric of sustenance differently. Food, land, skill, opportunity, capacity, and openings in the world are not finally self-generated possessions. They are made by Allah for us inside the place where He settled us.

### 3. The Verse Ends With a Small Number and a Large Shame

Then the close:

*Qalilan ma tashkurun.*

Little is it that you give thanks.

[PAUSE]

The phrase is short, but it lands heavily. It does not say you never give thanks at all. It says the proportion is painfully small.

That is often how ingratitude works. It can live inside a life that still occasionally says *alhamdulillah*, while most of the heart's actual posture remains entitlement, distraction, complaint, or forgetfulness.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Gratitude Begins by Seeing That We Were Placed Before We Performed

The verse quietly shatters a common human illusion: that we stand on earth by our own making.

But Allah says: We established you. We made for you your means of living.

[PAUSE]

That should produce humility before it produces thankfulness. Because until a person sees that his footing itself is gifted, he will keep treating existence as earned and provision as self-authored.

### Theme 2: Ingratitude Is Misreading Reality

When the verse says *little is it that you give thanks*, it is not merely criticizing poor manners. It is diagnosing a false reading of the world.

If Allah established you, if Allah made your means of living, then gratitude is simply truthfulness toward reality. Ingratitude is a refusal to interpret your life correctly.

[PAUSE]

That makes this verse an important bridge into the Adam-Iblis section that follows. The battle there will hinge on pride, resentment, and refusal of the posture of grateful surrender. This verse quietly prepares us by naming the normal human failure first: little gratitude.

## Closing Synthesis

This short ayah gives us a whole theology of earthly life.

We are settled by Allah, sustained by Allah, and tested by whether those gifts become shukr. The tragedy is not that blessings are absent. The tragedy is that gratitude is so often small compared to what has been given.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this verse is to ask: do I mostly experience my life as something I built, or as something Allah first made possible for me to live inside?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the verse lands here: **when a human being forgets that both footing and livelihood were made for him by Allah, gratitude shrinks and entitlement grows, but when he remembers how placed and provided for he really is, shukr becomes the truthful way to inhabit the earth.**
