# Make for Us a God Like Their Gods

## Surah Al-Araf (7:138-140)

[ayah:7:138] وَجَـٰوَزْنَا بِبَنِىٓ إِسْرَٰٓءِيلَ ٱلْبَحْرَ فَأَتَوْا۟ عَلَىٰ قَوْمٍ يَعْكُفُونَ عَلَىٰٓ أَصْنَامٍ لَّهُمْ ۚ قَالُوا۟ يَـٰمُوسَى ٱجْعَل لَّنَآ إِلَـٰهًا كَمَا لَهُمْ ءَالِهَةٌ ۚ قَالَ إِنَّكُمْ قَوْمٌ تَجْهَلُونَ

[ayah:7:139] إِنَّ هَـٰٓؤُلَآءِ مُتَبَّرٌ مَّا هُمْ فِيهِ وَبَـٰطِلٌ مَّا كَانُوا۟ يَعْمَلُونَ

[ayah:7:140] قَالَ أَغَيْرَ ٱللَّهِ أَبْغِيكُمْ إِلَـٰهًا وَهُوَ فَضَّلَكُمْ عَلَى ٱلْعَـٰلَمِينَ

*Wa jawazna bi-bani Isra'ila al-bahra fa-ataw ala qawmin ya'kufuna ala asnamin lahum. Qalu ya Musa ij'al lana ilahan kama lahum alihah. Qala innakum qawmun tajhalun.*

**And We took the Children of Israel across the sea, then they came upon a people devoted to idols of theirs. They said, 'O Musa, make for us a god just as they have gods.' He said, 'Indeed, you are an ignorant people. Indeed, these people - destroyed is what they are engaged in, and false is what they used to do. He said, 'Is it other than Allah that I should seek for you as a god while He has favored you over the worlds?'**

## Introduction

External rescue can happen faster than internal purification.

That is one of the hardest truths in the Qur'an's portrayal of Bani Isra'il. They have crossed the sea. They have watched the downfall of Fir'awn. They have lived through the public vindication of Allah's promise. Yet almost immediately they see a people devoted to idols and ask Musa for something similar. The body has crossed the water, but the imagination has not yet crossed out of the world of visible cultic reassurance.

[PAUSE]

This is why the scene is so spiritually important. It tells us that miracles do not automatically mature taste. Freedom from oppression does not by itself heal the inner habit of wanting something seen, grasped, and culturally familiar to steady the heart. Musa's rebuke is sharp because the request is not harmless curiosity. It is a revealing symptom of how quickly the liberated soul can still desire old forms of captivity.

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. They Pass By a People "Devoted" to Their Idols

The Qur'an says:

*Ya'kufuna ala asnamin lahum.*

A people devoted over idols of theirs.

[PAUSE]

The verb *ya'kufun* carries staying, attaching oneself, lingering in devoted fixation. This is not casual cultural ornament. It is absorbed religious occupation. Bani Isra'il do not merely see statues. They see visible forms around which a whole pattern of attachment has been organized.

And that is what attracts the request. Not only the object, but the psychological clarity of having something seen and held.

### 2. The Request Reveals Jahalah, Not Mere Curiosity

They say:

*Ij'al lana ilahan kama lahum alihah.*

Make for us a god as they have gods.

[PAUSE]

The comparison is explicit: *kama*. Like theirs. The request is imitative desire. They want religious shape on a familiar social pattern.

Musa answers:

*Innakum qawmun tajhalun.*

Indeed, you are a people acting in ignorance.

This is not just lack of information. *Jahl* in Qur'anic moral language is a state of foolish, misdirected perception, a soul not placing things where they belong. After everything they have seen, the request shows not absence of evidence but corruption of reading.

### 3. Their Religion Is Headed for Ruin, and Allah's Favor Should Have Been Enough

Musa says of the idolaters:

*Mutabbarun ma hum fihi wa batilun ma kanu ya'malun.*

Destroyed is what they are in, and false is what they used to do.

[PAUSE]

The point is not simply that idol worship is wrong in abstract theology. It is headed for collapse. It is spiritually doomed from the inside. What looks stable in the present is already marked for ruin before Allah.

Then he turns back to his people:

*A-ghayra Allahi abghikum ilahan wa huwa faddalakum ala al-alamin.*

Should I seek for you a god other than Allah while He has favored you over the worlds?

The argument is built on memory of grace. How can the soul that was just carried through the sea still crave another god?

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: We Often Crave Visible Religions When Trust Still Feels Unstable

There is something deeply recognizable in Bani Isra'il's request.

Many people do not consciously want to worship idols, but they do want a form of security that works like an idol. Something visible, graspable, ambient, and culturally reinforced. Something that can sit in front of the eyes and quiet uncertainty. The heart often says, in subtler language: give me a version of trust I can hold in my hand.

[PAUSE]

That is why this scene remains contemporary. We may not carve images, yet we still seek concrete replacements for unseen dependence: systems, symbols, brands of belonging, personalities, political forms, or habits of consumption that make us feel religiously stabilized without the harder work of tawhid. The issue is not always the object. Often it is the craving for a god-like certainty on familiar human terms.

### Theme 2: Deliverance Does Not Remove the Need for Inner Education

It would have been easy to assume that the sea crossing solved everything. But the Qur'an refuses that simplification. Salvation from Fir'awn was real, but it did not erase the need for تربيه of the heart. In fact, liberation exposed how much inner work still remained.

[PAUSE]

This matters for all of us. We imagine that once Allah rescues us from one obvious bondage, we will automatically become clear-sighted. But often the first days after rescue reveal what we still carry. Old desires, borrowed imaginations, and shallow tastes come with us across the sea unless Allah reforms them through ongoing guidance.

Musa's rebuke is therefore mercy. He does not indulge the request as a stage in spiritual exploration. He names it as jahalah, tells them the idolaters' world is already ruined, and sends them back to memory of Allah's favor. Good teachers do not always answer confused hearts softly. Sometimes they must answer truly.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs expose the speed with which an outwardly rescued people can still crave inwardly familiar forms of shirk.

Bani Isra'il cross the sea and then immediately encounter a people devoted to idols. They ask Musa for a god on the same pattern. He answers by naming the request as ignorance, the idolaters' path as doomed and false, and Allah's recent favor over them as the decisive proof that no other deity should even be sought.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask: what "visible gods" do I still crave after Allah has already shown me enough of His power and mercy to make tawhid sufficient? Where am I still asking for something graspable because unseen trust feels too exposed?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the unit lands here: **crossing the sea does not automatically cleanse the imagination, so the freed soul must still be taught that visible objects of devotion are false and doomed, and that Allah's own favor should have made every rival god unnecessary.**
