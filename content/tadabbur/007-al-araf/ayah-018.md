## Introduction

The argument is over. The reprieve has been granted. Iblis has declared his four-direction war — that he will come at humanity from before them and behind them, from their right and from their left (7:16-17). He has made his threat. And now it's Allah's turn to close the scene.

What follows is a single ayah — one sentence — that does three things simultaneously. It expels. It sentences. And it expands the scope of the sentence from one creature to every creature who will ever choose to walk behind him.

And the way it does this — through two rare passive participles, a pronoun shift that changes everything, and the single most emphatic oath structure in the Arabic language — is worth sitting with carefully.

---

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### I. Two Words for How You Leave: مَذْءُومًا مَّدْحُورًا

*Ukhruji minhā **madh'ūman madhūrā**.*

"Get out from it — **despised** and **expelled**."

Two words. Both passive participles. Both in the accusative case — which in Arabic grammar makes them *ḥāl* (circumstantial complements), describing the *state* in which the action occurs. Not "get out, and then be despised." But "get out *while being* despised, *while being* expelled." The states are simultaneous with the exit. They don't follow it. They accompany it.

Let me unpack each one.

*Madh'ūman* comes from the root ذ-أ-م (*dha'ama*) — to censure, to despise, to find contemptible. The word is rare in the Quran. It appears only here. And it's a passive participle: not "one who despises" but "one who *is despised*." The contempt is not Iblis's attitude — it's his condition. He didn't choose it. It was placed on him.

*Madhūran* comes from the root د-ح-ر (*daḥara*) — to drive out, to push away forcefully, to repel. Again passive: "one who *is driven out*." Not someone who walks away. Someone who is *shoved*. The root carries physical force — this is not a quiet departure. It is an ejection.

[PAUSE]

And listen to the sounds. *Madh'ūman* — that hamza in the middle, that glottal interruption, makes the word choppy and harsh in the mouth. *Madhūran* — the heavy *dāl* and *ḥā'* combination is guttural, forceful. Neither word is pleasant to say. The phonetic texture matches the meaning: ugly words for an ugly exit.

Two simultaneous ḥāl constructions in a single sentence is heavy. Arabic can do it — but it's like a judge reading two charges at once. You are despised. You are expelled. Both. Now. Permanently. The participle form in Arabic carries a sense of settled state, not temporary condition. This isn't "you are being despised right now." This is "despised is what you *are*."

### II. The Pronoun That Changed the Scope

Now the sentence pivots — and this is where it becomes terrifying.

*La-man tabi'aka minhum la-amla'anna jahannama **minkum** ajma'īn.*

"Whoever follows you among them — I will surely fill Jahannam with **you all** together."

Read that pronoun carefully. *Minkum* — "from **you** (plural)."

Throughout this entire scene — 7:12, 7:13, 7:14, 7:15 — Allah has been addressing Iblis in the second person singular. *Mā mana'a**ka*** — "what prevented **you** (singular)." *Innaka mina l-ṣāghirīn* — "**you** (singular) are of the degraded." *Innaka mina l-munẓarīn* — "**you** (singular) are of those granted respite."

Singular. Singular. Singular.

And then, in this final ayah, without warning: *minkum* — **you, plural**.

[PAUSE]

The addressee has expanded. Iblis is no longer being sentenced alone. The sentence now includes every human being who will ever choose to follow him. The pronoun shift is the legal expansion. It's the moment the verdict, which seemed personal, becomes collective.

And the expansion happens *before the following even occurs*. Iblis hasn't recruited anyone yet. No human has been born. No whisper has been whispered. But the sentence is already plural. Allah speaks about the followers in the future tense (*man tabi'aka* — "whoever *will follow* you") but issues the verdict in the present: *minkum* — from you, all of you, right now.

This is not a threat contingent on future behaviour. This is a sentence that has already been written for a crime that hasn't been committed yet — but will be. The certainty is absolute. Allah knows they will follow. And the pronoun *minkum* is the proof: He's already addressing them.

### III. The Strongest Oath Arabic Can Build

And then the oath itself.

*La-amla'anna jahannama minkum ajma'īn.*

"I will **most certainly** fill Jahannam with you, all together."

The verb here — *la-amla'anna* — is constructed with triple emphasis:

First: the *lām* prefix (لَ) — the oath particle, announcing a sworn statement.

Second: the verb *amla'a* — Form I imperfect first person singular: "I fill."

Third: the energetic *nūn* suffix (نَّ) — the emphatic ending that transforms a statement into a vow.

*Lām* + verb + *nūn*. This is the single most emphatic verbal construction in the Arabic language. There is nothing stronger. When Arabic speakers encounter this triple emphasis, it signals an irrevocable divine commitment. Not "I might fill." Not "I intend to fill." Not even "I will fill." But: **I will — and nothing in existence can prevent it — fill Jahannam with you.**

[PAUSE]

And the word *ajma'īn* — "all together, every single one" — closes the door on exceptions. No one who follows Iblis will slip through. No follower will escape on a technicality. The oath is total. The pronoun is plural. The emphasis is maximal.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### I. Being Sentenced for a Decision You Haven't Made Yet

There's something that should stop us in this ayah. Allah sentences the followers of Iblis — *minkum ajma'īn*, "you all together" — before any of them exist. Before a single human has been whispered to. Before the first child of Adam draws breath.

How does that work? Isn't this predetermination? Isn't Allah punishing people for something they haven't done?

This is one of those places where the modern mind — trained on legal presumption of innocence, on the idea that punishment must follow crime chronologically — feels a real tension. And the tension is worth sitting in rather than rushing past.

Consider this: a parent who knows their child's character can predict, with painful accuracy, how that child will respond to a particular temptation. The parent doesn't *cause* the response. They don't *force* it. But they know it's coming — because they know the child, not because they've determined the outcome.

Now scale that up to infinite knowledge. Allah's foreknowledge of who will follow Iblis is not the same as Allah's determination that they must. The Quran, in this very surah, will go on to describe the choice. Adam will be given a garden and a prohibition. He will be told where the danger lies. And he will still eat from the tree — not because he was forced to, but because the whisper found something in him that responded.

The pronoun *minkum* doesn't erase choice. It encompasses it. It says: I already know what your choices will be, and I have already prepared the consequence. The choice is still yours. But so is the result.

[PAUSE]

And there's a quiet mercy embedded in the harshness. Because the same God who says "I will fill Jahannam with you" has also, in this same passage, given *thumma* — patient, deliberate intervals — throughout the creation of humanity. The oath about Jahannam is *certain*. But the road to it is *long*. Between the certainty of the sentence and the arrival of the day, there is an entire human lifetime of *thumma*. Of intervals. Of chances. Of the space to choose differently.

The sentence is written. The door to avoiding it is still open.

### II. What It Means to "Follow" Without Knowing You're Following

*Man tabi'aka minhum.* "Whoever *follows* you among them."

The verb *tabi'a* — to follow, to walk behind, to trail in the footsteps of — is deceptively simple. Because when we hear "followers of Iblis," we imagine conscious allegiance. Satanists. People who knowingly choose evil. People who have signed up for the opposing team.

But the Quran's use of *tabi'a* is broader and more frightening than that. To *follow* Iblis doesn't require knowing you're following him. It requires only that the direction you're walking in is the one he pointed you toward.

Think about Iblis's argument in 7:12: "I am better than him." That's not just a theological position. That's the founding logic of every hierarchy based on origin rather than action. Every time a human being looks at another and thinks — not even says, just *thinks* — "I am better than them because of where I come from, what I'm made of, what family I was born into" — they are walking in the direction Iblis pointed. They don't know they're following him. They think they're just observing a fact about the world. But the logic is his. The DNA of the argument is his. And the direction is the one he declared war from.

[PAUSE]

Or think about his Form V crime — *tatakabbara*, the self-generated performance of greatness. Every time a human inflates themselves not because they've done something worthy but because they need to feel bigger than the people around them — that's *tatakabbur*. The exact verb form. The exact reflexive, self-serving mechanism. And most people who do it don't think of it as following Iblis. They think of it as confidence. Self-worth. Knowing their value.

The line between healthy self-respect and Iblisian self-inflation isn't always obvious. But the Arabic offers a diagnostic: ask whether the greatness you feel requires someone else to be smaller. If yes — that's Form V. That's *tatakabbur*. And that's the road Iblis built.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture

This single ayah is the capstone of a five-verse arc (7:11-15, with 7:16-17 as Iblis's declaration in between). The scene moves through:

**Patient creation** (ثُمَّ...ثُمَّ) → **Command** (اسْجُدُوا) → **Refusal** (إِلَّا إِبْلِيسَ) → **Interrogation** (مَا مَنَعَكَ) → **The argument** (أَنَا خَيْرٌ) → **Expulsion** (فَاهْبِطْ) → **Reprieve** (الْمُنظَرِينَ) → **Iblis's war declaration** (7:16-17) → **The final sentence** (7:18).

And notice how the sentence in 7:18 mirrors the creation in 7:11. In 7:11: "We created **you**" — *khalaqnākum*, second person plural, addressing all of humanity. In 7:18: "I will fill Jahannam with **you**" — *minkum*, second person plural, addressing Iblis and his followers.

The creation was addressed to the plural. The destruction is addressed to the plural. The scene begins with humanity being given existence and ends with a portion of humanity being promised fire. Same pronoun. Different destination. The *kum* that received creation is the same *kum* that faces judgment.

### Questions to Carry

- When was the last time your mind built a beautiful argument for something your heart already knew was wrong? Did you follow the argument — or did you recognize the machinery?

- Iblis was sentenced while being called by two passive participles: *despised* and *expelled*. These were states placed on him, not states he chose. When you resist Allah's command — what states are being placed on you that you can't yet see?

- "Whoever follows you among them" — in which areas of your life might you be walking in a direction Iblis pointed, without realizing you're following?

- The sentence was already plural before any follower existed. What does it mean to live in a world where the consequence is already written — but the choice is still genuinely yours?

[PAUSE]

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The sentence was spoken to one creature, but the pronoun at the end was plural — because the punishment was always going to include everyone who chose to walk in the direction he pointed.**

### Closing Invitation

This ayah ends the Iblis scene in Al-A'raf. What comes next — 7:19 onward — is Adam and Hawwa in the garden. The test is about to begin. The whisper is about to arrive. And now you know, before the story even continues, what is at stake: not just the garden, not just the tree, but a sentence that has already been written with a pronoun that already includes you.

The question is whether you'll be in the *minkum* that fills Jahannam — or the *minkum* that was addressed in 7:11, the one that was created, fashioned, and honoured with the prostration of angels.

Same pronoun. Different story. And the choice — despite everything — is still yours.

### Du'a

*Allāhumma, we hear the weight of this sentence and we ask You not to include us among those it was written for. Make us see the moments when we are following without knowing we are following. Give us the clarity to recognize when our arguments are serving our arrogance, and the humility to stop building them. You said You would fill Jahannam — and Your word is the most certain truth in existence. Let us not be the ones who prove it. Āmīn.*
