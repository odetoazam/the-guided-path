# Their Omen Was With Allah

## Surah Al-Araf (7:130-131)

[ayah:7:130] وَلَقَدْ أَخَذْنَآ ءَالَ فِرْعَوْنَ بِٱلسِّنِينَ وَنَقْصٍ مِّنَ ٱلثَّمَرَٰتِ لَعَلَّهُمْ يَذَّكَّرُونَ

[ayah:7:131] فَإِذَا جَآءَتْهُمُ ٱلْحَسَنَةُ قَالُوا۟ لَنَا هَـٰذِهِۦ ۖ وَإِن تُصِبْهُمْ سَيِّئَةٌ يَطَّيَّرُوا۟ بِمُوسَىٰ وَمَن مَّعَهُۥٓ ۗ أَلَآ إِنَّمَا طَـٰٓئِرُهُمْ عِندَ ٱللَّهِ وَلَـٰكِنَّ أَكْثَرَهُمْ لَا يَعْلَمُونَ

*Wa laqad akhadhna ala Fir'awna bi-al-sinina wa naqsin mina al-thamarati la'allahum yadhdhakkarun.*

*Fa-idha ja'athumu al-hasanatu qalu lana hadhihi. Wa in tusibhum sayyi'atun yattayyaru bi-Musa wa man ma'ahu. Ala innama ta'iruhum inda Allahi wa lakinna aktharahum la ya'lamun.*

**And We certainly seized the people of Fir'awn with years of drought and a shortage of fruits so that they might remember. But when good came to them, they said, 'This is ours.' And if a bad thing struck them, they took Musa and those with him as a bad omen. Unquestionably, their omen was only with Allah, but most of them do not know.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

Arrogance has a very predictable theology of events.

When ease comes, it treats that ease as self-explanatory. Of course things went well. Of course abundance belongs to us. Of course our position is natural. But when hardship comes, arrogance suddenly becomes deeply interpretive. Then it must locate an external contaminant, a disturbing presence, a blamed person whose arrival supposedly ruined the atmosphere of success.

[PAUSE]

That is exactly how Fir'awn's people read their world. Years of drought and loss of fruit come as a divine discipline meant to provoke remembrance. Yet when a good turn arrives, they claim it as their due. When hardship strikes, they pin it on Musa and those with him. The Qur'an exposes both movements as blindness: blessing is not self-owned, and hardship is not decoded correctly through superstition and scapegoating. Their "omen" is with Allah.

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. Hard Years Are Sent as a Mercy of Reminder

The passage begins:

*Akhadhna ala Fir'awna bi-al-sinin wa naqsin mina al-thamarat.*

We took the people of Fir'awn with years and a decrease in fruits.

[PAUSE]

The wording is striking. Allah *took* them with these conditions. The drought-years and the shrinking of produce are not random weather. They are a measured seizure, a disciplined touch meant to interrupt delusion.

Then the purpose:

*La'allahum yadhdhakkarun.*

So that they might remember.

This is essential. The hardship is not narrated first as destruction. It is narrated as a call back. Before final punishment, there is reminder. Before collapse, there is a chance to read the distress correctly.

### 2. Good Is Claimed, Bad Is Blamed

Then the surah reveals their interpretive habit:

*Fa-idha ja'athumu al-hasanatu qalu lana hadhihi.*

When the good reaches them, they say: this belongs to us.

[PAUSE]

The phrase *lana hadhihi* is short and revealing. Not gratitude. Not humility. Not fear that blessing is a trust. It is possessive entitlement. This is ours.

Then the reversal:

*Wa in tusibhum sayyi'atun yattayyaru bi-Musa wa man ma'ah.*

If a bad thing strikes them, they take Musa and those with him as an omen.

The verb carries the sense of treating someone as a source of ill luck, reading misfortune through a blamed presence. Truth becomes the scapegoat for the consequence that arrogance does not want to trace back to its own sins.

### 3. Their Omen Is With Allah

Then comes the corrective:

*Ala innama ta'iruhum inda Allah.*

Unquestionably, their omen is only with Allah.

[PAUSE]

The sentence cuts through both entitlement and superstition. Their portion, decree, and moral consequence are not controlled by Musa's presence. They are with Allah. He is the one who governs what reaches them and why. But because they do not know, they keep reading reality through ego and blame instead of through divine justice.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Entitlement Turns Blessing into Self-Congratulation

There is a spiritual disease in which blessing no longer produces shukr, only ownership language.

This is ours. We earned it. We deserve it. The season is going well because we are who we think we are. Once a heart settles into that grammar, even mercy becomes fuel for deeper blindness. Abundance stops pointing upward and starts curving back into the self.

[PAUSE]

That is dangerous because the same heart is then unable to receive hardship as reminder. If the good was ours by right, then the bad must be somebody else's fault. The heart has already sealed its interpretive system. It can no longer imagine that both ease and distress are arriving from Allah with moral meaning.

### Theme 2: Scapegoating Truth Is Easier Than Repenting

Fir'awn's people do something extremely familiar. They make the truthful presence in their midst responsible for the discomfort now exposing them.

That happens in every age. Someone brings principled speech, real repentance, moral disruption, or inconvenient truth, and instead of asking what Allah might be saying through the disturbance, people decide the disturbance itself is the problem. The caller becomes "bad energy," the repentant person becomes divisive, the truth-teller becomes the reason the mood changed.

[PAUSE]

The Qur'an names that mechanism and strips it of dignity. Musa is not their omen. Their omen is with Allah. In other words: stop using people as a superstitious explanation for a moral world you refuse to read correctly. The issue is not the presence of revelation among you. The issue is what your own deeds have made you fit to receive.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayahs teach us how arrogant hearts misread both mercy and affliction.

Allah seizes Fir'awn's people with hard years and loss of fruit so that they might remember. When good comes, they claim it. When hardship comes, they blame Musa and those with him. Then the Qur'an tears down the whole interpretive structure: their omen is with Allah, though most of them do not know.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask: when ease comes into my life, do I instinctively say "this is mine," and when hardship comes, do I look for a scapegoat before I look for the moral lesson Allah may be placing in front of me?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the unit lands here: **hardship first comes as a merciful reminder, but arrogant hearts turn blessing into entitlement and affliction into blame, until the Qur'an corrects them by returning both outcome and meaning to Allah alone.**
