# Its Knowledge Is Only With Allah

## Surah Al-Araf (7:187-188)

[ayah:7:187] يَسْـَٔلُونَكَ عَنِ ٱلسَّاعَةِ أَيَّانَ مُرْسَىٰهَا ۖ قُلْ إِنَّمَا عِلْمُهَا عِندَ رَبِّى ۖ لَا يُجَلِّيهَا لِوَقْتِهَآ إِلَّا هُوَ ۚ ثَقُلَتْ فِى ٱلسَّمَـٰوَٰتِ وَٱلْأَرْضِ ۚ لَا تَأْتِيكُمْ إِلَّا بَغْتَةً ۗ يَسْـَٔلُونَكَ كَأَنَّكَ حَفِىٌّ عَنْهَا ۖ قُلْ إِنَّمَا عِلْمُهَا عِندَ ٱللَّهِ وَلَـٰكِنَّ أَكْثَرَ ٱلنَّاسِ لَا يَعْلَمُونَ

[ayah:7:188] قُل لَّآ أَمْلِكُ لِنَفْسِى نَفْعًا وَلَا ضَرًّا إِلَّا مَا شَآءَ ٱللَّهُ ۚ وَلَوْ كُنتُ أَعْلَمُ ٱلْغَيْبَ لَٱسْتَكْثَرْتُ مِنَ ٱلْخَيْرِ وَمَا مَسَّنِىَ ٱلسُّوٓءُ ۚ إِنْ أَنَا۠ إِلَّا نَذِيرٌ وَبَشِيرٌ لِّقَوْمٍ يُؤْمِنُونَ

*Yas'alunaka 'ani al-sa'ati ayyana mursaha. Qul innama 'ilmuha 'inda Rabbi. La yujalliha liwaqtiha illa Huwa. Thaqulat fi al-samawati wa al-ard. La ta'tikum illa baghtah. Yas'alunaka ka'annaka hafiyyun 'anha. Qul innama 'ilmuha 'inda Allahi wa lakinna akthara al-nasi la ya'lamun.*

**They ask you about the Hour: when will it come to rest? Say: its knowledge is only with my Lord. None will reveal it at its time except Him. It weighs heavily in the heavens and the earth. I am nothing but a clear warner and a bearer of glad tidings for a people who believe.**

## Introduction

These ayahs protect tawhid by placing luminous limits around the Messenger.

People ask him about the Hour as if he might hold its secret timing. The answer does not flatter their curiosity. It returns the knowledge of the Hour to Allah alone. Then the next ayah pushes the clarification further: the Prophet himself does not independently possess benefit or harm for his own soul, nor knowledge of the unseen that would let him live above vulnerability.

[PAUSE]

That is not a lowering of the Messenger. It is part of his truthfulness. His greatness is not that he competes with divine attributes. His greatness is that he delivers revelation without stealing what belongs only to Allah.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Hour Is Anchored Beyond Human Access

Ayah 187 opens:

*Yas'alunaka 'ani al-sa'ah: ayyana mursaha.*

They ask you about the Hour: when will it come to rest?

[PAUSE]

The phrasing is striking. *Ayyana mursaha* does not merely ask "when is it?" in a flat sense. *Mursa* carries the feel of anchoring, setting down, coming to rest at its appointed fixation. They want the date, the settled point, the hidden timetable.

The answer is immediate:

*Qul innama 'ilmuha 'inda Rabbi.*

Say: its knowledge is only with my Lord.

The restriction matters. *Innama.* Only. The knowledge is not shared out. It is not partially disclosed here for speculation and only completed there. Its knowledge is with Allah.

Then:

*La yujalliha liwaqtiha illa Huwa.*

None will unveil it at its time except Him.

The verb *yujalliha* carries unveiling, bringing into full manifestation. So even the Hour's appearance belongs to Allah alone. He knows it, and He is the one who discloses it when its moment arrives.

### 2. The Hour Is Heavy and Sudden

The ayah continues:

*Thaqulat fi al-samawati wa al-ard.*

It weighs heavily in the heavens and the earth.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the most arresting descriptions of the Hour. It is heavy. The unseen future event casts a weight even now. The heavens and earth are not indifferent to it.

Then:

*La ta'tikum illa baghtah.*

It will not come to you except suddenly.

So the Hour carries two qualities at once: immense weight and utter suddenness. It is not trivial, and it is not manageable. Human beings often try to neutralize the Last Day by turning it into curiosity, chronology, or dramatic theory. The ayah resists that. It is too heavy to trivialize and too sudden to domesticate.

### 3. The Prophet Is Not Given the Unseen as Personal Possession

Then ayah 188 broadens the clarification:

*Qul la amliku li-nafsi naf'an wa la darran illa ma sha'a Allah.*

Say: I possess no benefit or harm for myself except what Allah wills.

[PAUSE]

This is stunningly pure servanthood. The Prophet is taught to deny independent ownership even over benefit and harm for his own self. So how could he be imagined as autonomously holding the timetable of the unseen?

Then comes the counterfactual:

*Wa law kuntu a'lamu al-ghayba la-istakthartu mina al-khayr wa ma massaniya al-su'.*

If I knew the unseen, I would have acquired much good, and no harm would have touched me.

This is a deeply human sentence. If unseen knowledge were at his personal disposal, he would gather advantage and avoid injury. But harm touched him. Difficulty touched him. So the ayah itself teaches us how to read prophetic life correctly: not as independent mastery of the unseen, but as obedient servanthood under revelation.

Then the closing identity:

*In ana illa nadhirun wa bashirun li-qawmin yu'minun.*

I am nothing but a warner and a bearer of glad tidings for a people who believe.

The Messenger is not reduced here. He is defined. Warning and glad tidings are enough. They are enormous. But they remain bounded by servanthood and revelation.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Curiosity About the Unseen Can Become a Way of Avoiding Submission

One reason people ask about the Hour is sincere fear and longing. But another reason is less noble: knowing the timetable can feel easier than obeying the message.

If the date were known, the soul imagines it would finally become serious. Until then, it can postpone. The Qur'an refuses that bargain. You are not given the schedule. You are given the warning. The ignorance of the timing is not a defect in guidance. It is part of guidance.

[PAUSE]

That is why the ayah's repetition matters. Twice the response returns the knowledge to Allah. Human beings want to relocate control downward. The Qur'an returns it upward and leaves us with responsibility instead of chronology.

### Theme 2: Love of the Prophet Must Preserve the Difference Between Lord and Servant

Ayah 188 is one of the great correctives against exaggeration.

The Prophet is the most honored of creation, yet he is commanded to say that he does not independently own benefit or harm for himself and does not possess the unseen as a private faculty. This does not diminish him. It illuminates the beauty of his 'ubudiyyah. He is great because he is perfectly truthful about what belongs to Allah and what belongs to the servant.

[PAUSE]

This also protects believers from seeking safety in the wrong place. Our refuge is not in imagining created beings as secretly sharing divine prerogatives. Our refuge is in Allah, who sent us a Messenger whose warning is true and whose glad tidings are true precisely because he does not speak from stolen divinity.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayahs purify both eschatology and prophethood.

When people ask about the Hour, the Messenger is told to return its knowledge to Allah alone, to affirm that none unveils it at its appointed time except Him, and to describe it as both heavy in the heavens and earth and sudden in its arrival. Then he is commanded to declare that he does not independently possess benefit or harm even for himself, and that if he knew the unseen as personal property he would gather more good and avoid harm, but instead his revealed role is to warn and give glad tidings.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask: am I more interested in hidden timetables than in the obedience already required of me now? And when I honor the Messenger, do I honor him in the Qur'an's way - by loving the truth of his servanthood and the clarity of his warning - or by subtly giving him what he himself was commanded to deny?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the unit lands here: **the Qur'an answers anxious curiosity about the Hour and exaggerated assumptions about prophetic access by returning unseen knowledge, benefit, harm, and disclosure to Allah alone, while defining the Messenger's magnificence through truthful servanthood as warner and bearer of glad tidings.**
