# They Say, "We Will Be Forgiven"

## Surah Al-Araf (7:167-169)

[ayah:7:167] وَإِذْ تَأَذَّنَ رَبُّكَ لَيَبْعَثَنَّ عَلَيْهِمْ إِلَىٰ يَوْمِ ٱلْقِيَـٰمَةِ مَن يَسُومُهُمْ سُوٓءَ ٱلْعَذَابِ ۗ إِنَّ رَبَّكَ لَسَرِيعُ ٱلْعِقَابِ ۖ وَإِنَّهُۥ لَغَفُورٌ رَّحِيمٌ

## Introduction

Not every punishment ends at the moment of impact. Some become a condition stretched across history.

That is how this passage opens. After the Sabbath-town story, the surah widens the frame and shows a declaration hanging over a people through time: recurring humiliation, recurring testing, recurring opportunities to return. And yet even here the Qur'an refuses moral laziness. It says they are scattered as nations, and among them are righteous people, while others are less than that. So the passage is not only about historical consequence. It is about Allah's exactness. He can judge a people severely without ceasing to know the difference between the upright remnant and the corrupted heir.

[PAUSE]

Then the final ayah brings the danger closer to the heart. The issue is no longer merely what enemies do to a community from outside, but what heirs of the Book do from inside: taking the passing goods of the lower life while soothing themselves with a cheap sentence, *sa-yughfaru lana* - we will be forgiven. The tragedy is not ignorance without scripture. It is self-deception beside inherited revelation.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Declaration of Ongoing Humiliation Is Framed Inside Allah's Two-Sided Lordship

Ayah 167 begins:

*Wa idh ta'adhdhana Rabbuka la-yab'athanna 'alayhim...*

When your Lord declared that He would surely send upon them...

[PAUSE]

The verb *ta'adhdhana* carries a forceful announcing, a made-known declaration that will not remain hidden. Then comes the content: until the Day of Resurrection there will be those who subject them to grievous punishment. This is not a passing political setback. It is a historical condition under divine decree.

But the verse does not end there. It closes with:

*Inna Rabbaka la-sari'u al-'iqab wa innahu la-ghafurun rahim.*

Your Lord is swift in punishment, and He is surely forgiving and merciful.

That pairing matters. Allah is not trapped inside one attribute in our sight. The same Lord who punishes swiftly is still *ghafur rahim*. The passage refuses the simplification that history of chastisement means the doors of mercy have been erased. Punishment is real, but mercy remains a live truth.

### 2. Scattering Does Not Mean Moral Sameness

Ayah 168 says:

*Wa qatta'nahum fi al-ardi umaman.*

We broke them up in the earth as nations.

[PAUSE]

This is dispersal, fragmentation, loss of unified settlement. But immediately the ayah qualifies the picture:

*Minhumu al-salihuna wa minhum duna dhalik.*

Among them are the righteous, and among them are other than that.

The Qur'an will not let us flatten a scattered people into one single moral summary. Some are *salihun*. Others are below that. Then the ayah names the method of Allah with them:

*Wa balawnahum bi-al-hasanati wa al-sayyi'ati la'allahum yarji'un.*

We tested them with good things and bad things so that perhaps they might return.

This is a profound line. Blessings test. Hardships test. Ease is not proof of approval, and pain is not proof of abandonment. Both can be forms of *bala'* meant to produce *ruju'*, a returning back to Allah.

### 3. The Most Dangerous Sentence in the Passage Is "We Will Be Forgiven"

Ayah 169 describes later heirs:

*Fa-khalafa min ba'dihim khalfun warithu al-kitab...*

After them came successors who inherited the Book.

[PAUSE]

The tragedy begins with privilege. They inherited the Book. They are not outside revelation.

Then the ayah exposes them:

*Ya'khudhuna 'arada hadha al-adna wa yaquluna sa-yughfaru lana.*

They take the fleeting goods of this lower world and say: we will be forgiven.

The word *'arad* is telling. It is passing stuff, a transient gain, something that does not endure. And *al-adna* lowers the whole scene further: the nearer, lower life. So they trade the inheritance of the Book for what is brief and low, then cover the trade with presumptive hope in forgiveness.

The Qur'an then tears away the excuse:

*Wa in ya'tihim 'aradun mithluhu ya'khudhuhu.*

If a gain like it comes to them, they take it again.

[PAUSE]

That repetition exposes the lie. This is not a stumble followed by remorse. It is a pattern accompanied by religious self-soothing.

Then the ayah asks:

*A-lam yu'khadh 'alayhim mithaqu al-kitabi an la yaqulu 'ala Allahi illa al-haqq?*

Was not the covenant of the Book taken from them that they would say about Allah nothing but the truth?

And yet they had already *darasu ma fihi* - studied what was in it. Their problem is not lack of access. It is betrayal beside knowledge.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: One of the Most Dangerous Forms of Sin Is to Turn Divine Mercy Into Cover for Repeating the Same Worldliness

The sentence *sa-yughfaru lana* can sound pious on the surface. It mentions forgiveness. It remembers mercy. But in this ayah it is the speech of delusion, because it is attached to a repeated willingness to seize the same lower gain again and again.

[PAUSE]

This is a terrifying spiritual pattern. A person does not deny Allah's mercy. He abuses it. He does not reject revelation openly. He keeps it nearby while treating it as insurance. He sins, consoles himself with future forgiveness, and when the same opportunity returns, he takes it again without real rupture in the soul.

That can happen to anyone with religious literacy. We can know the texts, inherit the language, study the covenant, and still use sacred knowledge to make ourselves emotionally comfortable inside repeated compromise. At that point mercy is no longer being received as a door into tawbah. It is being weaponized against tawbah.

### Theme 2: Allah Tests Communities Through Both History and Immediate Gain

This passage moves between civilizational scale and intimate scale. On one level there is scattering, humiliation, historical vulnerability, and long arcs of testing across peoples and generations. On another level there is one immediate temptation: a piece of worldly gain in front of the hand.

[PAUSE]

That means the crisis of a community is not only what happens to it geopolitically. It is also what its heirs do when the lower life offers itself cheaply. A people may speak endlessly about historical suffering while remaining unable to refuse immediate advantage purchased by betrayal of truth. The Qur'an will not let us separate those levels.

And the passage ends by lifting the eye again:

*Wa al-daru al-akhiratu khayrun lilladhina yattaqun.*

The home of the Hereafter is better for those who have taqwa.

The cure for low bargains is not merely stronger willpower. It is a restored scale of value. If the akhirah becomes real again, the lower goods shrink back to their proper size.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs trace a painful movement from historical chastisement to internalized self-deception.

Allah declares a long condition of humiliation over them, yet the same verse keeps mercy alive by naming Him swift in punishment and still forgiving and merciful. They are scattered across the earth, but the Qur'an still distinguishes the righteous among them from those below that, and it explains that both blessings and afflictions were sent as tests so they might return. Then the deepest wound appears: heirs of the Book who know the covenant, study the text, repeatedly grab the passing goods of the lower world, and pacify themselves with the phrase, "We will be forgiven."

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this unit is to ask: where have I turned hope in Allah's forgiveness into a cushion for repeating the same compromise? Do I still feel that worldly gain is small because the akhirah is greater, or has the passing *'arad* of this life quietly become heavy in my hand again?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the unit lands here: **a people can suffer long consequences in history and still be tested anew in every generation, but one of the gravest failures of the heirs of revelation is to know the covenant, prefer the lower gain, and keep using mercy-language to make repeated betrayal feel safe.**
