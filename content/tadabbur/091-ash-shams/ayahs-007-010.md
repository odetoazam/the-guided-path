## The Ayah

[ayah:91:7] وَنَفْسٍ وَمَا سَوَّىٰهَا
[ayah:91:8] فَأَلْهَمَهَا فُجُورَهَا وَتَقْوَىٰهَا
[ayah:91:9] قَدْ أَفْلَحَ مَن زَكَّىٰهَا
[ayah:91:10] وَقَدْ خَابَ مَن دَسَّىٰهَا

*Wa nafsin wa ma sawwaha*
*Fa-alhamaha fujuraha wa taqwaha*
*Qad aflaha man zakkaha*
*Wa qad khaba man dassaha*

And by the soul and the One who proportioned it,
Then inspired it with its wickedness and its righteousness —
Truly, the one who purifies it has succeeded,
And truly, the one who buries it has failed.

---

## Introduction

Surah Ash-Shams opens with the longest series of oaths in the entire Quran. The sun and its morning light. The moon when it follows. The day when it reveals. The night when it covers. The sky and how it was built. The earth and how it was spread.

Six cosmic phenomena. Six witnesses summoned before a courtroom.

And then — the seventh oath. The crescendo. The thing all six were building toward.

**A soul.**

Not the universe. Not the galaxies, the orbits, the immeasurable expanse. After all of that — Allah swears by *you*. By what is inside you. By the thing you carry between your ribs that no telescope can detect and no microscope can examine.

And what comes after this oath is one of the most psychologically precise passages in the Quran. Four ayahs that answer a question every human being has asked in one form or another:

*Why do I feel pulled in two directions at once?*

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Soul as Soil — An Agricultural Thread Hidden in Plain Sight

There is a thread running through these four ayahs that most translations erase completely. To see it, you need the Arabic roots.

Let's start with a concept that might seem unrelated: farming.

In the ancient Arab world, success wasn't abstract. It was agricultural. You survived if the earth gave you something back. You broke the hard, dry ground with a plow. You planted. You waited. You tended. And if the crop grew tall and clean — you had *falah*. You had made it.

Now watch what Allah does in these four ayahs.

[PAUSE]

The word for "He proportioned it" in ayah 7 is *sawwaha* — from the root س-و-ي. This is a Form II verb: *fa''ala*. In Arabic, Form II takes a simple action and makes it intensive, thoroughgoing. Form I of this root, *sawiya*, would mean "to be equal." But Allah didn't use Form I. He used Form II: **to actively, deliberately make something proportioned**. This is not a soul that just *happened* to be balanced. This is a soul that was **crafted** into balance — fashioned with deliberate, painstaking proportion, the way a potter works clay until every side is even.

The nafs was *made*. And it was made right.

Then in ayah 9, the word for success: *aflaha*. Root: ف-ل-ح. And here is where the thread appears. *Falaha* in Arabic means **to plow**. To split open hard ground. *Fallah* — the word still used across the Arab world today for a farmer — comes from this exact root. When the Quran says *qad aflaha*, it is saying: **the one who plowed has harvested**. The one who broke open the hard soil of the self and worked it — they made it.

Stay with that.

[PAUSE]

Now the word for "purifies it" in the same ayah: *zakkaha*. Root: ز-ك-و. And this root lives in the same world. *Zaka* in Arabic describes what a plant does when it grows tall and clean and healthy. *Zakat* — the pillar of Islam — comes from this root: wealth that is purified by being given away, the way a field is purified by being pruned. But *zakkaha* is Form II — intensive, thoroughgoing. This is not passive purity. This is someone **actively, relentlessly cultivating** the nafs the way a farmer cultivates a field. Pulling weeds. Turning soil. Clearing what chokes growth.

*Aflaha... zakkaha.* Plowed... cultivated. The one who worked the nafs like a farmer works soil — they succeeded.

[PAUSE]

And then — ayah 10. The word for what the other person does: *dassaha*. Root: د-س-و. And this word means **to bury**. To push something down beneath the surface. To take something that was alive, that was growing, that was meant to see the sun — and shove it underground.

Do you see it?

**The entire passage is an agricultural metaphor.** The soul was proportioned like prepared earth. Success is plowing and cultivation. And failure — failure is **burial**. Taking what Allah planted inside you and pushing it into the dirt.

One person takes the nafs and grows it upward.
The other takes the exact same nafs and buries it downward.

Same soil. Same seeds. Opposite directions.

[PAUSE]

Here's what's remarkable about this thread. It is not decorative imagery. It is the **architecture of the ayahs themselves**. The roots are doing the theological work that the translation cannot. In English, you get "succeeded" and "purified" and "corrupted" — and those are fine words. But in Arabic, you get a farmer plowing, a field growing, and a burial.

And that difference matters. Because when you say someone "failed morally," it sounds like an exam they didn't study for. But when you say someone **buried their own soul in the ground** — that is an image you carry with you. You feel the weight of the dirt. You feel the wrongness of pushing something alive beneath the earth.

### 2. The Ilham — What Was Placed Inside, and In What Order

Now let's return to ayah 8, because something precise happens here that most people walk right past.

*Fa-alhamaha fujuraha wa taqwaha.*

The word *alhama* — Form IV of the root ل-ه-م. Form IV in Arabic is causative: it means to **cause** something to happen. The base root *lahima* means to swallow, to gulp down. Form IV: **to cause something to be swallowed whole**. To place something so deep inside a person that it becomes indistinguishable from their own nature.

This is not *ta'lim* — teaching from outside. It is not *wahy* — revelation sent down. *Ilham* is something placed **within** that feels like it came from within. It sits in a place you cannot locate. You cannot point to the moment you learned it. It is simply... there.

And what was placed there?

*Fujuraha wa taqwaha.*

**Her** wickedness and **her** righteousness.

Notice the possessive suffix: *-ha*. Her. The nafs's own. Allah didn't place *His* idea of fujur and taqwa into you. He placed **yours**. They belong to the nafs. They are native to it. You were born already knowing.

[PAUSE]

But here is the detail that stops you cold if you notice it.

**Fujur comes first.**

*Fujuraha wa taqwaha.* Not taqwa first. Not righteousness leading the way. Wickedness is named before righteousness.

Why?

Let's sit with this. The root of *fujur* is ف-ج-ر — the same root that gives us *fajr*, the dawn. And what does the dawn do? It **bursts** through darkness. It tears the night open. That is what fujur is: the tearing open of a moral boundary. A rupture. The moment you cross a line you knew was there.

And the root of *taqwa* is و-ق-ي — to **shield**, to protect. Taqwa is holding the boundary. Building a barrier between yourself and harm.

One root tears open. The other holds together.

These are not just opposites in meaning. They are opposites in their physical imagery, in the kinetic energy embedded in the roots themselves. Fujur explodes outward. Taqwa holds inward. And both of these impulses — the urge to rupture and the urge to protect — were placed inside you by the same Hand.

[PAUSE]

So why is fujur named first?

Think about this through the lens of what you actually experience. When does a child first encounter moral awareness? Not through a lesson in righteousness. A child learns what is right by first feeling the pull of what is wrong. You felt the temptation *before* you understood the restraint. You knew the sting of guilt *before* you could name the virtue. Fujur comes first because it is the first signal the nafs receives — the pull toward the boundary, and then the voice inside that says *don't*.

Without the pull, there is nothing to resist. Without the rupture impulse, the shielding impulse has nothing to shield against. Fujur is named first because **taqwa only exists in response to fujur**. The second could not function without the first.

And this — this is the design. Not a defect.

### 3. The Asymmetry of Success and Failure

One more thing lives in these ayahs that you will miss unless you look at the verb forms.

*Qad **aflaha** man zakkaha.*

*Aflaha* — Form IV. Causative. The one who **caused success to happen**. The one who made it occur through effort, through work, through agency.

*Wa qad **khaba** man dassaha.*

*Khaba* — Form I. The simplest, most basic, most unadorned verb form in Arabic. Just... failed.

Do you feel the asymmetry?

Success is a Form IV verb — you had to **cause** it. It required effort, agency, active intervention. You don't stumble into *falah*. You plow for it.

But failure? Failure is a Form I verb. The simplest form. The default. The thing that just... happens. You don't need to work at failure. You don't need a special verb form for it. You just bury what you know and let gravity do the rest.

[PAUSE]

And now pair that with the action verbs. The one who succeeds *zakkaha* — Form II, intensive, thoroughgoing purification. Active, deliberate, sustained work.

The one who fails *dassaha* — and here the action is burial. Concealment. Pushing something down. You don't even have to attack the nafs. You just have to **hide** it. Smother it. Ignore what you know is there.

This is not a portrait of spectacular evil versus spectacular virtue. This is a portrait of **cultivation versus burial**. The farmer who tends the field versus the person who covers the seed with dirt and walks away.

And that is terrifying in its accuracy. Because most people who lose their nafs don't lose it in a single dramatic act of rebellion. They lose it the way you lose a garden you stopped tending. Slowly. Under accumulated layers of not-quite-looking at what you know is there. One more layer of rationalization. One more season of "I'll deal with it later." One more inch of soil over a seed that was meant to see the sun.

*Dassaha.* They buried it.

[PAUSE]

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: You Were Not Made Broken — You Were Made in Tension

There's a conversation that happens, in some form or another, in every generation. It goes like this:

*"If God made me, why do I feel pulled toward things that are wrong? Why is the darkness in me? If I was made by a loving Creator, why does the nafs incline toward destruction?"*

In the modern world, this question takes different shapes. Self-help culture says: eliminate the negative. Think positive. Remove the toxic thought patterns. There is a multi-billion-dollar industry built on the idea that the darkness inside you is a malfunction to be fixed.

And religious culture sometimes says the opposite: the darkness is a punishment, a curse, the consequence of some primordial fall. You are broken from the start, and the best you can hope for is damage control.

These four ayahs reject both stories.

[PAUSE]

*Fa-alhamaha fujuraha wa taqwaha.* **He** inspired it with its wickedness and its righteousness.

Both.

Not one imported from outside. Not one as a punishment and the other as a reward. Both placed inside the same nafs by the same Creator as part of the same design.

This is not a defect. This is the **architecture**.

Think about it this way. Imagine a bowstring. A bowstring does nothing lying flat on a table. It is potential without tension. It becomes powerful only when it is pulled in two opposing directions — the rigid arc of the bow pulling one way, the archer's hand pulling the other. The tension between those two forces is not a flaw in the bowstring's design. **The tension is the design.** Without it, the arrow doesn't fly.

The nafs is the bowstring. Fujur pulls one way. Taqwa pulls the other. And the human being is the archer who decides what direction the arrow flies.

Remove the pull of fujur, and taqwa becomes meaningless — an arrow lying flat on a table. Remove the pull of taqwa, and fujur has no resistance — a string with nothing to fight against. It is the tension between them, placed there deliberately, that gives the human being the one thing no other creation possesses: **the capacity for moral choice**.

The angels have taqwa without fujur. They obey, but they cannot *choose* to obey. The choice was never on the table. And whatever beauty exists in angelic worship, it is not the beauty of a being who felt the full weight of the other direction and chose this one anyway.

That is the beauty Allah designed the nafs for. Not innocence. Not the absence of darkness. But the **choosing** — again and again and again — to cultivate instead of bury.

[PAUSE]

So when you feel the pull — and you will feel it, because it was placed there by the One who made you — do not ask "what is wrong with me?" Ask instead: "what is this tension *for*?"

It is for the same thing the bowstring is for. It is to give the arrow a direction.

### Theme 2: Tazkiyah Is a Verb — And So Is Dassiyah

There is no neutral position in these ayahs. Read them again. There is no third category. No "the one who left the nafs alone." No "the one who neither purified nor buried."

*Qad aflaha man zakkaha.*
*Wa qad khaba man dassaha.*

Succeeded or failed. Cultivated or buried. Up or down.

[PAUSE]

Here's where this maps onto something every living person has felt.

You know that moment when you see something clearly — a fault in yourself, a truth you've been avoiding, a conversation you need to have, a habit that is slowly suffocating something good in you — and you choose not to look at it?

Not to fight it. Not to reject it. Just... to not look.

You push it down. You cover it. You tell yourself a small story: *Not now. It's not that serious. I'll think about it tomorrow. Everyone does this. It's fine.*

That is *dassaha*. That is the burial. And notice — the Quran doesn't describe this as a violent act. It doesn't use a word for destruction or annihilation. It uses a word for **concealment**. The nafs doesn't die in this scenario. It gets buried alive.

And here is what's terrifying: the burial is *active*. *Dassaha* is a verb with an agent. "Whoever **buries** it." You are doing something. This is not neglect. This is not passive drift. Every time you push down what you know, you are shoveling another layer of soil over a living thing.

[PAUSE]

The opposite is *zakkaha* — and this is also active. Form II: intensive, thoroughgoing. You do not purify the nafs once and walk away. Tazkiyah is not an event. It is a practice. It is the farmer returning to the field every single morning. Pulling the weeds that grew overnight. Turning the soil after the rain. Checking for rot beneath the surface.

There is a reason the Prophet, peace be upon him, would ask Allah for purification of the nafs. Not once. Not at the beginning of prophethood. *Continuously*. If the best human being who ever lived was still farming, still cultivating, still tending — what does it mean for anyone to think their nafs is finished?

Here is what these ayahs are really saying:

**You are never standing still.** At every moment, with every choice, with every small decision to look or look away — you are either pulling the nafs upward toward the light, or you are pushing it further underground. There is no coasting. There is no autopilot. The nafs is either growing or being buried. Those are the only two options.

And the honest question — the one these ayahs place in your lap — is not "am I a good person?" It is: **"Which direction is my nafs moving right now?"**

[PAUSE]

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Passage

Step back and look at the structure of these four ayahs as a whole. There is a design here that emerges only after you've walked through the details.

**Ayah 7:** What Allah did — He proportioned the nafs (*sawwaha* — crafted it into balance)
**Ayah 8:** What Allah placed inside — fujur and taqwa, both, by design (*alhamaha*)
**Ayah 9:** What the human does in response — cultivates it (*zakkaha*) → success
**Ayah 10:** What the human does in response — buries it (*dassaha*) → failure

The first half is divine action. The second half is human response. And between them — in the tiny conjunction *fa* that opens ayah 8 — is the handoff. The moment where Allah says: *I made this. I proportioned it. I placed both impulses inside. Now...*

**What will you do?**

The entire passage pivots on that handoff. Allah builds. The human chooses. And the agricultural metaphor — soil, plowing, cultivation, burial — runs through both halves, binding divine creation and human response into a single continuous image: a field that was prepared by one Hand and must be tended by another.

[PAUSE]

### Questions to Carry

- When you feel pulled in two directions at once — do you treat that tension as a malfunction to eliminate, or as the very mechanism that makes choice meaningful?

- Where in your life right now are you *dassaha* — burying something you know is there, one layer at a time, not looking at it?

- What would it look like to treat your nafs the way a farmer treats a field — returning to it daily, pulling what chokes, tending what grows?

- If both impulses were placed inside you by design, what changes about how you view your own struggles?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Allah placed both the impulse to rupture and the impulse to shield inside the same soul by design — and your entire existence is the question of whether you will cultivate that soil or bury it.**

### The Closing Invitation

These ayahs do not ask you to be sinless. They do not ask you to be free from the pull of fujur. That pull is part of the design — it is what makes your taqwa worth anything at all.

What they ask is simpler and harder: tend the field. Go back to it. Again tomorrow. Again the day after. Pull what needs pulling. Water what needs watering. And never, ever stop looking at what is growing inside you — because the moment you stop looking is the moment the burial begins.

### Du'a

*Allahumma, You are the One who proportioned this nafs and placed within it both its darkness and its light. We did not choose the tension — but You gave us the capacity to choose within it. Help us to be farmers, not gravediggers. Help us to return to the field of the nafs every morning with the courage to look, the patience to tend, and the honesty to see what is growing and what is dying. Purify in us what we cannot purify alone, and do not let us be among those who bury what You planted.*
