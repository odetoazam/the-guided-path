# That You Might Use Reason
## When Allah Closed the Family Law Passage by Asking You to Think
### Al-Baqarah 2:240-242

---

[ayah:2:240] وَٱلَّذِينَ يُتَوَفَّوْنَ مِنكُمْ وَيَذَرُونَ أَزْوَٰجًا وَصِيَّةً لِّأَزْوَٰجِهِم مَّتَـٰعًا إِلَى ٱلْحَوْلِ غَيْرَ إِخْرَاجٍ ۚ فَإِنْ خَرَجْنَ فَلَا جُنَاحَ عَلَيْكُمْ فِى مَا فَعَلْنَ فِىٓ أَنفُسِهِنَّ مِن مَّعْرُوفٍ ۗ وَٱللَّهُ عَزِيزٌ حَكِيمٌ

[ayah:2:241] وَلِلْمُطَلَّقَـٰتِ مَتَـٰعٌۢ بِٱلْمَعْرُوفِ ۖ حَقًّا عَلَى ٱلْمُتَّقِينَ

[ayah:2:242] كَذَٰلِكَ يُبَيِّنُ ٱللَّهُ لَكُمْ ءَايَـٰتِهِۦ لَعَلَّكُمْ تَعْقِلُونَ

*Walladhīna yutawaffawna minkum wa yadharūna azwājan waṣiyyatan li-azwājihim matā'an ilal-ḥawli ghayra ikhrāj. Fa-in kharajna fa-lā junāḥa 'alaykum fī mā fa'alna fī anfusihinna min ma'rūf. Wallāhu 'azīzun ḥakīm. Wa lil-muṭallaqāti matā'un bil-ma'rūf. Ḥaqqan 'alal-muttaqīn. Kadhālika yubayyinullāhu lakum āyātihi la'allakum ta'qilūn.*

**"And those who are taken in death among you and leave wives behind — for their wives is a bequest: maintenance for one year without turning them out. But if they leave of their own accord, then there is no blame upon you for what they do with themselves in an acceptable way. And Allah is Exalted in Might and Wise.**

**And for divorced women is a provision according to what is acceptable — a duty upon the righteous.**

**Thus does Allah make clear to you His verses that you might use reason."**

## Rebuilding the World

We've arrived at the end.

Twenty-two ayahs ago — at 2:221 — Allah opened the family law section with a prohibition: do not marry polytheists. From that single boundary, He built an entire architecture of human relationships: how to form a marriage, how to end one, who owes what to whom, how long a woman waits after divorce, how long a widow waits after death, who nurses the child, who pays for it, what happens to the mahr, what happens to the consolation gift, what happens when there's no mahr, what happens when one party waives their right, what happens when the community tries to interfere, what happens when you can't stop thinking about her, and — right in the middle of all of it — guard the prayers, because when life falls apart the prayer is what holds you together.

Twenty-two ayahs. Every single one addressing the most intimate, most emotionally charged, most legally complex area of human life: the family.

And now Allah is going to close the entire section. Not with a ruling. Not with a warning. Not with a prohibition.

With a **question**.

*La'allakum ta'qilūn* — "that you might use reason."

After all of that — after all the legislation, all the protection, all the graduated obligations and graduated permissions — Allah steps back, looks at the whole structure He just built, and says: **I made this clear so you would THINK.**

What does it mean that the Quran's most comprehensive legal passage closes with an appeal to the intellect? And what are we supposed to be reasoning ABOUT?

---

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### Element 1: The Two Exits — One Prohibited, One Permitted

2:240 contains a morphological contrast so precise that it IS the legal ruling. The same root — خ-ر-ج (to exit, to go out) — appears twice, in two different forms, with two completely opposite legal statuses.

First: **غَيْرَ إِخْرَاجٍ** — *ghayra ikhrāj* — "without expulsion."

The word **إِخْرَاج** — *ikhrāj* — is the maṣdar (verbal noun) of Form IV: أَخْرَجَ. Form IV is the CAUSATIVE — to CAUSE someone to exit. Not leaving. **Being made to leave.** Being pushed out, evicted, expelled. Someone is doing this TO her. Someone is forcing her door open and putting her belongings on the street.

The prohibition is: **غَيْرَ** — without. There shall be NO ikhrāj. No expulsion. No one may CAUSE her to leave.

Then, a few words later: **فَإِنْ خَرَجْنَ** — *fa-in kharajna* — "but if they leave."

This is Form I: **خَرَجَ** — simple departure. She leaves. Of her own will. Under her own power. With her own feet walking through her own door by her own choice.

[PAUSE]

Same root. Two forms. Two completely different realities.

Form IV (إِخْرَاج — causative): she is EXPELLED. **Prohibited.**
Form I (خَرَجْنَ — simple): she LEAVES. **Permitted.**

The entire legal ruling is encoded in the morphology. You don't need a fatwa. You need to read the verb forms. The woman may not be PUSHED out. She may WALK out. The difference between a crime and a right is the difference between Form IV and Form I of the same root.

And if she does walk out — if she chooses to leave the home of her deceased husband before the year is up — then **فَلَا جُنَاحَ عَلَيْكُمْ** — no blame upon the community for what she does **فِىٓ أَنفُسِهِنَّ** — with THEMSELVES. That phrase again — *fī anfusihinna* — the same phrase from 2:234. Her self. Her autonomous choice. Her life to direct.

The widow is not a possession of her deceased husband's family. She is a **self** — a *nafs* — who may be protected from expulsion but may not be imprisoned in the name of protection.

### Element 2: The Seven-Word Revolution

Now 2:241. One sentence. Seven Arabic words. And with them, Allah extends the safety net to every divorced woman who has ever lived.

**وَلِلْمُطَلَّقَـٰتِ مَتَـٰعٌۢ بِٱلْمَعْرُوفِ**

*Wa lil-muṭallaqāti matā'un bil-ma'rūf*

"And for the divorced women is a provision according to what is acceptable."

The first word that demands attention: **ٱلْمُطَلَّقَـٰتِ** — *al-muṭallaqāt*. This is a passive participle, Form II, feminine plural, with the definite article. Each of those grammatical features carries weight.

**Passive participle (مُفَعَّلَة):** the women who WERE divorced. The divorce happened TO them. They are the recipients of the action, not its initiators.

**Form II (تَفْعِيل):** intensive. The divorce was thorough, complete, final.

**Feminine plural:** all of them. Every single one.

**Definite article (الـ):** THE divorced women — not "some" or "certain" or "those who meet conditions." THE muṭallaqāt. All. Without exception.

Back in 2:236, Allah prescribed a consolation gift for a specific category: women divorced before consummation without a specified mahr. That was a narrow case. Here — with four grammatical features compressed into a single word — He extends the provision to EVERY divorced woman, regardless of circumstance.

And then: **حَقًّا عَلَى ٱلْمُتَّقِينَ** — *ḥaqqan 'alal-muttaqīn* — "a binding truth upon the God-conscious."

Remember 2:236? There it was **حَقًّا عَلَى ٱلْمُحْسِنِينَ** — a binding truth upon the *muḥsinīn*, the doers of excellence. That was the specific case — the short marriage, the bare minimum situation. The standard was iḥsān: active production of beauty.

Here, closing the entire passage, the standard shifts. It is no longer iḥsān. It is **تَقْوَى** — taqwa. God-consciousness. The standard is no longer "be excellent." It is "**be aware of Allah.**"

The muḥsin does beautiful things because beauty is his standard. The muttaqī does right things because **Allah is watching.**

This shift — from excellence to awareness — is the shift from the specific to the universal. In the narrow case, Allah asked for beauty. In the universal case, He asks for something deeper: the consciousness that He is present in every financial transaction, every custody handover, every moment where a divorced woman's provision is being decided.

[PAUSE]

---

### Element 3: The Closing Word — When Allah Asked You to Tie Your Mind Down

And then the passage closes.

**كَذَٰلِكَ يُبَيِّنُ ٱللَّهُ لَكُمْ ءَايَـٰتِهِۦ لَعَلَّكُمْ تَعْقِلُونَ**

*Kadhālika yubayyinullāhu lakum āyātihi la'allakum ta'qilūn*

"Thus does Allah make clear to you His verses that you might use reason."

Two verbs here, both doing extraordinary work.

**يُبَيِّنُ** — *yubayyinu* — Form II of بين. Form I (*bāna*) means something became clear, was evident. Form II (*bayyanna*) means to MAKE something clear — actively, thoroughly, intensively. The doubled yā' in the Form II pattern makes the clarification EMPHATIC. Allah is not passively revealing. He is ACTIVELY, THOROUGHLY separating meaning from ambiguity, truth from confusion, law from chaos.

The entire twenty-two ayah passage was an act of *tabyīn* — an intensive, systematic clarification of the most complex area of human law. Every ruling was placed, every condition specified, every exception noted, every divine name chosen to match the domain. This is not a God who issues vague principles and expects you to figure it out. This is a God who **meticulously clarifies**.

And then the destination of all that clarification:

**لَعَلَّكُمْ تَعْقِلُونَ** — "that you might use reason."

The verb **تَعْقِلُونَ** — *ta'qilūn* — from the root ع-ق-ل. And here is where the Arabic reaches deeper than any translation can follow.

The original, physical meaning of عقل is **to tie**. To hobble a camel by binding its forelegs together so it doesn't wander off. The *'iqāl* — from the same root — is the rope or cord used to bind the camel.

When Arabic extended this root to mean "reason" or "intellect," the metaphor was precise: your *'aql* is what **ties your mind to reality**. It is what prevents your understanding from wandering into error, just as the 'iqāl prevents the camel from wandering into the desert.

So when Allah closes this passage with *la'allakum ta'qilūn*, He is saying: I made all of this clear — the divorce law, the iddah, the mahr, the custody, the prayer, the consolation, the widow's provision — **so that you would TIE YOUR UNDERSTANDING to it.** So your mind wouldn't wander. So you'd see the coherence, the purpose, the architecture — and bind yourself to it through comprehension, not mere obedience.

[PAUSE]

This is not blind compliance. The closing of the family law passage is an appeal to **reason**. Allah doesn't say: "that you might obey" (*ta'malūn*). He doesn't say: "that you might fear" (*tattaqūn*). He doesn't say: "that you might believe" (*tu'minūn*). He says: **that you might THINK.**

The implication is staggering: if you actually THINK about this passage — if you trace the logic of every ruling, the proportionality of every obligation, the protection built into every provision — you will arrive at the conclusion that this system works. That it is internally coherent. That it serves the human beings it addresses. That it is **wise**.

And the God who built it? He closed 2:240 with: **عَزِيزٌ حَكِيمٌ** — Exalted in Might and WISE.

*Ḥakīm* — the intensive *fa'īl* pattern. Not "wise in this moment" but "wisdom is His essential nature." The entire family law passage is the product of a God whose permanent attribute is wisdom. And the proof is: if you use your reason, you'll see it.

[PAUSE]

---

Now let's ask what this closing means — not for the ayahs that precede it, but for how we navigate the spaces these ayahs address.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Law As Architecture, Not Punishment

There is a common modern perception — held by Muslims and non-Muslims alike — that Islamic family law is a set of rigid rules imposed from above. Obey because God said so. Don't ask why. The law is the law.

2:242 dismantles this perception in a single sentence.

**لَعَلَّكُمْ تَعْقِلُونَ** — "that you might use reason."

Allah did not design the family law passage to be obeyed in ignorance. He designed it to be **understood**. The entire structure — from the prohibition on marrying polytheists to the consolation for divorced women — is built to be examined, questioned, traced, analyzed, and REASONED through. The law is not a wall. It is a building. And you are invited to study the blueprints.

Think about what happens when you walk through this passage with your *'aql* engaged:

The iddah for a divorced woman is three menstrual cycles (2:228). Why? To determine pregnancy. To give both parties a cooling period. To protect the woman's right to return to the marriage if reconciliation is possible.

The iddah for a widow is four months and ten days (2:234). Why longer? Because grief requires more time than anger. Because the community needs longer to adjust. Because the scholars note that four months is approximately when a pregnancy becomes physically manifest.

The mahr is half if divorce comes before consummation (2:237). Why half? Because the marriage barely existed. Full mahr for a marriage that was never lived would be unjust. No mahr at all would leave the woman with nothing from a contract she entered in good faith. Half is the balance.

The consolation gift is proportional to the man's means (2:236). Why proportional? Because a flat rate would crush the poor and be meaningless to the wealthy. Proportionality ensures that every compensation is felt by the giver and meaningful to the receiver.

Every ruling HAS a reason. And Allah closes the passage by telling you to FIND IT.

[PAUSE]

This is not an invitation to override the law with personal reasoning. The law stands. But the law stands on foundations of wisdom, and Allah wants you to see those foundations. Not so you can evaluate whether Allah's ruling is "good enough." So that your obedience moves from the surface to the core — from compliance to conviction.

A person who prays because they were told to pray and a person who prays because they understand what prayer does to the soul — both are praying. But only one of them is using their *'aql*. And Allah, at the close of this passage, tells you which one He wants you to be.

[PAUSE]

---

### Theme 2: The Arc from Muḥsinīn to Muttaqīn — Who Allah Is Calling You to Become

Step back now and trace something extraordinary across the entire passage.

In 2:236 — the first consolation gift ruling, the narrow case — Allah declares it: **حَقًّا عَلَى ٱلْمُحْسِنِينَ** — a binding truth upon the doers of excellence.

In 2:241 — the universal consolation ruling, the final word — Allah declares it: **حَقًّا عَلَى ٱلْمُتَّقِينَ** — a binding truth upon the God-conscious.

The structure is identical. The standard shifts.

The *muḥsin* is someone who does beautiful things. Excellence is their craft. They give the consolation gift not because they have to but because they are the kind of person who produces beauty even in difficult situations. This is a character trait — an internal standard of quality.

The *muttaqī* is someone who is AWARE OF ALLAH. They give the provision not because it's beautiful (though it is) but because they know that the God who legislated this provision **sees them**. This is not a character trait — it is a relationship. The muttaqī acts out of consciousness of the Other, not merely out of internal excellence.

The passage begins by asking you to be excellent. It ends by asking you to be **aware**.

[PAUSE]

Why does the standard rise? Because the scope widens. In the narrow case (2:236), only a specific category of women is addressed. The moral ask is: be excellent to her. In the universal case (2:241), ALL divorced women are addressed. The moral ask must rise to match: be aware of Allah in EVERY interaction with EVERY divorced woman.

Excellence can be selective. You can be excellent in some areas and mediocre in others. But taqwa — God-consciousness — is totalizing. If you are aware of Allah, you are aware of Him everywhere: in the custody handover, in the support payment, in the way you speak about your ex, in the provision you make for someone who is no longer your wife but who remains, by divine decree, someone you owe *matā'* — a provision according to what is acceptable.

The passage asks you to grow. It starts with beauty. It ends with awareness. And between the two — across twenty-two ayahs of meticulous legislation — Allah has been training you. Teaching you what beauty looks like in practice. Teaching you what awareness demands in specifics. So that by the time you arrive at 2:242, you are not just obedient. You are REASONING. You see the architecture. You understand the wisdom. You have become, through the passage itself, someone closer to both iḥsān and taqwa than you were when you entered it.

[PAUSE]

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Entire Passage (2:221-242)

Now step all the way back. See the whole structure:

**2:221** — WHO to marry (the boundary)
**2:222-223** — HOW to live together (intimacy legislation)
**2:224** — Don't use oaths to block good
**2:225-232** — HOW to end a marriage (divorce legislation, iddah, ma'rūf)
**2:233** — The CHILD caught between (custody, breastfeeding, provision)
**2:234-235** — The WIDOW (death's iddah, the acknowledgment of desire)
**2:236-237** — The MAHR and the surplus (pre-consummation divorce, faḍl)
**2:238-239** — THE PRAYER (the interruption — the anchor that holds everything)
**2:240-241** — The PROVISION (widow's bequest, universal consolation)
**2:242** — THE REASON (why all of this was made clear)

The passage moves from ENTRY (who to marry) through LIVING (how to be intimate) through ENDING (how to divorce) through PROTECTING (the child, the widow, the financially vulnerable) through ANCHORING (guard the prayers) through PROVIDING (for everyone who was left behind) to UNDERSTANDING (use your reason).

And notice what sits at the center — the structural *wusṭā* of the passage: **2:238-239, the prayer command.** The prayer is the CENTER of the family law. It is the middle. The best. The anchor. Just as it is the center of the day, the center of the passage, and the center of the life that this passage is legislating.

The architecture is not accidental. It is *ḥakīm* — wise. And Allah, who is عَزِيزٌ حَكِيمٌ (Exalted in Might and Wise), asks you at the very end:

**لَعَلَّكُمْ تَعْقِلُونَ**

Did you see it? Did you tie your mind to it? Did you reason through the structure and find the wisdom?

Because if you did — if you used the *'aql* Allah gave you to examine the system Allah built — then the obedience that follows is not blind. It is the deepest kind of compliance: the kind that UNDERSTANDS.
