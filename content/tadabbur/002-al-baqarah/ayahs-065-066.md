## Rebuilding the World

The Sabbath was not a suggestion. It was a covenant obligation — part of the mīthāq that was taken under a mountain (2:63). Observe the seventh day. Do no work. Do no commerce. Let the world stop for one day in seven so that you remember who sustains you the other six.

And the fish knew.

Classical scholars — Ibn Kathir transmitting from Ibn Abbas and others — describe the scene with vivid detail. This was a coastal village among Bani Israel. On every day except Saturday, the fish in the sea behaved normally — scattered, difficult to catch. But on the Sabbath, the fish would come to the surface in enormous, visible numbers. Swimming close to shore. Practically offering themselves. Six days of scarcity. One day of overflowing abundance — on the one day they were forbidden to take it.

This was not a coincidence. This was a test. And the test was designed with surgical precision: **the thing you want is right there, visible, available, effortless to obtain — and you may not touch it.** Not because the thing is harmful. Not because the thing is impure. But because you were told not to. The prohibition has no logical explanation that satisfies the appetite. It only has obedience.

And so they found a loophole.

According to the classical reports, they didn't fish ON the Sabbath. They set their nets and dug their channels on Friday. The fish swam in on Saturday, trapped by the infrastructure. On Sunday, they collected the catch. Technically — *technically* — no one fished on the Sabbath. The nets did the work. The water did the work. The fish did the work. Human hands were clean.

Or were they?

**The Arabic in these two verses does something remarkable: it uses the same verb of transgression that described their ongoing state in 2:61, delivers a divine decree using the same verb that creates the universe, and then splits the aftermath into two different audiences receiving two different things. Watch how the grammar dismantles every defense they built.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### Element 1: The Reflexive Transgression — They Did It to Themselves

*I'tadaw* — **ٱعْتَدَوْا۟**

This is Form VIII from the root *'a-da-wa* (عدو) — to be hostile, to transgress, to exceed bounds. We met this exact root and form at the end of 2:61: *wa kānū ya'tadūn* — "and they used to transgress." The same root. The same Form VIII. The connection across these verses is not accidental — the Quran is linking the general habit of transgression (2:61) with this specific instance of it (2:65).

But the form is doing deeper work. Form VIII in Arabic (*iftaʿala*) is the reflexive or middle voice — the action reflects back upon the doer. When we say *i'tadā* (Form VIII), the grammar says: **they transgressed upon THEMSELVES.** The violation was not against someone else. It was not against the fish. It was not even, ultimately, against the Sabbath. It was against themselves.

Think of a person who cheats on their diet. They're not harming the diet. The diet doesn't care. They're not harming the food. The food doesn't care. They're harming *themselves* — their own discipline, their own word, their own integrity. The transgression loops back. And the Form VIII catches that loop in the grammar.

And notice: *minkum* — **مِنكُمْ** — "from among YOU." The transgressors came from within the community. They weren't outsiders. They weren't enemies who infiltrated the group. They were *minkum* — from you, of you, part of you. The corruption was homegrown. The loophole wasn't imported. It was invented by the people who knew the law best.

[PAUSE]

This is always how it works. The most dangerous violations of a principle never come from people who are ignorant of the principle. They come from people who know it intimately — intimately enough to find the seams, the gaps, the technical exemptions. The Sabbath-breakers weren't uneducated about the Torah. They were *experts.* And their expertise didn't protect them from violation — it enabled it.

### Element 2: The Ontological Imperative — "BE Apes"

Now we reach the most arresting moment in the passage:

*Fa-qulnā lahum kūnū qiradatan khāsiʾīn* — **فَقُلْنَا لَهُمْ كُونُوا۟ قِرَدَةً خَـٰسِـِٔينَ**

"So We said to them: **BE apes, despised.**"

Let's feel the weight of the verb before anything else. *Kūnū* — **كُونُوا۟** — imperative of *kāna* (كان) — to be. This is the most foundational verb in Arabic. It doesn't describe an action. It describes **existence itself.** And its imperative — *kun* / *kūnū* — is the verb of divine creation.

This is the same *kun* from *kun fa-yakūn* — **كُنْ فَيَكُونُ** — "Be, and it is." The verb Allah uses to create the heavens and the earth. The verb that brings things into existence from nothing. When Allah says *kun,* reality obeys. There is no process. There is no transition. There is no "gradually becoming." There is *kun* — and there IS.

[PAUSE]

Allah did not say: *We transformed them into apes.* He did not say: *We made them resemble apes.* He did not use *masakhnā* (to metamorphose) or *ḥawwalnā* (to change) or *ṣayyarnā* (to cause to become). He used the ontological imperative: **BE.**

The difference is not cosmetic. A transformation happens *to* someone — they are passive recipients. But *kūnū* — be — is addressed TO them as a command. "BE apes." As if to say: **this is not something being done to you from outside. This is what you ARE from inside. I am only making visible what you have already become.**

[PAUSE]

And the object: *qiradatan* — **قِرَدَةً** — apes. Indefinite. Not "the apes" (al-qiradah) but "apes" — generic, categorical, stripped of individuality. When they were human, they had names, families, histories, individual identities. When the *kūnū* landed, all of that vanished. They became *qiradatan* — a category. Anonymous. Undifferentiated. No longer persons. Just... apes.

Then the adjective: *khāsiʾīn* — **خَـٰسِـِٔينَ** — from the root *khasaʾa* (خسأ) — to drive away, to repel, to shoo off. This is the word Arabs use for driving away a dog: *ikhsaʾ* — get away, be gone. The active participle *khāsiʾ* means: one who is perpetually driven away. Permanently unwelcome. Always shooed.

Say the word out loud: *khāsiʾīn.* The harsh خ, the sibilant س, the hamza — the phonetics themselves sound like spitting. Like someone saying *get away from me.* The word sounds like what it means.

They are not just apes. They are apes that nobody wants. Apes that are driven off. Apes that inspire not curiosity or fascination but revulsion. The complete picture is: *formless, anonymous, driven away.* Everything that makes a human being dignified — name, identity, welcome — is removed.

### Element 3: Two Audiences, Two Gifts — The Architecture of Consequence

Verse 66 pivots from the event to its purpose:

*Fa-ja'alnāhā nakālan limā bayna yadayhā wa mā khalfahā wa maw'iẓatan lil-muttaqīn.*

**"So We made it a deterrent for what was before it and what came after it, and a lesson for the God-conscious."**

Two words carry the architecture: **نَكَـٰلًا** (*nakālan*) and **مَوْعِظَةً** (*maw'iẓatan*). Both describe the purpose of the event. But they serve different audiences and do different work.

*Nakāl* — **نَكَـٰلًا** — from *nakala* (نكل) — to deter, to restrain, to shackle. A nakāl is a deterrent punishment — punishment whose primary function is not to satisfy justice for the individual but **to deter others.** The punished person is almost incidental. The real audience is everyone watching.

Think of a speed camera on the highway. Its purpose isn't to punish the one driver who gets caught. Its purpose is to make every other driver slow down. The ticket is the nakāl. The *deterrence* is the point.

And who receives this nakāl? *Limā bayna yadayhā wa mā khalfahā* — "for what was before it and what came after it." Literally: "for what was between its hands and what was behind it." The "hands" metaphor points forward in time — what is in front of you, approaching. The "behind" points backward — what has already come. The nakāl reaches in BOTH temporal directions: it deters those who were alive at the time AND those who come after.

But then the verse names a second gift: *wa maw'iẓatan lil-muttaqīn* — **وَمَوْعِظَةً لِّلْمُتَّقِينَ** — "and a lesson for the God-conscious."

*Maw'iẓah* — from *wa'aẓa* (وعظ) — to counsel, to admonish gently, to give sincere advice. A maw'iẓah is softer than a nakāl. It is instruction, not shackling. Guidance, not deterrence. And it is given to the **muttaqīn** — the people who already guard themselves (Form VIII from وقي — the same root as *tattaqūn* in 2:63).

[PAUSE]

Here is the asymmetry that the verse constructs deliberately:

**The general public gets نَكَال — deterrence.** Fear. Shackles. The sight of consequence.
**The muttaqīn get مَوْعِظَة — a lesson.** Wisdom. Counsel. Understanding.

Both audiences see the same event. But they receive different things from it. The person who has no taqwa sees the apes and feels *fear* — the nakāl works on them through dread. The person who has taqwa sees the apes and gains *understanding* — the maw'iẓah works on them through insight.

**The same event is a chain for one audience and a teacher for another.** What determines which gift you receive is not the event itself — it's who you are when you encounter it. The nakāl and the maw'iẓah are both present in the same story. Your taqwa determines which one finds you.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Loophole Mentality — When Cleverness Becomes Dehumanization

There's a particular kind of person — and you've met them — who lives in the technical exemptions.

They don't lie. They "omit." They don't cheat. They "optimize." They don't violate the rule. They "find a workaround." The letter of the law is always respected. The spirit of the law is always gutted. And when you point this out, they look at you with genuine confusion: *But I didn't break any rule. Show me the rule I broke.*

The Sabbath-breakers were this person. They didn't fish on Saturday. They set up an automated system that fished FOR them on Saturday. If you had confronted them, they would have had a perfectly logical defense. The nets were set on Friday. The fish were collected on Sunday. At no point did a human hand touch a fishing line on the Sabbath. Case closed.

And the Quran looks at this defense and says: *kūnū qiradatan khāsiʾīn.* Be apes. Despised.

[PAUSE]

Why apes?

The classical scholars — including Mujahid and others narrated through Ibn Kathir — understood this as a literal, physical transformation. They became apes. They lived three days. They died without offspring. The transformation was real, embodied, and final.

But the spiritual dimension that tadabbur invites us to explore alongside the literal event is this: **an ape imitates.** An ape mimics human behavior without understanding it. An ape can sit at a table without knowing what a meal means. An ape can hold a tool without grasping its purpose. An ape performs the *form* of human action without the *substance* of human intention.

And isn't that exactly what the Sabbath-breakers did? They performed the *form* of Sabbath observance — no work on Saturday, technically — without the *substance* of Sabbath obedience — which was to STOP, to REST, to TRUST that Allah provides even on the day you don't work. The form was perfect. The substance was hollow. They were already imitating obedience without living it.

**The ape is the body of obedience without the soul of it.** And when you strip the soul from obedience, what remains is mimicry. What remains is an ape wearing human clothes. The *kūnū* didn't create the ape. It revealed it.

[PAUSE]

This reaches into every religious community, including ours. Wherever the loophole mentality lives — the Islamic finance product that technically avoids *ribā* while replicating its every effect, the faster-who-already-broke-fast-in-his-heart, the prayer performed with perfect posture and zero presence — the ape is near. Not because the person is evil. But because the gap between form and substance is the gap between human and animal. An animal follows appetite. A human follows principle. When you find a way to follow appetite while looking like you're following principle — you are performing human behavior without the substance that makes it human.

### Theme 2: The Event That Teaches in Two Languages

Every difficult experience in life speaks in two languages.

To the person without awareness, the difficult experience is simply punishment — random, overwhelming, terrifying. It produces fear, resentment, or despair. It shackles them. It is *nakāl* — deterrence through dread.

But to the person with *taqwa* — with active self-guarding, with spiritual consciousness — the same difficult experience is a *maw'iẓah* — a teacher. It produces insight. It illuminates patterns. It reveals the connection between choices and consequences that was always there but wasn't visible until the consequence landed.

The story of the Sabbath-breakers is the Quran's example of this principle. The same event — people turned into apes for violating the Sabbath — is simultaneously a nakāl for the general audience and a maw'iẓah for the muttaqīn. The apes are the same apes. The story is the same story. But what you TAKE from it depends on what you bring to it.

[PAUSE]

Think of a business that collapses because the founder cut ethical corners. To the public, it's a scandal — a deterrent, a reason to be careful, a spectacle of consequences. That's nakāl. But to the person who is actually trying to build something with integrity — the entrepreneur who stays up at night asking whether their practices are sound — the same collapse is a lesson. Not a fearful warning but a *clarifying* one. It illuminates the connection between integrity and longevity. It teaches through understanding, not through shackles. That's maw'iẓah.

And here is the deeper point: the verse says the maw'iẓah is *lil-muttaqīn* — **for** the God-conscious. Not "available to" or "offered to." FOR them. As if it is a gift wrapped inside the tragedy. The muttaqīn don't receive the lesson by accident. They receive it because their taqwa QUALIFIES them to receive it. The same way a medical student sees a diagnosis where a layperson sees only pain — the taqwa gives you the eyes to see the lesson inside the punishment.

And the word *taqwa* here closes a circle that began in 2:63. In that verse, the covenant was given with the hope: *la'allakum tattaqūn* — "that you might guard yourselves." The Torah was meant to PRODUCE taqwa. Now, in 2:66, the story of the Sabbath-breakers serves as a *maw'iẓah* for those who actually HAVE taqwa. The covenant's purpose (producing taqwa) and the covenant's violation (consumed by the muttaqīn as a lesson) are linked by the same root: وقي. **The Torah was supposed to make you God-conscious. And the story of those who violated it IS the lesson for those who became God-conscious.**

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architectural Thread

The two verses form a clean two-part structure:

**Verse 65 — THE EVENT:**
- You already knew (عَلِمْتُمُ — past knowledge, undeniable)
- Those who transgressed from among YOU (مِنكُمْ — internal, not external)
- Concerning the Sabbath (فِى ٱلسَّبْتِ — the specific violation)
- We said: BE apes, despised (كُونُوا۟ قِرَدَةً خَـٰسِـِٔينَ — the decree)

**Verse 66 — THE PURPOSE:**
- We made IT (جَعَلْنَـٰهَا — the entire episode)
- A nakāl — deterrent — for those present and those after (general audience)
- A maw'iẓah — lesson — for the muttaqīn (specific audience)

The event descends. The purpose expands. The apes were local and temporary. The lessons are universal and permanent.

And the word أخذ that echoed through 2:63 (We TOOK your covenant; TAKE what We gave you with strength) finds its dark fulfillment here. They didn't take the Torah with strength. They took the Sabbath with loopholes. And the same divine authority that said *khudhū* (take!) now says *kūnū* (be!). The imperative that offered guidance now delivers consequence. **The voice hasn't changed. The direction has.**

### Questions to Carry

- Where in your life are you technically compliant but spiritually absent? Where have you found a loophole that satisfies the letter of the law while gutting its spirit?

- When you encounter a difficult event — a loss, a failure, a public downfall — do you receive it as *nakāl* (a shackle of fear) or as *maw'iẓah* (a lesson of understanding)? What determines which one finds you?

- The transgressors were *minkum* — from among the community, not outsiders. Where is the Sabbath-breaking mentality growing inside your own community — and are you one of the watchers, the enablers, or the honest ones who name it?

- If the gap between form and substance is the gap between human and ape — where is that gap widest in your own worship?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**They found a loophole around the Sabbath and called it obedience — but when Allah said "Be apes," He wasn't changing what they were; He was revealing what they'd already become by performing the form of worship while hollowing out its soul, and the same story that chains the careless teaches the conscious.**

### The Closing Invitation

The next time you are tempted to find the technical workaround — the halal label on the haram substance, the five-daily-prayer posture over the prayerless heart, the Ramadan fast with the starving body and the full ego — pause.

Ask: *Am I taking this with quwwah — or with cleverness?*

The difference between the two is the difference between a human being holding a covenant and an ape mimicking one. And the only person who knows which one you are — in the quiet, private space where no one watches — is you and the One who said *kūnū.*

### Du'a

*Allāhumma, protect us from the loophole mentality that strips the soul from obedience while preserving its form. Do not let us become apes in human clothing — performers of worship without its substance, holders of covenants without their weight. Make us among those who receive the maw'iẓah, not just the nakāl — who learn from the stories of the fallen with understanding, not just fear. And when You test us with abundance on the day of prohibition — when the fish swim close and the nets are easy — give us the strength to keep our hands still and our trust in You alive.*
