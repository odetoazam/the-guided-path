# The Owner Changes the Rules
## Sovereignty, Abrogation, and the Questions That Replace Submission
### Al-Baqarah 2:106-108

---

## INTRODUCTION

Imagine you move into an apartment. You sign a lease. The lease says no pets. Six months later, the landlord sends an updated lease: pets are now allowed on the third floor.

You would not storm into the landlord's office and say, "But the lease said no pets! How can you change your own rules? This proves you were never a real landlord."

That would be absurd. The person who owns the building has the right to change the rules in their building. The change does not prove inconsistency. It proves *authority*.

Now imagine someone did react that way — not because they genuinely cared about pets, but because they were looking for a reason to break the lease entirely.

That is what is happening in Medina.

The Muslim community is young. The Prophet ﷺ has been in Medina for perhaps a year, maybe two. The qiblah has just changed — from Jerusalem to Makkah. Some rulings from the early Meccan period are being updated. The community is adjusting. And from the outside, voices are saying: "Look — he told them one thing, now he tells them another. If this were really from God, it wouldn't change."

The Quran has just spent sixty-five verses (2:40-105) narrating the history of Bani Isra'il — their covenants, their selective obedience, their distortion of scripture, their excessive questioning of Musa. The listener is still carrying the weight of that history.

And now the Quran turns to address the very objection those voices are raising. But instead of defending abrogation, the Quran does something that should stop us in our tracks.

It *claims* abrogation.

Not apologetically. Not with explanation. With the majestic plural: **"Whatever We abrogate..."** The first word of defense is not "We had good reason." The first word is *"We."*

Here is what we are going to discover: the Quran does not argue that abrogation is acceptable. It argues that abrogation is an *expression of sovereignty*. And then it warns the new ummah — in a devastating callback to everything it just narrated — that the desire to question this process is the exact pattern that destroyed the people who came before them.

## PART 1: THE LINGUISTIC JOURNEY

### Element 1: نُنسِهَا — The Word That Splits Into Two Meanings

Let me begin with a word that the classical scholars spent centuries discussing, and it sits right in the middle of the first ayah.

<!-- ayah:2:106 partial -->
مَا نَنسَخْ مِنْ ءَايَةٍ أَوْ نُنسِهَا

*Mā nansakh min āyatin aw nunsihā* — "Whatever verse We abrogate *or nunsihā*."

That last word. *Nunsihā*. What does it mean?

Here is where the Quran does something that only a text read in multiple *qira'āt* — multiple authorized prophetic readings — can do. The word carries two entirely different meanings depending on which reading you follow, and both readings are authentic, both transmitted through verified chains back to the Prophet ﷺ himself.

In the majority reading — the reading of 'Asim, Hafs, and most of what you hear in the Muslim world today — the word is **نُنسِهَا** (*nunsihā*), from the root *nūn-sīn-yā* (ن-س-ي). Form IV: *ansā, yunsī* — to **cause to forget**. Allah is not saying the verse is forgotten accidentally. He is saying: *We cause it to be forgotten*. The forgetting is divinely orchestrated. The community's memory is in His hands just as much as the revelation itself.

But in the reading of Ibn Kathir al-Makki — one of the seven canonical readers — the word is **نُنْسِئْهَا** (*nunsi'hā*), from a different root entirely: *nūn-sīn-hamza* (ن-س-أ). This means to **delay**, to **postpone**. In this reading, Allah is saying: whatever verse We abrogate or *delay its implementation* — We bring something better or equivalent.

[PAUSE]

Now, most people encounter this as: "scholars disagreed about this word." And that framing treats it like a problem to be solved — which one is right?

But the Quran was revealed in both readings. Both were recited by the Prophet ﷺ. Both are Quran.

Which means both meanings are operating simultaneously.

Think about what that gives us. The concept of abrogation now has two dimensions. There is the verse whose ruling is replaced and whose text is **caused to be forgotten** by the community — it fades from active recitation by divine design. And there is the verse whose ruling is **postponed** — held in reserve, delayed until the right time.

The classical scholars treated these as two types within abrogation. Al-Tabari explores both at length. Some ayahs were abrogated in ruling and text — the community no longer recites them. Others were abrogated in ruling but the text remains. And the "delay" reading opens a third category: revelation whose time simply has not yet come.

Notice what this means for the objection. The critics said: "How can God change His mind?" The Quran answers: He does not change His mind. He manages time. Some things are replaced. Some things are caused to fade. Some things are held back until their moment arrives. **None of this is indecision. All of it is orchestration.**

And the verb form makes this precise. *Nunsihā* is Form IV — the causative. In Arabic, Form I of this root would be *nasiya* — "he forgot." That is a passive, accidental event. Something slipped from memory. But Form IV — *ansā, yunsī* — means to **make** someone forget. The agent is doing it deliberately.

When Allah says *nunsihā*, He is not describing an accident of human memory. He is describing a sovereign act over human memory itself. The same God who sends the revelation also controls whether the community retains it.

[PAUSE]

Let's sit with that for a moment. Your memory of certain things — their vividness, their accessibility, whether they stay on the surface or sink to the bottom — is not entirely in your hands. You have experienced this. There are things you tried to memorize and couldn't retain. There are things you didn't try to memorize and can't forget. Memory is not a filing cabinet you control. And this ayah is telling us: at the level of divine revelation, Allah is the one managing what stays and what fades.

This should give us a particular kind of humility about what we carry. The knowledge you retain of the Quran — the surahs you remember, the ayahs that come to mind at critical moments, the phrases that echo in your heart at 3 AM — that retention is not merely the product of your study habits. It is, at some level, a gift. *He* decides what stays with you.

### Element 2: The Iltifat — The Camera That Shifts Across Three Verses

Here is something happening across these three ayahs that you will miss if you read them separately, but cannot miss once you see it.

Watch who Allah is talking *to*.

<!-- ayah:2:106 partial --> أَلَمْ تَعْلَمْ

*A-lam ta'lam* — "Do you not know?" The verb is second person **singular**. "You" — one person. This is addressed to the Prophet ﷺ directly. It is intimate. It is reassurance spoken to one man: *Do you not know that Allah has power over all things?*

<!-- ayah:2:107 partial --> أَلَمْ تَعْلَمْ ... وَمَا لَكُم

The ayah begins the same way: *a-lam ta'lam* — still singular, still the Prophet. "Do you not know that to Allah belongs the dominion of the heavens and earth?" But then, mid-verse, the pronoun shifts: **وَمَا لَكُم** — *wa-mā lakum* — "and **you all** have not, besides Allah, any protector or helper." Suddenly it is **plural**. The camera has turned. What began as a word to one man now faces an entire community.

<!-- ayah:2:108 partial --> أَمْ تُرِيدُونَ

Now it is entirely plural. *Am turīdūna* — "Or do **you all** wish to question your Messenger?" The Prophet is no longer the one being addressed. He is the one being questioned. The audience is the ummah.

[PAUSE]

In Arabic rhetoric, this kind of shift — from singular to plural, or from second person to third — is called *iltifāt*. It is not a mistake. It is not inconsistency. It is one of the most sophisticated rhetorical devices in the language, and the Quran uses it with devastating precision.

Here is what the shift does across these three verses.

Verse 106 is **reassurance**. The Prophet ﷺ is hearing the objections — "You changed the qiblah, you contradicted yourself." Allah speaks to him alone: *Do you not know that I have power over all things?* This is a private word. A hand on the shoulder. You are not confused about this, and neither am I.

Verse 107 is the **pivot**. It begins in the same private tone — still singular, still addressed to the Prophet. But mid-sentence, the scope explodes outward. The truth is no longer private reassurance; it is public reality. *And you all* — everyone listening, every person who will ever hear this — *have no protector or helper besides Him.* The intimate conversation becomes a cosmic announcement.

Verse 108 is the **challenge**. Now the community faces the full weight of the question alone. *Or do you wish to question your Messenger?* The Prophet is not addressed. He is the subject. And the community must answer for itself.

The emotional architecture is: comfort → expansion → confrontation. Allah begins by consoling His Messenger, then turns to face the ummah with the same truth — but now it carries the weight of accountability.

And notice what sits between the reassurance and the challenge: **mulk**. Sovereignty. The dominion of the heavens and the earth. The pivot point — the moment the camera swings from singular to plural — is the declaration of ownership. Because that ownership is the answer to both the Prophet's need for reassurance and the community's temptation to question.

[PAUSE]

### Element 3: يَتَبَدَّلِ — The Self-Inflicted Exchange

The final verse ends with a warning, and the warning carries a verb that most translations flatten into nothing.

<!-- ayah:2:108 partial --> وَمَن يَتَبَدَّلِ ٱلْكُفْرَ بِٱلْإِيمَـٰنِ فَقَدْ ضَلَّ سَوَآءَ ٱلسَّبِيلِ

*Wa-man yatabaddali al-kufra bil-īmāni fa-qad ḍalla sawā'a al-sabīl.*

"Whoever **exchanges** disbelief for faith has certainly strayed from the even path."

The word is *yatabaddal*. Let me show you what lives inside it.

In Arabic, the root *bā-dāl-lām* (ب-د-ل) means to change, to replace, to exchange. But Arabic does not just give you a root and leave it. It gives you *forms* — morphological patterns that modify the root's meaning with surgical precision.

Form I: *badala* — simple exchange. Neutral. Something was changed.

Form II: *baddala* — intensive. To change something thoroughly, to replace it deliberately. The *tashdīd* (doubling) on the middle letter adds force. When the Quran says Allah *baddala* their bad deeds for good ones (25:70), it uses Form II — a thorough, complete transformation.

Form V: *tabaddala* — and this is our word. Form V is the **reflexive** of Form II. What does "reflexive of an intensive exchange" mean?

It means: **to do a thorough exchange upon yourself**. To take something and trade it — not because someone forced you, not because circumstances left you no choice, but because you *actively, deliberately, thoroughly* did the swap yourself.

[PAUSE]

*Yatabaddal*. He exchanges kufr for iman. He picks up disbelief and puts down faith. And he does it **reflexively** — to himself, for himself, by himself.

No one forced him.

This is not a description of someone who was misled. This is not someone who was confused by a difficult argument. This is someone who — when presented with the question "will you trust the One who owns everything, or will you keep questioning?" — made a trade. Deliberately. Thoroughly. And the trade was: I will take kufr and I will put down iman.

And then listen to what comes next. **فَقَدْ ضَلَّ سَوَآءَ ٱلسَّبِيلِ** — *fa-qad ḍalla sawā'a al-sabīl* — he has certainly strayed from the *sawā'* of the path.

*Sawā'* does not mean "the path." It means **the middle**, **the even ground**, **the straight center** of the path. From the root *sīn-wāw-yā* (س-و-ي), it carries the meaning of balance, evenness, equanimity. The path was smooth and level. The path was even. And this person walked off it.

Notice the sonic contrast. **ضَلَّ** — *ḍalla* — is one of the heaviest sounds in Arabic. The emphatic *ḍād* (the letter that Arabic is named after — *lughat al-ḍād*) followed by the doubled *lām*. It sounds like something hitting the ground. Something heavy falling. And immediately after it: **سَوَآءَ ٱلسَّبِيلِ** — smooth, flowing *sīn* sounds, one after another. *Sawā'a al-sabīl*. The path itself is smooth. The path itself is flowing. It is the person who stumbled, who fell, who hit the ground with the weight of their own choice.

The Quran does not say the path was hard. The Quran says **the person was heavy**.

[PAUSE]

---

**Bridge:** The linguistic architecture has shown us three things: a God who manages memory itself, a revelation that shifts its gaze from one man to an entire ummah, and a warning about an exchange that the person does to themselves. Now let us ask what this reveals about reality — and about us.

## PART 2: THE THEMATIC DEPTHS

### Theme 1: The Owner Changes the Rules in His Own House

There is a phenomenon in corporate life that anyone who has worked in a company longer than a year will recognize.

A policy changes. Maybe the remote work policy. Maybe the vacation policy. Maybe how expenses are reimbursed. And within minutes, the internal chat channels light up: "Why did they change it?" "This doesn't make sense." "The old way was fine." "This proves management doesn't know what they're doing."

Notice what is happening psychologically. The employees are not really asking "why." They are expressing something deeper: **I was comfortable with the old way, and the change threatens my sense of control.** The question "why did you change it?" is rarely a request for information. It is a protest against the fact that *someone else had the authority to change something that affected me without my permission*.

Now — most people, if they sit with this honestly, will recognize that management *does* have the authority to change company policy. You may not like the change. You may think it was a bad decision. But the authority to make it is not in question. The CEO does not need your vote to change the expense policy. That is what it means to own the company.

Here is what the Quran does with this objection. It does not explain *why* certain rulings changed. It does not give a point-by-point defense of abrogation. Instead, it makes one single move that renders all the objections irrelevant:

**It asserts ownership.**

<!-- ayah:2:107 partial -->
أَلَمْ تَعْلَمْ أَنَّ ٱللَّهَ لَهُۥ مُلْكُ ٱلسَّمَـٰوَٰتِ وَٱلْأَرْضِ

*A-lam ta'lam anna Allāha lahū mulku al-samāwāti wal-arḍ.*

"Do you not know that to Allah belongs the **mulk** — the dominion, the sovereignty — of the heavens and the earth?"

The word is *mulk*, not *milk*. In Arabic, *milk* (م-ِل-ك) is ownership of property. You own a house, a car, a piece of land — that is *milk*. But *mulk* (م-ُل-ك) is **sovereignty** — the kind of ownership that comes with legislative authority. A king has *mulk*. A property owner has *milk*. The difference is: *milk* means you own a thing. *Mulk* means you own a thing **and you make the rules about how it operates**.

Allah is not saying "I own the heavens and the earth" in the way you own your furniture. He is saying: I hold sovereign dominion over all of reality. I am the Legislator. The rules are Mine to make — and Mine to change.

[PAUSE]

And then comes the devastating consequence: **وَمَا لَكُم مِّن دُونِ ٱللَّهِ مِن وَلِىٍّ وَلَا نَصِيرٍ** — "And you have not, besides Allah, any *waliyy* or *naṣīr*."

Two words. Two seals on the exit doors.

*Waliyy* — from the root *wāw-lām-yā* (و-ل-ي) — means an intimate protector. A close ally. Someone who is *near* to you, who guards you because of a relationship. This is the exit door of connection: maybe I can find someone who loves me enough to protect me from this Sovereign's decisions.

*Naṣīr* — from the root *nūn-ṣād-rā* (ن-ص-ر) — means a helper who provides victory, a military ally, someone who can fight on your behalf. This is the exit door of power: maybe I can find someone *strong* enough to shield me from this Sovereign's authority.

Both words are in the *fa'īl* intensive pattern — these are not temporary allies or part-time protectors. These are *permanent* categories. And the Quran negates both: you have no permanent intimate protector and no permanent powerful helper besides Him.

In other words: there is no back door. There is no one who loves you enough to exempt you from His sovereignty. There is no one powerful enough to overrule Him. Every escape route you might imagine — through connection or through force — is closed.

And both negation particles *min* in the phrase **مِن وَلِىٍّ وَلَا نَصِيرٍ** are emphatic. Arabic uses this doubled *min* construction in negation to emphasize totality: not even the slightest trace of a protector, not even the smallest hint of a helper. Zero. Absolute.

So when someone objects to abrogation — "Why did the ruling change?" — the Quran's answer is not a committee meeting. It is: *Do you know who you are talking to? The Owner of every atom in the heavens and the earth. And you have no one — no intimate ally, no powerful champion — besides Him.*

This is not cruelty. This is the most freeing truth in existence. Because if Allah is the only Sovereign, then you do not need to manage the universe. You do not need to approve every divine decision. You do not need to audit the Legislator. You can simply... submit. And the word for that — *islām* — is what this entire religion is named after.

[PAUSE]

Here is the practical question this leaves us with, and it is not abstract. Where in your life are you auditing God's decisions? Where are you sitting with a ruling, a circumstance, a door that was closed or opened, and saying: "This doesn't make sense. The old way was better. Why did He change this?"

The Quran's answer is not "Let Me explain." The Quran's answer is: "Do you know whose house you are standing in?"

[PAUSE — major pause]

### Theme 2: The Questions That Replace Submission

There is a type of question that is not really a question.

You see it in meetings. Someone raises their hand and says, "I just want to understand — why are we doing it this way?" And everyone in the room knows: they do not want to understand. They want to not do it. The question is a vehicle for resistance dressed in the language of curiosity.

Modern internet culture has a word for this: *sealioning*. Asking endless, superficially reasonable questions not to learn but to exhaust, to delay, to undermine — while maintaining the appearance of good faith. "I'm just asking questions." "I just want to understand." "Can you show me evidence?" Each question sounds reasonable in isolation. Together, they form a wall between the person and submission.

<!-- ayah:2:108 partial -->
أَمْ تُرِيدُونَ أَن تَسْـَٔلُوا۟ رَسُولَكُمْ كَمَا سُئِلَ مُوسَىٰ مِن قَبْلُ

*Am turīdūna an tas'alū rasūlakum kamā su'ila Mūsā min qabl.*

"Or do you **want** to question your Messenger just as Moses was questioned before?"

The verb *turīdūna* — you **want**, you **desire**, you **intend**. Form IV of the root *rā-wāw-dāl*. This is not someone who stumbled into a question. This is someone whose *irādah* — whose will, whose intention — has settled on questioning as a posture. They have decided: questioning is where I live.

And then the devastating comparison: **kamā su'ila Mūsā min qabl** — "just as Musa was questioned before."

Notice the passive voice. *Su'ila* — "was asked." Musa *was questioned*. The people who did the questioning are not named. They are hidden behind the passive construction. Why?

Because it is not about specific individuals. It is about a **pattern**. The passive universalizes the behavior. It is not "just as the Israelites questioned Musa." It is "just as Musa was questioned" — by anyone, by everyone, by the very phenomenon of excessive questioning itself.

And the listener — the person sitting in Medina hearing this for the first time — has just spent sixty-five verses watching that pattern unfold.

Show us God openly (2:55). We can't stand one kind of food (2:61). What color is the cow? What kind of cow? (2:67-71). Every time a messenger came to them with what their souls did not desire (2:87). We hear and we disobey (2:93).

The Quran has built the case for sixty-five verses. It has shown the community, in exhaustive detail, what happens when a nation substitutes questions for submission. And now it turns to the new ummah and says: **Is that what you want? Is that the pattern you want to inherit?**

[PAUSE]

The word *kamā* — "just as" — is doing enormous work here. It is not a vague comparison. It is a **mirror**. The Quran is holding up the entire Bani Isra'il section — everything it just narrated — and saying: *This is a mirror. You are looking at what you could become.*

Every excessive question asked of Musa that the listener remembers is now a warning. "Show us God openly" — that was not curiosity. That was a demand dressed as a spiritual need. "What color is the cow?" — that was not diligence. That was avoidance dressed as precision. Each question, when you strip away its reasonable exterior, was a mechanism for one thing: **not submitting**.

Here is what is psychologically profound about this. The person who asks excessive questions genuinely believes they are being thoughtful. They are not lying when they say, "I just want to understand." They truly experience the impulse as a desire for clarity. But the Quran sees through the experience to the function. And the function is: as long as I am asking, I am not obeying. As long as the conversation is open, the demand is deferred. The question becomes a place to live instead of a bridge to cross.

And this is not just an ancient pattern. This is the modern condition.

How many people are "still researching" Islam — for years? How many are "not sure about this ruling" — for a decade? How many have a question about qadr, or about a specific hadith, or about a historical event, that they carry not as a genuine inquiry but as a **permission slip** to delay submission?

The question becomes the residence. Not the road.

[PAUSE]

The ayah ends: **وَمَن يَتَبَدَّلِ ٱلْكُفْرَ بِٱلْإِيمَـٰنِ فَقَدْ ضَلَّ سَوَآءَ ٱلسَّبِيلِ** — "And whoever *yatabaddal* — exchanges — kufr for iman has certainly strayed from the even path."

We already explored the Form V verb *yatabaddal* — the reflexive exchange, the self-inflicted swap. But notice what is being exchanged and in which direction.

The person takes **al-kufr** — disbelief — and puts down **al-īmān** — faith. The preposition *bā'* (بِ) here marks what is being given up. They are purchasing kufr *with the currency of* iman. Faith is the price they paid. Disbelief is the product they bought.

And how did this exchange happen? Not through a dramatic moment of apostasy. Not through a single decision. Through questions. Through that slow, steady process of questioning the Messenger's authority, questioning the change in legislation, questioning the divine right to manage revelation — until the questions themselves became the new creed. Until *questioning* replaced *believing* as the posture of the soul.

The path was **sawā'** — even, smooth, level. The path was not difficult. The path did not mislead anyone. The person strayed from an even path. They left solid ground for fog. And they did it — *yatabaddal*, reflexive, Form V — to themselves.

## CLOSING SYNTHESIS

### The Architecture

Now that we have traveled through the linguistic details and the thematic depths, step back and see the design of these three verses as a single unit.

**Verse 106: DIVINE ACTION** — "We abrogate, We cause to be forgotten, We bring what is better." The subject is Allah. The verbs are all first person plural. The tone is declaration.

**Verse 107: DIVINE AUTHORITY** — "To Him belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth." The subject is still Allah, but now the grammar of *ownership* appears. The action in verse 106 is grounded in the authority of verse 107. He does what He does because He *is* who He is.

**Verse 108: HUMAN RESPONSE** — "Or do you wish to question your Messenger?" Now the subject shifts to the human being. The question is: given what you now know about divine action and divine authority, what will *you* do?

The structure is: **Action → Authority → Response**. Allah acts. Allah owns. What will you do?

And the iltifat — the pronoun shift — maps perfectly onto this structure. Verse 106 is singular (the Prophet, comforted). Verse 107 pivots from singular to plural (the truth expands). Verse 108 is entirely plural (the community must answer).

The Prophet is comforted privately. The truth is declared universally. The community is challenged publicly.

And woven through all three is the concept that binds them: **sovereignty is not arbitrary power; it is the foundation of every divine act**, from the first revelation to the last abrogation to the management of human memory itself.

### Questions to Carry

- Where in your life are you treating God's decisions as if they need your approval?

- When you have a question about a ruling, a qadr, a closed door — is the question a bridge you intend to cross, or a house you intend to live in?

- What would it look like — this week, practically — to respond to something you don't understand with submission first and questions second, rather than questions first and submission... eventually?

- If the path is sawā' — even, smooth, level — what are the questions you carry that have pulled you off the even ground?

- When Bani Isra'il asked "What color is the cow?" — was there a moment in your own spiritual life where you recognize yourself in that question?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The One who owns everything has the right to change anything — and the one who responds to that change with questions instead of submission has traded the even path for their own fog, and they did it to themselves.**

### The Closing Invitation

These ayahs do not ask us to stop thinking. They do not ask us to suppress genuine questions. The Prophet's companions asked questions — deeply, sincerely, and the Quran records their questions with honor.

What these ayahs ask is that we check the *function* of our questions. Are they doors we walk through, or walls we hide behind? Are they stepping stones toward obedience, or substitutes for it?

The Owner of the heavens and the earth has spoken. He has explained some things and left others unexplained. He has changed some rulings and preserved others. He has caused some things to be forgotten and brought others forward.

The invitation is not to understand all of it. The invitation is to trust the One who does.

### Du'a

*Allāhumma, You are the Owner of the heavens and the earth, and we are tenants in Your house. Give us the humility to accept Your rulings without requiring that You explain them, the wisdom to know when our questions are doors and when they are walls, and the courage to step forward onto the even path — even when we cannot see where it leads. You are al-Qadīr, and Your power has never been in question. It is our submission that has.*
