ABSENCE FLAGS:
1. In 2:120, the condition for their satisfaction is حَتَّىٰ تَتَّبِعَ مِلَّتَهُمْ — until you follow their millah. There is no middle option presented. 3. In 2:121, the pronoun bihī — "in it" — is ambiguous. Does it refer to the Kitāb (the Book) or to the Prophet ﷺ himself? The ambiguity may be deliberate — those who recite the Book properly believe in BOTH.

# They Will Never Be Pleased — And the Few Who Read It Right
## The Unappeasable Critic, the Guidance That Needs No Qualifier, and the Right the Book Has Upon Its Reader
### Al-Baqarah 2:120-121

---

[ayah:2:120] وَلَن تَرْضَىٰ عَنكَ ٱلْيَهُودُ وَلَا ٱلنَّصَـٰرَىٰ حَتَّىٰ تَتَّبِعَ مِلَّتَهُمْ ۗ قُلْ إِنَّ هُدَى ٱللَّهِ هُوَ ٱلْهُدَىٰ ۗ وَلَئِنِ ٱتَّبَعْتَ أَهْوَآءَهُم بَعْدَ ٱلَّذِى جَآءَكَ مِنَ ٱلْعِلْمِ ۙ مَا لَكَ مِنَ ٱللَّهِ مِن وَلِىٍّ وَلَا نَصِيرٍ

[ayah:2:121] ٱلَّذِينَ ءَاتَيْنَـٰهُمُ ٱلْكِتَـٰبَ يَتْلُونَهُۥ حَقَّ تِلَاوَتِهِۦٓ أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ يُؤْمِنُونَ بِهِۦ ۗ وَمَن يَكْفُرْ بِهِۦ فَأُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ هُمُ ٱلْخَـٰسِرُونَ

*Wa-lan tarḍā 'anka al-yahūdu wa-lā al-naṣārā ḥattā tattabi'a millatahum. Qul inna hudā Allāhi huwa al-hudā. Wa-la'ini ittaba'ta ahwā'ahum ba'da alladhī jā'aka mina al-'ilm, mā laka mina Allāhi min waliyyin wa-lā naṣīr. Alladhīna ātaynāhumu al-kitāba yatlūnahu ḥaqqa tilāwatihī ulā'ika yu'minūna bih. Wa-man yakfur bihī fa-ulā'ika humu al-khāsirūn.*

**And never will the Jews or the Christians be pleased with you until you follow their way. Say: "Indeed, the guidance of Allah — it is THE guidance." And if you were to follow their desires after what has come to you of knowledge, you would have against Allah no protector or helper. Those to whom We have given the Book — they recite it with the right of its recitation. Those are the ones who believe in it. And whoever disbelieves in it — those are the losers.**

---

## INTRODUCTION

There is a person in everyone's life — and sometimes an entire community, or an entire culture — that will never be satisfied with you. Not because you are doing something wrong. But because the only thing that would satisfy them is for you to stop being who you are and start being who they want you to be.

You know this person. You have tried to accommodate them. You adjusted here. You compromised there. You softened your language. You downplayed your convictions. You showed up at their events. You used their vocabulary. And every time you thought you had met their standard — the standard moved. Because the standard was never a standard. It was a destination. And the destination was: become me.

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, in the early years of Medina, was navigating this exact dynamic at a civilizational scale. He had moved to a city where Jewish and Christian communities held theological, political, and economic power. He was reaching out. He was building coalitions. He was looking for common ground. And the Quran — which sees beyond diplomatic surfaces — now tells him the truth that every accommodator eventually learns the hard way:

**They will never be pleased with you. Not until you follow their entire way.**

Not their suggestions. Not their recommendations. Not their "feedback." Their **millah** — their complete religious and civilizational framework. Nothing less will do.

And then the Quran says something that sounds deceptively simple but is, in fact, one of the most philosophically radical statements in the entire Book:

**قُلْ إِنَّ هُدَى ٱللَّهِ هُوَ ٱلْهُدَىٰ** — "Say: Indeed, the guidance of Allah — **it** is THE guidance."

Here is what we will discover: the word *lan* creates a permanent door that will never open, the word *millah* reveals that the demand was never about specifics but about total surrender, and the phrase *ḥaqqa tilāwatih* introduces a concept that should unsettle every person who reads the Quran — the idea that the Book itself has a *right* upon you that you may or may not be fulfilling.

## PART 1: THE LINGUISTIC JOURNEY

### Element 1: لَن — The Door That Will Never Open

The ayah opens with one of the most final words in Arabic grammar.

<!-- ayah:2:120 partial --> وَلَن تَرْضَىٰ عَنكَ ٱلْيَهُودُ وَلَا ٱلنَّصَـٰرَىٰ

*Wa-lan tarḍā 'anka al-yahūdu wa-lā al-naṣārā.*

The word is **لَن** (*lan*). In Arabic, there are different ways to negate the future. *Lā* negates the present or general future — "he does not / will not." *Mā* negates the past — "he did not." But *lan* is the emphatic negation of the future — **"he will never."** It is the strongest way Arabic can say: this will not happen. Not now, not later, not under any circumstances.

When the Quran says *lan tarḍā* — they will **never** be pleased — it is not reporting a current mood that might change. It is declaring a permanent condition. This is not "they are currently unhappy with you and might come around." This is: **the door of their approval will never open for you as long as you remain who you are.**

The verb *tarḍā* — from *rā-ḍād-yā* (ر-ض-ي) — means to be pleased, to be satisfied, to approve. It carries warmth when positive — *raḍiya Allāhu 'anhu* (may Allah be pleased with him) is the highest honor. But here, negated by *lan*, it becomes a cold fact: their *riḍā* — their approval, their acceptance, their satisfaction — is permanently unavailable to you.

[PAUSE]

And then the condition: **حَتَّىٰ تَتَّبِعَ مِلَّتَهُمْ** — *ḥattā tattabi'a millatahum* — "until you follow their *millah*."

The verb *tattabi'a* is Form VIII of *tā-bā-'ayn* (ت-ب-ع). Form I (*tabi'a*) means to follow. Form VIII (*ittaba'a*) adds deliberate, self-motivated following — to follow **of your own accord**, to insert yourself into someone else's path as a conscious choice. The Quran is not describing forced conversion. It is describing the voluntary abandonment of your own guidance in order to adopt theirs.

And the word *millah* — this word deserves attention. Arabic has many words for "way" or "path." *Ṭarīqah* is a way or method. *Madhhab* is a school of thought. *Sunnah* is a practice or tradition. But *millah* is something bigger than all of these. *Millah* is an entire **civilizational religious framework** — the totality of a community's belief system, practices, identity, and worldview. When Ibrahim says *millata abīkum Ibrāhīm* (22:78), he is naming the complete Abrahamic framework.

The Quran uses the singular — *millatahum*, their millah — even though Jews and Christians have very different religious frameworks from each other. This is pointed. Despite their differences, their demand upon the Prophet is structurally the same: abandon your path and adopt ours entirely. And notice — no middle ground is offered. There is no *ḥattā tattabi'a ba'ḍa millatihim* — "until you follow some of their way." The condition is total. Partial conformity will not unlock the door. The door is labelled *millah* — all or nothing.

This is the Quran freeing the Prophet from the trap of accommodation. You cannot half-satisfy an unappeasable demand. You cannot partially follow their millah and receive partial approval. The system is binary: either you are entirely them, or you are entirely not. And since you will never be entirely them — *lan* guarantees it — you are free. Free to stop trying.

### Element 2: هُوَ ٱلْهُدَىٰ — The Pronoun That Excludes Everything Else

After the diagnosis of unappeasable opposition comes the Quran's most compact theological assertion:

<!-- ayah:2:120 partial --> قُلْ إِنَّ هُدَى ٱللَّهِ هُوَ ٱلْهُدَىٰ

*Qul inna hudā Allāhi huwa al-hudā.*

"Say: Indeed, the guidance of Allah — **it** is THE guidance."

This sentence has a grammatical structure that Arabic uses to make exclusive identifications. The word **هُوَ** (*huwa*) — "it/he" — sitting between two noun phrases is what Arabic grammar calls **ضَمِير الفَصْل** (*ḍamīr al-faṣl*) — the pronoun of separation. Its function is to make the identification between the two nouns **exclusive**. It says: this thing, and nothing else, is that thing.

Without *huwa*, the sentence would be: *inna hudā Allāhi al-hudā* — "Indeed, Allah's guidance is the guidance." Already strong. But with *huwa*, it becomes: "Indeed, Allah's guidance — **it and it alone** — is THE guidance."

The pronoun of separation slams the door on every alternative. There is no other guidance that qualifies as *al-hudā* — THE guidance, with the definite article. There are paths. There are opinions. There are traditions. There are millah-s. But *al-hudā* — the real, the true, the definitive guidance — is one thing: *hudā Allāh*. Everything else that calls itself guidance is either a fragment of it, a distortion of it, or a substitute for it.

[PAUSE]

And notice how this answers the preceding problem. The Prophet is being told: they will never be satisfied until you follow their way. The natural human response to such social pressure is: "Maybe I should adjust. Maybe I should find common ground. Maybe their way has something mine doesn't."

The Quran cuts that line of thought at the root. Not by saying "their way is wrong" — though the Quran addresses that elsewhere. But by saying something more powerful: **Allah's guidance IS the guidance.** Full stop. It does not need to be supplemented. It does not need to be blended. It does not need to accommodate anything because it does not lack anything.

The phrase reframes the entire negotiation. The Prophet is not in a position of deficit, seeking approval from those who have something he doesn't. He is in a position of completeness, carrying the only thing that deserves the definite article *al-hudā*. The approval he is being denied is approval he does not need.

### Element 3: حَقَّ تِلَاوَتِهِۦ — The Right the Book Has Upon You

Then the passage pivots to a nuance that many readers of 2:120 miss, because they stop too soon.

<!-- ayah:2:121 partial --> ٱلَّذِينَ ءَاتَيْنَـٰهُمُ ٱلْكِتَـٰبَ يَتْلُونَهُۥ حَقَّ تِلَاوَتِهِۦٓ

*Alladhīna ātaynāhumu al-kitāba yatlūnahu ḥaqqa tilāwatih.*

"Those to whom We have given the Book — they recite it with the **right of its recitation**."

The phrase **حَقَّ تِلَاوَتِهِۦ** (*ḥaqqa tilāwatih*) is a cognate accusative — *maf'ūl muṭlaq* — which in Arabic serves as an emphatic qualifier. But the emphasis here is not just "they recite it well." The word *ḥaqq* means **right, due, what is owed**. The Book has a *ḥaqq* — a right — and the reciter either fulfills it or violates it.

This is a revolutionary concept. We are accustomed to thinking of the Book as something that serves us — we read it for guidance, for comfort, for knowledge. But this ayah inverts the relationship. The Book has a claim upon you. It has a right. It is owed something by the one who opens it. And the right it is owed is *tilāwah* — which means both **recitation** (pronouncing it properly, giving its letters and sounds what they deserve) and **following** (from the root *tā-lā-wāw*, to follow, to come after, to walk behind). *Tilāwah* is simultaneously reading and following. When you recite the Quran properly, you are following it. When you follow its commands, you are completing its recitation.

So *ḥaqqa tilāwatih* means: to give the Book what it deserves — in pronunciation, in understanding, in reflection, in obedience, in implementation. To read it and let it change you. To recite it and let it lead you. To open it and then follow where it goes.

[PAUSE]

And notice: those who give the Book this right — **أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ يُؤْمِنُونَ بِهِۦ** — *ulā'ika yu'minūna bih* — "those are the ones who believe in it." The demonstrative *ulā'ika* (those) points with emphasis. Not everyone who opens the Book believes in it. Not everyone who memorizes it believes in it. Not everyone who quotes it believes in it. Only those who give it *ḥaqqa tilāwatih* — who fulfill the Book's right upon them — those are the ones who actually believe.

This is a distinction that should make every Muslim pause. You can carry the Quran in your hand, on your phone, in your memory — and still not be among those described in this ayah. Because the criterion is not possession of the Book. It is the quality of engagement with it. Does the Book receive from you what it is owed? Do you give it its *ḥaqq*?

And the alternative: **وَمَن يَكْفُرْ بِهِۦ فَأُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ هُمُ ٱلْخَـٰسِرُونَ** — "whoever disbelieves in it — those are **the** losers." The pronoun of separation *hum* appears again — *humu al-khāsirūn* — they, exclusively, are the losers. And *al-khāsirūn* — the active participle with the definite article — means THE quintessential losers. Not people who experienced a loss. People whose defining identity is loss itself.

[PAUSE]

## PART 2: THE THEMATIC DEPTHS

### Theme 1: The Freedom of Knowing You Can Never Please Them

There is a psychological phenomenon that therapists see frequently: the person who has spent their entire life trying to earn the approval of someone who will never give it. A parent. A community. A culture. An institution.

The pattern is always the same. You do what they ask. You adjust what they critique. You soften what they find offensive. You amplify what they praise. And each time you think you have arrived — each time you think *now* they will be satisfied — the bar moves. The standard shifts. The approval remains just out of reach.

And then one day, a therapist or a wise friend says the sentence that changes everything: "They will never be pleased with you."

Not "they are hard to please." Not "they have high standards." **They will never be pleased with you.** The door is closed. *Lan tarḍā.* The negation is permanent. The future is settled. No amount of accommodation will open that door, because the door was never designed to be opened by accommodation. It was designed to keep you in a permanent state of trying.

[PAUSE]

The moment you hear *lan* — the moment you truly internalize that the approval is permanently unavailable — something remarkable happens. You are **free**.

Not free from pain. The rejection still hurts. But free from the cycle. Free from the hamster wheel of accommodation. Free from the endless adjustment that never leads anywhere. Free from the question "What do I need to change about myself to be accepted?" — because the answer has been given, and the answer is: "Everything. They want your *millah*. Your entire identity. And you are not willing to give that."

The Quran gives this freedom to the Prophet ﷺ — and through him, to every Muslim, and to every human being who has ever been trapped in the cycle of seeking approval from the unappreasable. **Lan tarḍā.** They will never be pleased. Now stop trying. Not with hostility. Not with bitterness. With clarity. With the freedom that comes from knowing the truth about the dynamic.

And in place of their approval, the Quran installs something infinitely more stable: **إِنَّ هُدَى ٱللَّهِ هُوَ ٱلْهُدَىٰ**. Allah's guidance IS the guidance. You are not in deficit. You are not missing something they have. You are carrying the thing that deserves the definite article. The search for their *riḍā* was always a distraction from the *hudā* you already have.

[PAUSE — major pause]

### Theme 2: The Book Has Rights — And Most of Us Are in Debt

The concept of *ḥaqqa tilāwatih* introduces a relational framework between the reader and the Book that is unlike anything in modern reading culture.

We live in an age where the reader is sovereign. You read what you want, when you want, how you want. You annotate. You highlight. You skip. You put it down and pick it up. The book serves you. Your engagement with it is on your terms.

The Quran inverts this. The Book has a **ḥaqq** — a right. And you, the reader, are either fulfilling that right or you are in default. The Book is not a passive object waiting for your attention. It is an active claimant with demands upon you.

What does *ḥaqqa tilāwatih* actually look like? The classical scholars offered several layers:

**Al-Tabari and Ibn Abbas** emphasized: following its rulings. Making halal what it makes halal and haram what it makes haram. The *tilāwah* is not complete when the mouth finishes the word. It is complete when the life changes.

**Other scholars** emphasized: reciting it properly — giving each letter its due, observing the rules of tajwīd, letting the Arabic carry its full sound and meaning. The Book has a right to be *heard* correctly, not just read silently.

**The composite picture**: the Book's right includes how you pronounce it (giving each letter its makhraj and ṣifāt), how you understand it (engaging with its meaning, not just its surface), how you feel it (letting it move your heart, not just inform your mind), and how you live it (implementing what you understand).

Most of us — and this should sit uncomfortably — are in debt to the Book. We have received the Quran. We own copies of it. We hear it recited. But have we given it *ḥaqqa tilāwatih*? Have we given it what it deserves?

[PAUSE]

The ayah says: those who give it its right — those are the ones who actually believe. The implication is uncomfortable: the quality of your belief is connected to the quality of your engagement with the Book. You cannot separate faith from tilāwah. You cannot claim full iman while giving the Quran partial attention.

And this applies to everyone — Muslim, Christian, Jewish. *Alladhīna ātaynāhumu al-kitāb* — "those to whom We gave the Book." The criterion is not which Book but how you read it. Those who give their scripture what it deserves — who follow where it leads, who live what it teaches, who let it speak with full authority into their lives — those are believers. Those who don't are *al-khāsirūn* — the losers.

## CLOSING SYNTHESIS

### The Architecture

These two ayahs form a chiastic structure that moves between two opposing types of relationship with truth:

**2:120 — THE WRONG RELATIONSHIP: Following ahwā' (desires)**
- The opposition demands total conformity (millatahum)
- Their demands are reclassified from theology to desires (ahwā')
- Following desires leads to loss of divine protection (no waliyy, no naṣīr)
- Result: severance from Allah

**2:121 — THE RIGHT RELATIONSHIP: Following ḥaqqa tilāwah (the Book's right)**
- Those who give the Book its due recite it with its right
- Their engagement is reclassified from reading to believing (yu'minūna)
- Following the Book properly IS faith itself
- Result: the rejecters are al-khāsirūn; the followers are the believers

The pivot between the two is the sentence **إِنَّ هُدَى ٱللَّهِ هُوَ ٱلْهُدَىٰ** — "Allah's guidance IS the guidance." This sentence sits between the wrong relationship and the right one. It is the compass needle that resets the direction: stop following their desires, start giving the Book its right.

And the word *tilāwah* — following/recitation — appears in 2:121 as the corrective mirror of *tattabi'a* (following their way) in 2:120. Both are about following. The question is: following what? Their *millah* and *ahwā'*? Or the Book with its *ḥaqq*?

### Questions to Carry

- Who in your life — which person, community, or cultural pressure — are you trying to appease, when *lan tarḍā* has already been declared?

- What would change in your relationship with the Quran if you truly believed it has a *ḥaqq* upon you — a right you owe it — rather than being a resource you access at your convenience?

- When you open the Quran, are you giving it *ḥaqqa tilāwatih* — its full right of pronunciation, understanding, emotional engagement, and behavioral implementation? Or are you in debt?

- Is there a place in your life where you are following someone's *ahwā'* (desires dressed as theology) instead of following the *hudā* that has already arrived?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The people who demand you abandon your path will never be satisfied with anything less than your total surrender — but the guidance of Allah needs no supplement, and the only approval worth seeking is earned by giving the Book the right it has upon you.**

### The Closing Invitation

These ayahs invite a double liberation. First, from the cycle of seeking approval that will never come — *lan tarḍā* frees you from the hamster wheel. Second, into the relationship the Book actually demands — not your casual readership but your full *tilāwah*, reciting and following with the quality the Book deserves.

The Book is not asking for your schedule. It is asking for its right. And the distance between where most of us are with the Quran and where *ḥaqqa tilāwatih* places us is the distance between possessing a Book and believing in it.

Close that distance. That is where the iman lives.

### Du'a

*Allāhumma, free us from the cycle of seeking riḍā from those whose door will never open. Anchor us in the certainty that Your hudā — it is THE hudā — and it needs no supplement, no approval, and no accommodation. And grant us the honesty to face the debt we owe Your Book — the ḥaqq of tilāwah that most of us have not yet paid. Make us among those who recite it, follow it, live it, and are transformed by it — until we are among those of whom You say: ulā'ika yu'minūna bih.*
