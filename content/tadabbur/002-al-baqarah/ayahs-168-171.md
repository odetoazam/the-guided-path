passage_context: "These four ayahs mark a shift in Al-Baqarah's address. The preceding passage (2:163-167) argued for tawḥīd through creation signs and warned about the catastrophic end of those who take rivals. Now the Quran widens its lens from the believers and the People of the Book to ALL of humanity — يَـٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلنَّاسُ (O mankind). The subject pivots to something startlingly concrete: food. Eat what is ḥalāl and ṭayyib. But embedded in this dietary command is the Quran's most vivid metaphor for spiritual corruption: خُطُوَٰتِ ٱلشَّيْطَـٰنِ — the FOOTSTEPS of Shaytan. Not his commands. Not his whispers. His footsteps. A trail. A path you follow one step at a time, each step looking harmless, until you arrive somewhere you never intended to go. The passage then diagnoses the root disease: inherited tradition replacing revealed truth (2:170), and closes with one of the most devastating similes in the Quran — the disbeliever is compared not to an animal, but to a SHEPHERD shouting at livestock that hears nothing but noise (2:171). scholarly_note: "The simile in 2:171 is one of the most debated in Quranic rhetoric. The standard reading (majority of scholars including al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir): the example of THE ONE WHO CALLS the disbelievers to faith is like one who shouts at livestock — the disbelievers hear the sound but don't understand the meaning. Reading 2 (some scholars): the example of the disbelievers THEMSELVES is like someone who shouts at what cannot hear — they call upon idols that cannot respond. Both readings illuminate the same core reality: communication has broken down. In one reading, the caller speaks truth but the audience is livestock. In the other, the caller addresses stones. The word خُطُوَٰتِ (khuṭuwāt — footsteps) is significant: al-Razi notes that Shaytan's strategy is INCREMENTAL. He does not ask you to commit major sin directly. He leads you one step at a time, each step seemingly small, until the cumulative destination is catastrophic. This is why the Quran uses 'footsteps' rather than 'commands' — the metaphor captures the gradualism of spiritual corruption. The word ṭayyib (2:168) is distinguished from ḥalāl by scholars: ḥalāl means legally permissible; ṭayyib means wholesome, pure, good for you. Something can be ḥalāl but not ṭayyib (permissible but harmful). Allah commands both."

## INTRODUCTION

Nobody plans to end up in the wrong place.

Think about the worst decision you've ever made — the one that, looking back, you can't believe you walked into. You didn't make that decision all at once. You made it in increments. A small compromise here. A slight adjustment there. Each step looked reasonable in isolation. Each step felt like nothing. And then one morning you looked up and realized you were somewhere you never intended to be.

That is exactly the metaphor Allah chooses for Shaytan's strategy. Not "commands." Not "whispers." Not "deceptions." **خُطُوَٰتِ** — *khuṭuwāt* — **footsteps**.

A trail of steps. Left for you to follow. One at a time. Each one small enough to seem harmless. Each one leading, incrementally, inevitably, to a destination you would have refused if you'd seen it at the beginning.

The Quran has just spent five ayahs (2:163-167) arguing for tawḥīd through the testimony of the universe and warning about the catastrophic end of those who take rivals. Now it widens the address to all of humanity — **يَـٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلنَّاسُ** — and pivots from cosmic theology to something startlingly concrete: what you put in your mouth. Eat what is lawful and good. And in the same breath: do not follow Shaytan's footsteps. As though the connection between what you eat and whose trail you follow is not accidental. As though the smallest daily choices — what you consume, what you permit, what you normalize — are the very steps the trail is made of.

And then, having named the trail, the Quran diagnoses the disease that makes people follow it: **inherited tradition replacing revealed truth**. "We found our fathers doing this." And the Quran's response is devastating: "Even though their fathers understood **nothing**?"

## PART 1: THE LINGUISTIC JOURNEY

### Element 1: خُطُوَٰتِ ٱلشَّيْطَـٰنِ — Why Allah Chose "Footsteps" and Not "Commands"

In Arabic, when you want to say someone is influencing you, you have options. You can say they **أَمَرَ** — commanded you. You can say they **وَسْوَسَ** — whispered to you. You can say they **أَغْوَىٰ** — led you astray. All of these appear elsewhere in the Quran when describing Shaytan.

But here, Allah doesn't use any of them. He uses **خُطُوَٰتِ** — *khuṭuwāt* — **footsteps**. The plural of *khuṭwah* — a single step. A footprint left on a path.

Let's sit with what this word does that no other word could do.

A command is singular. Someone tells you to do something, and you either obey or refuse. The moral clarity is immediate. "Do this evil thing" — and you can recognize it as evil and say no.

But footsteps are **incremental**. They are a trail. You don't follow a trail by making one decision — you follow it by making a hundred small ones. Each step is tiny. Each step, on its own, looks like nothing. You're not committing to the destination. You're just taking **one more step**. And then one more. And then one more. And at no point does the trail announce: "You are now walking toward destruction." It just keeps going, step by step, until you arrive.

[PAUSE]

Listen to the word: **خُطُوَٰتِ** — *khuṭuwāt*. The *khā'* breathes out softly. The *ṭā'* lands — emphatic, heavy, like a foot pressing into soil. The *wāw* stretches between them. The word has the rhythm of walking. Step. Step. Step. It even *sounds* incremental.

And the verb that precedes it is equally precise: **وَلَا تَتَّبِعُوا۟** — *wa-lā tattabi'ū*. "Do not follow." This is Form VIII — *ittaba'a* — which carries the sense of **deliberate, volitional pursuit**. Not passive stumbling. Not accidental wandering. Form VIII means: you are actively choosing to place your foot in his footprint. You are **opting in**, step by step.

This is the paradox the Quran names without resolving: every step feels like your choice — and it is. But the trail was laid by someone else. You are free at every step to stop. And Shaytan's genius is that he never gives you a reason to stop at any *individual* step, because no individual step looks like the destination.

[PAUSE]

Now notice how the ayah closes: **إِنَّهُۥ لَكُمْ عَدُوٌّ مُّبِينٌ** — "Indeed, he is to you an enemy, **mubīn** — manifest, clear, self-evident."

*Mubīn* is a Form IV active participle from the root ب ي ن — the same root we saw in 2:159 (*bayyināt*) and 2:160 (*bayyanū*). It means something that **makes itself clear**. Shaytan's enmity is not hidden. It is not subtle. It is not ambiguous. It is **self-evidently** hostile.

And yet people follow his footsteps.

The Quran is telling you: the enemy is obvious. His enmity is clear. And his strategy works anyway — because it doesn't operate at the level of obvious enmity. It operates at the level of **footsteps**. One small step at a time. Each step too small to trigger your alarm. Each step too tiny to look like the work of a clear enemy. The strategy's brilliance is precisely its distance from the enmity's clarity. You know he's your enemy. You just don't recognize his footprint.

[PAUSE]

---

### Element 2: أَلْفَيْنَا — The Word That Exposes How Tradition Actually Works

**قَالُوا۟ بَلْ نَتَّبِعُ مَآ أَلْفَيْنَا عَلَيْهِ ءَابَآءَنَآ**

"They said: Rather, we follow what we **alfaynā** — what we *found* — our fathers upon."

The word is **أَلْفَيْنَا** — *alfaynā* — Form IV of the root ل ف ي. And it doesn't mean what most people think "found" means.

In Arabic, *wajada* means "to find" in the sense of seeking and discovering. You look for your keys, and you *wajada* them. There's intention. There's a search that precedes the finding.

But *alfā* — *alfaynā* — means something different. It means **to come upon, to stumble across, to find without looking**. It's the finding of someone who wasn't searching. You didn't investigate. You didn't evaluate. You didn't compare options. You simply **woke up in the middle of it**.

[PAUSE]

This is the Quran's surgical diagnosis of how inherited tradition functions psychologically. When these people say "we follow what we *alfaynā* our fathers upon," they are inadvertently confessing: **we didn't choose this. We found ourselves in it.** We were born into it. It was already there when we arrived. We never examined it. We never questioned whether our fathers were right. We just... followed. Because it was already the trail beneath our feet.

And the Quran's response is merciless in its precision:

**أَوَلَوْ كَانَ ءَابَآؤُهُمْ لَا يَعْقِلُونَ شَيْـًٔا وَلَا يَهْتَدُونَ**

"Even if their fathers understood **nothing** — nor were they guided?"

The word **شَيْـًٔا** — *shay'an* — "anything at all." Not "their fathers understood little." Not "their fathers were imperfect." Not even "their fathers were sometimes wrong." Their fathers did not use their *'aql* — **at all**. شَيْـًٔا — zero comprehension. And they were not *muhtadūn* — they had not found guidance, even for themselves (Form VIII — *yahtadūn* — the reflexive: they didn't even manage to guide *themselves*).

This is the Quran's refusal to grant inherited ignorance any partial credit. The question is rhetorical but devastating: would you still follow them if you knew — really knew — that they had **no understanding and no guidance**? And the answer the Quran expects is so obvious it doesn't even need to be stated. Of course not. No sane person follows a blind guide. But that is exactly what inherited tradition without examination produces: a line of people following a line of people, none of whom can see where they're going.

[PAUSE]

---

### Element 3: يَنْعِقُ — The Shepherd Who Shouts at Livestock

And then comes the simile. And it is devastating.

**وَمَثَلُ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ كَمَثَلِ ٱلَّذِى يَنْعِقُ بِمَا لَا يَسْمَعُ إِلَّا دُعَآءً وَنِدَآءً**

The word that carries the entire comparison is **يَنْعِقُ** — *yan'iqu*. Say it out loud. *Yan-'i-qu*. The *'ayn* catches in the throat — guttural, harsh. The *qāf* snaps. The word doesn't sound like speech. It sounds like **shouting**. Like barking. Like a noise made at something that cannot process language.

Because that's exactly what it is. *Na'aqa* in Arabic is the specific word for **the shout a shepherd directs at livestock**. Not a conversation. Not a call between equals. Not a reasoned appeal. A raw, blunt, loud noise aimed at creatures who will never understand the words — only the volume.

The scholars have two readings of this simile, and both are illuminating:

**Reading 1** (majority — al-Tabari, Ibn Kathir): The example of **the one who calls** the disbelievers to truth is like a shepherd who shouts at livestock. The caller speaks truth. The audience hears noise. The message is real. The reception is animal.

**Reading 2** (some scholars): The example of **the disbelievers themselves** is like someone who shouts at what cannot hear — they call upon idols, upon false gods, upon objects that have no capacity to respond. They are the shepherd. The stone is the sheep. And the stone is deafer than any sheep.

Both readings converge on the same horror: **the channel of meaningful communication is broken**. In one reading, truth arrives but is processed as noise. In the other, the caller shouts into a void that has never heard and will never answer. Either way, meaning does not survive the journey from speaker to listener.

[PAUSE]

And then the closing trio:

**صُمٌّ بُكْمٌ عُمْىٌ فَهُمْ لَا يَعْقِلُونَ**

"Deaf, dumb, blind — so they do not reason."

Three words. Three faculties. All sealed.

**صُمٌّ** — *ṣumm* — deaf. The emphatic *ṣād* is heavy, the doubled *mīm* closes like a door being shut on sound. They don't hear.

**بُكْمٌ** — *bukm* — mute. The *bā'* presses the lips together, the *mīm* seals them. They can't speak the truth even if they received it.

**عُمْىٌ** — *'umy* — blind. The *'ayn* catches in the throat, the *mīm* darkens. They can't see the signs that are everywhere — the same signs that 2:164 just laid out for *qawmin ya'qilūn*.

And all three words are in the *fa'l* intensive pattern — not *aṣamm* (temporarily deaf) but *ṣumm* (constitutionally deaf). The pattern suggests permanence. This isn't a passing condition. It's a **settled state** — the result of choosing, step by step, footstep by footstep, not to hear, not to speak, not to see, until the faculties themselves atrophied.

**فَهُمْ لَا يَعْقِلُونَ** — "so they do not reason." The *fā'* here is causal: **because** they are deaf-dumb-blind, **therefore** they cannot use their *'aql*. The reasoning capacity depends on input. If you've sealed off every channel — hearing, sight, speech — the mind has nothing to work with. The *'aql* doesn't fail on its own. It fails because you starved it of data.

[PAUSE]

---

## PART 2: THE THEMATIC DEPTHS

### Theme 1: The Gradualism of Corruption — How You End Up Where You Never Planned to Go

There's a psychological phenomenon called the **boiling frog** — the idea (whether or not it's scientifically accurate) that a frog placed in slowly heating water won't jump out because the temperature change is so gradual it doesn't register. By the time the water is lethal, the frog is already cooked.

The Quran named this phenomenon fourteen centuries ago. It called it *khuṭuwāt al-Shayṭān*.

Think about how addiction works. Nobody wakes up one morning and decides: "Today I will become an addict." Addiction begins with a single seemingly harmless step. One drink at a social event. One click out of curiosity. One compromise that nobody else will know about. And each step is, on its own, utterly unremarkable. Each step would be impossible to identify as "the moment things went wrong" — because no individual step *is* the moment. The moments accumulate. The trail extends. And one day you are somewhere you never planned to be, and you cannot point to a single step that was the turning point, because there was no turning point. There was only a trail.

This is why the Quran says *khuṭuwāt* — **steps**, plural — and not *amr* — a command, singular. A command can be identified and refused. A step can't. A command announces itself. A step is silent.

And notice: the Quran places this warning in the context of **food**. *Kulū mimmā fī al-arḍi ḥalālan ṭayyiban* — eat what is lawful and wholesome. And *then*: don't follow Shaytan's footsteps. The juxtaposition is not random. The daily, mundane, seemingly insignificant choices about what you consume — what you eat, what you watch, what you scroll through, what you let into your mind — **these are the footsteps**. They don't look like moral decisions. They look like lunch. They look like entertainment. They look like nothing.

But each one is a step on a trail. And the trail has a destination. And the one who laid the trail is, as the Quran says, **'aduwwun mubīn** — a clear enemy whose enmity you can see if you look but whose footsteps you follow because you don't look down.

[PAUSE]

The practical question isn't "have you committed a major sin?" That's the wrong question. The right question — the *khuṭuwāt* question — is: **where are you headed?** What trail are you on? What are the small, daily, unremarkable choices that are moving you in a direction you haven't examined? Not the dramatic moral failures. The quiet ones. The ones that look like nothing.

Because Shaytan's menu, according to 2:169, has only three items: **sū'** (evil), **faḥshā'** (gross indecency), and **saying about Allah what you don't know**. That third one — **وَأَن تَقُولُوا۟ عَلَى ٱللَّهِ مَا لَا تَعْلَمُونَ** — is often overlooked. It means: speaking about God's religion without knowledge. Making claims about what Allah wants, what Islam says, what is ḥarām or ḥalāl, **without actually knowing**. This is not a marginal sin. It is listed alongside evil and obscenity as one of Shaytan's **core objectives**.

And it gets there through footsteps. No one wakes up and says, "Today I will fabricate religious rulings." But one confident opinion leads to another, each slightly bolder than the last, each slightly less grounded in actual knowledge, until you are speaking on behalf of the Creator of the heavens and the earth about things you have not studied, have not verified, and do not understand. *Khuṭuwāt*.

[PAUSE]

### Theme 2: The Tyranny of "How We Found It" — When Heritage Replaces Thinking

There's a word in modern psychology for what 2:170 describes: **cultural inertia**. The tendency of groups to continue doing what they've always done, not because anyone has evaluated whether it works, but because "that's how it's always been."

Every human being is born into a pre-existing world. You arrive, and the world is already furnished. The beliefs are already in place. The social norms are already established. The "way we do things" is already running. Nobody hands you an orientation packet and says, "Here are the assumptions of this culture — please evaluate them before proceeding." You just absorb them. You **alfā** them. You find yourself already standing on a trail you didn't choose.

And that's not necessarily wrong. Tradition carries wisdom. Cultural continuity preserves knowledge. Not every inherited practice is misguided. The Quran is not making a blanket argument against tradition.

What the Quran is arguing against is **unexamined** tradition. The specific pathology named in 2:170 is: when you are offered revelation — *ittabi'ū mā anzala Allāh*, "follow what Allah has revealed" — and you respond by saying, "No. We follow what we *found* our fathers upon." The crime isn't following your fathers. The crime is following your fathers **instead of** revelation, **without examining** whether your fathers were right.

And the Quran's question cuts to the bone: **أَوَلَوْ كَانَ ءَابَآؤُهُمْ لَا يَعْقِلُونَ شَيْـًٔا** — "Even if their fathers understood *nothing*?"

This is not a question about whether the fathers were good people. It's a question about whether goodness is **heritable without examination**. Can you inherit truth the way you inherit eye color — passively, automatically, without engagement? The Quran's answer is no. Truth requires *'aql* — the active binding of evidence to conclusion. And *'aql* is not something you can outsource to a previous generation. Your father's understanding does not become yours by inheritance. It becomes yours only by **engaging the same evidence he should have engaged**.

[PAUSE]

This matters in every Muslim community today. How many practices — social, cultural, familial — continue not because anyone has traced them to the Quran or Sunnah but because "that's how we've always done it"? How many attitudes toward women, toward converts, toward mental health, toward money, toward marriage — persist not because they are Islamic but because they are **inherited**?

The Quran doesn't say "your culture is wrong." It says: **evaluate**. Bring your tradition to the light of revelation and see if it holds. If it does — follow it with confidence, now as a conscious choice, not an inherited default. If it doesn't — you have been given something better. Follow what Allah has revealed. Not because tradition is evil, but because unexamined tradition has no immunity against error, and error compounded over generations doesn't become truth through repetition.

The person who follows a tradition they have examined against revelation is a person of *'aql*. The person who follows a tradition *instead of* revelation is, as the Quran says, like livestock that hears the shepherd's shout and processes nothing but noise.

[PAUSE]

## CLOSING SYNTHESIS

### The Architecture

These four ayahs trace a single downward spiral, and the architecture is merciless in its logic:

**2:168** — The invitation: eat what is good, and don't follow Shaytan's footsteps. The language is open, generous, universal — *yā ayyuhā al-nās*. The earth is spread before you, full of ḥalāl and ṭayyib. The only prohibition is the trail.

**2:169** — The exposure: Shaytan's menu has exactly three items — evil, obscenity, and speaking about Allah without knowledge. Nothing more. His entire program is destruction dressed as one small step after another.

**2:170** — The mechanism: when offered revelation, they refuse and cling to inherited tradition. The mechanism of the footsteps is now named: it's not that they can't think. It's that they've outsourced their thinking to ancestors who, the Quran says, understood **nothing**.

**2:171** — The consequence: they become like livestock. They still have ears, mouths, and eyes — but all three are sealed. *Ṣumm. Bukm. 'Umy.* The faculties exist. They just no longer function. And because they don't function, the *'aql* — the capacity to bind evidence to conclusion — has nothing to work with. So they don't reason. Not because they can't. Because they **starved** the reasoning of input, one sealed faculty at a time.

The spiral: **generous invitation → refusal by footsteps → inherited tradition instead of thought → sealed faculties → the end of reasoning.**

And it all began with a step that looked like nothing.

### Questions to Carry

- What are the small, daily, unremarkable choices in your life that might be footsteps on a trail you haven't examined? Not the dramatic decisions — the quiet ones. The ones that look like nothing.

- Is there a belief, practice, or attitude you hold not because you've examined it against revelation but because you **alfaynā** — you found yourself in it? What would happen if you examined it?

- The Quran lists "saying about Allah what you do not know" as one of Shaytan's three core objectives. When was the last time you spoke confidently about Islam on a topic you hadn't actually studied?

- If the livestock in the simile has ears but only hears noise — what Quranic truth have you heard so many times that it's become noise? Where has familiarity replaced understanding?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Shaytan doesn't drag you to destruction — he leaves a trail of steps for you to follow, each one looking like nothing, and the person who follows inherited tradition instead of revealed truth is like livestock that hears the shepherd shout but understands nothing of what he says.**

---

### Closing Invitation

The next time you're about to make a small decision — what to consume, what to accept, what to repeat, what to normalize — pause for a moment and look down. Ask: **is this a footstep?** And if it is: **whose trail is it on?**

You were given *'aql*. You were given the capacity to bind what you see to what it means. You were given revelation that is clear — *bayyināt* — and a God who is *Shākir* and *'Alīm* — appreciative and fully aware of every effort you make to think, to examine, to choose consciously rather than by default.

Don't be the one who, when offered revelation, says: "I'll just follow what I found."

You were made for more than that.
