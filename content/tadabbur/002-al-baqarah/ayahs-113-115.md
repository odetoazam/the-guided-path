1. 2:113: وَهُمْ يَتْلُونَ ٱلْكِتَـٰبَ — "while they recite THE Book." Not "their books" (kutubahum). Singular: al-Kitāb. Despite having books from the same divine source, they deny each other. The oneness of the source makes the denial absurd.

3. 2:114: مَسَـٰجِدَ ٱللَّهِ — "mosques of ALLAH." Not "mosques of the Muslims." The possessive is divine, not communal. The masjid belongs to Allah, not to the community that built it.

# From "You're Nothing" to "Wherever You Turn"
## The Narrowness of Mutual Denial and the God Who Cannot Be Contained
### Al-Baqarah 2:113-115

---

[ayah:2:113] وَقَالَتِ ٱلْيَهُودُ لَيْسَتِ ٱلنَّصَـٰرَىٰ عَلَىٰ شَىْءٍ وَقَالَتِ ٱلنَّصَـٰرَىٰ لَيْسَتِ ٱلْيَهُودُ عَلَىٰ شَىْءٍ وَهُمْ يَتْلُونَ ٱلْكِتَـٰبَ ۗ كَذَٰلِكَ قَالَ ٱلَّذِينَ لَا يَعْلَمُونَ مِثْلَ قَوْلِهِمْ ۚ فَٱللَّهُ يَحْكُمُ بَيْنَهُمْ يَوْمَ ٱلْقِيَـٰمَةِ فِيمَا كَانُوا۟ فِيهِ يَخْتَلِفُونَ

[ayah:2:114] وَمَنْ أَظْلَمُ مِمَّن مَّنَعَ مَسَـٰجِدَ ٱللَّهِ أَن يُذْكَرَ فِيهَا ٱسْمُهُۥ وَسَعَىٰ فِى خَرَابِهَآ ۚ أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ مَا كَانَ لَهُمْ أَن يَدْخُلُوهَآ إِلَّا خَآئِفِينَ ۚ لَهُمْ فِى ٱلدُّنْيَا خِزْىٌ وَلَهُمْ فِى ٱلْـَٔاخِرَةِ عَذَابٌ عَظِيمٌ

[ayah:2:115] وَلِلَّهِ ٱلْمَشْرِقُ وَٱلْمَغْرِبُ ۚ فَأَيْنَمَا تُوَلُّوا۟ فَثَمَّ وَجْهُ ٱللَّهِ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ وَٰسِعٌ عَلِيمٌ

*Wa-qālati al-yahūdu laysati al-naṣārā 'alā shay'in, wa-qālati al-naṣārā laysati al-yahūdu 'alā shay' — wa-hum yatlūna al-kitāb. Kadhālika qāla alladhīna lā ya'lamūna mithla qawlihim. Fa-Allāhu yahkumu baynahum yawma al-qiyāmati fī-mā kānū fīhi yakhtalifūn. Wa-man aẓlamu mimman mana'a masājida Allāhi an yudhkara fīhā ismuhu wa-sa'ā fī kharābihā? Ulā'ika mā kāna lahum an yadkhulūhā illā khā'ifīn. Lahum fī al-dunyā khizyun wa-lahum fī al-ākhirati 'adhābun 'aẓīm. Wa-lillāhi al-mashriqu wal-maghrib. Fa-aynamā tuwallū fa-thamma wajhu Allāh. Inna Allāha Wāsi'un 'Alīm.*

**The Jews said, "The Christians are not upon anything," and the Christians said, "The Jews are not upon anything" — though they both recite the Scripture. Thus spoke those who have no knowledge, words similar to theirs. So Allah will judge between them on the Day of Resurrection concerning that over which they used to differ. And who is more unjust than one who prevents the mosques of Allah from His name being remembered in them, and strives toward their ruin? It was not for such people to enter them except in fear. For them in this world is disgrace, and for them in the Hereafter is a great punishment. And to Allah belongs the east and the west. So wherever you turn, there is the Face of Allah. Indeed, Allah is All-Encompassing, All-Knowing.**

## INTRODUCTION

Two siblings stand in the kitchen, each pointing at the other.

"He started it."
"No, she started it."

They use the same words. They mirror each other's posture. They are so alike in their accusation that a stranger walking in could not tell who was accusing whom. And the parent, watching from the doorway, does not need to determine who started it. The parent sees something neither child can see: **they are both doing the same thing while claiming the other one is wrong.**

This is what the Quran witnesses in seventh-century Medina. Two communities — the Jewish and Christian communities — each declaring the other worthless. Not just wrong on a particular point. Not just mistaken about a theological detail. **"Not upon anything."** The phrase is total dismissal. You are standing on nothing. You have nothing. You *are* nothing.

And the Quran's observation is devastating: **وَهُمْ يَتْلُونَ ٱلْكِتَـٰبَ** — *wa-hum yatlūna al-kitāb* — "while they recite the Scripture." The very Book that should unite them in humility before God is the backdrop against which they tear each other apart.

But the passage does not stop at social commentary. It escalates — from verbal denial to physical exclusion — and then explodes outward into one of the most expansive theological statements in the entire Quran. The communities say "you're nothing." The communities bar people from the houses of Allah. And then Allah says: the east and the west belong to Me. Wherever you turn, there is My Face.

Here is the arc we will follow: from the narrowest human impulse — total dismissal of the other — through the most violent form of religious gatekeeping — barring access to sacred space — to the widest divine reality — a God whose Face is present in every direction you could possibly turn.

## PART 1: THE LINGUISTIC JOURNEY

### Element 1: The Mirror — When the Same Sentence Refutes Itself

Read 2:113 slowly. Out loud if you can. And notice what happens structurally.

<!-- ayah:2:113 partial --> وَقَالَتِ ٱلْيَهُودُ لَيْسَتِ ٱلنَّصَـٰرَىٰ عَلَىٰ شَىْءٍ وَقَالَتِ ٱلنَّصَـٰرَىٰ لَيْسَتِ ٱلْيَهُودُ عَلَىٰ شَىْءٍ

The Jews said: "The Christians are not upon anything."
The Christians said: "The Jews are not upon anything."

The structure is identical. Every word is the same. Only the names are swapped. It is the same sentence, repeated with the subjects reversed — like two mirrors facing each other, each reflecting the other's reflection into infinity.

This is not just narration. This is **rhetorical proof**. The Quran does not need to argue that both claims are absurd. It simply places them side by side and lets the symmetry speak. When two people make identical claims of total dismissal against each other, using the same words, from the same posture — the argument refutes itself. The mirror is the argument.

And the phrase they both use — **لَيْسَتِ ... عَلَىٰ شَىْءٍ** (*laysati... 'alā shay'*) — is deliberately extreme. *Shay'* means "a thing" — the most minimal, vague, generic word in Arabic. They are not saying "they are wrong about the Trinity" or "they misunderstand the Torah." They are saying: the other group is **not even standing on a thing**. Not upon anything at all. Complete, total, absolute dismissal.

And then comes the observation that turns this from sad to ironic:

<!-- ayah:2:113 partial --> وَهُمْ يَتْلُونَ ٱلْكِتَـٰبَ

*Wa-hum yatlūna al-kitāb.*

"While they recite **the** Scripture."

Two things to notice. First, the verb *yatlūna* is in the imperfect tense — present, continuous, ongoing. Not *talū* (they recited, past tense). They **are** reciting. Right now. Actively. While they deny each other, they are simultaneously in the act of reading scripture.

Second — and this is the deeper point — the Quran says *al-kitāb* with the definite article. **The** Book. Singular. Not *kutubahum* (their books, plural). The Quran recognizes a singular scriptural source behind the diversity of their texts. They both draw from the same divine well — and they use what they drew from it to deny each other's existence.

And then the Quran does something that should make every Muslim uncomfortable:

<!-- ayah:2:113 partial --> كَذَٰلِكَ قَالَ ٱلَّذِينَ لَا يَعْلَمُونَ مِثْلَ قَوْلِهِمْ

*Kadhālika qāla alladhīna lā ya'lamūna mithla qawlihim.*

"Thus spoke **those who have no knowledge** — words similar to theirs."

Who are "those who have no knowledge"? The Quran does not name them. The scholars have offered different interpretations — some say the pre-Islamic Arabs, some say any ignorant person who mimics this behavior. But the deliberate vagueness is itself the message. This is a **pattern**, not a one-time historical event. Whoever speaks like this — whoever takes total dismissal of another community as their posture, whoever says "they're not upon anything" — is, in the Quran's framing, speaking the words of those who do not know.

The Quran is not just narrating what Jews and Christians said to each other in Medina. It is warning: **this is what ignorance sounds like.** And anyone — including Muslims — who reproduces this pattern of total dismissal is speaking *mithla qawlihim* — words similar to theirs.

[PAUSE]

### Element 2: مَسَـٰجِدَ ٱللَّهِ — Whose Building Is This?

The passage escalates. From words to action. From verbal denial to physical exclusion.

<!-- ayah:2:114 partial --> وَمَنْ أَظْلَمُ مِمَّن مَّنَعَ مَسَـٰجِدَ ٱللَّهِ أَن يُذْكَرَ فِيهَا ٱسْمُهُۥ وَسَعَىٰ فِى خَرَابِهَآ

*Wa-man aẓlamu mimman mana'a masājida Allāhi an yudhkara fīhā ismuhu wa-sa'ā fī kharābihā.*

"And who is more unjust than one who **prevents the mosques of Allah** from His name being remembered in them, and **strives toward their ruin**?"

The rhetorical structure — *wa-man aẓlamu mimman* ("who is more unjust than one who...") — is one of the strongest condemning structures in the Quran. It appears several times, always for the most severe crimes. The implied answer is: no one. There is no one more unjust than this.

But notice the possessive in **مَسَـٰجِدَ ٱللَّهِ** — *masājida Allāh* — the mosques **of Allah**. Not the mosques of the Muslims. Not the mosques of the community. Not the building that the congregation paid for. The possessive is divine.

This is not a minor grammatical point. It is a theological revolution.

Every masjid on earth — regardless of who built it, who funded it, who administers it — belongs to Allah. The building does not belong to the board of directors. It does not belong to the imam. It does not belong to the congregation. It does not belong to the ethnic community that established it. It belongs to the One whose name is supposed to be remembered in it.

And when someone prevents Allah's name from being remembered in **His own building** — whether through physical barring, or through policies that exclude, or through atmospheres that alienate, or through gatekeeping that decides who is "Muslim enough" to enter — they are committing what the Quran considers among the highest forms of injustice.

[PAUSE]

The verb *mana'a* — to prevent, to bar, to block — is Form I, simple action. One act of blocking. And the Quran pairs it with something worse: **وَسَعَىٰ فِى خَرَابِهَآ** — *wa-sa'ā fī kharābihā* — "and strives toward their ruin." *Sa'ā* means to walk purposefully, to strive, to work toward a goal. This is not accidental neglect. This is intentional pursuit of destruction. Someone is **walking toward** the ruin of the places where Allah's name is remembered.

And the word *kharāb* — ruin, desolation — sounds like what it means. The harsh *khā*, the rolling *rā*, the heavy *bā*. The word sounds like a building collapsing. Stones falling. Emptiness where there should be prayer.

The classical scholars debated the historical referent. Some said it refers to the Quraysh barring Muslims from Masjid al-Haram. Others said it refers to the Roman destruction of Bayt al-Maqdis in Jerusalem. Both readings are valid — and both enrich the meaning. Whether the crime is preventing pilgrimage to the Ka'bah or demolishing the Temple in Jerusalem, the principle is the same: no human being has the right to stand between God's creatures and God's house.

But the Quran uses the plural — *masājid*, mosques — and names no specific perpetrator. The principle is deliberately universal. It applies to every sacred space, in every era, against every form of exclusion.

[PAUSE]

### Element 3: فَأَيْنَمَا تُوَلُّوا۟ فَثَمَّ وَجْهُ ٱللَّهِ — The Sentence That Breaks Open the Walls

After the narrowness of mutual denial and the violence of physical exclusion, the Quran opens a door so wide that nothing can ever close it.

<!-- ayah:2:115 partial --> وَلِلَّهِ ٱلْمَشْرِقُ وَٱلْمَغْرِبُ فَأَيْنَمَا تُوَلُّوا۟ فَثَمَّ وَجْهُ ٱللَّهِ

*Wa-lillāhi al-mashriqu wal-maghrib. Fa-aynamā tuwallū fa-thamma wajhu Allāh.*

"To Allah belongs the east and the west. So wherever you turn, there is the Face of Allah."

Let's take this apart slowly, because every word is carrying weight.

**وَلِلَّهِ** (*wa-lillāhi*) — "And **to Allah belongs**." The lām of possession. Before naming what belongs to Him, the Quran establishes the ownership. Everything that follows is His.

**ٱلْمَشْرِقُ وَٱلْمَغْرِبُ** (*al-mashriqu wal-maghrib*) — "the east and the west." These are *maf'il* pattern nouns — nouns of place. *Mashriq* is the place of sunrise (from *sharaqa*, to rise). *Maghrib* is the place of sunset (from *gharaba*, to set). Between them lies every compass point, every direction, every corner of the earth. The east and the west is a merism — a figure of speech where two extremes stand for everything between them.

**فَأَيْنَمَا** (*fa-aynamā*) — "so **wherever**." The *fā* is consequential: because the east and west belong to Him, **therefore** wherever. The logic is: divine ownership of all directions leads to divine presence in all directions. *Aynamā* is a conditional of place — it places no restriction. Wherever. Any direction. Any location. Any place you could possibly stand.

**تُوَلُّوا۟** (*tuwallū*) — "you turn." Form II of the root *wāw-lām-yā*. Form II adds intensity: not just a casual glance, but a deliberate, full turning. You turn your face, your body, your orientation.

**فَثَمَّ** (*fa-thamma*) — "then **there**." *Thamma* is an adverb of distant place — "there," at that location. Wherever you turned, at that point — there.

**وَجْهُ ٱللَّهِ** (*wajhu Allāh*) — "the Face of Allah."

[PAUSE]

The word *wajh* returns. In 2:112, it was the human face: *man aslama wajhahu lillāh* — whoever submits his face to Allah. Now it is the divine Face: *fa-thamma wajhu Allāh* — there is the Face of Allah.

The human submits their face → and finds that the Face they submitted to is **everywhere**.

The classical scholars approach *wajhu Allāh* with care and with different lenses. The Athari tradition affirms the Face as a real divine attribute — Allah has a Face (*wajh*) in a manner that befits His majesty, without resemblance to created faces and without asking "how." Other scholars, including some early authorities, read *wajh* here as *qiblah* (direction) or *jihah* (the direction Allah is pleased with) — meaning: wherever you turn in prayer, Allah accepts that direction. This reading connects the ayah to the qiblah change: even if the direction of prayer shifts, every direction belongs to Him.

Both readings enrich the meaning. Whether the Face of Allah is His actual divine attribute present everywhere, or the direction of His pleasure accepting your prayer from any compass point — the effect on the listener is the same: **you cannot turn anywhere that He is not**.

The communities said: "You're nothing." The gatekeepers said: "You can't come in." But Allah said: "The east and the west are Mine. Wherever you turn — I am already there."

No one can lock you out of a building that is everywhere. No one can bar you from a presence that has no walls.

[PAUSE]

And the closing names: **إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ وَٰسِعٌ عَلِيمٌ** — *Inna Allāha Wāsi'un 'Alīm.*

*Wāsi'* — the All-Encompassing, the Vast. From the root *wāw-sīn-'ayn* (و-س-ع), meaning to be wide, to encompass, to contain. The fā'il pattern makes it an active attribute: He is actively encompassing. Not "He was wide once" but "He is, right now, in this moment, wider than anything you can conceive." His mercy is *wāsi'*. His knowledge is *wāsi'*. His presence is *wāsi'*.

*'Alīm* — the All-Knowing. The fa'īl intensive pattern. He does not merely know. Knowing is His permanent, essential nature.

The pairing is precise. *Wāsi'* answers the narrowness of the exclusion: you tried to make God's space small, but He is vast. *'Alīm* answers the ignorance behind the denial: you spoke from not knowing (*lā ya'lamūna* in 2:113), but He is the One who knows.

The passage began with people who didn't know (*lā ya'lamūna*). It ends with the One who knows everything (*'Alīm*). It began with narrowness — "you're nothing," "you can't come in." It ends with the widest thing imaginable — a Face in every direction, a God who is *Wāsi'*.

---

**Bridge:** The words have shown us a mirror that refutes itself, a building that belongs to Allah and not to any gatekeeper, and a Face that cannot be excluded from any direction. Now let's ask what these truths do to us.

## PART 2: THE THEMATIC DEPTHS

### Theme 1: The Disease of Total Dismissal — And Why Every Community Catches It

In the age of social media, there is a behavior so common it has become invisible. Someone encounters a person, a community, a movement, a school of thought — and within seconds, the verdict is passed: "They're not on anything."

"Oh, that group? They're not real Muslims."
"That scholar? He doesn't know anything."
"That community? They're not upon anything."

The phrase changes in each generation. The pattern does not.

What the Quran witnesses in 2:113 is not a uniquely Jewish-Christian phenomenon. It is a **human** phenomenon. It is what happens when group identity becomes the primary lens through which reality is evaluated. When "who you are" matters more than "what is true," then anyone who is not part of your "who" is automatically not part of the truth.

And the Quran's observation — *wa-hum yatlūna al-kitāb* — cuts to the root of the absurdity. They are **reciting** the Scripture. The very text that should teach them humility before God and generosity toward His other servants is the backdrop against which they deny each other's worth. The Book that says "do not transgress" is in their mouths while they transgress.

This maps onto the contemporary Muslim world with painful precision. Communities that recite the same Quran declare each other invalid. Schools of thought that share the same foundational texts produce fatāwā invalidating each other. Individuals who pray the same five prayers in the same direction deny each other's Islam.

And the Quran's verdict is not that one side is right and the other is wrong. The verdict is: **كَذَٰلِكَ قَالَ ٱلَّذِينَ لَا يَعْلَمُونَ** — this is what those who don't know sound like. The behavior itself — total dismissal — is the mark of ignorance, regardless of which side is doing it.

[PAUSE]

The practical question: when you hear yourself saying — even in your head — "they're not on anything," stop. Ask: am I making a specific, evidenced critique of a specific claim? Or am I doing what this ayah describes — total dismissal of an entire community's standing before God?

The first is scholarship. The second is the disease of 2:113. And the Quran says: Allah will judge between them on the Day of Resurrection. Not you.

[PAUSE — major pause]

### Theme 2: The God Who Is Wider Than Your Building

There is a quiet revolution happening in 2:115 that most people read too quickly to notice.

For most of human history — and still today — people have built sacred spaces and then fought over who gets to enter them. Temples, churches, mosques, synagogues: built to honor God, then used to control access to God. The gatekeeping instinct is ancient. "This is our space. This is where we meet God. If you want to meet God, you meet Him on our terms, in our building, through our gate."

And then the Quran says: **فَأَيْنَمَا تُوَلُّوا۟ فَثَمَّ وَجْهُ ٱللَّهِ**.

Wherever you turn, there is the Face of Allah.

This sentence does not abolish the masjid. The masjid remains *masājid Allāh* — the places where His name should be remembered, and barring access to them is the highest form of injustice. The masjid matters. Sacred space matters.

But the sentence tells you: **God is not confined to the building.** The building is for you. You need a place to gather, a direction to face, walls that hold your community together. But God — the *Wāsi'*, the All-Encompassing — is not contained by walls. You can lock someone out of a building. You cannot lock them out of the east and the west.

This is radical comfort for anyone who has ever been excluded from a sacred space. For the woman told she is not welcome. For the convert who feels like an outsider. For the person whose race, class, ethnicity, language, or appearance made them feel unwelcome in the house of God. For the Muslim barred from Masjid al-Haram by political regimes. For every person who was ever told "this is not your space."

The Quran says: the east and the west are His. Wherever you turn, His Face is there. No gatekeeper has jurisdiction over the compass.

[PAUSE]

And notice the closing pair of names: *Wāsi'un 'Alīm*. He is Encompassing — wider than any building, wider than any institution, wider than any community's imagination of where He is allowed to be. And He is All-Knowing — He knows the sincere heart that was turned away from the door, and He knows the gatekeeper who thought the building belonged to them.

The width of God is the answer to the narrowness of people. The narrower the human gatekeeping, the more the Quran expands the divine scope. You say "you're nothing"? His mercy encompasses everything. You bar the door? His face is in every direction.

The invitation is not to abandon the masjid. The invitation is to stop thinking God is trapped inside it. Build the masjid. Maintain the masjid. Pray in the masjid. But know — with every fiber of your faith — that the God you worship there is *Wāsi'*. He is wider than your building, wider than your community, wider than your comprehension.

And when you walk out the door of the masjid, He is still there. When you drive to work, He is still there. When you stand in a field with no building in sight and no qiblah marker and nothing but the horizon — you turn, and *fa-thamma wajhu Allāh*.

He is already there.

[PAUSE — major pause]

## CLOSING SYNTHESIS

### The Architecture

These three ayahs trace a single arc from maximum human contraction to maximum divine expansion.

**2:113 — VERBAL NARROWNESS:** "You're nothing." "No, you're nothing." Each group denies the other's standing. The narrowest possible inter-human posture: total invalidation.

**2:114 — PHYSICAL NARROWNESS:** The crime escalates from words to action. Barring people from the houses of Allah. Striving toward the ruin of sacred space. The ultimate form of religious gatekeeping.

**2:115 — DIVINE EXPANSION:** "To Allah belongs the east and the west." The walls break open. The gatekeepers are irrelevant. Wherever you turn — in any direction, any geography, any building or no building — there is the Face of Allah.

The arc moves from **human narrowness** → **human violence** → **divine width**.

And the word *wajh* (face) threads through the passage like a rope connecting 2:112 to 2:115. There, the human submitted their face. Here, the divine Face is found everywhere. The human who orients their face toward Allah discovers that the Face of Allah was already oriented toward them — from every direction, at every moment, in every place.

### Questions to Carry

- When you look at another Muslim community — a different masjid, a different school of thought, a different cultural expression of Islam — do you hear the echo of *laysati... 'alā shay'*?

- Is there a sacred space where you have been a gatekeeper — deciding who belongs and who doesn't — when the building was never yours?

- When you feel locked out — from a community, a space, a sense of belonging — do you remember that the east and the west belong to Him, and wherever you turn, His Face is there?

- What would it change in your prayer if you carried the awareness that *fa-thamma wajhu Allāh* — before you even set down the prayer mat, His Face was already in that direction?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**When humans narrow God's mercy to their tribe and bar His creatures from His houses, Allah answers by declaring that every direction belongs to Him — and wherever you turn, His Face is already there, because He is wider than any building, any gate, or any human imagination.**

### The Closing Invitation

The passage invites two simultaneous postures. The first is vigilance against the narrowing impulse — the instinct to dismiss, to gate-keep, to say "you're not on anything." Watch for it. It is the speech of those who do not know, and the Quran placed it in this passage so you would recognize it when it came from your own mouth.

The second is confidence in the width of God. You cannot be locked out of His presence. No gatekeeper on earth has jurisdiction over His Face. Build the masjid. Protect the masjid. But know that the God you seek there is *Wāsi'* — wider than the building, wider than the community, wider than the earth itself.

You turn. And He is there.

That has always been enough.

### Du'a

*Allāhumma, You are Wāsi' — wider than our buildings, our communities, and our comprehension. Protect us from the narrowness that dismisses Your other servants and the arrogance that bars Your creatures from Your houses. When we turn — in any direction, in any moment, in any place — let us find Your Face, and let that finding be enough to shatter every wall we have built around Your mercy. You are 'Alīm — You know who was locked out and who held the key, and You know which of us spoke from knowledge and which of us spoke the words of those who did not know. Forgive us for every time we were the gatekeeper. And welcome us — all of us — into the width of Your presence.*
