passage_context: "These two ayahs arrive within the larger family law section of Al-Baqarah (2:221-242), addressing a specific and surprisingly common scenario: what happens when a marriage contract has been signed but the marriage was never consummated — and then a divorce occurs? The nikah has been performed, the contract is real, but the couple never lived together as husband and wife. No intimacy. No shared life. Just a contract, and now its dissolution. 2:236 addresses the case where no mahr was specified, and 2:237 addresses the case where a mahr WAS specified. In both cases, the Quran goes beyond mere legal ruling to establish something extraordinary: the principle that even when a contract is broken at its earliest stage, the breaking must be done with generosity, not bare minimum compliance. 2:236 introduces the concept of mut'ah (a consolation gift) scaled to the man's means — the wealthy gives according to his wealth, the poor according to his poverty — and calls this a DUTY upon the muḥsinīn (the doers of good/excellence). 2:237 then introduces an even more radical concept: the right of EITHER party to waive their financial entitlement — and declares that waiving is CLOSER TO TAQWA than insisting on what you're owed. The passage culminates in one of the most emotionally piercing imperatives in the entire divorce legislation: وَلَا تَنسَوُا۟ ٱلْفَضْلَ بَيْنَكُمْ — 'and do not forget GRACIOUSNESS between you.' The word فَضْل means surplus, excess, grace — going BEYOND what is required. In the middle of legislating the bare minimum (half the mahr), Allah asks for the surplus. In the middle of dissolving a contract, He asks for grace."
scholarly_note: "The scholars are unanimous that مَا لَمْ تَمَسُّوهُنَّ (you have not touched them) refers to sexual intercourse. The word مَسّ (touch) is used as a kinaya (euphemism) for consummation throughout the Quran, as noted by Ibn Abbas and al-Tabari. The mut'ah (consolation gift) in 2:236 is obligatory according to the Hanafi and Maliki schools for women divorced before consummation without a specified mahr. The Shafi'i school considers it recommended (mustaḥabb). The phrase عَلَى ٱلْمُوسِعِ قَدَرُهُۥ وَعَلَى ٱلْمُقْتِرِ قَدَرُهُۥ establishes that the mut'ah is scaled to means — the مُوسِع (one who has amplitude/wealth, Form IV active participle of وسع) gives according to his amplitude, and the مُقْتِر (one who is restricted in means, Form VIII active participle of قتر) gives according to his restriction. In 2:237, the phrase أَوْ يَعْفُوَا۟ ٱلَّذِى بِيَدِهِۦ عُقْدَةُ ٱلنِّكَاحِ (or the one in whose hand is the knot of marriage foregoes it) is debated among scholars. Ali ibn Abi Talib and Ibn Abbas held that 'the one in whose hand is the knot' is the HUSBAND — and his 'waiving' means paying the FULL mahr instead of just half, as an act of generosity. Other scholars, including Mujahid, understood it as the woman's GUARDIAN (wali), who may waive the wife's share on her behalf. The majority position favors the first reading: the husband can choose to pay the full mahr even though he only owes half. The phrase وَأَن تَعْفُوٓا۟ أَقْرَبُ لِلتَّقْوَىٰ (and to waive is closer to taqwa) — the scholars note that the 2MP (masculine plural) form تَعْفُوا۟ addresses BOTH parties: both the wife waiving her half, and the husband paying the full amount. Either act of generosity is 'closer to taqwa.' The closing imperative وَلَا تَنسَوُا۟ ٱلْفَضْلَ بَيْنَكُمْ is understood by al-Qurtubi and others as a command to maintain GRACE and GENEROSITY even during the dissolution of a relationship. الفَضْل here means surplus goodness — going beyond the legal minimum."

ABSENCE FLAGS:
1. 2:236: مَا لَمْ تَمَسُّوهُنَّ أَوْ تَفْرِضُوا۟ لَهُنَّ فَرِيضَةً — "you have not touched them NOR specified for them an obligation." Two absences — no consummation AND no mahr. The marriage exists only on paper. Neither the physical bond nor the financial bond has been established. Yet even in this most skeletal of marriages, Allah STILL requires compensation. The ABSENCE of both intimacy and mahr does not produce an absence of obligation.
2. 2:237: The phrase إِلَّآ أَن يَعْفُونَ — "unless THEY (feminine) waive." Who are the يَعْفُونَ? The 3FP form could refer to the women themselves OR (according to some readings) to the kin-group that acts on their behalf. The SUBJECT is deliberately broad — opening the door for the woman's own agency in waiving what she is owed.
3. 2:237: وَأَن تَعْفُوٓا۟ أَقْرَبُ لِلتَّقْوَىٰ — "and to waive is CLOSER to taqwa." The ABSENCE of a definitive statement ("waiving IS taqwa") is theologically precise. Not waiving is not sin. It is simply further from taqwa. The gap between "closer" and "is" is the space of human choice. Allah invites but does not compel.
4. 2:237 closes with بَصِيرٌ (Seeing). NOT غَفُورٌ (Forgiving). After an ayah that talks about waiving rights and generosity, you might expect forgiveness. Instead: SEEING. Allah SEES whether you chose the minimum or the surplus. The divine name is not about mercy here — it's about witness. Your graciousness (or lack of it) is observed.

ADDRESS FLAGS:
- 2:236-237: Entirely 2MP — addressed to the MEN throughout. The women are spoken about in 3FP. The legislation is placed on the men's shoulders — the compensation, the provision, the obligation to give even when nothing is owed.
- 2:237: وَلَا تَنسَوُا۟ — 2MP. "Do not YOU forget." But the فَضْل is بَيْنَكُمْ — "between YOU (plural)." The address is to both parties (not just the men), but the grammatical form is masculine plural — inclusive of everyone in the transaction.

# Do Not Forget Graciousness Between You
## When Allah Turned a Broken Contract into an Invitation to Become Better Than Fair
### Al-Baqarah 2:236-237

---

[ayah:2:236] لَا جُنَاحَ عَلَيْكُمْ إِن طَلَّقْتُمُ ٱلنِّسَآءَ مَا لَمْ تَمَسُّوهُنَّ أَوْ تَفْرِضُوا۟ لَهُنَّ فَرِيضَةً ۚ وَمَتِّعُوهُنَّ عَلَى ٱلْمُوسِعِ قَدَرُهُۥ وَعَلَى ٱلْمُقْتِرِ قَدَرُهُۥ مَتَـٰعًۢا بِٱلْمَعْرُوفِ ۖ حَقًّا عَلَى ٱلْمُحْسِنِينَ

[ayah:2:237] وَإِن طَلَّقْتُمُوهُنَّ مِن قَبْلِ أَن تَمَسُّوهُنَّ وَقَدْ فَرَضْتُمْ لَهُنَّ فَرِيضَةً فَنِصْفُ مَا فَرَضْتُمْ إِلَّآ أَن يَعْفُونَ أَوْ يَعْفُوَا۟ ٱلَّذِى بِيَدِهِۦ عُقْدَةُ ٱلنِّكَاحِ ۚ وَأَن تَعْفُوٓا۟ أَقْرَبُ لِلتَّقْوَىٰ ۚ وَلَا تَنسَوُا۟ ٱلْفَضْلَ بَيْنَكُمْ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ بِمَا تَعْمَلُونَ بَصِيرٌ

*Lā junāḥa 'alaykum in ṭallaqtumu-n-nisā'a mā lam tamassūhunna aw tafriḍū lahunna farīḍah. Wa matti'ūhunna 'alal-mūsi'i qadaruhu wa 'alal-muqtiri qadaruhu matā'an bil-ma'rūf. Ḥaqqan 'alal-muḥsinīn. Wa in ṭallaqtumūhunna min qabli an tamassūhunna wa qad faraḍtum lahunna farīḍatan fa-niṣfu mā faraḍtum illā an ya'fūna aw ya'fuwa-lladhī biyadihi 'uqdatu-n-nikāḥ. Wa an ta'fū aqrabu lit-taqwā. Wa lā tansawu-l-faḍla baynakum. Innallāha bimā ta'malūna baṣīr.*

**"There is no blame upon you if you divorce women you have not touched nor specified for them an obligation. But give them a gift of compensation — the wealthy according to his capability and the poor according to his capability — a provision according to what is acceptable, a duty upon the doers of good.**

**And if you divorce them before you have touched them and you have already specified for them an obligation, then half of what you specified — unless they forego the right, or the one in whose hand is the marriage contract foregoes it. And to forego it is nearer to righteousness. And do not forget graciousness between you. Indeed, Allah is Seeing of what you do."**

## Rebuilding the World

Imagine a marriage that never really began.

The contract was signed. The families celebrated. The mahr may or may not have been agreed upon. But before the couple ever lived together — before they shared a home, before the first conversation as husband and wife over morning tea, before intimacy, before the routines and arguments and inside jokes that make a marriage a marriage — it ended.

Maybe they realized they were incompatible. Maybe the families clashed. Maybe circumstances changed. Whatever the reason, the marriage exists only as a piece of paper. No physical bond was formed. No shared life was built.

In most legal systems, ancient and modern, this kind of dissolution is treated as a technicality. The contract is voided. The paperwork is handled. Everyone goes home. Bare minimum.

The Quran does something radically different.

It takes this bare-minimum scenario — a marriage that barely existed — and turns it into a master class in what it means to be a good human being. Not just a law-abiding one. Not just a fair one. A GOOD one. An excellent one. A *muḥsin*.

And it will do this through one of the most emotionally piercing commands in the entire Quran — a sentence that has nothing to do with mahr or contracts or legal technicalities, and everything to do with who you are when nobody owes you anything and you still choose to give.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### Element 1: The Two Men and Their Measures

In 2:236, after establishing that no blame falls on a man who divorces before consummation, Allah issues a command:

**وَمَتِّعُوهُنَّ** — *wa matti'ūhunna* — "and give them a consolation gift."

The verb is **مَتَّعَ** — Form II of م-ت-ع. Form I (*mata'a*) means to provide, to give enjoyment. Form II (*matta'a*) INTENSIFIES it: give them thorough, generous, sustained provision. This isn't a token gesture. The doubled tā' in the Form II pattern makes the giving HEAVIER, more deliberate, more complete.

But then comes the extraordinary part. Allah doesn't just say "give generously." He creates two portraits — two men standing side by side, each measured by a different scale:

**عَلَى ٱلْمُوسِعِ قَدَرُهُۥ وَعَلَى ٱلْمُقْتِرِ قَدَرُهُۥ**

*'Alal-mūsi'i qadaruhu wa 'alal-muqtiri qadaruhu*

"Upon the one of amplitude, his measure. And upon the one of restriction, his measure."

Two active participles. Two Form IV patterns. Two completely different images.

**ٱلْمُوسِعِ** — *al-mūsi'* — from the root و-س-ع, spaciousness. This is a Form IV active participle: one who CAUSES spaciousness. Not just "the rich man." The one who HAS amplitude — whose wealth creates room, whose means are expansive. Say the word: *mūsi'*. The wāw opens, the sīn flows, the 'ayn widens. The word SOUNDS like what it means. Spaciousness sounds spacious.

**ٱلْمُقْتِرِ** — *al-muqtir* — from the root ق-ت-ر, restriction, constriction. This is a Form VIII active participle: one who is straitened IN HIMSELF — the restriction is reflexive, internal. Not someone whose wealth was taken away. Someone whose means are tight from within. Say it: *muqtir*. The qāf strikes, the tā' clips, the rā' cuts short. The word SOUNDS like what it means. Restriction sounds tight.

[PAUSE]

And here is what's remarkable: **both men owe the consolation gift.** Not just the wealthy one. The straitened one too. Allah doesn't say, "If you're poor, you're exempt." He says: your measure is your measure. The wealthy man gives according to his spaciousness. The poor man gives according to his restriction. Both give. Both compensate. The scale changes. The obligation doesn't.

The word **قَدَرُهُ** — *qadaruhu* — "his measure" — appears twice, once for each man. Same word. Same concept. Different calibrations. Your *qadar* is what you can actually bear — your capacity, your proportion, your appropriate scale. The wealthy man's generosity and the poor man's generosity are equally valid, equally honored, equally required — as long as each gives according to his OWN measure, not someone else's.

This is Islamic economics in a single phrase: **proportional obligation, not uniform obligation.**

[PAUSE]

And then the hammer falls. This entire obligation — the consolation gift, the scaled provision — is declared:

**حَقًّا عَلَى ٱلْمُحْسِنِينَ**

*Ḥaqqan 'alal-muḥsinīn*

"A binding truth upon the doers of excellence."

Not *'alal-mu'minīn* — "upon the believers." Not *'alal-muslimīn* — "upon the Muslims." Upon the **مُحْسِنِينَ** — *al-muḥsinīn* — "the doers of *iḥsān*."

This word is a Form IV active participle from حسن — beauty, goodness, excellence. *Muḥsin* is not someone who merely avoids harm. It is someone who **actively produces beauty**. Someone who makes things better than they found them. Someone who doesn't just meet the minimum — they exceed it.

Allah is saying: this obligation — to compensate a woman even when you barely owe her anything — is the hallmark of the **muḥsin**. If you want to know whether you are among the doers of excellence, here is your test: not how you behave when you're obligated, but how you behave when you could get away with the bare minimum and choose not to.

[PAUSE]

### Element 2: The Three Appearances of *'Afā* — The Verb That Means "To Let Go of What You're Owed"

Now move to 2:237. The scenario shifts: a mahr WAS specified, but the marriage was still not consummated. The ruling: **فَنِصْفُ مَا فَرَضْتُمْ** — "then half of what you specified." She gets half the mahr. That's the law.

But then Allah opens a door.

The verb **عَفَا** — *'afā* — appears THREE times in rapid succession. From the root ع-ف-و, which carries the meaning of pardoning, waiving, releasing, letting go. But here is the crucial nuance: *'afā* doesn't mean charity to someone who doesn't deserve it. It means **releasing a claim you legitimately hold**. You were OWED this. It is YOUR RIGHT. And you are choosing — freely, deliberately — to let it go.

**First appearance:** إِلَّآ أَن **يَعْفُونَ** — *ya'fūna* — "unless THEY (the women) waive." The wife has a right to half the mahr. She can choose to waive it. The 3FP form places the agency squarely with the women — they are not being waived FOR, they are waiving. Their dignity is in the choice.

**Second appearance:** أَوْ **يَعْفُوَا۟** ٱلَّذِى بِيَدِهِۦ عُقْدَةُ ٱلنِّكَاحِ — "or HE waives — the one in whose hand is the knot of marriage." The scholars differed on who this person is. Ali ibn Abi Talib and Ibn Abbas said it is the HUSBAND — and his "waiving" means going BEYOND the half and paying the FULL mahr, even though he only owes half. His act of grace is to give MORE than what the law requires.

**Third appearance:** وَأَن **تَعْفُوٓا۟** أَقْرَبُ لِلتَّقْوَىٰ — "and that YOU (all) waive is closer to taqwa." Now the address shifts to 2MP — addressing EVERYONE in the transaction. Both parties. The wife who could waive her half. The husband who could pay the full amount. Either act of generosity — either direction of grace — draws you CLOSER to taqwa.

[PAUSE]

Three times the same verb. Three different subjects. Three different directions of generosity. The wife lets go of what she's owed. The husband gives more than what he owes. And ANYONE in the transaction who chooses grace over strict entitlement moves closer to God-consciousness.

And notice the precision of **أَقْرَبُ لِلتَّقْوَىٰ** — *aqrabu lit-taqwā* — "**CLOSER** to taqwa." Not "this IS taqwa." Not "waiving IS righteousness." CLOSER. The *af'al* comparative form — *aqrab* — places waiving on a spectrum. Insisting on your legal right is not sin. It is not condemned. It is simply... further from the destination. Waiving draws you NEARER.

The gap between "closer" and "is" — between the comparative and the absolute — is the space of human freedom. Allah invites but does not compel. He shows you where the destination is. He tells you which path is shorter. He does not drag you down it.

[PAUSE]

---

### Element 3: The Sentence That Changes Everything

And then, after the legal ruling, after the three appearances of waiving, after the invitation toward taqwa — Allah drops a sentence that has nothing to do with mahr, nothing to do with contracts, nothing to do with legal obligations at all:

**وَلَا تَنسَوُا۟ ٱلْفَضْلَ بَيْنَكُمْ**

*Wa lā tansawu-l-faḍla baynakum*

"And do not forget **graciousness** between you."

The verb is **تَنسَوُا۟** — from نسي — to forget. Not to abandon. Not to reject. To FORGET. Listen to it: *tansawū*. The nūn-sīn-wāw fades, slips away, dissolves. Forgetting sounds like something slipping through your fingers without noticing.

This is not a prohibition against malice. It is a prohibition against **negligence**. Allah isn't saying: "Don't deliberately withhold grace." He's saying: "Don't let it SLIP YOUR MIND." In the heat of a breakup — in the anger, the hurt, the financial calculations, the pride, the lawyers — grace is the first casualty. Not because anyone decided to be ungracious. Because everyone FORGOT.

And the word being protected from forgetting is **ٱلْفَضْلَ** — *al-faḍl*. This is not *al-ḥaqq* (the right, what is owed). It is not *al-'adl* (justice, what is fair). It is *al-faḍl* — the SURPLUS. The EXTRA. The overflow beyond what is required.

*Faḍl* is what you give when you've already met your obligation and you still have goodness left. It is the tip on top of the bill. The handwritten note inside the severance package. The kind word after the divorce papers are signed. The thing nobody can demand from you because it exceeds the contract — but its absence impoverishes both parties.

Allah doesn't say: "Don't forget JUSTICE between you." Justice is the floor. It's already been legislated — half the mahr, the consolation gift, the proportional compensation. Justice has been handled.

What Allah says is: **don't forget the ceiling.** Don't forget that you were once connected to this person. Don't forget that a contract existed between you, even if it barely began. Don't forget that how you END things reveals more about who you are than how you began them.

[PAUSE]

Now let's ask what this reveals about who Allah is calling us to become — not just in divorce court, but everywhere a relationship ends.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Measure of a Person Is What They Give When They Don't Have To

You've probably had this experience. You return a product to a store. The policy says: refund within 30 days, receipt required. You have the receipt. It's within 30 days. The store processes the refund. Everything is correct. Everything is fair.

But you walk away feeling nothing. The transaction was accurate. It wasn't generous.

Now imagine a different version. You return the product. The cashier says, "I'm sorry this didn't work out for you. Let me process your refund — and here, take this coupon for next time. We appreciate your business." Same refund. Same legal outcome. But the surplus — the kind word, the extra gesture — transforms the transaction from bare compliance into something you actually remember.

That difference — between compliance and grace — is the difference between *'adl* and *faḍl*. Between justice and graciousness. Between what the law requires and what the heart chooses.

The Quran has just spent nine ayahs legislating the MINIMUM. Two divorces, then keep with ma'rūf or release with iḥsān. The iddah. The breastfeeding. The widow's waiting period. The mahr obligations. These are the legal floors — the minimum standard below which you may not fall.

But in 2:236-237, Allah reveals what He ACTUALLY wants. Not the floor. **The ceiling.**

He wants the *muḥsin* — the one who actively produces excellence. He wants the person who gives the consolation gift even when no mahr was specified. He wants the husband who pays the full amount when he only owes half. He wants the wife who waives her claim to bring peace. He wants the *faḍl* — the surplus goodness that no contract can require and no court can enforce.

[PAUSE]

Think about what this means for how we navigate every ending — not just marital ones. A business partnership dissolves. A friendship fades. An employment contract terminates. In every case, the law provides a minimum. Severance pay. The terms of the agreement. The bare requirements.

But the Quran asks: after the minimum is met, what do you choose to give?

Do you give the bare half? Or do you give the full amount? Do you walk away in silence? Or do you walk away with a kind word?

The answer reveals nothing about the law. It reveals everything about you.

**حَقًّا عَلَى ٱلْمُحْسِنِينَ** — "a binding truth upon the doers of excellence."

You want to know if you're a *muḥsin*? Here's your diagnostic: what do you do when the contract says you owe nothing, and you give something anyway?

[PAUSE]

### Theme 2: The Knot, the Letting Go, and the Taqwa Between Them

There is a physical image buried in 2:237 that most people read past.

**عُقْدَةُ ٱلنِّكَاحِ** — *'uqdatu-n-nikāḥ* — "the knot of marriage."

Not the contract. Not the agreement. The KNOT. From عقد — to tie. *'Uqdah* is the result of the tying — the physical knot itself. Say it: *'uq-dah*. The 'ayn catches your throat, the qāf strikes hard, the dāl closes. It sounds like something pulled tight.

A marriage, in the Quranic image, is not a piece of paper. It is a knot. Two separate ropes brought together and twisted around each other until they hold. And a divorce — a *ṭalāq* — is the untying. The release. The loosening of what was once pulled taut.

Now look at what Allah does with this image. The person "in whose hand is the knot" — the one who holds the tied end — has the power to let go. Not just of the knot. Of the MONEY. Of the claim. Of what they're owed.

And the letting go — the *'afw* — is not described as loss. It is described as proximity to **taqwa**.

*Taqwa* comes from the root و-ق-ي — to protect, to shield. It is not fear in the trembling sense. It is the AWARENESS that drives you to protect yourself from displeasing Allah. It is the shield you carry, not against the world, but against your own worst impulses.

When you let go of what you're owed — when you waive the half, or give the full — you are not losing money. You are gaining proximity to taqwa. You are moving CLOSER to the state of awareness that protects your relationship with Allah.

The Quran is saying: the knot can be untied two ways. It can be untied with each party pulling their share of rope toward themselves — each insisting on what they're owed, each counting every last coin. This is legal. This is fair. This is *'adl*.

Or it can be untied with each party offering their share of rope to the other — releasing the claim, waiving the right, choosing grace over arithmetic. This is *faḍl*. And it is **أَقْرَبُ لِلتَّقْوَىٰ** — closer to taqwa.

[PAUSE]

And then the final command — the one that holds the whole passage together:

**وَلَا تَنسَوُا۟ ٱلْفَضْلَ بَيْنَكُمْ**

Don't forget the graciousness. Between you.

*Baynakum* — between you. Not "toward her" or "toward him." Between you BOTH. The graciousness is not one-directional. It flows between both parties. It is the quality of the SPACE between two people — even two people who are coming apart.

You can fill the space between you with lawyers and spreadsheets and righteous anger about what you're owed. Or you can fill it with *faḍl* — the surplus kindness that remembers you were once connected, that honors what the contract was supposed to build even as it dissolves.

The Quran has legislated the law. Now it legislates the spirit. And the spirit is: **don't let the ending be smaller than what it's ending.**

[PAUSE]

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture

See the two ayahs as a paired structure:

**2:236 — No mahr specified, no consummation:**
- Legal ruling: No blame in divorcing.
- Obligation: Consolation gift (mut'ah).
- Scale: Proportional to means (mūsi' and muqtir).
- Standard: *Bil-ma'rūf* — according to what is acceptable.
- Identity: A duty upon the *muḥsinīn* — the doers of excellence.

**2:237 — Mahr specified, no consummation:**
- Legal ruling: Half of what was specified.
- Exception: Unless either party waives.
- Invitation: Waiving is closer to taqwa.
- Imperative: Don't forget graciousness between you.
- Witness: Allah is Seeing.

The movement across the two ayahs is: **minimum → proportion → grace → taqwa → the space between you.**

Each step goes FURTHER than the last. The first ayah establishes the legal floor. The second ayah reveals the spiritual ceiling. And the sentence that bridges them — *wa lā tansawu-l-faḍla baynakum* — is the Quran's single most powerful statement about how to end things.

Notice the closing divine name: **بَصِيرٌ** — *Baṣīr* — All-Seeing. The same name that closed 2:233 (the custody ayah). The pattern: when the Quran legislates actions between people — financial obligations, custody, compensation — it closes with the God who SEES. Not the God who hears your excuses. The God who sees what you actually did. What you actually gave. What you actually held back.

He sees whether you gave the bare half or the generous whole. He sees whether you waived with grace or insisted with spite. He sees the quality of the space between you.

**بَصِيرٌ.**

[PAUSE]

### Questions to Carry

- Think of a relationship — professional, personal, familial — that ended. Did you give the minimum, or did you give the *faḍl*? What would have changed if you'd chosen the other?

- When you negotiate — a salary, a contract, a settlement — do you calculate what you're OWED, or do you also calculate what GRACE looks like?

- In the small endings of everyday life — the last day at a job, the final conversation with someone moving away, the quiet dissolution of a friendship — are you a *muḥsin*? Do you actively produce beauty even in the closing act?

- What is the *faḍl* you have forgotten? What surplus kindness have you let slip from the space between you and someone you were once connected to?

---

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Even when a marriage barely existed and the law only requires half, Allah asks for the surplus — not as obligation but as invitation — because the measure of a person is not what they give when they must, but what they give when they don't have to.**

---

### The Closing Invitation

The next time something ends — a contract, a conversation, a connection — pause before you calculate what you're owed. Ask instead: what would the *muḥsin* give? What would bring me CLOSER to taqwa? What is the *faḍl* — the surplus goodness — that this moment needs?

The law sets the floor. Allah invites you to the ceiling.

And between the floor and the ceiling is the space that reveals who you actually are.

**وَلَا تَنسَوُا۟ ٱلْفَضْلَ بَيْنَكُمْ.**

Don't forget the graciousness between you.

### Du'a

*Allāhumma, make us among the muḥsinīn — those who produce beauty and excellence even when the minimum would suffice. When contracts break and connections dissolve, grant us the grace to give more than we owe and to waive more than we're owed. Protect us from the negligence that forgets the faḍl — the surplus kindness that lives in the space between us and those we've parted from. And remind us that You are Baṣīr — You see every coin withheld and every kindness offered — and that the closer path to You is always the one paved with what we didn't have to give. Āmīn.*
