## When the Quran Declared Its Credo, Named Its Color, and Sealed the Ibrahim Section

## INTRODUCTION

Every community in Medina was recruiting.

The Jewish community said: join us. Become a Jew. That is the path to guidance. The Christian community said: join us. Become a Christian. That is how you will be saved. Each group was certain that their label — their denominational identity, their institutional membership — was the ticket to divine favor.

And the young Muslim community, still finding its footing in a city that was not yet theirs, was hearing both pitches.

The Quran's response to this dual recruitment campaign is not: "No — become Muslims instead." That would simply be a third recruitment pitch. Instead, the Quran does something far more radical. It reaches past all three labels — past "Jewish," past "Christian," past even "Muslim" as a denominational category — and names a different kind of identity entirely.

**مِلَّةَ إِبْرَٰهِـۧمَ حَنِيفًا** — the way of Ibrahim, *ḥanīfan* — the one who inclines.

Not a label. A direction. Not a membership card. A posture of the soul.

And then comes the credo — the most comprehensive statement of faith in the Quran: we believe in everything revealed to everyone. Ibrahim, Isma'il, Ishaq, Ya'qub, the Tribes, Musa, 'Isa, and all the prophets. **لَا نُفَرِّقُ بَيْنَ أَحَدٍ مِّنْهُمْ** — we make no distinction between any of them.

And then — a word that appears nowhere else in the Quran quite like this: **صِبْغَةَ ٱللَّهِ** — the *dye* of Allah. The coloring of God. Your faith is not a badge you pin on. It is a dye that saturates your entire being. And who dyes better than Allah?

## PART 1: THE LINGUISTIC JOURNEY

### Element 1: حَنِيفًا — The One Who Leans

The counter-identity that the Quran offers in response to "Be Jewish or Christian" is a single word:

<!-- ayah:2:135 partial --> مِلَّةَ إِبْرَٰهِـۧمَ حَنِيفًا

*Millata Ibrāhīma ḥanīfā.*

"The way of Ibrahim — *ḥanīfan*."

*Ḥanīf* is from the root *ḥā-nūn-fā* (ح-ن-ف), which carries the physical meaning of **inclining**, **leaning**, **turning from one thing toward another**. In pre-Islamic Arabia, the word was used for individuals who rejected the idolatry of their society and sought the pure monotheism they believed Ibrahim had practiced. They were called the *ḥunafā'* — the ones who leaned away from what everyone else was doing toward something truer.

The *fa'īl* pattern of *ḥanīf* makes this a permanent attribute, not a momentary action. Ibrahim is not one who *happened* to incline on one occasion. Inclining IS his nature. He is permanently, constitutionally, essentially a being that leans — away from falsehood and toward truth. The inclination is not an act he performs. It is what he is.

And notice: the Quran does not say what Ibrahim inclines *toward*. Just *ḥanīfan*. An incliner. The object of the inclination is absent — and the absence is the point. When you turn away from everything false, you do not need to name the destination. There is only one direction left.

[PAUSE]

This is the identity the Quran offers. Not a denomination. Not a label. Not "become Muslim" in the institutional sense. **Become someone who leans**. Lean away from what is false. Lean toward what is true. And keep leaning. The inclination itself — the permanent posture of turning from *bāṭil* toward *ḥaqq* — is the millah of Ibrahim.

And then the Quran adds: **وَمَا كَانَ مِنَ ٱلْمُشْرِكِينَ** — "and he was not of the polytheists." This is aimed directly at the Arab pagans who claimed Ibrahim as one of their own. The Quran severs that claim with five words. Ibrahim was not yours. He was *ḥanīf* — the opposite of everything you represent.

### Element 2: لَا نُفَرِّقُ — The Credo of Non-Discrimination

The Muslim credo is delivered in 2:136 — and its most distinctive feature is a negation:

<!-- ayah:2:136 partial --> لَا نُفَرِّقُ بَيْنَ أَحَدٍ مِّنْهُمْ

*Lā nufarriqu bayna aḥadin minhum.*

"We make no distinction between any one of them."

The verb *nufarriqu* is Form II of *fā-rā-qāf* (ف-ر-ق): to separate, to divide, to make a distinction. Form II intensifies: to separate **thoroughly**, to discriminate **completely**. And the Quran negates it: *lā nufarriqu* — we do NOT make thorough distinction. We do not separate. We do not pick and choose.

And the object: **بَيْنَ أَحَدٍ مِّنْهُمْ** — "between any one of them." *Aḥad* — anyone, a single one — in the context of negation creates the absolute universal: not between **any single one** of them. No prophet is elevated above another. No prophet is rejected while others are accepted. No prophet is claimed while others are denied.

This is the defining feature of the Islamic credo. Judaism accepts the prophets up to and including the Tanakh but rejects Jesus and Muhammad. Christianity accepts Jesus but elevates him above the other prophets in a qualitatively different way. Islam says: **all of them, equally, from one Lord, without distinction**.

The list in 2:136 is comprehensive: Ibrahim, Isma'il, Ishaq, Ya'qub, the Tribes (*al-Asbāṭ*), Musa, 'Isa — and then the open-ended: **وَمَآ أُوتِىَ ٱلنَّبِيُّونَ مِن رَّبِّهِمْ** — "and what was given to **the prophets** from their Lord." The plural is unlimited. It is not a closed list. It covers every prophet, named and unnamed, known and unknown, across all of human history.

[PAUSE]

This is what makes the Muslim position unique in the Abrahamic conversation. It is not a competitor making a rival claim about one prophet. It is a position that **accepts the entire prophetic tradition as one stream** from one source. The argument is not "our prophet is better than yours." The argument is: "we believe in yours AND ours, and we make no distinction between them. Do you?"

### Element 3: صِبْغَةَ ٱللَّهِ — The Dye That Cannot Be Washed Out

And then comes the most visually stunning metaphor in the passage — perhaps in the entire section:

<!-- ayah:2:138 partial --> صِبْغَةَ ٱللَّهِ وَمَنْ أَحْسَنُ مِنَ ٱللَّهِ صِبْغَةً

*Ṣibghata Allāh. Wa-man aḥsanu mina Allāhi ṣibghah.*

"The dye of Allah! And who is better than Allah in dye?"

The word **صِبْغَة** (*ṣibghah*) is from the root *ṣād-bā-ghāyn* (ص-ب-غ), which means to dye, to immerse, to color. When you dye a fabric, you submerge it in color until the color becomes inseparable from the material. The fabric does not *wear* the color. The fabric *becomes* the color. You cannot peel it off. You cannot wash it out. The dye and the fabric are one.

The *fi'lah* pattern of *ṣibghah* denotes the manner or result of the action — not the act of dyeing but the **resulting color**. The mark left behind. The permanent alteration of the original material.

Many scholars, including Ibn Abbas and al-Tabari, connect this to the Christian practice of baptism. In the Syriac Christian tradition, converts were immersed in yellow-tinted water — a *ṣibgh* (dipping, immersion) — as a sign of purification and entry into the faith. The Quran appropriates the image and transforms it: your *ṣibghah* is water. Ours is Allah.

Think about what that means. The Christians dipped their converts in colored water, and the dye was a symbol — external, visible, washable. The Quran says: our dye is not water. Our dye is **Allah**. The coloring of our faith is not an external symbol. It is a saturation of the entire being by the presence of divine guidance. You are not painted. You are **dyed**. The color goes through.

[PAUSE]

And then the rhetorical question: **وَمَنْ أَحْسَنُ مِنَ ٱللَّهِ صِبْغَةً** — "And who is better than Allah in dye?" *Aḥsan* — more beautiful, more excellent. The comparative form. Who produces a more beautiful, more permanent, more thorough dye than Allah? Whose coloring goes deeper? Whose mark lasts longer?

The implied answer: no one. No human institution, no ritual practice, no baptismal water, no denominational label can dye you as deeply as Allah's religion dyes you. Because human dyes are applied from outside. Allah's dye comes from the inside. It is the dye of *iman* in the heart, *islām* in the limbs, and *iḥsān* in the consciousness. It colors everything — not just Sundays, not just Fridays, not just the hour of prayer but the entire fabric of your existence.

This is the Muslim identity: not a label. A dye.

## PART 2: THE THEMATIC DEPTHS

### Theme 1: The Credo That Accepts Everything — And Why It Is Revolutionary

In the modern world of interfaith dialogue, the most common posture is one of diplomatic tolerance. "We respect your beliefs." "All religions have truth." "We are more alike than different."

The Quran's credo in 2:136 is not diplomatic tolerance. It is something far more radical. It is **comprehensive acceptance**.

We do not "respect" the Torah. We **believe in what was revealed to Musa**. We do not "appreciate" the Gospel. We **believe in what was given to 'Isa**. We do not "acknowledge" the prophetic tradition. We **accept every prophet from their Lord without distinction**.

This is not the same as saying "all religions are equal" — the Quran clearly establishes that the message has been distorted by previous communities. But it IS saying: every **prophet** carried the same truth. Every **revelation** at its source was from the same Lord. And our position is that we believe in **all of it** — not selectively, not partially, not with reservations about certain prophets.

The revolutionary nature of this credo is often missed by Muslims themselves. We treat it as a theological formality. But think about what it actually claims. It claims that Musa's original mission and Muhammad's mission are the same mission. It claims that 'Isa's original teaching and Ibrahim's original path are the same path. It claims that the God of the Torah, the God of the Gospel, and the God of the Quran is ONE God — and the differences between the communities are not differences in the original message but differences in what happened to the message afterward.

**لَا نُفَرِّقُ بَيْنَ أَحَدٍ مِّنْهُمْ** — we make no distinction between any of them. This is not tolerance. This is claim of ownership. We claim Musa. We claim 'Isa. We claim Ibrahim. Not as Jews or Christians claim them — with selective editing. As **muslimūn** — those who submit to what they ALL brought.

[PAUSE — major pause]

### Theme 2: Are You More Knowing, or Is Allah?

The passage reaches its rhetorical climax in 2:140 with a question that is less a question and more a detonation:

<!-- ayah:2:140 partial --> قُلْ ءَأَنتُمْ أَعْلَمُ أَمِ ٱللَّهُ

*Qul a-antum a'lamu ami Allāh.*

"Say: Are **you** more knowing, or is **Allah**?"

This is the argument-ending question. The communities claimed that Ibrahim, Isma'il, Ishaq, Ya'qub, and the Tribes were Jews or Christians. The Quran responds: on what authority? Who told you that? Were you there (2:133 already asked this)? Do you have a revelation from Allah that says Ibrahim was Jewish? Because Allah — who WAS there, who spoke to Ibrahim directly, who knows what the patriarchs believed — is telling you otherwise.

The question *a-antum a'lamu ami Allāh* does not just challenge a specific claim. It challenges the entire posture of speaking about God's prophets from a position of human assumption rather than divine revelation. When you retroactively label a prophet — putting him in a category that didn't exist in his time — you are claiming to know more about that prophet than the God who sent him.

And then: **وَمَنْ أَظْلَمُ مِمَّن كَتَمَ شَهَـٰدَةً عِندَهُۥ مِنَ ٱللَّهِ** — "And who is more unjust than one who conceals a testimony he has from Allah?"

This is the *wa-man aẓlamu* construction — the formula for maximum injustice. And the crime named is *katama shahādah* — concealing testimony. They KNOW — in their own scriptures, in their own tradition — what the prophets actually taught. They know that Ibrahim was not Jewish in any denominational sense that postdated him by centuries. They know that the patriarchs called to one God. And they are concealing that knowledge. They are hiding the testimony to protect the denominational claim.

The final seal: **وَمَا ٱللَّهُ بِغَـٰفِلٍ عَمَّا تَعْمَلُونَ** — "And Allah is not *ghāfil* (unaware) of what you do." *Ghāfil* — heedless, unaware. Allah is NOT unaware. The negation of unawareness is the affirmation of complete awareness. He sees the concealment. He knows the testimony being hidden. And He is not looking away.

## CLOSING SYNTHESIS

### The Architecture

The seven verses form a complete argumentative arc:

**2:135 — THE COUNTER-IDENTITY:** Not Jewish, not Christian — the way of Ibrahim, ḥanīfan.
**2:136 — THE CREDO:** We believe in everything, in every prophet, without distinction.
**2:137 — THE CHALLENGE:** If they believe the same, they're guided. If not, they're in dissension — and Allah will suffice you.
**2:138 — THE METAPHOR:** Ṣibghat Allāh — the dye of Allah. Who dyes better than He?
**2:139 — THE SEPARATION:** He is our Lord AND yours. Our deeds are ours, yours are yours. We are mukhliṣūn — purely sincere.
**2:140 — THE DETONATION:** Were the patriarchs Jewish or Christian? Are you more knowledgeable than Allah? And who conceals testimony from God?
**2:141 — THE SEAL:** Tilka ummatun qad khalat. For them what they earned. For you what you earn.

The passage moves from identity → credo → challenge → metaphor → separation → detonation → seal. And the seal — 2:141 — is identical to 2:134, closing the bracket of the entire Ibrahim section (2:124-141).

### Questions to Carry

- What is the *ṣibghah* — the dye — that actually colors your life? Is it the dye of Allah, or the dye of your culture, your class, your online tribe, your professional identity?

- When you encounter someone from another faith — do you carry the spirit of *lā nufarriqu* (no distinction between prophets), or do you carry the spirit of denominational competition?

- If Ibrahim was *ḥanīf* — permanently inclined away from falsehood — where are you inclined toward falsehood without realizing it? What are you leaning toward that Ibrahim leaned away from?

- The Quran asked: are you more knowing, or is Allah? Where in your life are you speaking about Allah's plan, Allah's prophets, Allah's decisions with the confidence of someone who thinks they know better?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Islam's identity is not a label but a dye — the coloring of Allah Himself — and its credo is not to choose prophets but to accept them all, because the One who sent them all is the same One who knows what they actually taught, and no human claim can override His testimony.**

### The Closing Invitation

These ayahs invite you to step out of denominational competition and into something older and deeper: the posture of Ibrahim. *Ḥanīfan.* Leaning. Not toward a label but toward truth itself. Not carrying a badge but saturated with a dye — the *ṣibghah* of Allah — that colors everything you are.

The question was never "which team are you on?" The question was always: "have you been dyed?"

And if you have — if the color has gone through, not just onto the surface but into the fabric — then *tilka ummatun qad khalat*, that nation has passed. Their account is sealed. But yours is still open. And the dye is still available. And the One who dyes is *al-Samī' al-'Alīm* — He hears your turning and He knows its sincerity.

### Du'a

*Allāhumma, dye us with Your ṣibghah — not the dye of culture, not the dye of tribe, not the dye of denomination, but the dye of You. Make us ḥunafā' — people who lean, permanently, constitutionally, away from everything false and toward everything true. Let us carry the credo of lā nufarriqu — accepting every prophet, honoring every revelation, submitting to every truth from You without picking and choosing. And when the communities compete for labels and the denominations recruit for teams — remind us that Ibrahim was none of those things. He was ḥanīfan. And he was muslimann. And that was always enough.*
