## Introduction

[ayah:2:26] ۞ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ لَا يَسْتَحْىِۦٓ أَن يَضْرِبَ مَثَلًا مَّا بَعُوضَةً فَمَا فَوْقَهَا ۚ فَأَمَّا ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ فَيَعْلَمُونَ أَنَّهُ ٱلْحَقُّ مِن رَّبِّهِمْ ۖ وَأَمَّا ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ فَيَقُولُونَ مَاذَآ أَرَادَ ٱللَّهُ بِهَـٰذَا مَثَلًا ۘ يُضِلُّ بِهِۦ كَثِيرًا وَيَهْدِى بِهِۦ كَثِيرًا ۚ وَمَا يُضِلُّ بِهِۦٓ إِلَّا ٱلْفَـٰسِقِينَ

[ayah:2:27] ٱلَّذِينَ يَنقُضُونَ عَهْدَ ٱللَّهِ مِنۢ بَعْدِ مِيثَـٰقِهِۦ وَيَقْطَعُونَ مَآ أَمَرَ ٱللَّهُ بِهِۦٓ أَن يُوصَلَ وَيُفْسِدُونَ فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ ۚ أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ هُمُ ٱلْخَـٰسِرُونَ

*Inna llāha lā yastaḥyī an yaḍriba mathalan mā baʿūḍatan fa-mā fawqahā. Fa-amma lladhīna āmanū fa-yaʿlamūna annahu l-ḥaqqu min rabbihim. Wa amma lladhīna kafarū fa-yaqūlūna mādhā arāda llāhu bi-hādhā mathalan. Yuḍillu bihī kathīran wa yahdī bihī kathīran. Wa mā yuḍillu bihī illā l-fāsiqīn. Alladhīna yanquḍūna ʿahda llāhi min baʿdi mīthāqihī wa yaqṭaʿūna mā amara llāhu bihī an yūṣala wa yufsidūna fī l-arḍ. Ulāʾika humu l-khāsirūn.*

Indeed, Allah is not ashamed to present a parable of a mosquito or what is above it. As for those who believe, they know it is the truth from their Lord. But as for those who disbelieve, they say, "What did Allah intend by this as a parable?" He misleads many thereby and guides many thereby. And He does not mislead except the defiantly disobedient — those who break the covenant of Allah after contracting it, and sever what Allah has ordered to be joined, and cause corruption on earth. Those are the losers.

---

The Quraysh had a complaint.

It wasn't about monotheism — they could debate that. It wasn't about the afterlife — they could dismiss that. Their complaint was more specific, more cultural, and it tells us something remarkable about what actually bothered them about the Quran. What bothered them was the *mosquito*.

The Quran had used parables involving small, contemptible creatures — spiders, flies, mosquitoes. And the Arabs, who prized eloquence above almost everything, found this beneath the dignity of divine speech. A book from God should deal in noble images: eagles, lions, the stars, the sword. Not insects. Not blood-sucking pests. The objection was aesthetic: "If this were really from God, He wouldn't stoop to talk about mosquitoes."

And Allah's response is not to defend the choice. It is not to explain why mosquitoes are scientifically interesting (though they are). It is something far more devastating: He addresses the *objection itself* as a diagnostic tool. Your response to the parable — whether you receive it as truth or mock it as beneath you — **reveals what's already inside you.** The mosquito didn't divide people. It *exposed* a division that was already there.

What's stunning about these two verses is not the defense of divine parables. It's the revelation that **the same truth, landing on two different hearts, produces opposite results** — and that the difference is not in the truth but in the terrain it lands on.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. "Allah Is Not Ashamed" — The Form X Declaration

إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ لَا يَسْتَحْىِۦٓ أَن يَضْرِبَ مَثَلًا

*"Indeed, Allah is not ashamed to present a parable."*

The verb يَسْتَحْىِۦٓ — *yastaḥyī* — needs our full attention.

In Arabic, verb forms carry meaning in their structure, not just their roots. Form I — the basic form — of the root حيي is حَيِيَ, which means to be shy, to feel embarrassment. If Allah had used Form I — لَا يَحْيَا — it would have meant "He does not feel shyness." A simple negation of an emotion.

But He used Form X: يَسْتَحْىِۦٓ — *yastaḥyī*. Form X in Arabic is the form of *seeking, considering, or deeming*. When you say اسْتَكْبَرَ (Form X of كبر), you mean "he *considered himself* great, he *deemed himself* above something." When you say اسْتَغْفَرَ (Form X of غفر), you mean "he *sought* forgiveness."

So يَسْتَحْىِۦٓ doesn't merely mean "He feels shy." It means **"He considers Himself constrained by shyness"** or **"He deems it beneath Himself."** The negation — لَا يَسْتَحْىِۦٓ — therefore means: **Allah does not consider it beneath His dignity.** He does not *deem* this a lowering of His station. He has made a deliberate self-assessment and concluded: there is nothing degrading about a mosquito in My speech.

[PAUSE]

This distinction matters because the objectors assumed a hierarchy of dignity in language — noble images for noble speakers, lowly images for lowly ones. Form X dismantles that assumption at the grammatical level. The question was never "Is a mosquito dignified enough for God?" The question is: "Who told you that you get to decide what's dignified enough for God?" The Form X makes the assessment *His*, not theirs. He has evaluated. He is not ashamed. Your opinion about what divine speech should sound like is irrelevant to the One who authored the language you're using to critique Him.

And then: يَضْرِبَ مَثَلًا — *"to strike a parable."* The verb is ضرب — to strike, to hit. In Arabic, you don't "tell" a parable or "give" a parable. You **strike** it — like a blacksmith strikes metal, like a mint strikes a coin. The parable is forged, hammered into shape, impressed into reality. It is not a gentle illustration. It is a blow. And when Allah strikes a parable, the impact is felt whether you receive it as guidance or as mockery. The blow lands either way.

[PAUSE]

### 2. The Split Screen — فَيَعْلَمُونَ vs. فَيَقُولُونَ

Now watch what happens when the parable lands on two different hearts. The verse splits into parallel tracks:

فَأَمَّا ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ **فَيَعْلَمُونَ** أَنَّهُ ٱلْحَقُّ مِن رَّبِّهِمْ

*"As for those who believed — they KNOW that it is the truth from their Lord."*

وَأَمَّا ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ **فَيَقُولُونَ** مَاذَآ أَرَادَ ٱللَّهُ بِهَـٰذَا مَثَلًا

*"But as for those who disbelieved — they SAY, 'What did Allah intend by this as a parable?'"*

The asymmetry is devastating, and it lives in a single verb difference.

Believers: فَيَعْلَمُونَ — they **know**. The verb is عَلِمَ — internal cognition, settled awareness. They don't "say" it's the truth. They don't "think" it's the truth. They don't "feel" it's the truth. They **know**. The knowledge is quiet, internal, and complete. It doesn't need an audience. It doesn't need to be announced. It sits in the chest like a fact.

Disbelievers: فَيَقُولُونَ — they **say**. The verb is قَالَ — external speech, public utterance. They don't "wonder" internally or "doubt" privately. They *say* — out loud, to an audience, performatively. مَاذَآ أَرَادَ ٱللَّهُ بِهَـٰذَا — "What did Allah mean by this?" The question is not sincere inquiry. It is rhetorical mockery. They are performing confusion for an audience.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the Quran's most precise psychological portraits. **Faith produces knowledge. Disbelief produces talk.**

The believer encounters a mosquito parable and something settles inside them: *yes, this is from my Lord.* They don't need to argue for it. They don't need to defend it. The recognition is immediate and internal. The disbeliever encounters the same parable and the response is external, performative, social: *"What is this supposed to mean?"* — said with raised eyebrows, to an audience, with the implicit message: *"Surely a book from God would be more sophisticated than this."*

And notice what the believer's knowledge is attached to: أَنَّهُ ٱلْحَقُّ **مِن رَّبِّهِمْ** — "that it is the truth **from their Lord.**" Not just "the truth" in the abstract. The truth *from their Rabb* — from the One who has been nurturing them, sustaining them, raising them. The believer recognizes the parable not because they analyzed its literary merit but because they recognize the *voice*. A child can recognize their parent's voice even when the parent is saying something strange. The recognition is relational, not intellectual.

[PAUSE]

### 3. The Same Rain, Two Soils — يُضِلُّ بِهِۦ كَثِيرًا وَيَهْدِى بِهِۦ كَثِيرًا

Now the most theologically charged line in the passage:

يُضِلُّ بِهِۦ كَثِيرًا وَيَهْدِى بِهِۦ كَثِيرًا

*"He misleads many thereby, and He guides many thereby."*

The same pronoun — بِهِۦ, "by it/through it" — appears in both halves. The same instrument. The same parable. The same Book. And it does two opposite things to two different populations.

Now look at the verb forms. يُضِلُّ — *yuḍillu* — is Form IV, causative from ضلل. He *causes* misguidance. يَهْدِى — *yahdī* — is Form I from هدي. He *guides* — directly, simply, with no causative intermediary.

This asymmetry in verb forms is theologically precise. Guidance is **direct** — Form I, the simplest, most intimate form. Allah guides personally, immediately, without a mechanism between Himself and the guided person. But misguidance is **causative** — Form IV, where the action is indirect, where the agent *causes* a process rather than performing the act itself. The distinction: Allah guides you Himself. He allows your misguidance through a process — through your own choices, your own arrogance, your own refusal — that He then ratifies.

The misguidance is not arbitrary. The very next phrase proves it: وَمَا يُضِلُّ بِهِۦٓ إِلَّا ٱلْفَـٰسِقِينَ — **"And He does not mislead except the fāsiqīn."** The misguidance is restricted. It does not touch the humble, the sincere, the genuinely confused. It touches only the فَـٰسِقِينَ.

And who are the *fāsiqīn*? The root فسق gives us the image: a date that has burst out of its skin. A *fāsiq* is someone who has **broken out** of the boundary that was meant to contain them — the boundary of obedience, of covenant, of moral order. They didn't just drift. They *exited*. They tore through the skin of what was supposed to hold them.

[PAUSE]

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Why the Same Truth Builds One Heart and Breaks Another

There is a phenomenon in psychology called "confirmation bias" — the tendency to interpret new information in a way that confirms what you already believe. If you believe the world is hostile, every neutral event looks like evidence of hostility. If you believe you're unloved, every silence from your partner feels like proof.

But the Quran names something deeper than a cognitive bias. It names a *spiritual physics*: the same revelation, landing on two different hearts, produces opposite effects not because the revelation changed but because **the hearts are differently prepared.**

Think about rain. The same rain falls on a garden and on a parking lot. The garden absorbs it — and produces flowers, fruit, shade. The parking lot rejects it — and produces puddles, flooding, danger. The rain didn't change. The ground did.

This is exactly what يُضِلُّ بِهِۦ كَثِيرًا وَيَهْدِى بِهِۦ كَثِيرًا describes. The Quran is the rain. The hearts are the soil. And the difference between guidance and misguidance is not in what you received. It is in **what you were when it arrived.**

[PAUSE]

The disbeliever hears "mosquito" and sees beneath-dignity. The believer hears "mosquito" and sees the fingerprint of a Creator who is not too proud to teach through the smallest thing. Same word. Same parable. Same mosquito. Two completely different universes of meaning — generated not by the text but by the reader.

And this maps onto something every person has experienced. You've given advice to two different friends — the same advice, the same words. One friend received it, sat with it, let it change them. The other friend heard it, got defensive, and spent the next hour explaining why the advice doesn't apply to them. Same words. Same delivery. Opposite outcomes. Not because your advice was ambiguous. Because the **readiness** of the receiver determined everything.

Here is the practical edge: the Quran you read is the same Quran everyone reads. The difference in what it does to you is not in the text. It is in you. And the question this verse forces is not "Is the Quran clear enough?" but **"Am I receptive enough?"** Because if a mosquito parable can be recognized as truth by a believing heart and mocked as nonsense by a disbelieving one — then the variable was never the mosquito.

[PAUSE — major pause]

### Theme 2: The Three Fractures — يَنقُضُونَ, يَقْطَعُونَ, يُفْسِدُونَ

Verse 27 then defines the *fāsiqīn* through three actions. And the three actions form a descending spiral — each one worse than the last, each one a consequence of the previous.

**First**: يَنقُضُونَ عَهْدَ ٱللَّهِ مِنۢ بَعْدِ مِيثَـٰقِهِۦ — *"They break the covenant of Allah after it was firmly bound."*

The verb يَنقُضُونَ — from نقض — means to unravel, to undo thread by thread. Not to shatter suddenly (كَسَرَ) but to systematically dismantle what was carefully constructed. And what are they undoing? عَهْدَ ٱللَّهِ — the covenant of Allah. After مِيثَـٰقِهِۦ — after its *mīthāq*, its binding instrument.

*Mīthāq* is a mīf'āl noun from وثق — trust, reliability. A mīthāq is the instrument of binding, the mechanism that makes a covenant hold. Breaking a covenant after its mīthāq is not just violating an agreement. It is **destroying the very apparatus of trust** — like not just breaking a promise but ensuring that no promise can ever be believed again.

This is the first fracture: **the fracture between the human being and God.**

[PAUSE]

**Second**: وَيَقْطَعُونَ مَآ أَمَرَ ٱللَّهُ بِهِۦٓ أَن يُوصَلَ — *"And they sever what Allah commanded to be joined."*

يَقْطَعُونَ — from قطع — to cut, to sever. Listen to the word: *qāf-ṭā-ʿayn*. Three emphatic consonants in a row. The hardest, sharpest sequence of sounds in the Arabic phonetic inventory. The word *sounds* like severance. It *sounds* like something being torn apart.

And what are they severing? مَآ أَمَرَ ٱللَّهُ بِهِۦٓ أَن يُوصَلَ — "what Allah commanded to be joined." The object is deliberately unspecified. What should be joined? The classical scholars build their composite: family ties, bonds of faith, the connection between communities, the relationship between knowledge and action, the thread between revelation and implementation. The absence of specification universalizes the principle: **anything Allah ordered to be connected, they disconnect.**

This is the second fracture: **the fracture between human beings and each other.**

[PAUSE]

**Third**: وَيُفْسِدُونَ فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ — *"And they cause corruption on earth."*

يُفْسِدُونَ — Form IV, causative, from فسد. They don't just *become* corrupt. They *cause* corruption. They are agents of decay — actively spreading rot wherever they go. And the location: فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ — on earth. Not in a specific place. On the earth itself. The corruption is not contained. It spreads across the ground that Allah made as a فِرَٰش — a bed — in verse 22. The bed He spread for them, they corrupt.

This is the third fracture: **the fracture between human beings and the world they live in.**

[PAUSE]

Now see the spiral: they break their bond with God (vertical fracture). That break inevitably damages their bonds with other people (horizontal fracture). And that damage inevitably corrupts the world they inhabit (environmental fracture). The sequence is not accidental. It is causal. **When you sever your relationship with the One above, you cannot maintain your relationships with those beside you, and eventually you damage the ground beneath you.**

The verdict: أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ هُمُ ٱلْخَـٰسِرُونَ — *"Those are the losers."*

ٱلْخَـٰسِرُونَ — from خسر — to lose in a transaction, to come out with less than you started with. A خاسر is not someone who never had anything. A خاسر is someone who **had something and lost it**. They had a covenant — and broke it. They had connections — and severed them. They had an earth — and corrupted it. The loss is measured against what they were given. And what they were given was everything.

## Closing Synthesis

### Architectural Reveal

These two verses form a chiastic structure that mirrors itself:

**A** — Allah is not ashamed (His relationship to the parable)
  **B** — Believers KNOW it is truth (internal recognition)
    **C** — Disbelievers SAY "what did He mean?" (external mockery)
  **B'** — He guides many thereby (guidance for the receptive)
**A'** — He misleads only the fāsiqīn (His relationship to the misguided)

And verse 27 unpacks ٱلْفَـٰسِقِينَ in a descending triad:
- Break the covenant with Allah (upward fracture)
- Sever what should be joined (lateral fracture)
- Corrupt the earth (downward fracture)

The structure moves from heaven to earth — from God's dignity down through human hearts and finally to the ground. And the mosquito sits at the center of it all: a tiny creature that triggered a fracture line running from God's speech to the earth's surface.

### Questions to Carry

When you encounter something in the Quran that seems "strange" — a parable that doesn't match your expectations, a verse that challenges your assumptions — do you respond like the believers (فَيَعْلَمُونَ — you know, internally, that it is truth) or like the disbelievers (فَيَقُولُونَ — you say, externally, "what does this mean")?

What in your life right now is in the process of being *unraveled* — يَنقُضُونَ — not shattered suddenly, but slowly, thread by thread? A commitment? A relationship? A practice? Unraveling is harder to notice than breaking. By the time you see it, the rope is already thin.

The verse says the same Book guides some and misleads others. What is the condition of your soil right now? If the rain of the Quran fell on your heart today — would it produce flowers or puddles?

Which of the three fractures do you see in the world around you — and which one do you see in yourself? The covenant with God. The bonds between people. The corruption of the earth. They are connected. Where did the spiral begin?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The Quran's parables do not create the division between guidance and misguidance — they reveal a division that already exists inside the heart, and the only thing a mosquito did was show you which side of it you are standing on.**

### The Closing Invitation

The next time something in the Quran makes you uncomfortable — a verse that seems too harsh, too simple, too strange — pause before you speak. Ask yourself: is my discomfort a sign that the text is flawed, or that my heart is unprepared? Because the same parable that made the disbelievers mock is the same parable that made the believers recognize truth.

The mosquito didn't change. The hearts did.

### Du'a

*Allāhumma, You are not ashamed of the truth, in any form it comes — let us not be ashamed to receive it. Make us among those who know, not those who merely say. When Your parables land on our hearts, let them find soil — not stone. And protect us from the three fractures: the breaking of our covenant with You, the severing of our bonds with each other, and the corruption of the earth You spread as our bed. Let us not be among the khāsirūn — those who had everything and ended with nothing. Āmīn.*
