passage_context: "These five ayahs continue the dietary legislation section of Al-Baqarah (which began with 2:168's address to all mankind). Here the address narrows to the believers specifically — يَـٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟. The passage moves from the positive command (eat from the ṭayyibāt — good things) to the short prohibition list (four items only, with a mercy exception for necessity), and then makes a devastating pivot: those who CONCEAL what Allah revealed and SELL it for a small price. The concealers aren't just hiding truth — they are EATING fire into their bellies. The Quran connects the food imagery from 2:168-172 to the spiritual imagery of 2:174: what you consume physically is food; what the concealers consume is fire. The passage contains one of the most biting phrases of divine irony in the entire Quran: فَمَآ أَصْبَرَهُمْ عَلَى ٱلنَّارِ — 'How patient they are for the Fire!' The ta'ajjub (exclamatory) construction turns their endurance into mockery."
scholarly_note: "The phrase فَمَآ أَصْبَرَهُمْ عَلَى ٱلنَّارِ (2:175) is debated among scholars regarding its exact rhetorical function. The majority (including al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir) read it as ta'ajjub — an expression of amazement/mockery: 'How astonishing is their patience for the Fire!' or 'What makes them so enduring toward the Fire!' Al-Razi notes that this is one of the most powerful instances of divine sarcasm in the Quran — Allah is expressing mock wonder at their willingness to endure hellfire in exchange for worldly gain. Some scholars read mā as interrogative: 'What has made them so patient for the Fire?' — i.e., what delusion makes them think they can bear it? Both readings converge on the same devastating irony: these people ACT as though they can endure what is coming, when in fact they cannot. The dietary exception in 2:173 (فَمَنِ ٱضْطُرَّ — whoever is forced by necessity) is discussed by scholars as one of the foundational principles of Islamic jurisprudence: necessity permits the prohibited (al-ḍarūrāt tubīḥu al-maḥẓūrāt). The two qualifiers — غَيْرَ بَاغٍ وَلَا عَادٍ (neither desiring nor transgressing) — set the boundaries: necessity must be genuine, not manufactured, and the person must not exceed what survival requires."

ABSENCE FLAGS:
1. 2:172: إِن كُنتُمْ إِيَّاهُ تَعْبُدُونَ — "IF it is Him you worship." The conditional إِن (if) is startling. Allah doesn't say "since you worship Him" or "because you worship Him." He says IF. The condition introduces doubt: is it really Him you worship? Or do your eating habits suggest otherwise? The absence of certainty is the challenge.
2. 2:173: The prohibition list has ONLY four items — وَٱلدَّمَ (blood), ٱلْمَيْتَةَ (carrion), لَحْمَ ٱلْخِنزِيرِ (pork), وَمَآ أُهِلَّ بِهِۦ لِغَيْرِ ٱللَّهِ (what is dedicated to other than Allah). Just four. After the expansive "eat from the ṭayyibāt of what We provided" — the prohibited list is deliberately SHORT. The ABSENCE of a long prohibition list is itself the mercy: almost everything is permitted.
3. 2:174: مَا يَأْكُلُونَ فِى بُطُونِهِمْ إِلَّا ٱلنَّارَ — "they eat nothing into their BELLIES except the Fire." The word بُطُونِهِمْ (their bellies) is physically specific and deliberately visceral. The absence of any metaphorical softening is the point: the Fire is not figurative. It is as real as the food that fills their stomachs.
4. 2:174: وَلَا يُكَلِّمُهُمُ ٱللَّهُ — "And Allah will NOT speak to them." Three punishments are named, and the first is SILENCE. Not fire (though that comes). Not pain (though that comes too). The first punishment is divine silence. The ABSENCE of divine speech is presented as a punishment worse than fire — listed before the fire, as though silence from God is the greater devastation.

ADDRESS FLAGS:
- 2:172: يَـٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ — direct address to believers (2MP). Shifted from 2:168's يَا أَيُّهَا النَّاسُ (all mankind). The address has NARROWED. All humanity was told to eat ḥalāl ṭayyib. Now the believers specifically are told to eat the ṭayyibāt and be grateful.
- 2:173: 3rd person passive (حَرَّمَ — He forbade, أُهِلَّ — it was dedicated). The prohibitions are stated as divine facts, not as conversation.
- 2:174: إِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ يَكْتُمُونَ — 3rd person. The concealers are spoken ABOUT, not to. The distance is judgment.
- 2:175: فَمَآ أَصْبَرَهُمْ — 3rd person exclamatory. The mockery is directed at them but not addressed to them. They are being discussed, observed, wondered at — from above.

SONIC FLAGS:
- ثَمَنًا قَلِيلًا (thamanan qalīlan) — both words diminutive in sound, the tanwīn making them light, throwaway. A small price SOUNDS small.
- بُطُونِهِمْ (buṭūnihim) — the bā' and ṭā' are visceral, physical. You can almost feel the belly.
- أَصْبَرَهُمْ (aṣbarahum) — the emphatic ṣād grounds the word, the bā'-rā' rolls with mock wonder. Patience that becomes mockery.
- شِقَاقٍ (shiqāq) — the shīn-qāf-qāf is cracking, splitting. Dissension sounds like breaking.

Innamā ḥarrama 'alaykumu al-maytata wal-dama wa-laḥma al-khinzīri wa-mā uhilla bihi li-ghayri Allāh. Fa-mani iḍṭurra ghayra bāghin wa-lā 'ādin fa-lā ithma 'alayh. Inna Allāha Ghafūrun Raḥīm. Inna alladhīna yaktumūna mā anzala Allāhu mina al-Kitābi wa-yashtarūna bihi thamanan qalīlan — ulā'ika mā ya'kulūna fī buṭūnihim illā al-Nār. Wa-lā yukallimuhumu Allāhu yawma al-Qiyāmati wa-lā yuzakkīhim. Wa-lahum 'adhābun alīm. Ulā'ika alladhīna ishtarawu al-ḍalālata bil-hudā wal-'adhāba bil-maghfirah. Fa-mā aṣbarahum 'alā al-Nār. Dhālika bi-anna Allāha nazzala al-Kitāba bil-ḥaqq. Wa-inna alladhīna ikhtilafū fī al-Kitābi la-fī shiqāqin ba'īd.*

**O you who believe, eat from the good things which We have provided for you, and be grateful to Allah — if it is Him you worship. He has only forbidden to you dead animals, blood, the flesh of swine, and that which has been dedicated to other than Allah. But whoever is forced by necessity, neither desiring it nor transgressing — there is no sin upon him. Indeed, Allah is Forgiving and Merciful. Indeed, those who conceal what Allah has sent down of the Book and exchange it for a small price — those eat nothing into their bellies except the Fire. And Allah will not speak to them on the Day of Resurrection, nor will He purify them. And for them is a painful punishment. Those are the ones who have exchanged guidance for error and forgiveness for punishment. How patient they are for the Fire! That is because Allah has sent down the Book in truth. And indeed, those who differ over the Book are in extreme dissension.**

## INTRODUCTION

Imagine two meals. Sit with them for a moment.

The first: someone eats from what Allah has provided. The food is lawful. It's wholesome. It nourishes the body. The person eats, and then — **وَٱشْكُرُوا۟ لِلَّهِ** — they are grateful. The meal is a transaction of mercy: Allah gives, the person receives, and gratitude completes the circle.

The second: someone takes what Allah revealed — clear truth, sent down in the Book — and **sells it** for a small price. They conceal it. They trade it. They pocket the coins. And then the Quran tells you what they are actually eating: **مَا يَأْكُلُونَ فِى بُطُونِهِمْ إِلَّا ٱلنَّارَ** — "they eat nothing into their bellies except **the Fire**."

The same verb — **يَأْكُلُونَ** — eat. The same body part — **بُطُونِهِمْ** — bellies. But the content is different. One person fills their belly with *ṭayyibāt* — good, wholesome food. The other fills their belly with fire. And they don't know it yet.

These five ayahs are about two kinds of consumption. What you eat with your body. And what you consume with your soul. The passage moves from the widest possible generosity — eat from the good things, almost everything is permitted, the prohibition list is only four items, and even those are exempted under genuine necessity — to the most devastating consequence imaginable: a person who traded all of that generosity for a handful of coins, and who will discover on the Day of Resurrection that every coin was a coal.

And in between those two poles, the Quran delivers one of its most biting lines: **فَمَآ أَصْبَرَهُمْ عَلَى ٱلنَّارِ** — "How **patient** they are for the Fire!" Divine sarcasm. Allah expressing mock amazement at their endurance. As though to say: *You must be so brave, to trade forgiveness for punishment and then walk toward the Fire with such confidence. How impressive.*

## PART 1: THE LINGUISTIC JOURNEY

### Element 1: إِن كُنتُمْ إِيَّاهُ تَعْبُدُونَ — The Two-Letter Challenge Hidden in the Command to Be Grateful

The command to eat and be grateful seems straightforward. But embedded in it is a two-letter word that should stop every believer in their tracks:

**وَٱشْكُرُوا۟ لِلَّهِ إِن كُنتُمْ إِيَّاهُ تَعْبُدُونَ**

"And be grateful to Allah — **إِن** — **if** — it is Him you worship."

**إِن** — *in*. If. Conditional. Not *idhā* (when — used for certainty). Not *li-anna* (because — stating a fact). **If.**

This is startling. Allah is speaking to **الَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟** — those who believe. He's already identified them as believers. And then He says "if it is Him you worship" — as though the matter is not settled. As though their belief *claims* to worship Him but their *behavior* might tell a different story.

The scholars note that this *in* is not expressing doubt about whether they are believers. It is a **conditional challenge**: if your claim of worship is real, then gratitude is its proof. If you eat from what Allah gave you and don't thank Him for it — what does that say about the worship you claim?

This is the Quran's razor: **worship that doesn't produce gratitude is worship in name only**. The word *ta'budūn* is in the imperfect — ongoing, present, habitual. Are you *actively* worshipping Him? Right now? In your daily life? Then show it. Be grateful. If you're not grateful — examine what you're actually worshipping. Because something is getting your devotion. The only question is whether it's Him.

[PAUSE]

And notice the word order: **إِيَّاهُ تَعْبُدُونَ** — *iyyāhu ta'budūn* — "**Him** you worship." The object pronoun is fronted, placed before the verb. In Arabic, this fronting creates *ḥaṣr* — restriction, exclusivity. It doesn't just mean "if you worship Him." It means "if it is Him **alone** — Him and no one else — that you worship." The grammatical structure insists on exclusivity. The gratitude doesn't prove general religiosity. It proves *tawḥīd* — that your worship is directed at one God, exclusively, without rivals.

### Element 2: ثَمَنًا قَلِيلًا — The Price That Is Always Small

**وَيَشْتَرُونَ بِهِۦ ثَمَنًا قَلِيلًا**

"And they exchange it for **thamanan qalīlan** — a small price."

Let's stay with these two words, because they contain an entire theology of temptation.

**ثَمَنًا** — *thamanan* — "a price." Indefinite. Not "the price" but "a price." Just... some price. Any price. The indefiniteness is dismissive. It doesn't even matter what the price was. It could have been money. It could have been status. It could have been political favor. It could have been professional advancement. The Quran doesn't specify because the specifics are irrelevant. Whatever it was — it was a price. A transaction. An exchange.

**قَلِيلًا** — *qalīlan* — "small." And this is where the word's morphological pattern does its deepest work. *Qalīl* is the *fa'īl* intensive pattern — the same pattern as *'alīm* (all-knowing), *raḥīm* (merciful), *ḥakīm* (wise). The *fa'īl* pattern describes a **permanent, essential quality**. *Qalīl* doesn't mean "a price that happened to be small" or "a price that looked small in retrospect." It means **a price that is inherently, essentially, permanently small**.

[PAUSE]

What does this mean?

It means that **any price** received in exchange for concealing divine truth is, by its very nature, small. It could be a million dollars. It could be a kingdom. It could be the whole world. And it would still be *qalīl* — because the thing being traded is *ma anzala Allāhu mina al-Kitāb* — what Allah sent down of the Book. The value of divine truth is infinite. Any finite exchange for it is, mathematically, *qalīl*.

This is not about the amount. It's about the **ratio**. When you trade the infinite for the finite — no matter how large the finite — the trade is always a loss. The price is always small. Not because the world offered you too little, but because what you sold was worth more than anything the world could offer.

And the verb: **يَشْتَرُونَ** — *yashtarūna* — Form VIII of شري. Not "they sell" (*yabī'ūn*). They **purchase**. Form VIII is the reflexive: they buy *for themselves*. They are the customer in this transaction, not the vendor. They wanted this trade. They chose it. They went to the market of the world and spent their divine inheritance on a handful of coins.

And the verb is imperfect — ongoing. They **keep purchasing**. It's not a one-time mistake. It's a lifestyle. A business model. An ongoing enterprise of exchanging eternal light for temporary currency.

[PAUSE]

### Element 3: مَا يَأْكُلُونَ فِى بُطُونِهِمْ إِلَّا ٱلنَّارَ — When Fire Becomes Food

Now watch what the Quran does with the eating metaphor. The passage opened with **كُلُوا۟** — "eat!" — a command to the believers to eat from the *ṭayyibāt*. And now it tells you what the concealers are eating:

**أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ مَا يَأْكُلُونَ فِى بُطُونِهِمْ إِلَّا ٱلنَّارَ**

"Those — they eat nothing into their **bellies** except **the Fire**."

The physical specificity is deliberate. Not "they consume fire" — which could be metaphorical. But "they eat into their **بُطُونِهِمْ** — their **bellies** — nothing except the Fire." The word *buṭūn* is visceral. You can feel it. It's the same belly that receives the food from 2:172. The same organ. The same physical space. But what's filling it has changed.

The Quran is drawing a direct line: the money you received for concealing truth, the meal you bought with that money, the food that entered your belly — **that food is fire**. You think you're eating dinner. You're eating your own punishment. The transaction looks like a meal. It is, in reality, a consumption of the very thing that will consume you.

And the *ḥaṣr* construction — **مَا... إِلَّا** — "nothing... except" — ensures there is no ambiguity. Not "among the things they eat is some fire." **Nothing** except fire. Every bite. Every morsel purchased with the proceeds of concealment. All of it. Fire.

[PAUSE]

Then come three punishments, and their **order** is significant:

**وَلَا يُكَلِّمُهُمُ ٱللَّهُ يَوْمَ ٱلْقِيَـٰمَةِ** — "And Allah will not speak to them on the Day of Resurrection."

**وَلَا يُزَكِّيهِمْ** — "Nor will He purify them."

**وَلَهُمْ عَذَابٌ أَلِيمٌ** — "And for them is a painful punishment."

Notice: **divine silence comes first**. Before the absence of purification. Before the punishment. The first thing stripped away is not comfort. It's **communication**. Allah will not *yukallimuhum* — Form II, intensive speech, real engagement, personal address. The same God who said *yā ayyuhā alladhīna āmanū* — "O you who believe" — who addresses His servants with intimacy and directness — will be **silent** toward these people.

The silence is listed before the punishment because it is **worse** than the punishment. Fire burns the body. Silence from God burns the soul. To be spoken to by Allah is the highest honor. To be ignored by Him is the deepest devastation. And these are the people who had access to His Book — they knew His words — and they sold that access for *thamanan qalīlan*.

---

### فَمَآ أَصْبَرَهُمْ عَلَى ٱلنَّارِ — When Allah Uses Sarcasm

And then the Quran delivers its sharpest line:

**فَمَآ أَصْبَرَهُمْ عَلَى ٱلنَّارِ**

"**How patient they are** for the Fire!"

This is the *mā af'alahu* construction — *ta'ajjub* — the Arabic form for expressing amazement, astonishment, wonder. "How amazing is X!" "What an extraordinary Y!"

And Allah uses it here for **mock amazement**. He is expressing wonder — but the wonder is sarcastic. "How impressive their endurance must be! How brave they are, walking toward the Fire with such confidence! What patience they must have, to trade forgiveness for punishment and then carry on as though they can handle what's coming!"

The scholars note this as one of the most powerful instances of **divine irony** in the entire Quran. It's not anger. It's not even condemnation. It's something more devastating: Allah looking at these people and saying, essentially, *I'm impressed by your audacity. You traded guidance for error, and you act as though you can endure the consequences. Let's see how long that confidence lasts.*

The word *aṣbarahum* comes from *ṣabr* — patience, endurance. The very quality the Quran praises in believers (*inna Allāha ma'a al-ṣābirīn* — 2:153) is now turned on its head, weaponized as irony. The believers are patient *in the path of Allah*. These people are "patient" *for the Fire*. Same word. Opposite direction.

## PART 2: THE THEMATIC DEPTHS

### Theme 1: The Economy of Concealment — When Silence Has a Price Tag

Every community has truths that are inconvenient. Truths that, if spoken publicly, would cost someone money, status, or power. And every community has people who know those truths and choose not to speak them — not because they've forgotten, but because silence is more profitable than speech.

The Quran names this with surgical precision: **يَكْتُمُونَ مَآ أَنزَلَ ٱللَّهُ مِنَ ٱلْكِتَـٰبِ وَيَشْتَرُونَ بِهِۦ ثَمَنًا قَلِيلًا**. They conceal what Allah revealed. And they purchase with it a small price.

Notice the connection. It's not just concealment. It's concealment **with a transaction attached**. The hiding is not accidental or lazy. It is **paid for**. Someone benefits from the truth being hidden. And the Quran is saying: whatever that benefit is — however large it looks in this world — it is *qalīl*. Permanently, essentially, inherently small.

Think about the modern forms of this. A scholar who softens a ruling because the donor class won't tolerate the real answer. An imam who avoids a topic because addressing it would cost him his position. A community leader who knows something is wrong but stays quiet because speaking would fracture a profitable relationship. A content creator who frames the religion to maximize engagement rather than accuracy — because accuracy doesn't go viral.

None of these people are lying, exactly. They're **concealing**. *Yaktumūna*. They have access to what Allah revealed. They know what the Book says. And they choose, for a price — a salary, a reputation, a following, a seat at a table — not to say it.

The Quran doesn't call them liars. It calls them **customers**. *Yashtarūna*. They are purchasing something. And the purchase price is divine truth. The currency they spend is the Book of Allah. And what they get in return is *thamanan qalīlan* — something that will not last, that will not satisfy, that will not save them when the silence catches up.

[PAUSE]

And the consequence is architectural: the meal they bought with that price is fire. The speech they'll receive from Allah on the Day of Judgment is silence. The purification they'll undergo is nothing — because Allah will not purify them. Every layer of the consequence mirrors the transaction: you traded speech (the Book) for silence (concealment)? Allah trades His speech for silence toward you. You traded ṭayyibāt (good things) for thamanan qalīlan (a paltry price)? The food in your belly becomes fire.

The punishment doesn't come from outside the crime. It **is** the crime, reflected back.

[PAUSE]

### Theme 2: The Mercy in the Short List — Why Only Four Things Are Forbidden

Before the passage reaches its devastating climax, it does something that is easy to pass over but essential to feel: it gives you the prohibition list. And the list is **astonishingly short**.

**إِنَّمَا حَرَّمَ عَلَيْكُمُ ٱلْمَيْتَةَ وَٱلدَّمَ وَلَحْمَ ٱلْخِنزِيرِ وَمَآ أُهِلَّ بِهِۦ لِغَيْرِ ٱللَّهِ**

Four things. Dead animals. Blood. Pork. What was dedicated to other than Allah. That's it.

The restriction particle **إِنَّمَا** — *innamā* — "He **only** forbade" — limits the list. This is not a minimum list with more to come. This is the complete set. *Innamā* closes the door: only these. Nothing else from this category.

And then even within these four, an exception: **فَمَنِ ٱضْطُرَّ** — "whoever is **compelled by necessity**." The verb is Form VIII passive — *iḍṭurra* — "he **was forced**." Not "he chose desperation." Not "he decided he was in need." He was **compelled** — the passive voice removes his agency. Necessity came to *him*. He didn't go looking for it.

And the two conditions: **غَيْرَ بَاغٍ وَلَا عَادٍ** — "neither desiring it nor transgressing." *Bāghin* — not seeking it out of desire. *'Ādin* — not going beyond what survival requires. Under those conditions: **فَلَآ إِثْمَ عَلَيْهِ** — "there is no sin upon him."

[PAUSE]

What does this mean, felt at the level of the heart rather than the mind?

It means that Allah's relationship with prohibition is **reluctant**. He doesn't want to forbid things from you. The list is short because the default is permission. The earth was spread before you in 2:168 — "eat from whatever is on earth, lawful and good." The ocean of permission is vast. The islands of prohibition are four. And even on those islands, if a storm drives you there against your will, you are forgiven.

And the passage closes the mercy clause with: **إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ غَفُورٌ رَّحِيمٌ** — "Indeed, Allah is *Ghafūr* — Most Forgiving — and *Raḥīm* — Merciful." Both intensive forms. Not "He forgives sometimes." Not "He is merciful on occasion." *Ghafūr* — the *fa'ūl* pattern — forgiving as an overflowing, overwhelming quality. *Raḥīm* — the *fa'īl* pattern — merciful as a permanent essential nature.

This is the backdrop against which 2:174-175 must be read. The mercy is *this* vast. The permission is *this* generous. The exceptions are *this* gracious. And still — **still** — there are people who take this ocean of generosity and sell it for a handful of coins. **فَمَآ أَصْبَرَهُمْ عَلَى ٱلنَّارِ** — how patient they must be, to walk away from this much mercy and toward fire.

The sarcasm only works because the mercy is so vast. If the religion were oppressive — if the prohibition list were long, if the exceptions were rare, if the tone were harsh — then trading it for something else might be understandable. But the religion is *this* generous. And they traded it anyway. *For a small price.*

That's what makes Allah's mock amazement so devastating. It's not the anger of a tyrant whose subjects defected. It's the bewilderment of a Giver whose gift was sold in a pawn shop.

## CLOSING SYNTHESIS

### The Architecture

The five ayahs trace a deliberate mirror structure:

**2:172** — **Eat the good things.** Believers addressed. The provision is from Allah (*razaqnākum*). The response should be gratitude (*ushkurū*). The condition: if it's really Him you worship.

**2:173** — **The short prohibition list.** Only four items. Even those are exempted under necessity. Allah is *Ghafūr Raḥīm*.

**2:174** — **The inversion.** Those who conceal the Book and sell it. Their eating becomes fire. Allah's speech becomes silence. Purification becomes absence. Punishment becomes permanent.

**2:175** — **The exchange.** Guidance for error. Forgiveness for punishment. And the devastating ta'ajjub: *How patient they are for the Fire.*

**2:176** — **The root cause.** Allah sent down the Book **in truth** (*bil-ḥaqq*). Those who disagree about it are in *shiqāqin ba'īd* — extreme, far-reaching dissension.

The mirror: **2:172-173 show what the religion gives** — good food, short prohibitions, generous exceptions, forgiveness, mercy. **2:174-175 show what these people traded it for** — fire in their bellies, divine silence, no purification, painful punishment. The two sides of the passage are an invoice: on the left, what they received from Allah; on the right, what they exchanged it for. And every single line on the right side is worse than what was on the left.

### Questions to Carry

- The conditional *in* — "if it is Him you worship" — challenges every believer. What would your daily gratitude level reveal about who you are *actually* worshipping? Not who you claim to worship on Friday. Who your weekday behavior suggests you worship.

- Is there a truth you know from the Book that you are softening, hiding, or declining to speak because the price of speaking it is too high? What is the *thaman* — the price — you're collecting for that silence? And is it *qalīl*?

- The prohibition list is four items. Four. The permission list is everything else on earth. Do you live as though the religion is generous — or as though it is restrictive? Which version of Islam are you carrying in your daily experience?

- **فَمَآ أَصْبَرَهُمْ عَلَى ٱلنَّارِ** — How patient are *you* for the consequences of the small trades you're making? What are you "enduring" that you don't need to endure, because the mercy was always available and you walked past it?

---

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Allah made almost everything permissible and only four things forbidden — and even those He exempted under genuine need — but there are people who sell this ocean of mercy for a handful of coins, filling their bellies with fire while God watches in divine irony: how patient they must be for what is coming.**

---

### Closing Invitation

The next time you sit down to eat — anything, anywhere — remember the *in*. **If** it is Him you worship. The meal is a test of *tawḥīd*. Not because the food is complicated but because the gratitude reveals the heart.

And the next time you are tempted to soften a truth, bury a ruling, or trade clarity for comfort — remember the price. It is always *qalīl*. Permanently, essentially, inherently small. No matter how large it looks in the moment. Because what you are trading is infinite, and what you are receiving is not.

The mercy is vast. The prohibitions are few. The exceptions are generous. The door is open.

Walk through it. Don't sell it.
