# Their Hearts Look Alike
## When "Show Me a Sign" Is Not Really a Question
### Al-Baqarah 2:118-119

---

[ayah:2:118] وَقَالَ ٱلَّذِينَ لَا يَعْلَمُونَ لَوْلَا يُكَلِّمُنَا ٱللَّهُ أَوْ تَأْتِينَآ ءَايَةٌ ۗ كَذَٰلِكَ قَالَ ٱلَّذِينَ مِن قَبْلِهِم مِّثْلَ قَوْلِهِمْ ۘ تَشَـٰبَهَتْ قُلُوبُهُمْ ۗ قَدْ بَيَّنَّا ٱلْـَٔايَـٰتِ لِقَوْمٍ يُوقِنُونَ

[ayah:2:119] إِنَّآ أَرْسَلْنَـٰكَ بِٱلْحَقِّ بَشِيرًا وَنَذِيرًا ۖ وَلَا تُسْـَٔلُ عَنْ أَصْحَـٰبِ ٱلْجَحِيمِ

*Wa-qāla alladhīna lā ya'lamūna lawlā yukallimuna Allāhu aw ta'tīnā āyah. Kadhālika qāla alladhīna min qablihim mithla qawlihim. Tashābahat qulūbuhum. Qad bayyannā al-āyāti li-qawmin yūqinūn. Innā arsalnāka bil-haqqi bashīran wa-nadhīrā. Wa-lā tus'alu 'an aṣḥābi al-jaḥīm.*

**And those who have no knowledge said, "Why does Allah not speak to us, or a sign come to us?" Thus spoke those before them — words similar to theirs. Their hearts resemble one another. We have already made the signs clear for a people who attain certainty. Indeed, We have sent you with the truth as a bringer of glad tidings and a warner. And you will not be asked about the companions of the Hellfire.**

---

## INTRODUCTION

There is a demand that every generation makes. It wears different clothing in different centuries, but the skeleton underneath is always the same.

In seventh-century Medina, it sounded like: "Why doesn't God just talk to us directly? Why doesn't a miraculous sign come to us?"

In the twenty-first century, it sounds like: "If God is real, why doesn't He just show Himself? Why doesn't He write His name in the sky? Why can't I get a direct message instead of having to trust a book from fourteen hundred years ago?"

The demand feels reasonable. It feels intellectual. It feels like the honest question of someone who wants to believe but simply needs more evidence. And that is precisely why the Quran's response to it is so unsettling — because the Quran does not treat this as a reasonable question. It treats it as a **symptom**.

The Quran has just spent eighty verses narrating the history of Bani Isra'il — a nation that received direct divine speech, that saw miraculous signs, that witnessed the sea split and the mountain lifted and manna fall from the sky. And did those signs produce permanent faith? They did not. Every sign was followed by a new demand. Every miracle was followed by a new complaint.

So when a new generation stands in Medina and says, "Why doesn't God speak to us?" — the Quran has already built the case for its diagnosis. The answer is not: "Here is why God does not speak to you." The answer is a three-word sentence that should haunt every person who has ever made this demand:

**تَشَـٰبَهَتْ قُلُوبُهُمْ** — *tashābahat qulūbuhum* — "Their hearts resemble one another."

The problem is not the absence of signs. The problem is the condition of the heart that receives them.

## PART 1: THE LINGUISTIC JOURNEY

### Element 1: يُكَلِّمُنَا — The Demand for Intensive, Personal Speech

The demand begins with a specific verb that reveals the nature of what they are actually asking for.

<!-- ayah:2:118 partial --> لَوْلَا يُكَلِّمُنَا ٱللَّهُ

*Lawlā yukallimuna Allāh.*

"Why does Allah not *yukallimunā* — speak to us?"

The verb is *yukallimunā* — Form II of the root *kāf-lām-mīm* (ك-ل-م). In Arabic, Form I of this root (*kalama*) carries a simpler meaning — in some dialects and usages it can even mean to wound (a *kalm* is a wound). But Form II — *kallama, yukallim* — means to speak with someone **directly, thoroughly, personally**. The doubling of the middle letter (the *tashdīd* on the *lām*) adds intensity and thoroughness. This is not "say a word to us." This is "address us directly, personally, with sustained and repeated speech."

They are not asking for a hint. They are asking for a conversation. They want Allah to sit down — metaphorically — and talk to them the way one person talks to another. Directly. Personally. Face to face.

And the particle *lawlā* at the beginning is not a simple question. *Lawlā* is a particle of demand and reproach — "why doesn't...?" "shouldn't...?" It carries impatience. It carries entitlement. It is the tone of someone who believes they deserve something and is demanding to know why it has been withheld.

So the demand is: "Why doesn't God speak to us intensively and personally? Why are we getting a human messenger instead of a divine conversation?"

[PAUSE]

The demand sounds reasonable until you remember who is making it. The Quran identifies them at the start: **ٱلَّذِينَ لَا يَعْلَمُونَ** — *alladhīna lā ya'lamūn* — "those who do not know." The Quran has labelled their demand before they make it. These are not seekers who have exhausted every avenue. These are people who "do not know" — and their not-knowing is not ignorance waiting to be cured. It is the same condition described in 2:113 with the same phrase. Their not-knowing is a posture, not a deficit.

### Element 2: تَشَـٰبَهَتْ قُلُوبُهُمْ — The Diagnosis That Crosses Centuries

This is the line that changes everything in the passage. Three words. A complete diagnosis.

<!-- ayah:2:118 partial --> تَشَـٰبَهَتْ قُلُوبُهُمْ

*Tashābahat qulūbuhum.*

"Their hearts **resemble one another**."

Let's sit with the verb. *Tashābahat* is Form VI of the root *shīn-bā-hā* (ش-ب-ه), which means to resemble, to look alike. Form VI — *tafā'ala* — is the form of **mutual action**. When two or more parties do something to each other simultaneously, Arabic uses Form VI. *Taqātala* means they fought each other. *Tashāraka* means they shared with each other. *Tashābaha* means they **resembled each other** — a mutual, reciprocal likeness.

But who is resembling whom? Look at the context. The Quran just said: "Thus spoke those before them — words similar to theirs." The "before them" are the previous generations — Bani Isra'il who demanded to see God openly (2:55), who complained about the food (2:61), who questioned Musa endlessly about the cow (2:67-71). And "them" are the present demanders in Medina.

The hearts of those ancient generations and the hearts of this present generation **tashābahat** — they mutually resemble each other across time. The resemblance is not just in one direction. It is reciprocal. The ancient hearts look like the present ones, and the present ones look like the ancient ones. They are mirrors of each other separated by centuries.

[PAUSE]

And notice: the Quran says their **hearts** resemble each other. Not their words. Not their arguments. Not their cultures. Their **قُلُوب** (*qulūb*) — their hearts.

This is the Quran performing its deepest diagnostic move. The claim "why doesn't God speak to us?" sounds like an intellectual objection. It presents itself as a product of the mind — a rational demand for evidence. But the Quran looks past the words and into the organ that generated them, and it says: this is not an intellectual problem. This is a **cardiac** problem. The heart that produces this demand in the year 2026 is structurally identical to the heart that produced it in the year 1400 BCE. The centuries changed. The languages changed. The arguments changed. **The heart did not change.**

This is why more evidence will never resolve the demand. You cannot fix a heart problem by giving the brain more data. The person who says "show me a sign" does not actually need a sign. They need a heart transplant. They need the cardiac condition treated. The sign was never the missing ingredient. The willingness to receive the sign was.

[PAUSE]

And the word *qulūb* — hearts — begins with *qāf*, the deepest guttural consonant in Arabic. It is produced at the very back of the throat, almost at the point where sound meets breath. The heart in Arabic does not start with a soft sound. It starts at the bottom of the human vocal apparatus. The heart is not a surface organ. It is the deepest thing in you.

### Element 3: يُوقِنُونَ — The People for Whom the Signs Are Enough

After the diagnosis comes the prescription — or rather, the clarification of who does NOT need the prescription:

<!-- ayah:2:118 partial --> قَدْ بَيَّنَّا ٱلْـَٔايَـٰتِ لِقَوْمٍ يُوقِنُونَ

*Qad bayyannā al-āyāti li-qawmin yūqinūn.*

"We have **already** made the signs clear for a people who **attain certainty**."

Three things are happening here.

First: **قَدْ** (*qad*) before the perfect tense verb. This particle means "already, certainly, indeed." The signs have **already** been made clear. The Quran is not promising future clarification. It is declaring that the clarification has already happened. The objectors are not waiting for signs that haven't arrived. They are failing to see signs that have already been placed before them.

Second: **بَيَّنَّا** (*bayyannā*) — "We made clear." Form II of *bā-yā-nūn*. Form II intensifies: this is not "the signs happened to become clear." This is "We **actively, thoroughly, deliberately** made them clear." Allah did not leave the signs ambiguous. He did the work of clarification. The responsibility for seeing is now on the viewer, not the Displayer.

Third — and this is the key: **لِقَوْمٍ يُوقِنُونَ** — "for a people who *yūqinūn*." The verb is Form IV of the root *yā-qāf-nūn* (ي-ق-ن). Form IV is causative: to **bring about** certainty in oneself, to **attain** yaqīn through engagement. This is not passive certainty — the kind you're born with. This is active certainty — the kind you arrive at because you brought your full capacity to the encounter.

The signs are made clear — but only for people who bring the prerequisite. And the prerequisite is not intelligence, not scholarship, not piety in the conventional sense. It is *yaqīn* — the willingness to be convinced. The open cardiac condition that says: "If the evidence is clear, I will accept it, even if it disrupts everything I currently believe."

The ones who say "why doesn't God speak to us?" are not people of *yaqīn*. They are people whose hearts *tashābahat* — who carry the same cardiac condition as every generation that was shown a sign and demanded another one.

[PAUSE]

## PART 2: THE THEMATIC DEPTHS

### Theme 1: The Sign Was Never the Problem

There is a thought experiment that reveals the truth of this ayah faster than any commentary.

Imagine God wrote His name across the sky tonight. Letters of fire stretching from horizon to horizon. Every language simultaneously. Unmistakable. Undeniable. The greatest sign in the history of the world.

What would happen the next morning?

Someone would call it a weather phenomenon. Someone would say it was a government projection. Someone would start a podcast called "Debunking the Sky Writing." Someone would say: "Okay, but that doesn't prove which religion is right." Someone else: "That could have been any god." And by the third day, someone would say: "Why doesn't God do something *else*? That sign was good, but I need more."

This is not cynicism. This is the Quran's reading of human history, documented across eighty verses of Bani Isra'il's story. They saw the sea split — and then worshipped a calf. They saw manna fall from heaven — and then said, "We can't stand one food." They heard the divine speech at Sinai — and then said, "Show us God openly." Every sign was followed by a new demand, because **the signs were never the problem**.

The Quran's diagnosis — *tashābahat qulūbuhum* — is radical in what it implies. It implies that the demand for evidence is, in certain cases, not actually a demand for evidence at all. It is a **cardiac posture** that uses the language of evidence-seeking to avoid the vulnerability of submission.

Modern psychology has a concept that maps onto this: **motivated reasoning**. The phenomenon where a person's emotional commitments determine which evidence they accept and which they reject — with the entire process feeling, from the inside, like honest intellectual inquiry. The person genuinely believes they are "just following the evidence." But the evidence they follow was pre-selected by a heart that already decided what it was willing to accept.

The Quran saw this fourteen centuries ago and named it with surgical precision. The problem is not the evidence. The problem is the heart. *Tashābahat qulūbuhum* — the hearts across generations look alike because the hearts across generations are performing the same function: using the demand for signs as a substitute for the surrender of submission.

[PAUSE]

This should unsettle anyone — including every Muslim — who has ever thought: "If only I could see a clear miracle, my faith would be stronger." The Quran is saying: people who saw the clearest miracles in human history were not strengthened by them. The sea split and they still wavered. The mountain was lifted and they still broke their covenant. The signs came — and the hearts remained the same.

The question is not: "Are the signs clear enough?" The Quran answers that: *qad bayyannā al-āyāt* — We have already made them clear. The question is: "Is your heart in the condition to receive them?"

And that question cannot be answered by more evidence. It can only be answered by the hard, interior work of cultivating *yaqīn* — the willingness to be convinced, the readiness to submit when the truth becomes clear, even when submission costs you something.

[PAUSE — major pause]

### Theme 2: You Will Not Be Asked — The Prophet's Release

After the diagnosis, the Quran does something unexpected. It turns away from the objectors entirely and faces the Prophet ﷺ.

<!-- ayah:2:119 partial --> إِنَّآ أَرْسَلْنَـٰكَ بِٱلْحَقِّ بَشِيرًا وَنَذِيرًا

*Innā arsalnāka bil-haqqi bashīran wa-nadhīrā.*

"Indeed, **We** have sent **you** with the truth, as a bringer of glad tidings and a warner."

The pronouns shift. For most of this section, the Quran has been narrating in the third person — "they said," "those before them," "their hearts." Now: *innā* (We) and *-ka* (you). First person divine and second person prophetic. The most intimate pronoun pairing in the Quran. This is Allah speaking directly to His Messenger.

And what does He say? He says: You are sent *bil-haqq* — with the truth. Not "with some of the truth" or "with a version of the truth." With **the** truth. Definite article. Complete.

And your role is defined by two words in the *fa'īl* intensive pattern: **بَشِيرًا** (*bashīran*) — a permanent bringer of glad tidings. **وَنَذِيرًا** (*nadhīran*) — a permanent warner. These are not temporary assignments. The *fa'īl* pattern marks them as your identity. You ARE a bashīr. You ARE a nadhīr. This is not what you do. It is what you are.

And then — the release:

<!-- ayah:2:119 partial --> وَلَا تُسْـَٔلُ عَنْ أَصْحَـٰبِ ٱلْجَحِيمِ

*Wa-lā tus'alu 'an aṣḥābi al-jaḥīm.*

"And you will not be asked about the companions of the Hellfire."

This sentence is the closing gift of the entire Bani Isra'il section. For eighty verses, the Prophet ﷺ has been carrying the weight of narrating their failures, their objections, their refusals. He has been hearing "why doesn't God speak to us?" and "show us God openly" and "we can't stand one food." He has been facing communities that know the truth and reject it out of envy. He has been watching hearts that resemble each other across centuries in their resistance.

And now Allah says: you are not responsible for them. You delivered the truth. You brought the glad tidings and the warning. What they do with it — that is between them and Me. On the Day of Judgment, you will not be questioned about the people who chose the Fire.

Notice the word **أَصْحَـٰبِ** (*aṣḥāb*) — companions. Not "residents" or "inhabitants." *Ṣāḥib* means companion, close associate, someone you are inseparable from. The people of the Fire are its *aṣḥāb* — its companions. They do not just end up there. They **belong** there, in the way a companion belongs with the one they accompany. The relationship is permanent and self-chosen.

[PAUSE]

This theme of release carries a lesson that extends far beyond the Prophet's specific situation.

Every person who carries a message — a teacher, a parent, a da'iyah, a counselor, a friend who tries to guide — eventually hits the wall described in 2:118. They encounter the person whose demand for evidence is not really a demand for evidence. The person whose heart *tashābahat* — resembles every other resistant heart in history. The person who, no matter what you show them, will say: "But why doesn't God just..."

And the temptation for the carrier of truth is to absorb responsibility for the result. To feel that if only they argued better, presented more clearly, found the right angle — the person would accept. To carry the weight of another person's rejection as if it were their own failure.

The Quran says to the greatest human being who ever lived: *wa-lā tus'alu 'an aṣḥābi al-jaḥīm*. You will not be asked about them. If the Prophet ﷺ — the most eloquent, the most compassionate, the most perfectly equipped messenger in history — is told he is not responsible for other people's rejection, then neither are you.

Your job is *bashīr wa-nadhīr* — to bring the good news and the warning. Their job is to have the heart that receives it. And when the hearts *tashābaha* — when they mirror the resistance of every generation before them — that is not your failure. That is their cardiac condition. And it is between them and the One who made the signs clear.

## CLOSING SYNTHESIS

### The Architecture

These two ayahs close the Bani Isra'il section (2:40-119) with a structure that mirrors the entire section in miniature:

**2:118a — THE DEMAND:** "Why doesn't God speak to us or give us a sign?" The same demand that drove sixty-five verses of Bani Isra'il complaints.

**2:118b — THE DIAGNOSIS:** *Tashābahat qulūbuhum.* Their hearts resemble each other. The demand is not new, the problem is not informational, and the cure is not more signs. The cure is a different heart.

**2:118c — THE CLARIFICATION:** *Qad bayyannā al-āyāt.* The signs are already clear — for those who bring the cardiac prerequisite of yaqīn.

**2:119 — THE RELEASE:** The Prophet is sent with truth, as bashīr and nadhīr. And he is released from accountability for those who choose the Fire.

The section that began with *Yā Banī Isrā'īl udhkurū ni'matī* (2:40 — "O Children of Israel, remember My favor") ends with *wa-lā tus'alu 'an aṣḥābi al-jaḥīm* ("you will not be asked about the companions of the Fire"). It began with a call to remember. It ends with permission to let go.

### Questions to Carry

- When you say "I just need more evidence" — about God, about a ruling, about a direction in life — is it really about the evidence? Or is it about the heart that evaluates the evidence?

- Where does your heart *tashābaha* — resemble the hearts of those who came before? Where are you making the same demand as every generation, using updated language?

- Is there someone in your life whose rejection you carry as your own failure — when *wa-lā tus'alu* was spoken to someone far greater than you about a burden far heavier?

- What would it look like to bring the heart of *yaqīn* — genuine willingness to be convinced — to the signs that are already clear around you?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The demand for a sign is not a question seeking an answer — it is a cardiac condition that repeats in every generation, and the cure was never more evidence but rather a heart willing to arrive at certainty with the signs already given.**

### The Closing Invitation

These ayahs invite two things simultaneously.

For the seeker who carries genuine questions: know that the signs are already clear — *qad bayyannā al-āyāt*. The work is not to find more signs. The work is to cultivate the heart that can receive them. Bring the willingness to be convinced, and the conviction will come. That is what *yūqinūn* means — people who arrive at certainty because they brought the capacity for it.

For the carrier of truth — the teacher, the parent, the friend, the da'iyah — who is exhausted by resistance: know that the greatest Messenger in history was told he would not be asked about those who chose the Fire. Your job is *bashīr* and *nadhīr*. Their job is their heart. And when the hearts across generations look alike, that is a pattern older than you — a cardiac condition that no single human, no matter how eloquent, was ever designed to cure.

You deliver. He heals. And between the delivery and the healing is a space that belongs only to Him.

### Du'a

*Allāhumma, do not let our hearts tashābaha — do not let them resemble the hearts of those who demanded signs and were never satisfied. Give us the cardiac condition of yaqīn — the willingness to be convinced by what You have already made clear. Protect every bashīr and nadhīr among us from carrying the weight of other people's refusal. And when the demand inside us says "show me more" — give us the honesty to ask whether the problem is the signs or the organ that receives them.*
