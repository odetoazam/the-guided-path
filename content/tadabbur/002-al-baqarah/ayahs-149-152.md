# Remember Me and I Will Remember You
## The Triple Command, the Completed Favor, and the Most Reciprocal Promise in the Quran
### Al-Baqarah 2:149-152

---

## INTRODUCTION

The qiblah has been commanded three times now.

First in 2:144 — the intimate grant: *We saw your face turning, so We will turn you to a qiblah you love.* Second in 2:149 — the universal application: *from wherever you depart.* And now, third, in 2:150 — the same command again, with the reasons laid bare: *so that people have no argument, so that I may complete My favor, and so that you may be guided.*

Three times. The same direction. The same face. The same Ka'bah.

Why three?

Because each repetition carries a different weight. The first was about the Prophet's longing being fulfilled — personal, emotional, intimate. The second was about geography — wherever you are, face this direction. The third is about purpose — this direction is given to **complete** something. A favor that has been building since Ibrahim stood in a valley and lifted stones while praying *Rabbanā taqabbal minnā*.

And then — after the direction is established, after the favor is named, after the messenger is identified as the answer to Ibrahim's ancient prayer — comes a single sentence that has sustained more dhikr circles, more whispered prayers, more 3 AM conversations with Allah than perhaps any other sentence in the Quran:

**فَٱذْكُرُونِىٓ أَذْكُرْكُمْ**

"Remember Me — I will remember you."

Six words in Arabic. Two verbs. Same root. Opposite directions. You remember Him → He remembers you. The God of the heavens and the earth — the One who said *kun fa-yakūn* — promises to **remember you** if you remember Him.

## PART 1: THE LINGUISTIC JOURNEY

### Element 1: وَلِأُتِمَّ نِعْمَتِى — The Favor Being Completed

Hidden in 2:150, among the reasons for the qiblah command, is a phrase that reaches all the way back to the beginning of the surah:

<!-- ayah:2:150 partial --> وَلِأُتِمَّ نِعْمَتِى عَلَيْكُمْ

*Wa-li-utimma ni'matī 'alaykum.*

"And so that I may **complete** My favor upon you."

The verb is *utimma* — Form IV of *tā-mīm-mīm* (ت-م-م): to bring to completion, to bring to fullness, to finalize. This is the same root that described Ibrahim in 2:124: *fa-atammahunna* — he completed the words of his Lord. Ibrahim completed his test. Now Allah is completing His favor.

And the word **نِعْمَتِى** — *ni'matī* — "**My** favor." First person singular possessive. The favor is not abstract. It is Allah's, personally. And it is being brought to completion — not initiated, not halfway done, but **completed** — through the qiblah command.

What is this favor? Look at the trajectory. Allah chose Ibrahim (2:124). Ibrahim raised the Ka'bah (2:127). Ibrahim prayed for a messenger (2:129). The messenger was sent (2:151). The qiblah was turned toward the Ka'bah (2:144-150). Each step is a brick in the same building. And the qiblah — the command to face the house that Ibrahim built — is the **completion** of the favor. The arc that began with one man lifting stones now reaches its full circle: the entire ummah turns toward what he built, led by the messenger he prayed for.

The favor is not the Ka'bah. The favor is not the qiblah. The favor is the **completion** — the fact that everything connects, everything was planned, everything from Ibrahim's du'a to Muhammad's mission to the ummah's direction was one continuous arc of divine favor being brought to its *tamām* — its fullness.

### Element 2: فَلَا تَخْشَوْهُمْ وَٱخْشَوْنِى — The Pivot of Fear

In the middle of 2:150, a command appears that should make every listener sit up:

<!-- ayah:2:150 partial --> فَلَا تَخْشَوْهُمْ وَٱخْشَوْنِى

*Fa-lā takhshawhum wa-khshawnī.*

"So do not fear **them** — but fear **Me**."

The verb is *khashiya* — not *khāfa*. In Arabic, *khawf* is simple fear — the fear you feel when threatened. *Khashyah* is deeper — it is reverential awe, the fear that comes from knowing the greatness of the one you are afraid of. *Khashyah* is what a student feels before a great teacher. What a creation feels before its Creator. It is fear mixed with awe, respect, and awareness of magnitude.

The Quran uses the SAME verb for both the prohibition and the command. Don't have *khashyah* of them → have *khashyah* of Me. The verb doesn't change. Only the **object** changes. The type of fear stays the same — what shifts is where you direct it.

And notice the pronoun pivot: **هُمْ** (them, 3rd person plural) → **نِى** (Me, 1st person singular). The people you are tempted to fear — they are spoken of in the third person, at a distance. But Allah says **Me** — first person, direct, intimate. The fear that was going toward a distant "them" is redirected toward an intimate "Me."

This is one of the most psychologically liberating commands in the Quran. Because the fear of "them" — what people will say, how they will criticize, whether they will accept you — is one of the most paralyzing forces in human life. And the Quran says: take all that reverential energy and point it at Me. I am the only one who deserves your *khashyah*. They are not worth it.

### Element 3: فَٱذْكُرُونِىٓ أَذْكُرْكُمْ — The Six Words That Changed Everything

And then — the climax. Not just of this passage but of the entire trajectory from 2:40 to here. After one hundred and twelve verses of Bani Isra'il, Ibrahim, the qiblah, the direction, the face, the favor — it all arrives at this:

<!-- ayah:2:152 partial --> فَٱذْكُرُونِىٓ أَذْكُرْكُمْ

*Fa-dhkurūnī adhkurkum.*

"Remember Me — I will remember you."

Let me show you what is happening in the grammar, because the grammar is the theology.

**فَٱذْكُرُونِىٓ** (*fa-dhkurūnī*) — "so remember Me." This is an imperative: *udhkurū* (remember!, 2nd person masculine plural) + *nī* (Me, 1st person singular object pronoun with the protective *nūn al-wiqāyah*). You, all of you, remember **Me**.

**أَذْكُرْكُمْ** (*adhkurkum*) — "I will remember you." This is a jussive (*majzūm*) — the verb in the response to a conditional/imperative construction: *adhkur* (I remember, 1st person singular) + *kum* (you all, 2nd person masculine plural object pronoun). **I** remember **you**.

The root is *dhāl-kāf-rā* (ذ-ك-ر) — to remember, to mention, to be mindful of. The same root. The same Form I. The same basic action. On both sides of the equation.

But look at the pronouns. In the first half: **you** → **Me**. In the second half: **I** → **you**. The pronouns swap. The direction reverses. The one who was the subject becomes the object, and the one who was the object becomes the subject. You act on Him → He acts on you.

[PAUSE]

The grammatical symmetry is perfect. But the theological reality is not symmetrical at all — and that is where the beauty hides.

When you remember Allah, what does that involve? You say *subḥān Allāh* on your drive to work. You pause before eating and say *bismillāh*. You turn your face in prayer. You read a page of Quran. You mention Him in a gathering. Your remembrance is human: limited, distracted, intermittent, imperfect.

When **Allah** remembers **you** — what does that involve? The hadith qudsi answers: "If he remembers Me in himself, I remember him in Myself. If he remembers Me in a gathering, I remember him in a gathering better than it." Allah's remembrance of you means: He mentions you. By name. Among the angels. In the highest assembly. The God who holds the heavens in place **speaks about you** because you spoke about Him.

The grammar says: equal exchange. The reality says: the most lopsided deal in the history of creation. You give five minutes of dhikr. He gives you mention before the angels. You whisper His name in a quiet moment. He declares yours in a gathering you cannot imagine.

[PAUSE]

And the word *dhikr* itself — remember, mention, be mindful. It is not just verbal. *Dhikr* in Arabic encompasses: remembering in the heart (awareness), remembering with the tongue (mentioning), and remembering with the limbs (acting in accordance). When Allah says *udhkurūnī*, He is not saying "say My name more often." He is saying: **be aware of Me**. In your heart. On your tongue. In your actions. In the quiet moments and the loud ones. In the easy times and the hard ones. Let Me be the background frequency of your consciousness.

And then: **وَٱشْكُرُوا۟ لِى وَلَا تَكْفُرُونِ** — "And be grateful to Me, and do not be ungrateful to Me."

The pairing is *shukr* (gratitude) and *kufr* (ingratitude/denial). In this context, *kufr* is not theological disbelief — it is the opposite of *shukr*. The one who does not remember is not necessarily a disbeliever in the theological sense. They are an **ingrate** — someone who received the favor and forgot the Giver. The opposite of remembrance is not atheism. It is ingratitude. And the opposite of gratitude is not doubt. It is forgetting.

[PAUSE]

## PART 2: THE THEMATIC DEPTHS

### Theme 1: The Completed Favor — And What It Means That You Are Standing Inside It

There is a phenomenon in human psychology called **hedonic adaptation** — the tendency for the extraordinary to become ordinary through repetition. The first time you see the ocean, you gasp. The thousandth time, you glance and keep walking. The first time you hear the Quran, something stirs in your chest. The thousandth time, it might be background noise while you cook.

The Quran has just traced a favor that spans millennia. Ibrahim's du'a (2:129) → the messenger sent (2:151) → the qiblah established (2:144-150) → the favor completed (2:150). This is a favor that was conceived when the Ka'bah was being built, gestated across centuries of prophetic history, and delivered in the person of Muhammad ﷺ and the direction of prayer toward the house his ancestor raised.

And you — standing in prayer, facing that direction, reciting the Book that messenger brought — are standing inside the completed favor. You are the *ni'mah* being described. You are the answer to Ibrahim's du'a. You are the ummah that was prayed for while the mortar was still wet.

*Wa-li-utimma ni'matī 'alaykum.* So that I may **complete** My favor upon you.

The favor is complete. You are inside it. And the Quran's concern — expressed in the very next ayah — is that you might forget:

*Fa-dhkurūnī adhkurkum. Wa-shkurū lī wa-lā takfurūn.*

Remember Me. Be grateful. Do not be among the ingrates who stand inside a completed favor and forget who completed it.

[PAUSE — major pause]

### Theme 2: The Most Lopsided Deal in Creation

There is no relationship in human experience that maps onto what 2:152 describes. But there is one that comes close enough to carry the weight.

Imagine a child — two years old — who makes a card for their parent. Crayon scribbles. Illegible letters. Glitter glued to the wrong side. The card is, by any objective standard, a mess.

And the parent receives it — and weeps. Not because the card is beautiful. Because the child **thought of them**. The act of remembering was the gift. The quality of the card is irrelevant. The parent puts it on the refrigerator — the most visible surface in the house — and shows it to everyone who visits.

*Fa-dhkurūnī adhkurkum.*

Your dhikr is the crayon card. His dhikr of you is the refrigerator — except the refrigerator is the highest heaven, and the visitors are the angels, and the parent is the Lord of the worlds.

The deal is absurd in your favor. You give Him a whispered *subḥān Allāh* during your commute — and He mentions you before the *mala'* (the highest assembly). You remember Him for thirty seconds in your du'a — and He remembers you with a remembrance that is beyond your capacity to comprehend. The exchange is not equal. It was never meant to be equal. It is a transaction where the **generosity of the Receiver** is the entire point.

And this is why the ayah is so devastating in its simplicity. It does not say "worship Me and I will reward you." It does not say "obey Me and I will protect you." It says: **remember Me** — just be aware of Me, just keep Me in your consciousness — **and I will remember you**. The entry fee to divine remembrance is not perfection. It is not scholarship. It is not sainthood. It is **awareness**. Just remember. And He will remember.

[PAUSE]

The practical implication is almost too simple to believe. The next time you say *alḥamdulillāh* after eating — Allah remembers you. The next time you say *subḥān Allāh* at something beautiful — Allah remembers you. The next time you wake up for fajr and your first thought is of Him — Allah remembers you. The next time you resist a sin because you remembered He is watching — Allah remembers you.

You do not need to be perfect to be remembered by God. You need to **remember**.

## CLOSING SYNTHESIS

### The Architecture

These four verses close the qiblah section with a descending structure from the external to the internal:

**2:149 — DIRECTION (external, geographical):** From wherever you go out — turn your face.
**2:150 — PURPOSE (institutional, communal):** So that people have no argument, and so that the favor is completed.
**2:151 — MISSION (prophetic, connecting to Ibrahim):** Just as We sent a messenger — reciting, purifying, teaching.
**2:152 — RELATIONSHIP (internal, personal):** Remember Me. I will remember you.

The arc descends from the widest scale (geography — wherever you go) to the most intimate scale (the interior of your consciousness — remember Me). The qiblah is external. The remembrance is internal. And the section that began with the direction of the body ends with the direction of the heart.

And 2:152 answers a question that has been implicit since 2:40: what does Allah actually want from you? After one hundred and twelve verses of history, legislation, covenant, and correction — what is the bottom line?

**Remember Me. I will remember you. Be grateful. Don't be ungrateful.**

That is the bottom line. Not complicated theology. Not intricate law. **Remember the One who completed His favor upon you.** And He will remember you with a remembrance that dwarfs anything you can imagine.

### Questions to Carry

- When you stand in prayer facing the Ka'bah — do you feel that you are standing inside a completed favor that began with Ibrahim lifting stones?

- Where is your *khashyah* currently directed — toward "them" (what people think, say, and approve of) or toward Him?

- If *fa-dhkurūnī adhkurkum* means that every moment of awareness of Allah is reciprocated — how many moments of reciprocated divine remembrance are you generating each day?

- The Quran paired shukr (gratitude) with its opposite kufr (ingratitude) — not with doubt. Where in your life are you standing inside a blessing and forgetting the Giver?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The direction of your body in prayer is the completion of a favor that began with Ibrahim — but the direction of your heart in remembrance is the deal that makes the entire favor worthwhile: remember Him, and the Lord of the worlds will remember you.**

### The Closing Invitation

The qiblah section is over. The direction is established. The face is turned. The favor is complete. And the One who completed it asks for one thing:

*Fa-dhkurūnī.*

Remember Me. That is it. Not with perfection. Not with scholarly eloquence. Not with hours of isolation. Just: remember. Be aware. Let Me into your consciousness. Let your heart turn the way your face turns — toward the direction where I am.

And then: *adhkurkum*. I will remember you.

The crayon card is enough.

### Du'a

*Allāhumma, You completed a favor upon us that began when Ibrahim was lifting stones and praying. We are standing inside that favor — and most of the time we forget. Fa-dhkurūnī — You asked to be remembered. Here we are, remembering. With imperfect tongues and distracted hearts and lives that are too busy and too forgetful. But we are remembering. Adhkurnā — remember us. In Yourself. In Your highest assembly. With the remembrance that only You can give. And do not let us be among those who stood inside the completed favor and were ungrateful.*
