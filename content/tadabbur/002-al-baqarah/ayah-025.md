## Introduction

[ayah:2:25] وَبَشِّرِ ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ وَعَمِلُوا۟ ٱلصَّـٰلِحَـٰتِ أَنَّ لَهُمْ جَنَّـٰتٍ تَجْرِى مِن تَحْتِهَا ٱلْأَنْهَـٰرُ ۖ كُلَّمَا رُزِقُوا۟ مِنْهَا مِن ثَمَرَةٍ رِّزْقًا ۙ قَالُوا۟ هَـٰذَا ٱلَّذِى رُزِقْنَا مِن قَبْلُ ۖ وَأُتُوا۟ بِهِۦ مُتَشَـٰبِهًا ۖ وَلَهُمْ فِيهَآ أَزْوَٰجٌ مُّطَهَّرَةٌ ۖ وَهُمْ فِيهَا خَـٰلِدُونَ

*Wa bashshiri lladhīna āmanū wa ʿamilū ṣ-ṣāliḥāti anna lahum jannātin tajrī min taḥtihā l-anhāru, kullamā ruziqū minhā min thamaratin rizqan qālū hādhā lladhī ruziqnā min qablu, wa utū bihī mutashābihan, wa lahum fīhā azwājun muṭahharatun, wa hum fīhā khālidūn.*

And give glad tidings to those who believe and do righteous deeds: that they will have gardens beneath which rivers flow. Every time they are provided from it a fruit as provision, they will say, "This is what we were provided before." And they will be given it in resemblance. And for them therein are purified spouses, and they will abide therein eternally.

---

One verse ago, the Quran described a Fire whose fuel is people and stones — a horror so specific it sits in your chest like a weight. The passage could have ended there. The warning was given. The challenge was issued and pre-emptively declared impossible. The consequence was named. Case closed.

But the Quran never closes on punishment. This is one of its structural signatures — and one of the most overlooked. When Allah describes consequence for the rejectors, the very next breath turns to the believers. Not as an afterthought. As a **design principle**. Because the Quran's purpose is not to terrorize. It is to orient. And orientation requires two poles: what to turn from, and what to turn toward.

So after the Fire — immediately after, without a single word of transition — the voice shifts. Not from warning to comfort. From *crowd* to *one man*. The entire preceding passage was directed at ٱلنَّاسُ — mankind, second person plural. Now: وَبَشِّرِ — "give glad tidings" — second person *singular*. Allah turns from addressing all of humanity to speaking to one person: Muhammad ﷺ. And He says, essentially: *"Now, you — go tell them the good news."*

And what follows is not a catalogue of rewards. It is something far more intimate, far more psychologically strange, and far more beautiful: a description of a place where every fruit you eat triggers a memory of something you've already tasted — something that looks familiar but tastes like it was made for the person you've finally become.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. "Give Glad Tidings" — بَشِّرِ: Why the Prophet Became the Messenger of Joy

وَبَشِّرِ — *wa bashshir*.

This single word carries three layers that translation flattens into one.

First: the root. بشر — *bashara*. This root is about the *skin*, the surface of the body, the part of you that faces the world. The word *basharah* means the outer skin. When the Arabs described the first visible sign of emotion — the flush of joy on someone's face — they used this root. *Bushrā* is glad tidings: news so good it changes your surface, your expression, the way your face meets the world. Before you speak a word, your skin announces the joy.

Second: the form. بَشِّرِ is Form II — the intensive. Not Form I بَشَرَ (to bring news). Form II: بَشَّرَ — to **fill someone with glad tidings**, to saturate them with joy, to deliver the news in a way that doesn't just inform but *transforms their expression*. The doubled shīn in the middle of the word — *bash-shir* — makes the sound itself expansive. Say it aloud: the shīn opens like a smile spreading across a face.

Third: the pronoun. بَشِّرِ is second person singular masculine — *you*, one man. Not بَشِّرُوا (you all, give glad tidings). After twenty-four verses of addressing humanity collectively — يَٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلنَّاسُ, كُنتُمْ, تَفْعَلُوا۟ — Allah suddenly narrows the beam to one individual. Muhammad ﷺ. The warnings were announced to the crowd. The glad tidings are entrusted to one man, personally.

[PAUSE]

Think about what this means. The command to fear the Fire was collective — فَٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱلنَّارَ, "shield yourselves." All of you. But the command to deliver joy is personal — بَشِّرِ, "you, deliver this." The architecture of the Quran assigns warning to the community and good news to a *relationship*. As though joy, unlike fear, requires a personal carrier. Fear can be broadcast. Happiness needs to be *delivered by someone who knows your name.*

This is why the Prophet ﷺ matters — not just as a legislator or a warner, but as the man through whom Allah chose to deliver the most intimate news about the future of every believer. He is not just a messenger of law. He is, in this verse, a messenger of joy. And the Form II imperative tells him: don't just inform them. **Fill them with it.**

[PAUSE]

### 2. The Double Key — ءَامَنُوا۟ وَعَمِلُوا۟ ٱلصَّـٰلِحَـٰتِ

Now look at who receives this glad tiding: ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ وَعَمِلُوا۟ ٱلصَّـٰلِحَـٰتِ — "those who believed AND did righteous deeds."

Two verbs joined by وَ — "and." Not "or." Both are required. And the forms are revealing.

ءَامَنُوا۟ — *āmanū* — Form IV from the root أمن (safety, security, trust). Form IV is causative: they *caused themselves to be in a state of trust*. Belief in the Quran is not a feeling that washes over you. It is an act — a causative act of committing yourself to trust in something you cannot see. The Form IV tells you: these people didn't just feel faith. They **built** it.

وَعَمِلُوا۟ — *ʿamilū* — Form I from عمل. The most basic, most humble form. They worked. They did deeds. Not Form III (worked *with* someone) or Form VIII (exerted themselves intensively). Just Form I. Simple action. Ordinary effort.

Notice the asymmetry: faith is Form IV (elevated, causative, intensive). Deeds are Form I (basic, simple, ordinary). The Quran pairs a lofty inner act with a humble external one. Your belief needs to be deep. Your deeds don't need to be spectacular. The Garden is not reserved for heroes. It is promised to people who believe with conviction and then do *ordinary good* — consistently, persistently, without fanfare.

And the deeds are described as ٱلصَّـٰلِحَـٰتِ — *aṣ-ṣāliḥāt* — the righteous deeds. *Ṣāliḥ* from the root صلح means what is sound, correct, beneficial, in proper working order. A ṣāliḥ deed is not necessarily a dramatic one. It is a *functioning* one — a deed that does what it's supposed to do, that repairs rather than damages, that puts things in their proper place. Fixing a wrong. Keeping a promise. Feeding someone who is hungry. These are not extraordinary. They are *functional*. And the Quran says they are enough for the Garden.

[PAUSE]

### 3. The Shock of Recognition — هَـٰذَا ٱلَّذِى رُزِقْنَا مِن قَبْلُ

Now we arrive at the strangest, most psychologically rich moment in the entire verse.

كُلَّمَا رُزِقُوا۟ مِنْهَا مِن ثَمَرَةٍ رِّزْقًا ۙ قَالُوا۟ هَـٰذَا ٱلَّذِى رُزِقْنَا مِن قَبْلُ ۖ وَأُتُوا۟ بِهِۦ مُتَشَـٰبِهًا

*"Every time they are provided from it a fruit as provision, they say: 'This is what we were provided before.' And they are given it in resemblance."*

Stop here. Read it again.

They eat a fruit in Paradise. And their response is not "this is amazing" or "this is new" or "I've never tasted anything like this." Their response is: **"This is what we were given before."**

Recognition. Not wonder. *Recognition.*

[PAUSE]

The verb رُزِقُوا۟ — *ruziqū* — is passive. "They were provided." Not "they ate" or "they took." The fruit arrives as a *gift* — the giver unnamed, the divine passive preserving the majesty of the One who provides. And this passive appears twice: رُزِقُوا۟ (they were provided — in Paradise) and رُزِقْنَا (we were provided — before). The same passive. The same anonymous provider. The same root. The fruit of Paradise and the fruit of before — both came from the same Source, through the same mechanism: divine provision that arrives without you having to name the giver.

And then: وَأُتُوا۟ بِهِۦ مُتَشَـٰبِهًا — "and they are given it in resemblance." *Mutashābihan* — Form VI active participle from شبه. Form VI is the form of *mutual action*. Not "it resembles" (one-directional) but "it resembles-each-other" (a web of mutual resemblance). The fruits resemble each other. They resemble what came before. The resemblance runs in multiple directions simultaneously.

But here is the question that should haunt us: مِن قَبْلُ — **"from before."** Before *what*?

The classical scholars saw at least three layers here (and this is composite sketch territory — all of them are seeing something real):

**Layer one** (al-Tabari, many companions): "before" means from *this world*. The fruits of Paradise resemble the fruits of dunya — they look the same but taste incomparably better. The mango looks like a mango. The date looks like a date. But the experience is transformed. This reading gives us the principle of **recognition as a gateway** — Paradise doesn't alienate you with the completely foreign. It welcomes you with the hauntingly familiar, then *exceeds* it.

**Layer two** (Ibn ʿAbbās, others): "before" means from an *earlier occasion in Paradise itself*. Each time a fruit is served, it resembles the last serving — but is different. The same fruit, renewed, inexhaustible, never boring. This reading gives us the principle of **infinite novelty within familiarity** — a joy that never fades because it is always new while always feeling like home.

**Layer three** (some later scholars): "before" encompasses both — a total collapse of the boundary between what you had in this world and what you have in the next, so that Paradise feels not like a foreign destination but like the *fulfillment* of everything you ever tasted, saw, and loved in dunya.

Each layer deepens the same insight: **Paradise is not escape from your life. It is the perfected version of your life.**

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Psychology of Home — Why Paradise Looks Familiar

Think about the most comforting experience you know. Not the most exciting. Not the most thrilling. The most *comforting*.

For most people, it has something to do with recognition. It's the dish your mother made when you were sick. It's the street you grew up on. It's the voice of someone who has known you for decades. Comfort — real, deep, bone-level comfort — almost always comes from *encountering something you already know in a context where you feel safe.*

Psychology calls this "the mere exposure effect" — the finding that familiarity itself generates positive feeling. But the Quran names something deeper than a cognitive bias. It names a *design*. Allah didn't have to make the fruits of Paradise look like the fruits of dunya. He could have created entirely new categories of experience — flavors that have no earthly equivalent, sensations with no reference point. That would have been more "impressive." More alien. More worthy of an omnipotent Creator, perhaps.

Instead, He chose resemblance. مُتَشَـٰبِهًا. He chose to make the experience of Paradise *recognizable*. Not identical. Not a copy. But a *recognition* — the feeling of biting into something and realizing: *I know this. I've had this before. But never like this.*

[PAUSE]

Why? Why resemblance instead of total novelty?

Because Paradise is not designed to impress you. It is designed to **welcome you home.**

There is a profound difference between a place that amazes you and a place that receives you. A five-star hotel in a foreign country can amaze you. Your childhood bedroom — even if it's smaller, simpler, less glamorous — receives you. When you walk into it, something in your chest releases. Not because it's the best room you've ever been in. Because it's *yours*. Because every object in it has a memory attached. Because the dimensions of it match something in your body that remembers.

This is what Allah is describing. The fruit of Paradise triggers a memory. هَـٰذَا ٱلَّذِى رُزِقْنَا مِن قَبْلُ — "This is what we were given before." The residents of Paradise don't feel like tourists in a divine resort. They feel like people who have finally come home — to a home they didn't know they'd been missing, furnished with things they didn't know they'd been remembering.

[PAUSE]

And here is the practical edge: if Paradise is the perfected version of what you already know — then **what you do in this world is not disconnected from what you'll experience in the next**. The good you tasted in dunya — the sweetness of a friendship, the peace of a prayer, the relief of forgiveness, the beauty of a sunset — none of it was accidental, and none of it will be wasted. It was a preview. A sample. A taste of something that, in the next life, will arrive in its complete form, wearing the face of something you loved.

The sweetness you experienced in this world was not the sweetness. It was the *resemblance*.

[PAUSE — major pause]

### Theme 2: Purified, Not Replaced — أَزْوَٰجٌ مُّطَهَّرَةٌ

The verse then says: وَلَهُمْ فِيهَآ أَزْوَٰجٌ مُّطَهَّرَةٌ — "and for them therein are spouses, *muṭahharah* — thoroughly purified."

There's a conversation happening in modern Muslim spaces — particularly among women — about what the descriptions of Paradise mean for them. Is Paradise designed for men? Are the descriptions only about male desire? These are legitimate questions, and they deserve to be taken seriously.

So let's look at what the Quran actually says here. أَزْوَٰجٌ — *azwāj* — is the plural of *zawj*, which means a spouse, a pair, a counterpart. It does not specify gender. A *zawj* is whoever completes you — whoever is your other half in the pairing. The word applies equally to husbands and wives, to male and female, to any partnership that constitutes a pair. When the Quran says أَزْوَٰجٌ مُّطَهَّرَةٌ, it is describing purified *companions* — not specifically "women for men."

And then: مُّطَهَّرَةٌ. This is the Form II passive participle of طهر — purified. Not طَاهِرَة (*ṭāhirah* — pure by nature, Form I active participle). مُطَهَّرَة — **thoroughly, intensively purified by an external agent**. The Form II doubling tells us: this purification is not passive cleanliness. It is an active, comprehensive, divine act of removing every possible source of harm or discomfort.

Purified from *what*? The object is absent — deliberately. And the scholars built their composite sketch: purified from physical impurities (menstruation, bodily discomfort), from emotional impurities (jealousy, resentment, insecurity, petty resentments), from spiritual impurities (the distance between hearts that even the closest couples in dunya experience). Every layer of impurity that makes human relationships in this world imperfect — even the best ones — is removed.

[PAUSE]

This is not a description of a person being *replaced* with a better version. It is a description of a person being *purified* of everything that prevented them from being fully known and fully loved. The same principle as the fruit: not replacement. Recognition. You will encounter the people you loved — but the barriers, the misunderstandings, the walls that even love could not fully dissolve in dunya — those will be gone. What remains is the relationship itself, in its purest form, as it was always meant to be.

And the final phrase seals it: وَهُمْ فِيهَا خَـٰلِدُونَ — "and they will abide therein eternally." *Khālidūn* — not a verb but a participle. Not "they will stay" but "they are *permanently-abiding-ones.*" Eternity is not something they will do. It is something they will **be**. Their identity itself becomes eternal. No fear of loss. No anxiety about endings. No countdown. Just... the permanent state of being home.

[PAUSE — major pause]

## Closing Synthesis

### Architectural Reveal

Look at the structure of this single verse — it moves through four ascending layers:

**Layer 1 — Place**: جَنَّـٰتٍ تَجْرِى مِن تَحْتِهَا ٱلْأَنْهَـٰرُ — Gardens with rivers. The setting. Where you are.

**Layer 2 — Provision**: كُلَّمَا رُزِقُوا۟... قَالُوا۟ هَـٰذَا ٱلَّذِى رُزِقْنَا مِن قَبْلُ — Food that triggers recognition. What you eat.

**Layer 3 — Companionship**: أَزْوَٰجٌ مُّطَهَّرَةٌ — Purified spouses. Who you love.

**Layer 4 — Duration**: خَـٰلِدُونَ — Eternity. How long it lasts.

Place → Provision → People → Permanence. The verse moves from the external to the intimate to the eternal. It starts with landscape (broadest) and ends with identity (most personal). The architecture is a zoom — from the aerial view of gardens and rivers to the close-up of a face you love, to the final truth that none of it will ever end.

And notice: the previous verse (2:24) described Fire whose fuel is *people* and *stones* — two things. This verse describes a Garden with *fruits*, *spouses*, and *eternity* — three gifts. The punishment is sparse. The reward is abundant. Even the *word count* of this verse is longer than the Fire verse. Allah gave more space, more detail, more tenderness to describing the reward than the punishment. The mercy outweighs the warning — not just in theology, but in grammar.

### Questions to Carry

When you taste something sweet in this world — a meal, a friendship, a moment of peace — do you receive it as a random pleasure, or as a preview of what's coming? What changes if you see every worldly sweetness as a *mutashābih* — a resemblance — of its perfected Paradisial form?

What in your life right now do you hope to find in Paradise — not replaced, but *purified*? What relationship, what experience, what joy do you hope will be there, freed from everything that made it imperfect here?

The verse pairs belief with *ordinary* good deeds — ٱلصَّـٰلِحَـٰتِ. Not heroic ones. What are the small, functional, ṣāliḥ deeds in your daily life that you've been dismissing as insignificant — that this verse says are your ticket to the Garden?

If someone told you: "The place you're going looks exactly like what you already know — but perfected" — would that feel like a disappointment or a relief? What does your answer reveal about what you actually want from the future?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Paradise is not an alien reward for enduring this world — it is the perfected homecoming of everything you loved in it, arriving in a form you recognize but have never fully experienced, given by a Provider too generous to name Himself in the same sentence as a piece of fruit.**

### The Closing Invitation

The next time you eat something sweet — really sweet, the kind that makes you close your eyes — consider this: someone made that sweetness. And He made a version of it in a place where the sweetness never fades, where the one sitting beside you has been purified of everything that ever kept you apart, and where the word "ending" doesn't exist.

And the price of admission is belief — real, built, Form IV belief — paired with deeds that are ordinary, functional, and consistent.

The fruit of Paradise is waiting. And when you taste it, you'll say: *"I know this. I've had this before."*

And you'll be right.

### Du'a

Make us among those for whom the Prophet ﷺ carries the bushrā. Make our faith Form IV — deep, built, committed — and our deeds Form I — simple, steady, and ṣāliḥ. And let us taste enough of Your sweetness in this world to recognize it when we arrive. Āmīn.*
