# Your Lord Has Not Forgotten

## Surah Maryam (19:64-65)

[ayah:19:64] وَمَا نَتَنَزَّلُ إِلَّا بِأَمْرِ رَبِّكَ ۖ لَهُۥ مَا بَيْنَ أَيْدِينَا وَمَا خَلْفَنَا وَمَا بَيْنَ ذَٰلِكَ ۚ وَمَا كَانَ رَبُّكَ نَسِيًّا
[ayah:19:65] رَّبُّ ٱلسَّمَـٰوَٰتِ وَٱلْأَرْضِ وَمَا بَيْنَهُمَا فَٱعْبُدْهُ وَٱصْطَبِرْ لِعِبَـٰدَتِهِۦ ۚ هَلْ تَعْلَمُ لَهُۥ سَمِيًّا

*Wa mā natanazzalu illā bi-amri rabbik. Lahu mā bayna aydīnā wa mā khalfanā wa mā bayna dhālik. Wa mā kāna rabbuka nasiyyā. Rabbu al-samāwāti wa al-arḍi wa mā baynahumā fa-ʿbudhu wa-ṣṭabir li-ʿibādatih. Hal taʿlamu lahu samiyyā.*

**And we do not descend except by the command of your Lord. To Him belongs what is before us and what is behind us and what is between that. And never is your Lord forgetful. Lord of the heavens and the earth and whatever is between them — so worship Him and have patience for His worship. Do you know of any similarity to Him?**

## Introduction

Something shifts in the surah at this point.

For sixty-three ayahs, Surah Maryam has moved through the stories of prophets, a mother who bore a child alone, a father who left his household's idols, and a generation that abandoned prayer and followed desire. Then came the mercy: those who repented will enter gardens of permanence, promised by Al-Rahman in the unseen.

And now, without warning, a new voice enters.

*Wa mā natanazzalu* — and we do not descend. The speaker is no longer Allah addressing humanity. It is the angel addressing the Messenger. Classical tafsir consistently identifies this as Jibril speaking to the Prophet, peace be upon him. Ibn Kathir transmits that this ayah came after a period during which revelation had paused, and the Prophet longed for the angel's return. The silence was heavy. The absence felt like abandonment.

Jibril's response is not an apology. It is a correction of frame. We do not descend except by command. The pause was not neglect. It was obedience — the angel's obedience to a Lord who owns all of time and forgets nothing.

Then the voice expands beyond the moment. It declares lordship over the heavens and the earth and everything between them. It commands worship and a particular kind of patience — *iṣṭabir*, the intensive, reflexive form — and closes with a question that expects no answer: do you know of any equal to Him?

These two ayahs move from the intimate pain of a Messenger waiting for his Lord's word to the vast declaration that the Lord of all creation has never forgotten anyone. The personal becomes cosmic. The longing becomes theology.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. Natanazzalu — The Gradual Descent

The verb *natanazzalu* is Form V (tafaʿʿala) of the root ن-ز-ل (to descend, to come down). Form V adds two dimensions: gradualness and reflexivity. Where the base form *nazala* means simply "he descended," Form V means something closer to "we descend gradually, step by step, of our own movement."

The first person plural is striking. This is the angel speaking as an angel — not quoting Allah, not relaying a message, but testifying about his own condition. We do not descend. The angels include themselves in the constraint. They are not free agents who visit when they choose. Their descent is tethered entirely to divine command.

The negation-exception structure — *mā ... illā* (we do not ... except) — makes the constraint absolute. There is no descent without command. There is no angelic initiative outside permission. Every arrival of Jibril to the Prophet was an act of divine will, not angelic scheduling.

This matters because the Prophet's pain was real. When revelation paused, the silence felt personal. The angel's answer reframes the silence: the absence was not a lapse. It was a command — the same command that governs the descent.

### 2. Bi-Amri Rabbika — By the Command of Your Lord

The phrase *bi-amri rabbika* (by the command of your Lord) contains two key elements.

The word *amr*, from the root أ-م-ر (to command, to order), is one of the most theologically loaded terms in the Quran. It covers divine command, the creative imperative, and the management of all affairs. When the angel says he descends by *amr*, he places every revelation inside the architecture of divine will.

The word *rabbika* (your Lord) uses the possessive — your. Not "the Lord" abstractly. Your Lord. The One who raised you, who chose you, who sustains you. Even the angel, when explaining why he was absent, points the Messenger back to the personal relationship: your Lord commanded this silence, and your Lord commands this speech.

### 3. What Is Before Us, Behind Us, and Between

The middle clause expands into a sweeping declaration of divine ownership: *lahu mā bayna aydīnā wa mā khalfanā wa mā bayna dhālik*.

Three domains are named. What is before us. What is behind us. What is between that.

The classical commentators read this temporally: the future (what is before us — what we are walking toward), the past (what is behind us — what we have left), and the present (what is between the two). All three belong to Allah. The angel does not own his schedule. The Prophet does not own the timing. All of time is held by the Lord.

The threefold structure — *mā ... wa mā ... wa mā* — is rhythmically deliberate. It covers every possible category. There is no remainder. Nothing falls outside His ownership. The effect is total: if all of time belongs to Him, then the pause in revelation is not a gap in His attention. It is a portion of time He chose to own in silence.

### 4. Nasiyyā — Your Lord Is Not Forgetful

The first ayah closes with the emotional center of the passage: *wa mā kāna rabbuka nasiyyā*.

The word *nasiyy* is an intensified adjective from the root ن-س-ي (to forget). The form faʿīl carries habitual or characteristic force: not merely "He did not forget" but "He is not the kind of being who forgets." Forgetfulness is not in His nature. It has never been in His nature. *Kāna* (was, has ever been) extends this backward through all time.

This is the answer to every silence the servant has ever experienced. When duʿa' seems unanswered, when guidance seems delayed, when the heart calls out and the sky feels closed — *wa mā kāna rabbuka nasiyyā*. Your Lord has not been forgetful. The delay is not neglect. The silence is not absence.

The repetition of *rabbuka* (your Lord) — it appears twice in this single ayah — keeps the address personal. The angel is not delivering a philosophical treatise on divine omniscience. He is speaking to a man who missed him. Your Lord. Yours. He has not forgotten you.

### 5. Rabbu al-Samāwāt — The Scope Expands

The second ayah opens by naming the Lord directly: *Rabbu al-samāwāti wa al-arḍi wa mā baynahumā* — Lord of the heavens and the earth and whatever is between them.

The intimate address of 19:64 gives way to cosmic scope. The same Lord who owns the angel's schedule owns the heavens and the earth and everything between. The personal relationship does not shrink the Lord. The Lord who has not forgotten you is also the Lord of all creation.

The phrase *wa mā baynahumā* (and whatever is between them) mirrors the threefold structure of the preceding ayah. There, it was time: before, behind, between. Here, it is space: heavens, earth, between. Both time and space are exhaustively claimed.

### 6. Fa-ʿbudhu wa-ṣṭabir — Worship and Endure

From the declaration of lordship comes a double command: *fa-ʿbudhu wa-ṣṭabir li-ʿibādatih*.

The *fa* (so, therefore) links the command directly to the preceding truth. Because He is Lord of everything, worship Him. The logic is not persuasion. It is consequence.

The first command is *ʿbudhu* — worship Him. Simple, direct, unqualified.

The second command is *iṣṭabir* — and this is where the language becomes remarkable. The verb is Form VIII (iftaʿala) of the root ص-ب-ر (to be patient, to endure). In Form VIII, the expected form would be *iṣtabir*, but the emphatic ṣ causes the infixed tā' to assimilate into ṭā', producing *iṣṭabir*. More importantly, Form VIII adds a reflexive, intensive dimension to the root. Where *ṣabr* is patience, *iṣṭibār* is an endurance that you summon from within yourself — a patience that requires effort, a staying that costs something.

The preposition *li-ʿibādatih* (for His worship) specifies what the endurance is for. Not patience in general. Not patience with hardship abstractly. Patience for worship. The command acknowledges that worship itself can become heavy. That maintaining prayer, maintaining remembrance, maintaining connection to Allah — these require sustained inner effort. The angel does not pretend worship is effortless. He commands the effort.

### 7. Samiyyā — Do You Know of Any Equal?

The passage closes with a rhetorical question: *hal taʿlamu lahu samiyyā* — do you know of any namesake to Him?

The word *samiyy*, from the root س-م-ي (to name), means one who shares a name — by extension, an equal, a peer, a rival. The question expects the answer: no. There is no one who shares His name. There is no equal. There is no comparison.

This word echoes 19:7, where Zakariyya was told about Yahya: *lam najʿal lahu min qablu samiyyā* — We had not assigned to him before any namesake. The rare word *samiyy* bookends the surah's movement: Yahya had no human predecessor by that name, and Allah has no equal by any name. The surah opens a unique child into the world and now declares the absolute uniqueness of the Lord who placed him there.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### The Angel Who Explains His Own Absence

There is something extraordinary about an angel explaining himself. Jibril is not a being who owes the Prophet an account of his movements. He is the most honored of messengers, carrying the weight of revelation between heaven and earth. And yet, the Quran preserves his explanation.

This is mercy layered upon mercy. The silence in revelation caused the Prophet real distress. The Quran does not dismiss that distress. It does not say "you should not have felt that way." Instead, it sends the angel to explain: I do not come and go by my own choice. I come by command. And the One who commands has not forgotten you.

The lesson extends to every believer who has felt the silence of the divine. Every unanswered prayer, every morning when the heart feels heavy and the connection seems severed, every season of spiritual dryness — the angel's answer remains: He has not forgotten. The pause is not absence. It is a portion of time that belongs to Him, and He manages it with knowledge, not neglect.

### The Structure of All Time

The threefold claim — what is before us, what is behind us, what is between — is not merely poetic. It is a theological claim about the nature of divine sovereignty.

Human beings experience time as a sequence: past, present, future. We lose access to the past and cannot reach the future. We live in a narrow present that moves forward relentlessly. The angel's declaration dissolves that limitation at the level of ownership. All three portions of time — even the ones the angel himself cannot access simultaneously — belong to the Lord.

This means the silence between two revelations is not an empty gap. It is a stretch of time that belongs to Allah as fully as the moments of descent. The Prophet's waiting was not wasted time. It was owned time. And if the Lord owns it, it is purposeful.

### Patience That Costs Something

The command *iṣṭabir* does not pretend that worship is easy.

There is a version of spiritual teaching that makes devotion sound like a natural overflow of love — as if the truly devoted never struggle with prayer, never find fasting heavy, never feel the weight of maintaining connection. The Quran does not teach that version. It commands *iṣṭabir* — endure within yourself for His worship. Summon the patience. Sustain it. It will cost something, and the cost is part of the act.

This is honest. The Prophet himself was commanded to endure. The greatest human being who ever lived was told: be patient for worship. If he needed to hear it, the rest of us need it more.

The preposition *li* (for) is important. The patience is directed. It is not patience with suffering or patience with enemies or patience with the world. It is patience *for worship*. The hardest patience is sometimes not the patience of crisis but the patience of consistency — showing up again, praying again, turning back again, when the heart is tired and the world is loud.

### No Equal, No Comparison

The closing question — *hal taʿlamu lahu samiyyā* — is not asked for information. It is asked to seal the passage.

After declaring that the Lord owns all of time and all of space, the Quran asks: do you know of anyone like Him? The answer is built into the question. No. There is no equal. The Lord who has not forgotten you cannot be compared to anything you know. The patience He is asking of you is patience for a relationship that has no parallel.

This is why the passage moves from the intimate to the cosmic and back. Jibril begins with the personal: your Lord, your silence, your waiting. Then he expands: Lord of the heavens and the earth. Then he returns to the personal: worship Him, endure for Him. And finally he seals it: there is no one like Him.

The servant who endures is not enduring for an ordinary relationship. The servant who waits is not waiting on an ordinary promise. The One who commands the angel's descent and owns all of time and has no equal — that is the One who has not forgotten you.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayahs interrupt the surah's flow to address the most human of pains: the feeling of being forgotten by God.

The answer comes not from Allah directly but from the angel — the very being whose absence caused the pain. And the angel's answer is not comfort in the usual sense. It is truth. We do not descend except by command. All of time belongs to your Lord. And He has never been forgetful.

Then the scope breaks open. The Lord who manages the angel's schedule is the Lord of the heavens and the earth. Worship Him. Endure for His worship — not effortlessly, but with the costly patience of *iṣṭabir*. And know that there is no one like Him. No equal, no namesake, no comparison.

The passage resolves the silence. Not by filling it with noise, but by revealing what the silence contained: a Lord who owns every quiet moment and has not forgotten a single one of them.
