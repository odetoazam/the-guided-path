# Does Man Not Remember?

## Surah Maryam (19:66-67)

[ayah:19:66] وَيَقُولُ ٱلْإِنسَـٰنُ أَءِذَا مَا مِتُّ لَسَوْفَ أُخْرَجُ حَيًّا
[ayah:19:67] أَوَلَا يَذْكُرُ ٱلْإِنسَـٰنُ أَنَّا خَلَقْنَـٰهُ مِن قَبْلُ وَلَمْ يَكُ شَيْـًٔا

*Wa yaqūlu al-insānu a-idhā mā mittu la-sawfa ukhraju ḥayyā. A-wa lā yadhkuru al-insānu annā khalaqnāhu min qablu wa lam yaku shay'ā.*

**And man says: When I have died, will I really be brought forth alive? Does man not remember that We created him before, while he was nothing?**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

The surah has just delivered two of its most powerful reassurances. The garden is promised to those who repent. The angel has confirmed that your Lord has not forgotten. Worship and endure. There is no equal to Him.

And then, as if none of it was said, the human voice enters.

*Wa yaqūlu al-insān* — and man says. Not "a particular man." Not "the disbelievers." The Quran uses the generic *al-insān* — the human being as a category. This is a statement about what the species does. It questions the one thing the surah has just spent its entire arc establishing: that there is a life after this one, that Allah's promise arrives, that what is unseen is real.

The man asks: when I die, will I really be brought out alive?

The question drips with incredulity. The emphatic *la-sawfa* (indeed, I will?) and the hamza of interrogation (*a-idhā*) signal disbelief, not curiosity. This is not a seeker asking how resurrection works. This is a voice that finds the whole idea absurd.

Allah's answer is not anger. It is a question in return: does man not remember that We created him before, and he was nothing?

The argument is devastatingly simple. The one who doubts the second creation has already forgotten the first. And the first was harder — it was creation from nothing. The second is only a return.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. Al-Insān — The Human Category

Both ayahs name their subject identically: *al-insān*. Not a tribe. Not a nation. Not the disbelievers of Quraysh. The human being.

The root of *insān* is debated among classical grammarians. Some derive it from أ-ن-س (to be sociable, familiar) — the human is the creature who seeks companionship and comfort. Others connect it to ن-س-ي (to forget) — the human is the creature who forgets. Both derivations illuminate this passage. The human being who once was nothing has grown so comfortable in his existence that he forgets it was given. He is so familiar with being alive that death feels final and resurrection feels absurd.

The Quran does not address this denial to a fringe. It addresses it to *al-insān* — the human condition itself. The inclination to deny what cannot be seen, to trust only the current state of things, to find the unseen implausible — this is a species-level tendency, not a historical anomaly.

### 2. A-idhā mā Mittu — The Rhetorical Mask

The man's question is structured to express disbelief rather than inquiry: *a-idhā mā mittu la-sawfa ukhraju ḥayyā*.

The hamza at the beginning (*a-*) is the interrogative particle, but its function here is closer to incredulity — "really?" The temporal clause *idhā mā mittu* (when I have died) places the scenario firmly in what the speaker considers impossible territory. And the *la-sawfa* construction (indeed I will, in the future) mimics a promise with a tone of mockery. He is repeating the promise of resurrection back to himself as if to expose its absurdity.

The passive *ukhraju* (I will be brought forth) is also telling. He does not say "I will come out." He says "I will be brought out" — as if someone is going to perform this ridiculous act upon him. The passive voice distances him from the event. It is something done to him, and he finds it laughable.

The final word, *ḥayyā* (alive), carries the surah's signature *-iyyā* ending. But here it is used in disbelief. The same word that described Isa speaking from the cradle alive (19:33), the same quality that was the Prophet's promised state after death — now the denier uses it sarcastically. Alive? After I die? The word is the same. The posture toward it is opposite.

### 3. A-wa lā Yadhkuru — Does He Not Remember?

Allah's answer opens with a question: *a-wa lā yadhkuru al-insānu* — does man not remember?

The verb *yadhkuru* is from the root ذ-ك-ر (to remember, to mention, to reflect). The same root gives us *dhikr* — the remembrance that is the heart of worship. The question is not "does man not know?" It is "does man not remember?" The proof is not new information. It is old information that has been forgotten.

This is the Quran's characteristic move when addressing denial. It does not introduce novel evidence. It asks the denier to remember what he already knows. You were created. You were nothing. And then you became something. That transition — from nothing to something — already happened once. Why do you find it impossible that it would happen again?

### 4. Khalaqnāhu min Qablu — We Created Him Before

The verb *khalaqnāhu* (We created him) uses the first person plural of divine majesty. The root خ-ل-ق (to create, to bring into being) is one of the most foundational in the Quran. And the qualifier *min qablu* (from before, previously) locates this creation in the past — something already completed, already proven, already a fact of the denier's own existence.

The proof is the denier himself. His body is the evidence. His consciousness is the exhibit. He walks around in the product of divine creation and asks whether divine creation is possible. The Quran does not let this pass without comment.

### 5. Wa Lam Yaku Shay'ā — He Was Nothing

The ayah closes with the most devastating three words in the passage: *wa lam yaku shay'ā* — and he was not a thing.

The verb *yaku* is a contracted form of *yakun* (from the root ك-و-ن, to be). The contraction itself carries a sense of reduction — the being of the human before creation was so minimal that even the verb describing it loses a letter. He was not a thing. Not merely small, not merely weak, not merely humble. Nothing. Not a thing.

The word *shay'ā* (a thing), from the root ش-ي-أ, is the most general noun in Arabic. It means anything, any entity, any existent. The human before creation was not even that. He was below the threshold of thingness.

And from that nothing, Allah created him. Gave him bones, flesh, sight, speech, thought, memory, desire, doubt — the very doubt he is now using to deny the One who assembled him from nothing. The argument does not need elaboration. It is complete in a single line.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### The Forgetting That Produces Denial

The Quran frames denial of the Hereafter not as an intellectual conclusion but as an act of forgetting.

This is precise. The human being does not deny resurrection because he has examined the evidence and found it wanting. He denies it because he has forgotten the evidence he carries in his own body. He was nothing. Then he became something. That transition is the proof. And he forgot it.

This is why the Quran asks *a-wa lā yadhkuru* — does he not remember? The verb choice is not accidental. The cure for denial is not new data. It is dhikr — remembrance. The same dhikr that the surah has been built around from its first ayah. The same dhikr that the prophets modeled. The same dhikr that the later generation abandoned when they abandoned prayer.

Denial of the Hereafter is, at its root, a failure of memory. The denier has forgotten where he came from. And when you forget where you came from, you cannot imagine where you are going.

### The First Creation Was Harder

The argument is implicit but total: if Allah created the human from nothing, then recreating him from something (dust, bones, remnants) is easier, not harder.

The Quran makes this argument explicitly in other surahs — "Is not the One who created the heavens and the earth able to create the likes of them?" (36:81). But here in Maryam, it does not need the full argument. It only needs the reminder: you were nothing, and He made you. That is enough.

The power of this is that the denier cannot escape his own existence as evidence. He can doubt texts, traditions, reports, and testimonies. But he cannot doubt that he exists. And his existence was preceded by his nonexistence. Someone bridged that gap. The same Someone can bridge it again.

### The Voice of the Species

By using *al-insān* rather than a specific group, the Quran acknowledges that the denial of resurrection is not a Meccan problem or an Arab problem or a historical problem. It is a human problem. Every generation, every culture, every intellectual tradition has produced voices that say: when I die, that is the end.

The Quran does not argue with this voice philosophically. It does not construct a logical proof from premises to conclusions. It asks a question. Does man not remember? The question is aimed at the heart, not the mind. It asks the denier to sit with his own contingency — to feel, not just think about, the fact that he was once nothing and is now something.

That feeling, when it arrives, is the beginning of faith in the unseen.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayahs place the human voice directly after the angelic voice — and the contrast is devastating.

The angel just said: your Lord has not forgotten. The human now says: when I die, will I really be brought forth alive? The angel speaks of divine memory. The human demonstrates human forgetting.

Allah's answer does not punish the forgetfulness. It redirects it. Does man not remember? You were nothing. Then you became this. The One who made you from nothing can certainly bring you back from dust.

The argument is not complex. It does not need to be. The simplest proof is the one you carry in your own body. You exist. You once did not. Whoever made that happen the first time has not lost the ability to do it again.

The word *shay'ā* lingers. You were not a thing. And now you stand here questioning the power of the One who made you into one.
