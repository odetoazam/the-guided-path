# Jahannam Has Seven Gates, but the Godfearing Enter Gardens in Peace

## Surah Al-Hijr (15:43-48)

## Introduction

The Quran can move from terror to tenderness with almost no transition, and the nearness of those two scenes is itself part of the lesson.

One moment: Jahannam, appointed, gated, apportioned.

The next: gardens, springs, peace, security, hearts cleansed, faces turned toward one another, no fatigue, no expulsion.

[PAUSE]

This is not a random juxtaposition. Surah Al-Hijr has just exposed Shaytan's strategy, named the limits of his authority, and shown that those who follow him become his people. Now the surah shows where those roads actually end.

But notice something deeper. The contrast is not only between two locations. It is between two human conditions. One destiny is divided and apportioned. The other is gathered, healed, secure, and reconciled from the inside out.

The hook of the passage is this: **Al-Hijr contrasts Jahannam and Jannah not merely as opposite places, but as opposite forms of human existence: one fragmented under appointed ruin, the other welcomed into peace with healed hearts, restored brotherhood, and permanent rest.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **La-Maw'iduhum Ajma'in**: Hell Is Not an Accident, It Is an Appointed Meeting

The opening does not say simply that they will end up there. It says:

**wa inna Jahannama la-maw'iduhum ajma'in**

Jahannam is surely their **maw'id**, their appointed meeting place, all together.

[PAUSE]

The word **maw'id** comes from the root **w-'‑d**, promise or appointment. That gives the line a chilling precision. Hell is not random fallout. It is a fixed appointment for a people who kept walking a certain road.

And then: **ajma'in**. All together.

That collective note matters. The same surah has been showing us factional rejection, shared mockery, imitation, and followership under Shaytan. Now it shows the end of that shared road. They do not merely commit wrong separately. They meet a destination together.

Sin often sells itself as autonomy. The ayah reveals its actual social truth: whole populations can gather around distortion and then gather again at its appointed end.

### 2. Seven Gates and Assigned Portions: Ruin as Division and Distribution

Then the image narrows:

**laha sab'atu abwab. li-kulli babin minhum juz'un maqsum**

It has seven gates. For every gate there is an assigned portion of them.

[PAUSE]

The scene becomes architectural. Not one opening, but many. Not one undifferentiated mass, but apportioned groups.

The language of **juz' maqsum** is striking. A portion. Divided out. Assigned. Whatever else the exact distribution implies in classical tafsir, the emotional effect is clear: this is not a place of wholeness. It is a place of sorting, allotment, and divided destination.

That sits powerfully beside what comes later in the passage. Hell is gates and portions. Paradise is brothers facing one another. The Quran is making an argument not only about punishment and reward, but about fragmentation and restoration.

One of the hidden tragedies of sin is that it already begins dividing people long before the Hereafter. It partitions the self, distorts loyalties, breeds resentment, creates rivalries, and turns communities into angled rooms of suspicion. Jahannam is the final world where such fragmentation is no longer hidden.

### 3. **Naza'na ... Min Ghill**: Paradise Includes Inner Surgery

Then comes one of the most beautiful lines in the surah:

**wa naza'na ma fi sudurihim min ghill**

We removed whatever rancor was in their chests.

[PAUSE]

The verb **naza'na** comes from the root **n-z-'**, to pull out, to extract, to draw something away from where it is lodged. This is a strong verb. It is not cosmetic adjustment. It is removal.

And what is removed? **Ghill**: hidden rancor, resentment, inward bitterness, the kind of lodged emotional contamination that can sit inside the chest even when the outward relationship looks orderly.

This means Jannah is not merely beautiful scenery added onto an unchanged self. Paradise includes purification of the interior.

That is why the next words land so perfectly:

**ikhwanan 'ala sururin mutaqabilin**

As brothers, upon couches, facing one another.

The facing matters. In dunya, people can occupy the same room while inwardly turned away. They can smile while nursing comparison, jealousy, history, old injury, and defensive distance. The Quran says that in Jannah, Allah does not merely seat people near each other. He heals what made nearness hard.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: We Often Dream of Paradise as a Place, but the Quran Describes a Healed Self

When people imagine relief, they often imagine external conditions changing first.

A better home. No pressure. More beauty. No deadlines. No fear. No enemies. No exhaustion.

And the ayahs do give external beauty: gardens, springs, peace, safety, no fatigue, no expulsion.

[PAUSE]

But right in the middle of that scene, the Quran places the chest.

**Ma fi sudurihim min ghill.**

Whatever was in their chests.

This is profound because it tells us that one of the great burdens of dunya is not only what surrounds us. It is what sits inside us. Envy. Silent grievance. Old hurts. Competitive insecurity. Residual anger. The human inability to remain fully open in the presence of another human being.

Many people live in decent outward conditions and still feel inwardly cramped because the chest is carrying too much unresolved weight. The ayah says ultimate salvation is not complete until that interior burden is removed.

So one way to read this passage is not only as a description of the next life, but as a re-education about what our real sickness is. We do not only need better circumstances. We need cleaner hearts.

### Theme 2: Taqwa Ends in Peace, Because It Refused to Let Corruption Stay Beautiful

The passage begins with Jahannam for the people who followed the road of deviation. Then it turns:

**inna al-muttaqina fi jannatin wa 'uyun**

Surely the Godfearing are in gardens and springs.

[PAUSE]

The root idea of **taqwa** is protection, guarding, shielding the self against what destroys it. That makes this placement deeply fitting. The people of taqwa are not rewarded arbitrarily. They guarded themselves, by Allah's grace, from the very road whose end is now being shown.

And look at how their welcome is phrased:

**udkhuluha bi-salamin aminin**

Enter it in peace, secure.

Peace outside. Security inside. No threat approaching, no hidden danger waiting, no old fear walking behind them into the garden.

Then the final line seals the permanence:

**la yamassuhum fiha nasab, wa ma hum minha bi-mukhrijin**

No fatigue will touch them there, and they will never be expelled from it.

This is the exact opposite of the Iblis passage just before. He was told: get out. Here they are told: enter. He is under curse. They are under peace. He is distanced. They are made brothers. He continues within punishment's horizon. They remain without fatigue and without removal.

[PAUSE]

The contrast is so sharp because the Quran wants the heart to feel that roads matter. Followership, taqwa, hidden bitterness, and the stories we allow inside ourselves all move somewhere. The soul is always becoming more ready for one of these worlds.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs begin with Jahannam as an appointed collective destination, structured with seven gates and assigned portions. Then they turn suddenly and beautifully to the Godfearing in gardens and springs, entering in peace and security, having the rancor inside their chests removed, seated as brothers facing one another, never touched by fatigue and never expelled.

[PAUSE]

What the passage gives us, then, is more than threat and comfort. It gives us a map of two different moral futures. One future is partitioned, bitter, exposed, and trapped. The other is peaceful, secure, reconciled, rested, and permanent.

And maybe the most searching part is this: paradise is not described merely as escape from pain, but as the end of inward corruption. Allah does not only save the believers from a bad place. He saves them from being the kind of selves that could not have fully enjoyed nearness anyway.

### Questions to Carry

- What forms of **ghill** still lodge in your chest and quietly shape how you see others?
- Have you been imagining salvation mostly as changed circumstances, without asking for a changed interior?
- What current habit of rivalry, resentment, or hidden division is preparing the soul for the wrong world?
- How would your relationships change if you treated inward purification as part of your path to Jannah, not merely a personality preference?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Al-Hijr contrasts Hell and Paradise as two opposite human destinies: one apportioned through gates of ruin, the other entered in peace through hearts Allah has cleansed for brotherhood, rest, and permanence.**

### Du'a

*Allahumma, make us among the muttaqin whom You welcome with peace and safety. Remove from our chests every ghill that blocks brotherhood, distorts love, or makes nearness heavy. Protect us from the roads that divide the soul, and prepare us for a garden where nothing painful remains inside us or around us.*
