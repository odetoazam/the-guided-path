# Get Out From Here: The Curse Is On You, and Even Your Delay Is Measured

## Surah Al-Hijr (15:34-38)

[ayah:15:34] قَالَ فَٱخْرُجْ مِنْهَا فَإِنَّكَ رَجِيمٌ

[ayah:15:35] وَإِنَّ عَلَيْكَ ٱللَّعْنَةَ إِلَىٰ يَوْمِ ٱلدِّينِ

[ayah:15:36] قَالَ رَبِّ فَأَنظِرْنِىٓ إِلَىٰ يَوْمِ يُبْعَثُونَ

[ayah:15:37] قَالَ فَإِنَّكَ مِنَ ٱلْمُنظَرِينَ

[ayah:15:38] إِلَىٰ يَوْمِ ٱلْوَقْتِ ٱلْمَعْلُومِ

*Qala fa-ukhruj minha fa-innaka rajim.*

*Wa inna 'alayka al-la'nata ila yawmi al-din.*

**He said: Then get out from here, for surely you are banished. And surely upon you is the curse until the Day of Judgment. He said: My Lord, then grant me respite until the Day they are resurrected. He said: Then surely you are among those given respite, until the Day of the known appointed time.**

## Introduction

The argument is over.

There is no long debate here, no extended defense, no reopening of the case. The refusal has already exposed what needed exposing. Now the scene becomes judicial.

Get out.

You are banished.

The curse is on you.

And yet, astonishingly, the condemned one still asks for time.

[PAUSE]

That is the tension of this passage. It holds sentence and delay together. Rejection and reprieve stand side by side. The rebel is expelled from nearness, marked by curse, and still granted a measured extension. The surah teaches us that being left for a while is not the same thing as being accepted.

The hook of the passage is this: **Al-Hijr shows that divine rejection does not always look like immediate destruction; sometimes the rebel is cast out from mercy and then left to continue only within a time Allah still fully controls.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Fa-Ukhruj ... Rajim**: This Is More Than Movement, It Is Banishment

The first decree is sharp:

**fa-ukhruj minha fa-innaka rajim**

Get out from here, for surely you are **rajim**.

[PAUSE]

The command begins with motion: leave. But it does not end there. The verse does not merely relocate Iblis. It names his new condition.

The word **rajim** comes from the root **r-j-m**, whose concrete sense is pelting or driving something away by stoning. From that concrete image comes the extended meaning of being cast off, expelled, banished from honored proximity. So the passage is not only about where Iblis goes. It is about what he becomes.

That matters because sin is not only an action with consequences. It can become a state. A person can so persist in arrogant refusal that the heart is no longer merely committing rebellion but inhabiting distance from Allah.

The ayah's brevity intensifies this. There is no softening phrase between command and naming. Leave, because you are this. The sentence falls with finality.

### 2. **Al-La'nah Ila Yawm al-Din**: Exclusion Is Extended Through Time

The next ayah deepens the punishment:

**wa inna 'alayka al-la'nata ila yawmi al-din**

Upon you is the curse until the Day of Judgment.

[PAUSE]

In Quranic usage, **la'nah** is not merely anger. It is removal from mercy, being pushed away from the sphere of divine favor. The first ayah expelled him from place. This ayah names the continuing spiritual reality of that expulsion.

And then comes time.

Not for a moment. Not for a brief interval. **Ila yawmi al-din**. Until the Day when matters are judged, debts are answered, and realities are laid bare.

That means the passage is not describing a passing flare of punishment. It is describing a sustained condition. The rebellion of Iblis is not handled as an impulsive slip that evaporates into neutrality. It hardens into a long exclusion.

This is frightening because many of us imagine punishment only as dramatic collapse. We think if the roof has not fallen in, if life is still moving, if the body is still functioning, then perhaps all is well. But the ayah teaches a more unsettling possibility: the worst thing may already have happened if the heart has been distanced from mercy while still being allowed to go on.

### 3. **Fa-Anzirni ... Min al-Munzarin ... al-Waqt al-Ma'lum**: The Delay Is Granted, but On Allah's Terms

Then the condemned one speaks:

**rabbi fa-anzirni ila yawmi yub'athun**

My Lord, then grant me respite until the Day they are resurrected.

[PAUSE]

The verb **anzirni** comes from the root **n-z-r**, a root tied to waiting, looking ahead, and deferred expectation. In this form it means: give me delay, leave me until later, grant me time before the end overtakes me.

And Allah answers:

**fa-innaka min al-munzarin**

You are among those given respite.

The same root returns. The request is answered in its own verbal family. But then the Quran adds something decisive:

**ila yawmi al-waqti al-ma'lum**

Until the Day of the known appointed time.

Notice the difference. Iblis asks with one description: the Day they are resurrected. Allah answers with another: the Day of the known appointed time.

The grant is real, but it is not a surrender of terms. He receives delay, but not as owner of the timeline. He remains inside an appointment already known by Allah.

This is one of the great theological corrections of the passage. Even the enemy's continued life is not self-held space. It is measured allowance.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Delay Is Not Proof of Favor

Human beings regularly make a dangerous reading of time.

If the consequence has not come yet, we assume approval.

If the collapse has not happened yet, we assume safety.

If the path is still open, we assume we are welcome on it.

[PAUSE]

But these ayahs shatter that illusion. Iblis is cursed and delayed at the same time. Expelled and extended at the same time. Rejected and still moving through created time at the same time.

That means bare continuation is never enough evidence that all is well between us and Allah. A person can have room, years, energy, opportunity, and still be moving inside a deeply dangerous estrangement.

This is why the believer does not read time simplistically. More time is mercy when it leads to tawbah. More time is terror when it only deepens rebellion. The calendar alone cannot tell you which one it is.

So one of the most important questions in spiritual life is not merely, "How much time do I still have?" It is, "What kind of relationship with Allah is this time currently unfolding inside?"

### Theme 2: What Terrifies the Soul Most Is Not Always Death, but Distance

We often think the great fear is annihilation.

But this passage teaches something deeper. Iblis does not first plead for forgiveness. He pleads for postponement. He is more concerned with continuation than reconciliation.

[PAUSE]

That is a devastating portrait of a corrupted heart. When distance from Allah no longer feels unbearable, mere extension of life begins to look like victory. The person stops asking, "How do I return?" and starts asking only, "How do I remain?"

We are not Iblis, and the passage is not flattening every sinner into that category. But it does hold up a terrifying mirror. There are moments when we too become more interested in keeping our patterns alive than in healing the rupture they have opened. We ask for more runway, not a new heart. More time to continue, not the grace to stop.

The ayahs invite a different fear. Not simply fear of death, but fear of being alive while far. Fear of continuing in a state that has already lost tenderness, repentance, and nearness. Fear that the extension of our days might be mistaken by us for innocence when it is really only test.

[PAUSE]

That is why the believer keeps turning back quickly. Not because he assumes every sin makes him accursed, but because he knows distance hardens when it is not interrupted. Delay can either become a bridge to tawbah or a corridor deeper into ruin.

## Closing Synthesis

This passage is brief, but it is mercilessly precise.

Iblis is told to get out. He is named as banished. The curse remains upon him until the Day of Judgment. He asks for reprieve until resurrection. Allah grants him respite, but only until the known appointed time.

[PAUSE]

Everything here teaches measure under divine rule. Even rebellion does not escape Allah's decree. Even the enemy's lifespan is not self-owned. Even delay is scheduled.

And for us, the most searching lesson may be this: being left for a while does not tell us whether we are near or far. Only the state of the heart, and its willingness to return, tells that story truthfully.

### Questions to Carry

- Have you ever mistaken continued opportunity for divine approval?
- In your own failures, do you seek reconciliation first, or mostly just postponement of consequence?
- What forms of distance from Allah have started to feel normal to you?
- If more time were given to you today, would it function as a bridge back or as a corridor deeper in?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Iblis is expelled, cursed, and still given time, teaching us that delay is never proof of acceptance and that even the rebel's extension remains entirely inside Allah's measured decree.**

### Du'a

*Allahumma, do not let us be deceived by time. If You extend our days, make that extension a mercy that returns us to You, not a test that hardens us against You. Protect us from becoming comfortable in distance, and keep our hearts quick to repent before delay turns into delusion.*
