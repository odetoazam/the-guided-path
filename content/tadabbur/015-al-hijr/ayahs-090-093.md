# Those Who Made the Quran Into Fragments Will Be Asked About Everything They Used to Do

## Surah Al-Hijr (15:90-93)

[ayah:15:90] كَمَآ أَنزَلْنَا عَلَى ٱلْمُقْتَسِمِينَ

[ayah:15:91] ٱلَّذِينَ جَعَلُوا۟ ٱلْقُرْءَانَ عِضِينَ

[ayah:15:92] فَوَرَبِّكَ لَنَسْـَٔلَنَّهُمْ أَجْمَعِينَ

[ayah:15:93] عَمَّا كَانُوا۟ يَعْمَلُونَ

*Kama anzalna 'ala al-muqtasimin.*

*Alladhina ja'alu al-Qur'ana 'idin.*

*Fa-wa-rabbika la-nas'alannahum ajma'in.*

*'Amma kanu ya'malun.*

**Just as We sent down upon the dividers, those who made the Quran into fragments. So by your Lord, We will surely question them all about what they used to do.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

Not every rejection of revelation looks like simple refusal.

Sometimes people keep enough of the truth to speak in its language, borrow its vocabulary, or claim some relationship to it, while breaking it into pieces small enough to fit their desires.

[PAUSE]

That is the atmosphere of this passage. The surah has already exposed mockery, selective blindness, ruined nations, and the Prophet's burden as a clear warner. Now the warning sharpens further. There are people who do not receive revelation as a whole trust. They partition it, rename it, reduce it, weaponize parts of it against other parts, or accept from it only what serves them.

Then Allah swears by the Lord of the Prophet that all of them will be questioned.

The hook of the passage is this: **Al-Hijr teaches that one of the gravest ways to betray revelation is not only to reject it outright, but to break it into fragments that our ego can manage, because Allah will question us about what we actually did with the truth He sent down.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Al-Muqtasimin**: Revelation Can Be Met With a Dividing Mind

The warning comes:

**kama anzalna 'ala al-muqtasimin**

Just as We sent down upon the dividers.

[PAUSE]

The word **al-muqtasimin** comes from the root **q-s-m**, the field of dividing, apportioning, splitting into shares.

The classical tafsir tradition mentions forms of this dividing that all circle one core sickness: some people divided the revelation by believing one part and denying another; some divided their descriptions of it by calling it magic, poetry, or other labels; some fragmented the truth so it would no longer confront them as a unified demand.

That shared center matters. The problem is not careful distinction. The problem is a soul that meets divine speech with a partitioning instinct, always trying to make the truth smaller, more negotiable, less total.

### 2. **Ja'alu al-Qur'ana 'Idin**: The Quran Is Not Meant to Be Reduced to Pieces We Can Manage

Then the passage names the act:

**alladhina ja'alu al-Qur'ana 'idin**

Those who made the Quran into fragments.

[PAUSE]

The verb **ja'alu** matters because it frames this as an act of human treatment. They made it this way. The Quran did not arrive fragmented. Their reception fragmented it.

And then **'idin**: broken portions, separated pieces, dismembered segments. The word is jarring. It does not suggest a calm academic division. It suggests something once whole being treated in a way that violates its integrity.

This is a warning to every age. We can split revelation without physically tearing pages. We do it when we love comforting verses but avoid demanding ones. When we quote what honors us but bury what humbles us. When we invoke the Quran devotionally but refuse to let it judge our patterns of power, appetite, ego, or allegiance.

### 3. **Fa-Wa-Rabbika La-Nas'alannahum Ajma'in**: The Oath Restores the Full Seriousness of Moral Response

Then the tone hardens:

**fa-wa-rabbika la-nas'alannahum ajma'in**

So by your Lord, We will surely question them all.

[PAUSE]

The oath by the Lord of the Prophet brings solemn intimacy into the warning. This is not a vague consequence drifting somewhere in the unseen. Allah swears.

Then the emphatic form: **la-nas'alannahum**. We will most certainly ask them.

And then **ajma'in**: all of them, without exception.

The passage does not leave fragmentation as an intellectual error. It turns it into an accountable act. Revelation is not merely information to sort. It is a trust to answer for.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Ego Prefers a Usable Quran Over a Governing Quran

Human beings often want a version of revelation that can inspire without ruling, comfort without exposing, and decorate identity without reorganizing life.

So we begin to edit inwardly.

We keep what consoles us in grief.

We keep what flatters our community.

We keep what can be posted, quoted, sung, framed, or admired.

And we quietly distance ourselves from what would force repentance, surrender, restitution, or loss of control.

[PAUSE]

That is not neutral partiality. It is a kind of fragmentation. The Quran was sent as guidance, criterion, mercy, warning, and judgment all together. Once we begin extracting only the parts that harmonize with our existing self, we are no longer simply reading revelation. We are resizing it.

This passage asks us whether we want the Quran whole enough to disagree with us, whole enough to interrupt us, whole enough to say no where we want yes and yes where our fear wants no.

### Theme 2: Accountability Is Not Only for What We Did in General, but for What We Did With the Truth

The passage ends:

**'amma kanu ya'malun**

about what they used to do.

[PAUSE]

That is broader than verbal opinion. Allah does not say they will only be asked what they claimed. He says they will be asked about what they used to do.

This is spiritually piercing. A person may speak respectfully about revelation while treating it selectively in practice. He may affirm it in principle while building a life whose actual operations answer to other gods: image, tribe, desire, fear, money, self-protection.

So the questioning is not about slogans alone. It is about enacted response. What did the Quran become in your hands? A whole guidance? A partial ornament? A source of leverage? A text you turned into fragments so your life would remain mostly undisturbed?

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask where we have made revelation small enough to become manageable. Which parts of Allah's speech feel welcome in us, and which parts do we keep postponing, qualifying, trimming, or hiding from because they would require too much actual change?

## Closing Synthesis

Allah warns of what was sent upon the dividers, identifies them as those who made the Quran into fragments, then swears by the Lord of the Prophet that all of them will surely be questioned about what they used to do.

[PAUSE]

The architecture is severe and clear: division, fragmentation, oath, accountability. The Quran is not to be received as a shelf of options from which the self chooses a tolerable share. It comes as a whole claim upon the human being.

### Questions to Carry

- In what areas of life do you most want a usable Quran rather than a governing Quran?
- Which ayahs do you return to easily, and which ayahs do you keep at a safe distance because they would cost you something?
- Have you ever reduced revelation into fragments by receiving it devotionally but not obediently?
- If Allah asked you today what you actually did with the truth you already know, what answer would you hope to give?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**This passage warns that breaking revelation into ego-sized fragments is itself a punishable betrayal, because Allah will question people not only about what they said of the Quran, but about what they did with it.**

### Du'a

*Allahumma, do not let us receive Your Book with dividing hearts. Save us from taking from revelation only what flatters or comforts us while resisting what would purify and reorder us. Make the Quran whole and governing in our lives, and let us meet You having answered Your truth with surrender rather than fragmentation.*
