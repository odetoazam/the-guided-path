# The Cry Took Them at Sunrise, and in That Are Signs for Those Who Truly Read

## Surah Al-Hijr (15:73-77)

[ayah:15:73] فَأَخَذَتْهُمُ ٱلصَّيْحَةُ مُشْرِقِينَ

[ayah:15:74] فَجَعَلْنَا عَـٰلِيَهَا سَافِلَهَا وَأَمْطَرْنَا عَلَيْهِمْ حِجَارَةً مِّن سِجِّيلٍ

[ayah:15:75] إِنَّ فِى ذَٰلِكَ لَـَٔايَـٰتٍ لِّلْمُتَوَسِّمِينَ

[ayah:15:76] وَإِنَّهَا لَبِسَبِيلٍ مُّقِيمٍ

[ayah:15:77] إِنَّ فِى ذَٰلِكَ لَـَٔايَةً لِّلْمُؤْمِنِينَ

*Fa-akhadhathumu al-sayhatu mushriqin.*

*Fa-ja'alna 'aliyaha safilaha wa amtarna 'alayhim hijaratan min sijjil.*

*Inna fi dhalika la-ayatin li-al-mutawassimin.*

*Wa innaha la-bi-sabilin muqim.*

*Inna fi dhalika la-ayatan li-al-mu'minin.*

**So the cry seized them at sunrise. Then We made its upper part its lower part, and rained upon them stones of baked clay. Surely in that are signs for those who discern deeply. And indeed it is on an established road. Surely in that is a sign for the believers.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

There are moments when a civilization is judged not only by what it desired, but by what it trained itself not to see.

The city has already been shown intoxicated, shameless, and blind. Now the decree lands in full visibility. Dawn comes, but it does not bring relief. It reveals collapse.

[PAUSE]

Yet the Quran does something striking here. It refuses to let the destruction remain a closed past event. It turns the ruined site into an ongoing sign. The road remains. The traces remain. The question becomes: who can read what remains, and who will pass by history as though nothing is speaking?

The hook of the passage is this: **Al-Hijr turns the destruction of Lut's people into a living text on the landscape, teaching that catastrophe becomes guidance only for those who know how to read signs beyond surfaces and for believers whose faith lets them hear history speak.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Al-Sayhah Mushriqin**: Dawn Does Not Guarantee Safety

The first line is sudden:

**fa-akhadhathumu al-sayhatu mushriqin**

The cry seized them at sunrise.

[PAUSE]

The verb **akhadhat-hum** is forceful. It took them, seized them, overtook them. There is no room left for maneuver.

And the timing matters: **mushriqin**. At sunrise, in the morning light, at the hour when visibility should increase.

This is spiritually powerful because people often associate daylight with security. We think darkness is the dangerous hour and morning the relief. But the ayah shows that when Allah's decree arrives, the timing that usually comforts the creature does not shield him. Morning is only mercy when Allah makes it mercy.

The world can be fully lit and still be the hour of downfall.

### 2. **Ja'alna 'Aliyaha Safilaha**: Judgment Reverses the Whole Moral Architecture

Then:

**fa-ja'alna 'aliyaha safilaha**

We made its upper part its lower part.

[PAUSE]

This is not a partial wound. It is inversion. What was above is brought below. What stood is overturned.

The language carries more than physical ruin. It reveals a fittingness in judgment. A people who inverted moral order are answered by a world turned over. What they treated as normal was in fact upside down long before the stones fell. The destruction makes outwardly visible the inward inversion they had been living in all along.

Then comes:

**wa amtarna 'alayhim hijaratan min sijjil**

We rained upon them stones of baked clay.

Rain is usually imagined as mercy, fertility, and provision. Here the sky sends a different descent. Even familiar categories are reversed under judgment. The same Lord who sends water upon dead earth can send stones upon deadened hearts.

### 3. **Li-al-Mutawassimin ... Li-al-Mu'minin**: Not Everyone Reads Ruins the Same Way

Then the passage pivots from event to readership:

**inna fi dhalika la-ayatin li-al-mutawassimin**

Surely in that are signs for the **mutawassimin**.

[PAUSE]

This word is rare and rich. From the root **w-s-m**, mark, sign, trace, distinguishing feature. The **mutawassim** is one who reads marks, discerns traces, sees what surfaces point toward. He is not merely looking; he is interpreting.

Then the surah adds:

**wa innaha la-bi-sabilin muqim**

It is on an established road.

Meaning the signs are not hidden in unreachable places. The road is there. The traces are accessible. The evidence is passed by.

And then:

**inna fi dhalika la-ayatan li-al-mu'minin**

Surely in that is a sign for the believers.

The sequence is profound. There are signs for those who discern traces deeply, and there is a sign for believers. It is as though the passage teaches that faith and perception belong together. The more truly one believes, the less history becomes mute scenery.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Many People See Ruin but Do Not Read It

Human beings pass by overturned lives all the time.

Collapsed families.

Corroded reputations.

Communities eaten from within.

Public cultures that normalize what once would have shocked them.

And still many people only see incident, gossip, or spectacle. They do not read.

[PAUSE]

That is why **al-mutawassimin** is so important. The Quran is not praising mere exposure to events. It is praising a kind of moral sight that can read marks. A person with this sight does not stop at "something happened there." He asks: what was the moral logic? What signs preceded the collapse? What inner inversion became outward catastrophe? What warning is Allah leaving for those who come later?

Without that depth of reading, even a road lined with signs remains spiritually silent.

### Theme 2: Belief Makes the World More Legible

The final ayah ties sign-reading to the believers.

This means iman is not only a set of conclusions about the unseen. It is a way of perceiving the seen.

[PAUSE]

The believer looks at history differently. He does not romanticize downfall. He does not reduce judgment to random sociology. He does not turn the ruins of other people into a thrill. He receives them as warning, as proof, as instruction, as a mercy for those willing to learn before sharing the same end.

This also means that unbelief is not only refusal of doctrine. It can become inability to interpret reality correctly. A person may drive the same road, pass the same traces, read the same reports, and remain spiritually untouched because his framework cannot hear what the signs are saying.

The Quran is training us here to become people who travel through the world awake. To look at the remains of corruption and say: this is not dead history. This is still speaking. Allah did not preserve these signs for archaeology alone.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask what ruins in our own age we have normalized. What moral collapses do we pass every day without reading? Which public inversions have become so familiar that we no longer notice how upside down they are until the consequences become unignorable?

## Closing Synthesis

The cry seizes Lut's people at sunrise. Their world is overturned. Stones descend upon them. Then the Quran turns the scene outward: in this are signs for those who read traces deeply; the site remains on an established road; and in this is a sign for the believers.

[PAUSE]

So the passage does not leave judgment buried in the past. It places it on the road and teaches us that faith should make us better readers of what history, ruins, and visible consequences are saying about moral reality.

### Questions to Carry

- What collapses or ruins in the world around you have you seen but not truly read?
- Do you tend to treat the downfall of others as spectacle, sociology, or warning?
- Where has moral inversion become so normal that you only notice it once consequences arrive?
- What would it mean to travel through the world more like a **mutawassim** and less like a distracted passerby?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The destruction of Lut's people becomes guidance only when the heart learns to read ruins as signs, and the Quran teaches that believers should be among the people for whom history remains morally legible.**

### Du'a

*Allahumma, make us among the mutawassimin and the believers who read Your signs with awake hearts. Do not let us pass by warnings as though they were mute stones. Teach us to see what collapse is saying before we need collapse itself to teach us personally.*
