# Do Not Disgrace Me in Front of My Guests: Desire, Refusal, and Wandering in Intoxication

## Surah Al-Hijr (15:67-72)

[ayah:15:67] وَجَآءَ أَهْلُ ٱلْمَدِينَةِ يَسْتَبْشِرُونَ

[ayah:15:68] قَالَ إِنَّ هَـٰٓؤُلَآءِ ضَيْفِى فَلَا تَفْضَحُونِ

[ayah:15:69] وَٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ وَلَا تُخْزُونِ

[ayah:15:70] قَالُوٓا۟ أَوَلَمْ نَنْهَكَ عَنِ ٱلْعَـٰلَمِينَ

[ayah:15:72] لَعَمْرُكَ إِنَّهُمْ لَفِى سَكْرَتِهِمْ يَعْمَهُونَ

They said: Have we not forbidden you from the people? He said: These are my daughters, if you must act. By your life, indeed they were wandering blindly in their intoxication.**

## Introduction

There is a kind of public corruption that no longer hides.

It does not sneak. It gathers. It arrives excited. It feels entitled to another person's shame. It hears appeals to God, honor, and decency as obstacles rather than truths.

[PAUSE]

That is the atmosphere of this passage. The city comes not grieving but celebrating. Lut pleads first from hospitality, then from taqwa, then from dignity. None of it penetrates. The crowd speaks as though the prophet himself is the one who has crossed a line. And then Allah gives the final diagnosis: they are moving inside an intoxication so deep that it has become blindness.

The hook of the passage is this: **Al-Hijr shows that desire becomes civilizationally catastrophic when it no longer feels shame, no longer hears taqwa, and begins to treat a prophet's defense of dignity as the problem rather than the cure.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Yastabshirun**: They Come Delighted by What Should Have Terrified Them

The first shock of the scene is the verb:

**yastabshirun**

They came rejoicing, receiving what they take as good news.

[PAUSE]

The root **b-sh-r** is the same root of glad tidings, the same root used earlier in the surah for the angels' joyful promise to Ibrahim. But here it is turned toward moral inversion. What delighted Ibrahim's house was mercy from Allah. What delights the city is the arrival of prey for corrupted desire.

That contrast is devastating. The same root of joyful reception can be filled with entirely opposite contents. One household receives bushra from heaven. Another crowd rushes toward degradation and experiences it as exciting news.

This is how moral perception collapses. Evil is not only done. It is welcomed. The heart no longer commits wrong reluctantly. It begins to celebrate the opportunity for it.

### 2. **Fa-la Tafdahun ... La Tukhzun**: Lut Appeals to Social Honor and Fear of Allah Together

Lut says:

**fa-la tafdahun**

Do not disgrace me.

And then:

**wa-ttaqu Allaha wa la tukhzun**

Be mindful of Allah, and do not shame me.

[PAUSE]

The pair of verbs matters. **Tafdahun** from **f-d-h** is exposure, scandal, public humiliation. **Tukhzun** from **kh-z-y** is disgrace, abasement, being brought low in humiliation.

He is not only asking them to leave his guests alone. He is naming what their act does to moral space itself. It would violate hospitality, publicly dishonor him, and display shamelessness in the face of Allah.

And notice the order. He pleads through human dignity and divine consciousness together. The Quran is teaching us that social honor and taqwa are not enemies. Proper fear of Allah should protect people from humiliating one another. A society that mocks both has already become deeply unsafe.

### 3. **Fi Sakratihim Ya'mahun**: The Final Diagnosis Is Deeper Than Lust

Then the passage ends with one of the most piercing judgments in the surah:

**la'amruka innahum la-fi sakratihim ya'mahun**

By your life, they are surely wandering blindly in their intoxication.

[PAUSE]

The word **sakrah** is intoxication, stupefaction, the state in which perception is overwhelmed. But then the verse adds **ya'mahun** from the root **'-m-h**, wandering blindly, moving about without clear guidance, not merely unable to see but morally directionless.

This is crucial. The Quran does not reduce the scene to appetite alone. Appetite has become an altered state of perception. They are not simply wanting wrongly. They are seeing wrongly, moving wrongly, and losing their internal compass entirely.

That is why the verse comes as the final divine interpretation. From inside the city, the crowd probably feels bold, excited, maybe even socially supported. From above, Allah names it intoxicated blindness.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: One Sign of Deep Corruption Is When Shame Changes Sides

One of the most frightening things that can happen to a person or a culture is not merely that wrong becomes common. It is that shame relocates.

The wrongdoer is no longer ashamed.

The one resisting the wrong is.

[PAUSE]

That is exactly what happens here. Lut pleads: do not disgrace me, do not shame me. Why should a prophet be the one talking about embarrassment while the aggressors speak with confidence? Because corruption has advanced far enough that the violator no longer feels exposed. Instead, decency feels cornered and made to plead for space.

This still happens in ordinary life. The honest person is made to feel awkward. The chaste person is treated as naive. The one holding a line of dignity feels socially embarrassed while the transgressor feels breezy, witty, and affirmed. When that inversion sets in, the problem is already much deeper than one act. It is a collapse in the moral distribution of shame.

### Theme 2: Not Every Intoxication Is Chemical

The Quran's closing diagnosis is profoundly relevant.

**Fi sakratihim ya'mahun.**

In their intoxication, they wander blindly.

[PAUSE]

Many people think intoxication only names what enters the body through drink or substance. But the ayah widens the meaning. A person can be intoxicated by desire, by crowd-energy, by public permission, by fantasy, by appetite intensified through repetition, by the feeling that everyone around them is validating the same blindness.

This kind of intoxication may even be more dangerous because it is often mistaken for normality. No slurred speech, no stumbling body, no obvious external sign. Yet inwardly the person is just as overrun, just as unable to perceive rightly, just as unable to stop.

And once that state deepens, exhortation becomes difficult to hear. Hospitality, taqwa, human dignity, prophetic pleading, all pass by the person like weak sounds outside a locked room.

[PAUSE]

That is why guarding the heart early matters so much. Few people intend to arrive at full-blown blindness. They arrive by repeatedly treating lesser distortions as harmless. A little indulgence. A little shamelessness. A little group reinforcement. A little mockery of restraint. Over time the heart's sight dulls, and eventually it calls its own intoxication "clarity."

## Closing Synthesis

The people of the city come rejoicing over an opportunity for corruption. Lut pleads with them on behalf of his guests, invokes taqwa, and begs not to be disgraced. They answer as though he is the problem, not they. He offers the lawful and honorable path if they must act at all. Then Allah Himself closes the scene with the real verdict: they are wandering blindly in the intoxication that has overtaken them.

[PAUSE]

So the passage gives us more than one historical scandal. It gives us a map of moral collapse. Wrong becomes exciting, dignity becomes embarrassing, prophetic counsel becomes irritating, and the crowd mistakes its own stupefaction for life.

### Questions to Carry

- Where have you seen shame relocate onto the person trying to preserve dignity rather than the person violating it?
- What desires or crowd-energies in your own life begin to act like intoxicants, dulling moral sight without obvious outward signs?
- Are there places where you now welcome what should have frightened you?
- When taqwa speaks into your impulses, does it still feel authoritative, or has it started to sound like inconvenience?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**This passage shows that corruption becomes especially deadly when desire is no longer hidden but celebrated, when shame changes sides, and when intoxication becomes a whole way of seeing wrongly rather than a passing urge.**

### Du'a

*Allahumma, do not let our hearts celebrate what should have made us tremble. Protect us from the intoxicants of desire, crowds, and shamelessness that blind moral sight. Let taqwa remain weighty in us, and make us people who preserve dignity rather than people who feed on its collapse.*
