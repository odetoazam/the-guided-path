# The People of the Thicket Were Wrongdoers, and Both Ruins Lie on a Clear Route

## Surah Al-Hijr (15:78-79)

[ayah:15:78] وَإِن كَانَ أَصْحَـٰبُ ٱلْأَيْكَةِ لَظَـٰلِمِينَ

[ayah:15:79] فَٱنتَقَمْنَا مِنْهُمْ وَإِنَّهُمَا لَبِإِمَامٍ مُّبِينٍ

*Wa in kana ashabu al-aykati la-zalimin.*

*Fa-intaqamna minhum wa innahuma la-bi-imamin mubin.*

**And indeed the people of the thicket were certainly wrongdoers. So We took vengeance upon them, and indeed both of them are upon a clear route.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

The Quran does not let one ruined people stand alone as though history offered only a single warning.

As soon as the remains of Lut's people have been named as readable signs, the surah widens again. Another people. Another settled pattern of wrongdoing. Another requital. Another visible reminder on the road.

[PAUSE]

The brevity of this passage is part of its force. It does not need a long retelling to make the point. Once the moral grammar has been established, a few words are enough: they were wrongdoers, We took vengeance on them, and the traces remain visible.

The hook of the passage is this: **Al-Hijr teaches that divine warnings in history are not isolated anomalies but repeated, visible patterns, and the road itself becomes a witness against anyone who keeps treating every destroyed people as someone else's story.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **La-Zalimin**: The Moral Name Comes Before the Historical Detail

The passage says:

**wa in kana ashabu al-aykati la-zalimin**

The people of the thicket were surely wrongdoers.

[PAUSE]

Before details of the event, the Quran gives the moral name: **zalimin**.

That matters because the core issue is not geography first. It is not ethnicity first. It is not narrative color first. It is moral orientation. They were people of **zulm**: misplacement, wrongdoing, violation, putting things where they do not belong and failing to give realities their due.

The briefness sharpens the sentence. There is no decorative expansion. The verdict is clear and sufficient. Sometimes the Quran tells a long story. Sometimes it gives the distilled moral diagnosis in one line.

### 2. **Fa-Intaqamna Minhum**: Requital as a Measured Answer to Wrong

Then:

**fa-intaqamna minhum**

So We took vengeance upon them.

[PAUSE]

The root **n-q-m** carries the sense of requital, exacting response, not chaotic rage but punishment answering established wrongdoing. The **fa** matters too. It links the requital to the wrongdoing just named. Zulm is not morally weightless. It meets response.

This is an important Quranic correction in an age that often treats wrongdoing as either merely private preference or as something indefinitely sustainable. The verse says: no. There are forms of collective injustice and corruption that do not simply continue forever unaddressed.

### 3. **Wa Innahuma La-Bi-Imamin Mubin**: The Route Itself Becomes a Teacher

Then comes the line that ties the previous story to this one:

**wa innahuma la-bi-imamin mubin**

Indeed, both of them are on a clear route.

[PAUSE]

The pronoun points back to the two ruined sites now being held together in the surah's field of vision. And the word **imam** here carries the sense of a path, route, or course that is followed and visible. Not hidden. Not lost. Not buried from human passage.

This is remarkable. The road is not just a way to move past the ruins. It is part of the sign itself. The traveler who passes is not innocent of what he has seen. The route has been made clear enough that forgetfulness becomes less excusable.

The surah is teaching us that Allah not only acts in history. He also leaves historical pedagogy in the world.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Repeated Warnings Are a Mercy, Not Redundancy

Human beings often grow numb to repetition.

If one warning is ignored, the second may feel like an old story. The third may feel remote. The fourth may feel like religious overstatement.

[PAUSE]

But the Quran treats repeated warnings differently. It treats them as mercy. Not because repetition itself saves, but because Allah keeps placing signs in the path of human arrogance rather than destroying people without precedent, pattern, or reminder.

So when the surah moves from Lut's people to the people of the thicket in just two ayahs, it is not being repetitive in a weak sense. It is building a cumulative moral field. It is saying: look again. And again. And again. How many overturned stories must stand on the route before the traveler finally reads the road?

### Theme 2: The Road You Travel May Already Be Teaching You

One of the most piercing elements in this passage is that both sites are on a clear route.

This means guidance is not always delivered only in formal lessons, books, sermons, or private reflection. Sometimes Allah places instruction in the landscapes, institutions, broken cultures, public ruins, and inherited memories that a person keeps moving through.

[PAUSE]

The tragedy is that people can become highly mobile while remaining spiritually untraveled. They pass by sign after sign without inward movement. They know the route but not the lesson.

That remains true now. Many of us travel through worlds marked by visible consequences of greed, shamelessness, exploitation, arrogance, and moral inversion. The signs are not absent. Often the problem is that we have learned to treat them as normal scenery.

The believer asks for another kind of travel: not just movement across the road, but movement inside understanding.

## Closing Synthesis

The companions of the thicket are named as wrongdoers. Allah took vengeance upon them. And both their ruined place and the earlier ruined place remain set upon a clear route.

[PAUSE]

The passage is short, but it lands a weighty point: historical judgment repeats, visible warnings accumulate, and the road itself becomes part of Allah's address to later people. If we keep passing by without learning, the problem is no longer lack of signs.

### Questions to Carry

- What repeated warnings in history or in your surroundings have started to feel ordinary to you?
- Do you treat visible consequences of wrongdoing as lessons, or mostly as background scenery?
- In what ways are you moving through roads, systems, and stories that are already trying to teach you something?
- What would it look like to become inwardly teachable by the routes you travel every day?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**This brief passage teaches that divine warnings in history accumulate along the road, and that the person who keeps passing visible signs without learning can no longer blame a lack of reminders.**

### Du'a

*Allahumma, do not let repeated warnings become ordinary in our eyes. Make us people who learn from what You leave visible on the road of history, and do not let us move through clear signs with closed hearts.*
