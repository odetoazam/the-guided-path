# We Gave You the Seven Oft-Repeated and the Great Quran, So Do Not Stretch Your Eyes Toward What They Enjoy

## Surah Al-Hijr (15:87-89)

[ayah:15:87] وَلَقَدْ ءَاتَيْنَـٰكَ سَبْعًا مِّنَ ٱلْمَثَانِى وَٱلْقُرْءَانَ ٱلْعَظِيمَ

[ayah:15:88] لَا تَمُدَّنَّ عَيْنَيْكَ إِلَىٰ مَا مَتَّعْنَا بِهِۦٓ أَزْوَٰجًا مِّنْهُمْ وَلَا تَحْزَنْ عَلَيْهِمْ وَٱخْفِضْ جَنَاحَكَ لِلْمُؤْمِنِينَ

*Wa la-qad ataynaka sab'an mina al-mathani wa al-Qur'ana al-'azim.*

**And surely We have given you seven of the oft-repeated and the magnificent Quran. Do not ever stretch your eyes toward what We have given some groups among them to enjoy, and do not grieve over them, and lower your wing to the believers. And say: Indeed I am the clear warner.**

## Introduction

One of the Quran's most beautiful movements is the way it heals the prophet-heart after showing it the arrogance of those who refuse.

Not by flattering the rejecters. Not by minimizing their rejection. But by reminding the Messenger of what he has already been given, redirecting his gaze away from their glitter, lowering his wing toward the believers, and then sending him back into his mission with clarity.

[PAUSE]

That sequence matters for anyone trying to stay faithful while surrounded by people who seem to possess more ease, status, or visible enjoyment than the people of truth. The ayahs do not deny the emotional pull of that comparison. They retrain the gaze.

The hook of the passage is this: **Al-Hijr teaches that the cure for being over-impressed by the passing enjoyments of those who reject truth is to remember the incomparable gift of the Quran, soften oneself toward the believers, and stand in one's mission without envy or despair.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Sab'an mina al-Mathani wa al-Qur'an al-'Azim**: You Have Already Been Given What Outweighs Their Enjoyments

The passage opens:

**wa la-qad ataynaka sab'an mina al-mathani wa al-Qur'ana al-'azim**

We have surely given you seven of the oft-repeated and the magnificent Quran.

[PAUSE]

The verse begins with gift-language: **ataynak**. We gave you. Before any command about where not to look, Allah reminds the Prophet what is already in his possession by divine grant.

Then the phrase **sab'an mina al-mathani**. The classical tafsir tradition strongly ties this to al-Fatihah, the seven oft-repeated verses, while also hearing in **mathani** the quality of repetition, return, renewal, and recitation that keeps bringing the heart back.

And then comes the larger circle:

**wa al-Qur'ana al-'azim**

and the magnificent Quran.

This is a stunning recalibration. The one being addressed is not empty, deprived, or second-class before the world's enjoyments. He is the recipient of revelation. The verse wants that fact to become emotionally weighty again.

### 2. **La Tamuddanna 'Aynayka**: Envy Begins With a Lengthened Gaze

Then the command:

**la tamuddanna 'aynayka**

Do not stretch your eyes.

[PAUSE]

This is vivid. It does not say merely "do not want." It describes the eye extending, reaching, lengthening itself toward what others have been given for temporary enjoyment.

That is psychologically precise. Before envy hardens in the chest, the gaze often lingers. It stretches. It keeps returning. It starts to grant glamour to what is being seen.

The ayah treats that extended gaze itself as spiritually consequential. Not every look is the same. There is a look that notices, and there is a look that begins to surrender inward dignity.

And what is being looked at? **Ma matta'na bihi azwajan minhum**: what We have allowed certain groups among them to enjoy. The verse quietly demotes the object. It is **mata'**: provision for temporary enjoyment, not proof of worth.

### 3. **Ikhfidh Janahaka li-al-Mu'minin ... al-Nadhir al-Mubin**: Lower Yourself to the Believers and Stand Up in Warning

The verse continues:

**wa la tahzan 'alayhim**

Do not grieve over them.

Then:

**wa ikhfidh janahaka li-al-mu'minin**

Lower your wing to the believers.

[PAUSE]

This image is one of the Quran's gentlest images for humility, softness, nearness, and protective tenderness. It is not merely "be kind." It is bodily, relational lowering. Come close enough to shelter, enough to make the weak feel held rather than stared down.

Then immediately:

**wa qul inni ana al-nadhiru al-mubin**

Say: I am the clear warner.

The sequence is profound. The Messenger is not told to choose between gentleness and clarity. He is told to hold both. Humble wing with believers. Clear warning to the world.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Comparison Becomes Spiritually Dangerous When We Forget What We Have Already Been Given

The human being becomes very vulnerable when he stares at what others have without first remembering what Allah has already placed in his own hands.

Then the passing thing starts to feel ultimate.

The flashy thing starts to feel weighty.

The temporary enjoyment starts to feel like evidence of real success.

[PAUSE]

That is why the Quran does not begin by saying "stop looking." It begins by saying, in effect: remember what you have been given. Revelation first. Then discipline of gaze.

This remains true for us. If the heart loses touch with the Quran as gift, with prayer as gift, with guidance as gift, with iman as gift, then worldly distributions begin to look disproportionately large. The eye stretches because the soul feels poor. The ayah corrects that poverty by reintroducing divine provision at the deepest level.

### Theme 2: The Healthy Heart Does Not Move From Hurt Into Hardness

Another beautiful feature of the passage is that it does not let rejection deform the Messenger's posture.

He is not told: since they reject you, become cold.

He is not told: since they have more visible enjoyments, become bitter.

He is not told: since the world does not honor you correctly, withdraw into severity.

[PAUSE]

Instead he is told not to over-fixate on their enjoyments, not to sink into grief over them, to lower his wing to the believers, and to keep speaking his role clearly.

This is an extraordinary model for anyone doing sincere work. When others mock, prosper outwardly, or seem more celebrated, the soul is tempted toward two opposite distortions: envy toward the rejecters and impatience toward the believers. The Quran blocks both. Don't stretch your eyes toward them. Lower your wing to these.

That is a whole map for preserving purity in service.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask what has been stretching your eyes lately. Whose visible enjoyments or social placements have been making your own gift feel smaller? And then to ask the harder question: has that comparison begun to make you harsher, less patient, or less tender with the believers nearest to you?

## Closing Synthesis

Allah reminds the Prophet that He has already given him the seven oft-repeated and the magnificent Quran. Then He tells him not to stretch his gaze toward the temporary enjoyments of certain groups among the rejecters, not to grieve over them, to lower his wing to the believers, and to openly declare himself a clear warner.

[PAUSE]

The passage therefore offers a complete rehabilitation of spiritual posture: gratitude instead of comparison, disciplined gaze instead of fascination, tenderness instead of ego-wound, and clear mission instead of reactive confusion.

### Questions to Carry

- What has been stretching your eyes lately and quietly making your own gifts feel small?
- Do you still experience the Quran as a gift weighty enough to recalibrate envy?
- Has comparison with people outside truth made you less gentle with believers inside it?
- What would it look like today to lower your wing to the believers while still speaking with clarity about truth?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The Prophet is taught here that the antidote to fixation on the world's passing enjoyments is to remember the revelation already given, soften oneself toward the believers, and keep standing clearly in the mission Allah assigned.**

### Du'a

*Allahumma, do not let our eyes stretch toward what You gave others in a way that makes us ungrateful for what You gave us. Make the Quran heavy and magnificent in our hearts, lower our wings to the believers, and keep our speech clear without making our hearts hard.*
