# We Created the Human From Ringing Clay, and the Jann Before That From the Fire of Samum

## Surah Al-Hijr (15:26-27)

[ayah:15:26] وَلَقَدْ خَلَقْنَا ٱلْإِنسَـٰنَ مِن صَلْصَـٰلٍ مِّنْ حَمَإٍ مَّسْنُونٍ

[ayah:15:27] وَٱلْجَآنَّ خَلَقْنَـٰهُ مِن قَبْلُ مِن نَّارِ ٱلسَّمُومِ

*Wa la-qad khalaqna al-insana min salsalin min hama'in masnun.*

*Wa al-janna khalaqnahu min qablu min nari al-samum.*

**And surely We created the human being from dried ringing clay, from altered dark mud. And the jann, We created him before that from the fire of scorching samum.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

These two ayahs arrive before the refusal, before the curse, before the open declaration of enmity.

That matters.

Before Surah Al-Hijr tells us what Iblis said, it tells us what both parties were: created. The human being was created. The jann was created. One from clay with a dark, altered earthly origin. The other from a fiery, penetrating blaze. The surah names difference, but it names difference under a larger truth: neither began with himself.

This is one of the Quran's quiet ways of disarming arrogance before it speaks. The scene that will soon become charged with comparison begins with shared dependence.

The hook of the passage is this: **Al-Hijr starts the Adam arc by placing both the human and the jann under the same divine verb of creation, so material difference and temporal priority can never honestly become proof of self-made superiority.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Shared Verb: Both Lines Begin Under Createdness

The first thing to notice is the repeated verb: **khalaqna**.

In the first ayah: "We created the human being."

In the second: "the jann, We created him."

[PAUSE]

Before the story turns into rank, refusal, argument, or punishment, the surah places both beings under the same grammar. They are not self-originating realities. They are brought into being by Allah.

The opening also carries emphasis: **wa la-qad khalaqna**. The combination of the opening conjunction with *la-qad* gives the statement weight and certainty. It is as though the surah is saying: begin here without confusion. Whatever else you are about to hear, do not forget who made whom.

That repeated creation-verb matters spiritually because the ego loves to build identity from distinctions while skipping over dependence. We fixate on temperament, talent, material, history, role, or precedence. The ayah pulls all of that back under one prior truth: you are made.

### 2. **Min Qablu**: Earlier Does Not Mean Greater

The second ayah inserts a phrase that will become morally decisive when the story unfolds: **min qablu**.

The jann was created before.

[PAUSE]

The Quran does not deny precedence. It states it. But look carefully at what the verse does not do. It does not attach praise to the earlier creation. It does not say "before, and therefore higher." It does not say "before, and therefore more deserving." It gives a chronology, not a medal.

This is an important discipline of the Quran. It repeatedly teaches us that facts are not the same thing as claims. Being earlier is a fact. Being better is a claim. The soul often rushes from one to the other without noticing the theft it has committed in between.

We do this constantly in ordinary life. I was here first. I have been practicing longer. I come from the stronger family. I built this earlier. I knew this before you. The nafs quietly converts biography into entitlement.

These ayahs refuse that move before it even begins. Yes, the jann was created before. But the statement remains inside the sentence of creation. It is still a creaturely fact, not an argument for rank.

### 3. Clay and Samum: Different Materials, One Exposure

Then the surah names the materials.

The human being is from **salsal**, from **hama' masnun**. The language is earthy, heavy, tactile. It calls the listener downward into mud, alteration, and dried clay.

The jann is from **nar al-samum**. Here the imagery shifts from weight to penetration, from earth to heat. The word **samum** comes from the root **s-m-m**, a root associated in Arabic with something that penetrates and harms, which is why the classical lexicons and tafsir tradition connect it to scorching, deadly heat that enters deeply rather than merely touching the surface.

[PAUSE]

So the verse does not flatten the difference between the two creations. It wants you to feel it. One beginning sounds brittle and earthen. The other sounds fierce and burning.

But notice how the difference is framed. Both are preceded by **min**: from this, from that. The material is named as source, not as self-authorship. The ayah is not teaching two beings to boast about their ingredients. It is teaching the listener that even distinct origins remain only borrowed beginnings under Allah's will.

This becomes even more powerful when read inside the coming arc. The one who will later refuse will lock onto material and try to turn it into a ladder of superiority. But the surah has already told us how to read the materials properly. They are not trophies. They are evidence of being fashioned.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Ego Turns Description Into Hierarchy

One of the oldest sins in the moral life is taking a description and converting it into a verdict.

You were born in a certain place, with a certain temperament, from a certain family, with a certain intelligence, body, language, class position, education, or history. These are real descriptions. The error begins when the soul quietly concludes that description itself proves superiority.

[PAUSE]

That is why these two ayahs matter before the dialogue with Iblis begins. The surah is teaching us how to read difference before showing us what happens when difference is misread.

If you do not learn this lesson, then almost anything can become fuel for hidden arrogance. The fact that you started practicing before someone else. The fact that you know more. The fact that your wounds are deeper. The fact that your sacrifices are older. Even piety can become a disguised version of *min qablu* when the heart says: I was here first, so I must be above.

But Allah's speech cuts straight through that vanity. Earlier is still created. Different is still created. Stronger-seeming is still created. None of these facts escape the grammar of dependence.

A servant becomes safer the moment he stops reading his biography as a ladder and starts reading it as a test.

### Theme 2: Humility Begins With Honest Origin

There is mercy in the Quran reminding the human being of clay.

Not because the human is worthless, but because we are dangerous when we forget our beginnings. We become inflated, fragile, offended, and theatrical. We begin to demand from others the kind of recognition only Allah should matter enough to give.

[PAUSE]

Clay is a truth that lowers the fever of the ego. It tells you: you are not self-forged. You are not pure light. You are not an untouchable essence moving through the world by your own force. You began low. You began dependent. You began in a form no one would have worshiped unless Allah Himself gave it meaning, form, purpose, and honor.

And the same is true in a different register for the jann. Fiery origin is not autonomy either. Heat is still made. Penetrating force is still made. Quickness is still made. None of it breaks creaturehood.

This is why honest humility is not self-hatred. It is accurate ontology. It is simply refusing to lie about what you are. The liar says: my traits explain my greatness. The truthful servant says: my Lord created me, gave me what I have, and can take me back without difficulty.

[PAUSE]

When we sit with these ayahs long enough, they loosen one of the deepest knots in the heart. We stop needing our differences to certify our worth. We can let gifts be gifts, histories be histories, and distinctions be tests, because none of them need to become a throne.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayahs do not yet narrate the rebellion, but they prepare us to read it correctly.

The human being is created from ringing clay out of altered dark mud. The jann is created before from the fire of samum. Difference is real. Sequence is real. Material origin is real. But every part of the passage is held inside the same larger declaration: Allah created.

[PAUSE]

That means the surah is already dismantling the future argument before it appears. If both beings begin under createdness, then neither material nor precedence can honestly become proof of self-made nobility. The story will soon show what happens when one creature forgets that. These ayahs teach us not to forget it in our own smaller ways.

### Questions to Carry

- What fact about your story have you quietly inflated into a claim of superiority?
- Where have you treated "I was before" as though it meant "I deserve above"?
- Which of your differences have become excuses for comparison instead of invitations to gratitude?
- What would humility look like if you read every distinction in your life as created, assigned, and tested?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Before the Adam story becomes a story of arrogance, Allah introduces both the human and the jann with the same truth: each is only a creature whose origin, timing, and material all begin in Allah's act of creation.**

### Du'a

*Allahumma, do not let us turn what You described into a case for pride. Teach us to remember our beginnings without despair and our gifts without arrogance. Make every difference in us a reason for gratitude, every advantage a reason for responsibility, and every act of createdness a path back to humility before You.*
