# Every Messenger Speaks the Tongue of His People, and Musa Is Told to Remind Them of the Days of Allah

## Surah Ibrahim (14:4-6)

[ayah:14:4] وَمَآ أَرْسَلْنَا مِن رَّسُولٍ إِلَّا بِلِسَانِ قَوْمِهِۦ لِيُبَيِّنَ لَهُمْ ۖ فَيُضِلُّ ٱللَّهُ مَن يَشَآءُ وَيَهْدِى مَن يَشَآءُ ۚ وَهُوَ ٱلْعَزِيزُ ٱلْحَكِيمُ

## Introduction

After opening with the Quran's mission of bringing people from darkness into light, Surah Ibrahim tells us something crucial about how Allah's guidance meets human beings.

No messenger is sent except in the tongue of his people. Revelation does not arrive as a performance of distance. It arrives clothed in intelligibility, close enough to be understood, argued with, loved, or rejected. But immediately after that reassurance comes a corrective: even when the message is clear, guidance is still Allah's. Language removes the excuse of incomprehension. It does not turn hearts by mechanical force.

Then the surah gives Musa as the living example. He too is told to bring his people from darknesses into light. He too must remind them, not just inform them. He must call them back to the remembered days of Allah, those days when divine action became unmistakably visible in history. And his first act of reminding is not abstract theology. It is memory: remember the favor of Allah upon you when He saved you from Pharaoh's people.

The hook of the passage is this: **Surah Ibrahim teaches that prophetic speech comes in the listener's own tongue so the truth may be made plain, but only Allah opens or closes hearts, and one of the greatest means of remaining open is to remember the days when Allah rescued us from what would have destroyed us.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Bi-lisani qawmihi li-yubayyina lahum**: Allah Sends Revelation in the Tongue People Actually Live In

The surah lays down a general rule:

**wa ma arsalna min rasulin illa bi-lisani qawmihi li-yubayyina lahum**

We did not send any messenger except in the language of his people, so that he might make things clear for them.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the Quran's most merciful statements about communication.

Allah does not mock people by addressing them in a form they cannot enter. He sends messengers into real human communities, speaking the tongue those people dream in, argue in, trade in, and grieve in. Revelation comes near enough to confront without being obscured.

Then the purpose:

**li-yubayyina lahum**

so that he may clarify for them, make things plain.

That matters because faith is not meant to be built on deliberate vagueness. The messenger's role includes bayan, making the truth manifest enough that people can no longer hide from it behind claims of incomprehension.

But the ayah does not stop there.

### 2. **Fa-yudillu Allahu man yasha'u wa yahdi man yasha'**: Clarity Does Not Cancel Divine Sovereignty

Immediately after saying the message comes in the language of the people, the ayah says:

**fa-yudillu Allahu man yasha'u wa yahdi man yasha'**

then Allah leaves astray whom He wills and guides whom He wills.

[PAUSE]

This prevents two opposite mistakes.

The first mistake is to imagine that people reject revelation only because it was not explained well enough. The ayah forbids that simplification. Allah sent messengers in the people's own tongue precisely so the matter would be clear.

The second mistake is to imagine that once clarity is present, guidance is automatic. The ayah forbids that too. Hearts are not machines. Guidance remains with Allah.

So the messenger speaks clearly, patiently, accessibly, and truly. But he does not become the owner of hearts.

That is why the ayah closes with:

**wa Huwa al-'azizu al-hakim**

He is the Mighty, the Wise.

His might means no heart can escape His dominion.

His wisdom means His guidance and misguidance are never arbitrary, never random, never without moral depth.

This is not meant to produce passivity. It is meant to produce humility. We speak clearly, but we never imagine ourselves to be the generators of hidayah.

### 3. **Dhakkirhum bi-ayyami Allah ... udhkuru ni'mata Allahi 'alaykum**: Remembrance Is Part of Liberation

Then the surah gives Musa:

**an akhrij qawmaka mina al-zulumati ila al-nur**

Bring your people out of darknesses into the light.

The opening mission of the Quran is mirrored in the mission of Musa. Prophetic work is one in essence across history.

But then something is added:

**wa dhakkirhum bi-ayyami Allah**

and remind them of the days of Allah.

[PAUSE]

Not merely "teach them laws." Not merely "inform them of doctrine." Remind them of Allah's days.

These are the days in which His acts become unmistakably manifest in lived history: rescue, judgment, opening, collapse, deliverance, overturning. The servant needs memory because forgetfulness is one of the ways darkness returns after deliverance.

Then the ayah says:

**inna fi dhalika la-ayatin li-kulli sabbarin shakur**

there are signs in that for everyone steadfast and grateful.

This pairing is beautiful. Some realities can only be read correctly by the one who stays patient through hardship and grateful after rescue. Without patience, memory turns bitter. Without gratitude, memory turns casual and thin.

Then Musa begins exactly where Allah told him to begin:

**udhkuru ni'mata Allahi 'alaykum**

remember Allah's favor upon you.

And what favor is named?

Being saved from Pharaoh's people:

**yasumunakum su'a al-'adhab**

they were afflicting you with the worst torment,

**wa yudhabbihuna abna'akum**

slaughtering your sons,

**wa yastahyuna nisa'akum**

and sparing your women.

The oppression is named concretely. The Quran does not romanticize rescue by keeping the old wound blurry. It forces Bani Isra'il to remember what domination looked like in the body.

Then the closing line:

**wa fi dhalikum bala'un min rabbikum 'azim**

and in that was a tremendous trial from your Lord.

The language of **bala'** holds the weight of testing. The people are being told that even in the history of being saved from Pharaoh, they were under Allah's testing gaze. Oppression was real. Rescue was real. And both demand remembrance before Allah.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: People Can Hear the Truth in Their Own Tongue and Still Refuse It

This passage is deeply relevant for anyone who thinks the real problem of the age is merely better phrasing.

We often imagine that if only we found the perfect language, the perfect tone, the perfect medium, the perfect educational format, then hearts would open automatically. But Allah tells us something more sobering. He sent every messenger in the tongue of his people for clarity. Even then, guidance still remained with Him.

[PAUSE]

That means clarity matters enormously, but it is not omnipotent.

A person can understand the words and still resist the truth they carry.

He can know exactly what Allah is asking and still refuse because of pride, fear, social loyalty, appetite, trauma, or attachment to an old self.

This should soften us and sober us at the same time.

It should soften us because we stop speaking to people with contempt. We speak in ways that can really be heard.

And it should sober us because we stop idolizing technique. No method, no rhetorical brilliance, no educational sophistication can substitute for Allah opening the heart.

That applies outwardly to da'wah and inwardly to ourselves. Sometimes the person most fluent in the language of revelation is still resisting its demand. The issue is no longer vocabulary. It is surrender.

### Theme 2: One of the Great Defenses Against Relapsing Into Darkness Is Remembering the Days of Allah

Musa is told not only to lead his people out, but to remind them of the days of Allah.

This is a tremendous spiritual principle.

Human beings forget rescue very quickly. We normalize what once would have made us weep in gratitude. We survive something crushing, Allah opens a way we could not have created for ourselves, and after a little time we begin living as though the whole thing were ordinary, inevitable, or mostly our own doing.

[PAUSE]

That forgetfulness is dangerous because it prepares the soul to go back to bondage in subtler forms.

A person may not return to Pharaoh externally, but he can return to pharaonic patterns inwardly: fear of human power, service to domination, gratitude-starvation, living under the rule of old wounds, acting as though rescue came from other than Allah.

So remembrance is not nostalgia. It is protection.

Remember when Allah got you out of the relationship that was deforming you.

Remember when He saved you from the sin you thought had become your identity.

Remember when He opened provision after you thought the door was sealed.

Remember when He carried you through grief, confusion, illness, or collapse that should have broken you.

Those are among your days of Allah. If you do not revisit them with gratitude, you may keep the story and lose the lesson.

That is why this passage ends by praising the one who is **sabbar** and **shakur**. Memory needs both. Patience lets us endure the unfinished parts of our story. Gratitude lets us read the finished rescues properly.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs place prophetic communication and human memory in one frame.

Allah sends every messenger in the tongue of his people so the truth is made clear.

But clarity does not mean control of hearts. Guidance remains Allah's.

Then Musa is told to do more than convey ideas. He is told to remind his people of Allah's days and Allah's favor, especially the rescue from a regime that brutalized their bodies and threatened to remake their souls.

So the passage teaches us that the truth must be spoken clearly, heard humbly, and remembered historically. A heart that forgets how Allah saved it becomes vulnerable to new darkness.

Questions to sit with:

1. Do I hide behind claims of confusion in places where Allah's truth is already plain enough for me to obey?
2. Have I started trusting communication techniques more than the One who guides hearts?
3. Which "days of Allah" in my own life have I allowed to fade into the background?
4. Am I living like someone who was rescued by Allah, or like someone who thinks rescue was accidental?

One-sentence distillation: **Allah sends the truth in a language we can understand, but only He guides the heart, and one of the surest ways to stay in the light is to remember the days when He brought us out of darkness.**

Du'a: O Allah, let Your truth reach us clearly and humbly, guide our hearts after clarity comes, and make us a people who remember Your favors and Your rescuing days with patience and gratitude.
