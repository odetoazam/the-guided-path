# He Is Given Festering Water, Swallows It Bit by Bit, and Death Comes Without Release

## Surah Ibrahim (14:16-17)

[ayah:14:16] مِّن وَرَآئِهِۦ جَهَنَّمُ وَيُسْقَىٰ مِن مَّآءٍ صَدِيدٍ

[ayah:14:17] يَتَجَرَّعُهُۥ وَلَا يَكَادُ يُسِيغُهُۥ وَيَأْتِيهِ ٱلْمَوْتُ مِن كُلِّ مَكَانٍ وَمَا هُوَ بِمَيِّتٍ ۖ وَمِن وَرَآئِهِۦ عَذَابٌ غَلِيظٌ

*Min wara'ihi Jahannamu wa yusqa min ma'in sadid.*

*Yatajarra'uhu wa la yakadu yusighuhu wa ya'tihi al-mawtu min kulli makanin wa ma huwa bi-mayyit. Wa min wara'ihi 'adhabun ghaliz.*

**Ahead of him is Hell, and he will be given to drink water of festering discharge. He swallows it in desperate gulps and can hardly make it go down. Death comes to him from every place, but he is not one to die. And beyond him is a harsh punishment.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

The Quran sometimes warns at a distance. These two ayahs do the opposite.

They take us painfully close.

The surah has just said that every stubborn tyrant failed. Now it shows what failure feels like when no propaganda remains, no crowd remains, no land remains, and no false greatness remains. What is left is a body, a punishment, a drink that cannot be swallowed, and a death that comes near but never finishes its work.

The hook of the passage is this: **the Quran depicts punishment here not merely as pain imposed from outside, but as a trapped condition in which even the instinctive human hope for death as release is denied.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Min wara'ihi Jahannam**: What Is Ahead of Him Is Already Behind the Thin Veil

The ayah begins:

**min wara'ihi Jahannam**

Behind him, beyond him, ahead of him in waiting, is Jahannam.

[PAUSE]

The word **wara'** carries a sense richer than a simple directional label. It points to something beyond, behind the immediate screen, lying just past what the person presently occupies. The punishment is not introduced as a distant possibility. It is already there, just beyond the thin layer of delay.

Then comes:

**wa yusqa**

he is made to drink.

Notice the passivity. He is not choosing to drink. The act is done to him. The body that once acted in arrogance is now a body acted upon.

And what is he given?

**ma'in sadid**

water of festering discharge.

The word **sadid** is meant to revolt the listener. This is not ordinary thirst met by ordinary water. The drink itself has become punishment. Something normally associated with relief is turned into horror.

### 2. **Yatajarra'uhu wa la yakadu yusighuhu**: The Ayah Slows the Moment Into the Struggle of Swallowing

Then the Quran narrows the camera further:

**yatajarra'uhu**

he gulps it down bit by bit, forcing it.

[PAUSE]

The form of the verb carries strain. This is not the ease of drinking. It is repeated, reluctant swallowing. The body tries again and again to get through a moment it cannot bear.

Then:

**wa la yakadu yusighuhu**

and he can hardly make it go down.

The word **yusighu** is not merely "to swallow." It carries the sense of making something pass smoothly down the throat. The Quran does not just say he drinks with difficulty. It says the very process by which something becomes bearably swallowed is almost absent.

That is spiritually piercing. A person spent worldly life making falsehood "go down" smoothly, normalizing arrogance, zulm, exploitation, and denial. Now he meets a reality that will not go down at all.

### 3. **Wa ya'tihi al-mawtu min kulli makanin wa ma huwa bi-mayyit**: Death Surrounds Him, but the Exit Refuses to Open

Then comes one of the most terrifying lines in the surah:

**wa ya'tihi al-mawtu min kulli makanin**

death comes to him from every place.

[PAUSE]

The phrase does not say death comes once from one direction. It comes from every place. The sensation of dying surrounds him, presses him, reaches him from all sides.

But then the ayah breaks the instinctive expectation:

**wa ma huwa bi-mayyit**

yet he is not one to die.

This is the horror of suspended ending. The punishment includes nearness to death without the mercy of completion.

Then the ayah closes where it began:

**wa min wara'ihi 'adhabun ghaliz**

and beyond him is a harsh, thick, heavy punishment.

The scene refuses closure. Even after this torment, more remains beyond it. The sinner cannot outlast it, finish it, or pass through it into relief by his own endurance.

[PAUSE]

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: One of the Human Being's Last Imagined Escapes Is That Pain Will Eventually End Itself

In worldly life, people survive unbearable states partly because they tell themselves: this will end.

The illness will break.

The night will pass.

The grief will ease.

Even if not by healing, then at least by numbness, forgetfulness, or the finality of death. There is usually some imagined edge beyond which the suffering cannot keep reaching.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs shatter that assumption for the Hereafter. Here, death itself approaches from every direction but does not perform its usual role. It does not complete the ending. It does not close the account. It does not become mercy.

That matters spiritually because many sins are fueled by practical atheism about endings. We live as though consequences can always be outwaited, outnumbed, negotiated, or escaped by sheer passage of time. The Quran says: there is a realm where time does not save you from what you chose.

### Theme 2: The Body We Trained for False Ease Will Meet a Reality That Refuses to Go Down Smoothly

The line **la yakadu yusighuhu** invites a hard kind of self-recognition.

How much of our worldly life is spent learning how to make ugly things go down smoothly?

We normalize speech we should have refused.

We excuse appetites we should have restrained.

We help ourselves swallow compromises by renaming them maturity, realism, necessity, or just how life works.

[PAUSE]

The heart becomes practiced at moral ingestion. What should have choked us begins to pass easily.

But the Hereafter is the place where the lie finally loses its coating. The thing comes to the throat in its real form. There is no euphemism left, no crowd to normalize it, no self-deception to sweeten it. It cannot be made palatable anymore.

These ayahs are therefore a mercy now. They ask us, while repentance is still open: what are you training your soul to swallow?

[PAUSE]

## Closing Synthesis

The passage begins with Jahannam lying just beyond the present screen, and it ends with more harsh punishment still beyond what has already been described.

Between those two horizons stands the human body in collapse: forced to drink what should revolt it, trying to gulp what will not go down, visited by death from every side, yet denied the finality of dying.

The architecture is deliberate. The Quran is not only frightening the listener. It is correcting one of our deepest illusions: that if we persist long enough in falsehood, eventually the consequences will become manageable or simply end. They will not.

### Questions to Carry

- What have you been teaching your soul to swallow in worldly life that should have choked your conscience?
- Where do you still live as though time by itself will solve what only tawbah can solve?
- If death came to your conscience now from every direction, what unfinished obedience would you wish you had not delayed?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs show a punishment in which even the most basic human hope for release through death is withheld, so that the sinner finally meets a reality that can no longer be normalized, swallowed, or escaped.**

### Du'a

*Allahumma, do not let our hearts grow skilled at swallowing what angers You. Make us choke on falsehood now in the form of repentance, before there comes a day when reality itself will not go down and there is no escape from what lies ahead.*
