# You Know What We Hide and What We Reveal, So Make Me One Who Establishes Prayer and Forgive Us

## Surah Ibrahim (14:38-41)

[ayah:14:38] رَبَّنَآ إِنَّكَ تَعْلَمُ مَا نُخْفِى وَمَا نُعْلِنُ ۗ وَمَا يَخْفَىٰ عَلَى ٱللَّهِ مِن شَىْءٍ فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ وَلَا فِى ٱلسَّمَآءِ

[ayah:14:39] ٱلْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ ٱلَّذِى وَهَبَ لِى عَلَى ٱلْكِبَرِ إِسْمَـٰعِيلَ وَإِسْحَـٰقَ ۚ إِنَّ رَبِّى لَسَمِيعُ ٱلدُّعَآءِ

[ayah:14:40] رَبِّ ٱجْعَلْنِى مُقِيمَ ٱلصَّلَوٰةِ وَمِن ذُرِّيَّتِى ۚ رَبَّنَا وَتَقَبَّلْ دُعَآءِ

**Our Lord, surely You know what we hide and what we reveal, and nothing is hidden from Allah in the earth or in the sky. All praise is for Allah, who granted me, despite old age, Isma'il and Ishaq. Surely my Lord is Hearer of supplication. My Lord, make me one who establishes prayer, and from my descendants as well. Our Lord, accept my supplication. Our Lord, forgive me, my parents, and the believers on the Day the account is established.**

## Introduction

The du'a is closing, and it closes where serious prayer often closes: with exposure, gratitude, dependence, and forgiveness.

Ibrahim has already asked for safety, for distance from idols, for hearts to incline, for provision in a barren valley. Now the prayer turns inward and upward at once. Allah knows what is hidden and what is public. The sons granted in old age are remembered as gift, not entitlement. The central request returns again to prayer. And the final horizon is not worldly completion, but forgiveness on the Day of Account.

The passage is deeply tender, but it is not soft in the careless sense. It is clear-eyed. It knows that hidden things matter, that time passes, that children are gifts, that prayer must be kept standing, and that everyone is moving toward a Day when reckoning itself will stand upright.

The hook of these ayahs is this: **Ibrahim's closing du'a teaches us that the servant matures by living before Allah's full knowledge, receiving gifts as gifts, returning again and again to the establishment of prayer, and widening his hope for forgiveness beyond himself to all believers on the Day of reckoning.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Ta'lamu ma nukhfi wa ma nu'lin**: Du'a Begins From Being Fully Known

The first line says:

**rabbana innaka ta'lamu ma nukhfi wa ma nu'lin**

Our Lord, surely You know what we hide and what we reveal.

[PAUSE]

This is a powerful beginning for the final movement of a prayer. Ibrahim does not speak as though Allah only hears the polished sentence that reaches the tongue. He places the hidden and the disclosed together before Allah.

Then the statement becomes universal:

**wa ma yakhfa 'ala Allahi min shay'in fi al-ardi wa la fi al-sama'**

nothing whatsoever is hidden from Allah in the earth or in the sky.

The du'a is standing inside complete divine knowledge. This does not choke prayer. It purifies it. Once you know Allah already knows what you hide, du'a stops being performance and becomes truthfulness.

### 2. **Wahaba li 'ala al-kibar**: Ibrahim Reads His Sons as Gift, Not as Delayed Entitlement

Then comes praise:

**al-hamdu lillahi alladhi wahaba li 'ala al-kibari Isma'ila wa Ishaq**

all praise is for Allah, who gifted me Isma'il and Ishaq despite old age.

[PAUSE]

The key word is **wahaba**. He granted, gifted, bestowed. Ibrahim names his sons through the language of gift. He does not speak with the tone of owed fulfillment finally delivered. He speaks with الحمد.

And the phrase **'ala al-kibar** matters because it places the gift against human improbability. Time had already advanced. The normal expectation of life's sequence had thinned. Yet the gift came anyway.

That is why the ayah closes:

**inna rabbi la-sami'u al-du'a'**

surely my Lord is Hearer of supplication.

He is not only the giver of outcomes. He is the One who hears the servant while the outcome is still absent.

### 3. **Ij'alni muqima al-salati ... ighfir li**: The Du'a Ends Where the Life Must Stand

Then Ibrahim returns to the most central request:

**rabbi ij'alni muqima al-salati wa min dhurriyyati**

My Lord, make me one who establishes prayer, and from my descendants as well.

[PAUSE]

Not merely one who prays occasionally, but one who keeps prayer standing in life. This is striking after all the other gifts named in the passage. Even after sons are granted, sanctuary is established, and hearts are drawn, Ibrahim still asks above all to be made a person of prayer.

Then:

**rabbana wa-taqabbal du'a'**

our Lord, accept my supplication.

The one making du'a still asks that the du'a itself be received. This is adab before Allah. Even prayer is not presumed accepted just because it was uttered.

Then the final widening:

**rabbana ighfir li wa-li-walidayya wa-li-l-mu'minina**

our Lord, forgive me, my parents, and the believers.

The prayer expands outward. It does not end in the tight circle of the self. And it ends with:

**yawma yaqumu al-hisab**

the Day the account stands.

The Day is described through reckoning rising into undeniable presence. So the du'a ends not in abstraction, but in the reality before which forgiveness will matter most.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: One of the Greatest Mercies in Du'a Is That Allah Already Knows the Hidden Layer

Many people become paralyzed in prayer because they think they must first sort themselves out well enough to speak cleanly to Allah.

But Ibrahim begins here from the opposite direction. You know what we hide and what we reveal. The hidden confusion, the mixed intention, the private fear, the thing we could not phrase properly, all of it is already known.

[PAUSE]

This does not make truthfulness optional. It makes it possible. You do not have to introduce your interior world to Allah. You have to stop hiding from the One who already knows it.

### Theme 2: The Mature Believer Keeps Returning to Prayer and Forgiveness Even After Receiving Great Gifts

This is another beautiful balance in the passage.

Ibrahim remembers the extraordinary gift of sons in old age, but he does not let gift replace dependence. He moves quickly from الحمد to **muqima al-salat**, from gratitude to prayer, from prayer to acceptance, from acceptance to forgiveness.

[PAUSE]

That sequence heals a common spiritual mistake. Sometimes when people are deprived, they remember Allah. Then once gifted, they begin to live as though the gift itself were the arrival-point.

Ibrahim shows the opposite. The gift becomes more praise, more prayer, more dependence, and more expansive mercy-seeking for others.

That is how gratitude stays alive.

[PAUSE]

## Closing Synthesis

Ibrahim closes his du'a by standing in full honesty before Allah's knowledge of the hidden and the open.

He praises Allah for children given in old age, reading them as gift.

He returns to the central request that life itself be a life of established prayer.

He asks for acceptance of the du'a he is making.

And he widens the plea for forgiveness from himself to his parents to the believers, all under the shadow of the Day when account will stand.

This is what prophetic du'a sounds like when it matures: truthful, grateful, prayer-centered, and expansive in mercy.

### Questions to Carry

- What changes in your du'a when you begin from the fact that Allah already knows what you hide and what you reveal?
- Which gift in your life most needs to be turned into praise rather than quiet entitlement?
- If you had to name one thing you most want standing in your life on the Day of Account, would it be prayer?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs teach that the servant lives well by praying before Allah's complete knowledge, receiving late gifts as pure gift, asking above all to remain a person of prayer, and widening the plea for forgiveness toward all believers before the Day of Account.**

### Du'a

*Allahumma, You know what we hide and what we reveal, so make us truthful with You. Turn every gift into praise, make us and our children people who establish prayer, accept our supplications, and forgive us, our families, and the believers on the Day when the account stands.*
