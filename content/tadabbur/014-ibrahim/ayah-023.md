# They Are Admitted to Gardens by Their Lord's Permission, and Their Greeting There Is Peace

## Surah Ibrahim (14:23)

[ayah:14:23] وَأُدْخِلَ ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ وَعَمِلُوا۟ ٱلصَّـٰلِحَـٰتِ جَنَّـٰتٍ تَجْرِى مِن تَحْتِهَا ٱلْأَنْهَـٰرُ خَـٰلِدِينَ فِيهَا بِإِذْنِ رَبِّهِمْ ۖ تَحِيَّتُهُمْ فِيهَا سَلَـٰمٌ

*Wa udkhila alladhina amanu wa 'amilu al-salihat jannatin tajri min tahtiha al-anharu khalidina fiha bi-idhni rabbihim. Tahiyyatuhum fiha salam.*

**And those who believed and did righteous deeds will be admitted into gardens beneath which rivers flow, abiding there by permission of their Lord. Their greeting therein is: Peace.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

This ayah arrives like mercy after suffocation.

The previous hereafter scenes are full of exposure, helplessness, accusation, and failed alliances. Then the surah turns, and without noise or spectacle it gives the believers something all the previous scenes were starving for: entry, permanence, and peace.

The hook of the ayah is this: **Jannah is not presented here merely as reward after pain, but as a world whose deepest texture is secure admission by Allah and a settled atmosphere in which the language itself has become peace.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Wa udkhila alladhina amanu**: Entry Itself Is a Gift Done to Them

The ayah begins with a passive verb:

**wa udkhila**

they are admitted, made to enter.

[PAUSE]

That matters. The believers do not storm Jannah, seize it, or earn entry in a self-sufficient way. Entrance is granted to them. It is done for them.

Then they are described by the two things the Quran repeatedly joins:

**alladhina amanu wa 'amilu al-salihat**

those who believed and did righteous deeds.

Faith is not left abstract, and deeds are not left rootless. The pair arrives together because the Garden belongs to the people whose inward trust and outward action learned to belong together in dunya.

### 2. **Khalidina fiha bi-idhni rabbihim**: Permanence Still Rests on Divine Permission

Then the ayah gives one of the most beloved descriptions:

**khalidina fiha**

abiding there, remaining there permanently.

[PAUSE]

After so much human instability, the word **khalidina** lands with enormous tenderness. No eviction, no collapse, no reversal, no expiration.

But then the ayah adds something beautiful:

**bi-idhni rabbihim**

by permission of their Lord.

Even in Paradise, the believer is kept inside adab. His permanence is not independence. His safety is still under the Lord who brought him there. Jannah is security, but not autonomy from Allah. It is security *with* Him, from Him, by His permission.

### 3. **Tahiyyatuhum fiha salam**: The Speech of That World Has Been Healed

Then the final line:

**tahiyyatuhum fiha salam**

their greeting there is peace.

[PAUSE]

The word **tahiyyah** is more than a passing hello. It is the form of reception, recognition, and address that meets a person in that world.

And what is that form?

**salam**.

Not negotiation.

Not fear.

Not hidden rivalry.

Not accusation.

Not one more speech of blame.

Peace.

After all the broken language of dunya and all the devastating speech scenes of the Hereafter - followers pleading, leaders failing, Shaytan disowning - the language of Jannah is healed down to its greeting.

[PAUSE]

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: One of the Deep Human Longings Is Not Just for Survival, but for Safe Arrival

Many people live as though life is one long attempt merely to keep going.

Get through the week.

Manage the next burden.

Stay afloat in relationships, work, fear, regret, and inner noise.

But beneath that is a deeper longing: not just to survive movement, but to arrive somewhere finally safe.

[PAUSE]

This ayah answers that longing with the verb of admission. The believers are not left wandering in endless transition. They are brought in.

That is spiritually profound because much of dunya feels like exposure without arrival. The soul is always bracing. Jannah here is not described first by luxury, but by entry and abiding. You are in. And you remain.

### Theme 2: Peace in the Quran Is Not Merely the Absence of War, but the Healing of the Whole Atmosphere

The final word of the ayah is **salam**, and that is not accidental.

Human life is full of distorted speech. We greet each other while hiding envy. We reassure while carrying resentment. We negotiate, posture, flatter, threaten, and defend. Even our words can feel unsafe.

[PAUSE]

The ayah says the atmosphere of Jannah is so healed that even the greeting has become peace.

That means no one is guarding themselves there.

No one is scanning for subtext.

No one is waiting for betrayal in the next sentence.

No one is trying to secure their place through language.

What the heart has wanted for so long - to be received without fear - becomes the native speech of that world.

[PAUSE]

## Closing Synthesis

This ayah is short, but it contains an entire emotional world.

The believers are admitted, not self-installed.

They abide, not temporarily rest.

Their permanence remains under their Lord's permission, so security never becomes independence.

And the language of that place is peace.

Set beside the surrounding scenes of accusation, helplessness, and disowning, the contrast is overwhelming. The people of false reliance find no escape. The people of iman and righteous action are brought into a world whose first spoken texture is salam.

### Questions to Carry

- What forms of false security are you still clinging to instead of asking Allah for real admission into His peace?
- How much of your religious life is aimed merely at surviving, and how much is aimed at arriving with Allah?
- If Jannah's greeting is peace, what in your speech now needs to be purified so it begins to resemble the world you are asking for?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**This ayah presents Jannah as secure admission by Allah into a lasting world where even the basic language of relationship has been healed into peace.**

### Du'a

*Allahumma, admit us among those who believe and do righteous deeds, keep us by Your permission among the people of abiding nearness, and begin teaching our tongues the language of salam before the day we hope to hear it forever.*
