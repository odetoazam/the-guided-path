# Remember the Covenant, Then Stand Justly

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:7-8)

[ayah:5:7] وَٱذْكُرُوا۟ نِعْمَةَ ٱللَّهِ عَلَيْكُمْ وَمِيثَـٰقَهُ ٱلَّذِى وَاثَقَكُم بِهِۦٓ إِذْ قُلْتُمْ سَمِعْنَا وَأَطَعْنَا ۖ وَٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ عَلِيمٌۢ بِذَاتِ ٱلصُّدُورِ

[ayah:5:8] يَـٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ كُونُوا۟ قَوَّٰمِينَ لِلَّهِ شُهَدَآءَ بِٱلْقِسْطِ ۖ وَلَا يَجْرِمَنَّكُمْ شَنَـَٔانُ قَوْمٍ عَلَىٰٓ أَلَّا تَعْدِلُوا۟ ۚ ٱعْدِلُوا۟ هُوَ أَقْرَبُ لِلتَّقْوَىٰ ۖ وَٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ خَبِيرٌۢ بِمَا تَعْمَلُونَ

*Wa adhkuru ni'mata Allahi 'alaykum wa mithaqahu alladhi wathaqakum bihi idh qultum sami'na wa ata'na. Wa ittaqu Allah. Inna Allaha 'alimun bidhati al-sudur.*

*Ya ayyuha alladhina amanu kunu qawwamina lillahi shuhada'a bil-qist. Wa la yajrimannakum shana'anu qawmin 'ala alla ta'dilu. I'dilu huwa aqrabu lil-taqwa. Wa ittaqu Allah. Inna Allaha khabirun bima ta'malun.*

**And remember the favor of Allah upon you and His covenant by which He bound you when you said, "We hear and we obey." And fear Allah. Indeed, Allah is Knowing of what is within the breasts. O you who have believed, be steadfast for Allah, witnesses in justice, and do not let the hatred of a people lead you to not be just. Be just; that is nearer to taqwa. And fear Allah. Indeed, Allah is All-Aware of what you do.**

## Introduction

These two ayat tell us that justice is not sustained by ethics alone. It is sustained by remembrance.

That is why the sequence matters so much. First: remember Allah's favor upon you. Remember the covenant by which you were bound when you said, "We hear and we obey." Remember that you are not self-created moral agents inventing your own standards. You are a people who answered Allah. Then, on that foundation, the next ayah commands: stand for Allah as witnesses in justice, and do not let hatred of a people pull you away from fairness.

This is profoundly realistic. The Qur'an knows that injustice often begins long before a verdict. It begins when memory of Allah grows thin. It begins when covenant fades into slogan. It begins when the ego feels free to answer injury on its own terms. So before the surah commands justice under pressure, it reawakens the covenant underneath the believer's identity.

And then it gives one of the hardest moral commands in the Qur'an: do not let dislike of a people make you unjust. The verse does not ask you to feel no dislike. It asks you to keep dislike from becoming law inside your soul.

The hook of the passage is this: **the believer can only remain just in moments of hostility if he first remembers that he already pledged Allah hearing and obedience before the test of hatred ever arrived.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Wa adhkuru ni'mata Allahi 'alaykum wa mithaqahu**: The Ayah Begins by Tying Justice to Remembered Gift and Remembered Bond

The first command is:

wa adhkuru.

And remember.

This is not accidental preparation. The verse is about to call the believers into public moral steadiness, and it begins by summoning memory. The Qur'an knows that forgetfulness is one of the earliest causes of moral collapse.

[PAUSE]

Then it tells them what to remember:

ni'mata Allahi 'alaykum wa mithaqahu.

The favor of Allah upon you and His covenant.

The pairing is beautiful. Ni'mah is gift, care, provision, grace. Mithaq is not a casual promise. It is a thickened covenant, a binding pledge with seriousness in it. So the believer stands before Allah not only as one commanded, but as one favored and bound.

That matters because some people remember only command and grow hard, while others remember only favor and grow loose. The Qur'an joins them. Allah favored you. Allah bound you. Gratitude and obligation belong together.

### 2. **Sami'na wa ata'na**: The Community Once Defined Itself by Receptive Obedience

Then the verse reaches back to the moment of commitment:

idh qultum sami'na wa ata'na.

When you said, "We hear and we obey."

This is one of the most beautiful concise summaries of faith in the Qur'an. Hearing comes first, because obedience without reception is empty performance. But hearing alone is not praised if it stops there. It matures immediately into obedience.

[PAUSE]

The verse is reminding the believers of their own words. This is powerful morally. When Allah calls you back to justice, He does not merely impose from above. He reminds you of what you already said. In other words, the test of justice is not the moment Allah suddenly became demanding. It is the moment your earlier pledge becomes expensive.

That should change how we hear commands. Many of us experience a hard command as if Allah were asking something new and unreasonable from us. The Qur'an teaches us to hear differently: this is the unfolding of what "sami'na wa ata'na" always meant.

### 3. **Inna Allaha 'alimun bidhati al-sudur**: The Covenant Is Guarded at the Level of the Chest

Then the verse ends:

inna Allaha 'alimun bidhati al-sudur.

Indeed Allah is Knowing of what is within the breasts.

This is a searching ending. Before the next ayah even addresses outward justice, Allah locates His knowledge in the inner chamber. The صدر is the place where intentions, resentments, unspoken motives, and moral drift begin taking shape.

[PAUSE]

That means the verse is already warning us that covenant breach does not start at the visible act. It starts in the inner climate of the chest. A person may still speak religiously, still appear compliant, still know the right language, while something corrosive is already forming inside.

So the command to remember is not nostalgic. It is protective. It is meant to keep the interior from decaying before the exterior follows.

### 4. **Kunu qawwamina lillahi shuhada'a bil-qist**: Justice Is Not Occasional; It Is a Standing Posture for Allah

The next ayah commands:

kunu qawwamina lillahi shuhada'a bil-qist.

Be constantly standing for Allah, witnesses in justice.

Qawwamin is stronger than a passing act. It suggests sustained standing, repeated establishment, moral uprightness that becomes a posture rather than a moment. And notice the order: lillah first, then witness in justice. The believer does not uphold justice merely because it is socially admirable. He does so for Allah.

[PAUSE]

That order matters immensely. If justice is not anchored in Allah, then it will usually bend whenever fear, loyalty, tribe, ideology, or hurt becomes intense enough. But when one is standing for Allah, justice gains a reference point beyond the volatility of the ego.

Then shuhada'a bil-qist shows that witnessing itself must carry qist, equity and fairness. It is not enough to speak. One must speak justly.

### 5. **Wa la yajrimannakum shana'anu qawmin**: Hatred Must Not Be Allowed to Become a Judge

Then comes the severe moral warning:

wa la yajrimannakum shana'anu qawmin 'ala alla ta'dilu.

Do not let the hatred of a people drive you into not being just.

Shana'an is intense dislike, aversion with heat in it. And yajrimannakum carries the sense of pushing, compelling, dragging. The ayah is exposing a psychological truth: hatred wants motion. It wants to take the wheel.

[PAUSE]

And then the command comes in stark simplicity:

i'dilu.

Be just.

No long philosophical argument. No excuse management. No acknowledgment that injustice may feel understandable here. Just: be just.

Then the reason:

huwa aqrabu lil-taqwa.

That is nearer to taqwa.

This is one of the most elegant moral formulations in the Qur'an. Taqwa is not left as private inward caution alone. It is measured through whether justice survives dislike.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

One of the most dangerous lies the ego tells is that fairness is for calm moments, but strong emotions deserve exceptions.

We tell ourselves this in countless ways. "Under normal circumstances I would be balanced, but these people have a history." "Ordinarily I would be generous, but they wronged us first." "I still believe in justice, just not for them right now." What the Qur'an exposes is that this is not a slight adjustment of justice. It is the collapse of justice at the exact place where it matters most.

That is why the passage begins with covenant memory. If you only approach justice as a value you happen to like, then hatred will eventually outbid it. But if you approach justice as part of your answer to Allah, then the question changes. It is no longer "What do they deserve from my anger?" It becomes "What do I owe Allah even while anger is present?"

[PAUSE]

The verse is also deeply humane because it does not deny shana'an. It does not pretend people never feel hostility. It does not demand emotional numbness. It demands moral governance. That is harder, but also more realistic. The point is not to feel nothing. The point is that feeling should not become sovereign.

This is where many communities fail. They remember wrongs vividly and covenants weakly. They keep a precise archive of injuries and a blurred memory of "sami'na wa ata'na." Then, when the moment of judgment comes, they feel morally authorized to let bitterness sit on the bench.

The Qur'an refuses that arrangement. Remember the ni'mah. Remember the mithaq. Remember what you said. Then stand for Allah. Then be just. The sequence is deliberate because the soul needs anchoring before pressure comes.

[PAUSE]

There is also a subtle communal lesson here. The command is plural. A whole people are being told to become qawwamin lillah. That means justice is not only an individual virtue. It is supposed to become a collective posture. Communities should be training one another to resist the moral distortion of outrage, not rewarding one another for increasingly eloquent unfairness.

And that has consequences for how we speak, platform, testify, narrate conflict, and build loyalties. Are we helping one another remember the covenant before taking positions? Are we making room for qist, or are we emotionally engineering one another toward selective justice?

[PAUSE]

The line "Allah is Knowing of what is within the breasts" also guards against self-deception. A person may perform fairness outwardly while inwardly delighting in another's humiliation. Another may speak harsh truth but secretly be animated by revenge. The Qur'an keeps bringing us back inside because the morality of justice is not only in the verdict. It is also in the state from which the verdict emerges.

So this passage asks for something mature and difficult: a just heart, not just a correct statement; a remembered covenant, not just a borrowed ethic; a people who fear Allah enough that hatred is never allowed to draft the terms of fairness.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayat reveal that justice is covenantal before it is procedural.

First the believers are told to remember Allah's favor and the thick pledge they once made: we hear and we obey. Then they are commanded to become people who stand for Allah as witnesses in qist. Then the hardest part is named plainly: hatred of a people must not push you into abandoning justice. The reason is luminous and severe at once: justice, especially under pressure, is nearer to taqwa.

So the architecture is exact. Memory protects the chest. The protected chest can stand for Allah. Standing for Allah allows just witness. Just witness survives hatred. And that survival is one of the clearest measures of taqwa.

[PAUSE]

Ask yourself:

- What injuries do I remember more vividly than the covenant I made with Allah?
- When I judge people I dislike, do I still feel answerable to qist, or only to my own history with them?
- Am I cultivating a heart that can remain just under pressure, or only a rhetoric of justice for comfortable moments?
- In my circles, are we helping one another remember ni'mah and mithaq, or feeding each other's shana'an?

The distillation of the passage is this: **the believer remembers Allah's favor and covenant so that when hatred rises, justice still answers to Allah rather than to the ego's demand for imbalance.**

O Allah, keep Your covenant alive in our hearts, make us people who stand for You in justice, and do not let dislike of any people pull us away from qist and taqwa.
