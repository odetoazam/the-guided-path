# We Fear a Turn of Fortune

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:51-53)

[ayah:5:51] ۞ يَـٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ لَا تَتَّخِذُوا۟ ٱلْيَهُودَ وَٱلنَّصَـٰرَىٰٓ أَوْلِيَآءَ ۘ بَعْضُهُمْ أَوْلِيَآءُ بَعْضٍ ۚ وَمَن يَتَوَلَّهُم مِّنكُمْ فَإِنَّهُۥ مِنْهُمْ ۗ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ لَا يَهْدِى ٱلْقَوْمَ ٱلظَّـٰلِمِينَ

[ayah:5:52] فَتَرَى ٱلَّذِينَ فِى قُلُوبِهِم مَّرَضٌ يُسَـٰرِعُونَ فِيهِمْ يَقُولُونَ نَخْشَىٰٓ أَن تُصِيبَنَا دَآئِرَةٌ ۚ فَعَسَى ٱللَّهُ أَن يَأْتِىَ بِٱلْفَتْحِ أَوْ أَمْرٍ مِّنْ عِندِهِۦ فَيُصْبِحُوا۟ عَلَىٰ مَآ أَسَرُّوا۟ فِىٓ أَنفُسِهِمْ نَـٰدِمِينَ

[ayah:5:53] وَيَقُولُ ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوٓا۟ أَهَـٰٓؤُلَآءِ ٱلَّذِينَ أَقْسَمُوا۟ بِٱللَّهِ جَهْدَ أَيْمَـٰنِهِمْ ۙ إِنَّهُمْ لَمَعَكُمْ ۚ حَبِطَتْ أَعْمَـٰلُهُمْ فَأَصْبَحُوا۟ خَـٰسِرِينَ

*Ya ayyuha alladhina amanu la tattakhidhu al-yahuda wa al-nasara awliya'. Ba'duhum awliya'u ba'd. Wa man yatawallahum minkum fa-innahu minhum. Inna Allaha la yahdi al-qawma al-zalimin.*

*Fatara alladhina fi qulubihim maradun yusari'una fihim yaquluna nakhsha an tusibana da'irah. Fa'asa Allahu an ya'tiya bil-fathi aw amrin min 'indihi fa-yusbihu 'ala ma asarru fi anfusihim nadimin.*

*Wa yaqulu alladhina amanu aha'ula'i alladhina aqsamu bi-Allahi jahda aymanihim innahum lama'akum. Habitat a'maluhum fa-asbahu khasirin.*

**O you who have believed, do not take the Jews and the Christians as intimate allies. They are allies of one another. Whoever among you turns to them in such allegiance is indeed of them. Indeed Allah does not guide the wrongdoing people. So you see those in whose hearts is disease rushing into them, saying, "We fear that a turn of fortune may strike us." But perhaps Allah will bring victory or a matter from Him, and they will become regretful over what they had concealed within themselves. And those who believe will say, "Are these the ones who swore by Allah with their strongest oaths that they were surely with you?" Their deeds have come to nothing, and they have become losers.**

## Introduction

Fear has a way of choosing loyalties before the tongue admits what it has done.

When a person feels exposed, uncertain, politically vulnerable, socially threatened, or materially insecure, he begins looking for shelter. And the place he runs first often reveals what he truly believes will save him. Not what he says in calm moments. Not what he claims when identity is cheap. But what he trusts when the ground begins to shake.

That is the world of this passage.

These ayahs are not speaking about ordinary fairness, neighborliness, or human decency in the abstract. The passage itself explains its own atmosphere. It is speaking about people who, under pressure, rush into outside allegiance because they fear a hostile turn of events. They say openly: **We fear that a turn of fortune may strike us.** So they go running, not from compassion, but from insecurity. Not from principle, but from fear.

That is why 5:51 uses the language of **awliya'** and then 5:52 immediately shows diseased hearts hurrying into those very alliances. The text interprets itself. The issue here is not everyday civility. It is whom the heart takes as patron, shelter, deep allegiance, and practical camp when faith starts feeling costly.

Then the passage does something painful and merciful. It exposes the inside of this fear-driven behavior. It names the sickness in the heart. It names the hidden reasoning. It predicts the moment when the masks fall. And then it leaves us with one of the heaviest outcomes in the surah: **their deeds come to nothing, and they become losers.**

The hook of the passage is this: **the sick heart runs fastest toward worldly shelter exactly where it trusts Allah least.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **La tattakhidhu ... awliya'**: The Passage Is About Chosen Allegiance, Not Mere Contact

The command begins:

**La tattakhidhu** - do not take, do not adopt, do not make for yourselves.

That verb matters. It implies active selection. Deliberate placement. This is not merely living among people or dealing with them in the necessities of life. It is taking them into a role.

And what role?

**Awliya'.**

From the root of closeness, support, protective nearness, and allegiance. In this context, the word points to a kind of intimate patronal alignment, a moral-political sheltering relationship, a camp one entrusts oneself to.

[PAUSE]

The next line confirms that this is about bloc-level allegiance:

**Ba'duhum awliya'u ba'd** - some of them are awliya' of others.

The ayah is naming formations of loyalty. It is speaking about where one binds oneself for protection, identity, and strategic belonging. Then it intensifies:

**Wa man yatawallahum minkum fa-innahu minhum** - whoever among you turns to them in that allegiance is indeed of them.

This is not casual language. It means that when loyalty settles deeply enough, it reshapes identity. The place you keep running for shelter will eventually start claiming you.

[PAUSE]

That is why the verse closes:

**Inna Allaha la yahdi al-qawma al-zalimin** - Allah does not guide the wrongdoing people.

The ظلم here is profound. It is misplacement. Putting reliance where it does not belong. Wronging the covenant of faith by relocating the heart's dependence into a camp formed by fear rather than by trust in Allah.

### 2. **Fi qulubihim marad ... yusari'una fihim**: Disease Does Not Always Freeze the Soul; Sometimes It Makes It Hurry

Verse 52 gives us one of the Qur'an's most revealing portraits of hypocrisy:

**Fatara alladhina fi qulubihim marad** - so you see those in whose hearts is disease.

The problem begins inside. Before outward alliances become visible, there is already a sickness in the heart. Something unstable. Something unsound in trust, love, courage, or certainty.

Then look at the movement of the diseased heart:

**Yusari'una fihim** - they rush into them.

[PAUSE]

This is such a precise description. Illness here does not produce hesitation first. It produces haste. The sick heart becomes restless to secure itself through the wrong attachment. It moves quickly where the healthy heart would have paused, weighed, feared Allah, and examined its motives.

So not all spiritual sickness looks like laziness. Sometimes it looks like strategic speed. Immediate networking. Quick repositioning. Fast visible loyalty toward whatever appears most likely to preserve status, safety, or advantage.

Then the ayah lets us hear their inner logic:

**Nakhsha an tusibana da'irah** - we fear that a turn of fortune may strike us.

The word **da'irah** carries a circling event, a reversal, a changing turn that lands on people. The future may go badly for us, they think. Power may shift. Circumstances may harden. We might end up on the losing side. So let us secure backup shelter now.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the central spiritual tests of fear. When outcomes feel unstable, where do we run first? To Allah? Or to whatever worldly structure seems likeliest to absorb the blow?

The ayah is teaching us that fear itself does not corrupt the heart automatically. What exposes the heart is the direction fear sends it.

### 3. **Fa'asa Allahu an ya'tiya bil-fath**: The Future They Fear Is Not The Future Allah Has Written

Then Allah overturns their calculations:

**Fa'asa Allahu an ya'tiya bil-fath aw amrin min 'indih** - perhaps Allah will bring the opening, or a matter from Him.

This is one of the passage's deepest mercies. The people rushing in fear are acting as though the future belongs to the visible map before them. Allah reminds them that it does not. He may bring **fath** - opening, victory, decisive release - or another command and event from Him that changes the whole terrain.

[PAUSE]

So the diseased heart is not just morally wrong. It is strategically blind. It binds itself to visible strength because it cannot imagine the unseen intervention of Allah reordering what looked settled.

Then comes the exposure:

**Fa-yusbihu 'ala ma asarru fi anfusihim nadimin** - and they will wake up regretful over what they concealed within themselves.

The hidden interior comes to the surface through regret. What had been buried in the nafs becomes undeniable. Fear had already confessed it before the tongue did. But then reality comes and strips away the ambiguity.

That should make us pause. A person may conceal his deepest loyalties from others for a long time. But when Allah's decree lands, the hidden interior suddenly becomes public through shame.

### 4. **Aqsamu bi-Allahi jahda aymanihim ... habitat a'maluhum**: Loud Oaths Cannot Rescue a Hollow Interior

Verse 53 shows the moment of communal astonishment:

**Aha'ula'i alladhina aqsamu bi-Allahi jahda aymanihim innahum lama'akum?** - are these the ones who swore by Allah, with the utmost force of their oaths, that they were surely with you?

Their speech had once been emphatic. Their assurances sounded strong. Their loyalty had been verbally dramatized.

[PAUSE]

But the passage has already taught us the problem: the heart had chosen elsewhere before the mouth was finished swearing.

Then the verdict:

**Habitat a'maluhum** - their deeds came to nothing.

This is a terrifying outcome. Not merely that they made an error in judgment. Not merely that they were embarrassed socially. Their works collapse. Their outward efforts lose weight.

And then:

**Fa-asbahu khasirin** - they became losers.

The loss here is bigger than reputation. They lose the very moral capital they thought they were protecting through fearful alliances. They ran toward safety and arrived at ruin.

[PAUSE]

That is the bitter irony of the passage. They moved out of fear of losing their place in the world. In the end, the world they tried to secure becomes the stage on which their hidden disease is exposed and their deeds are emptied.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Fear Reveals What We Secretly Believe Will Save Us

These ayahs force us to ask a question we usually avoid:

When we feel threatened, what do we instinctively trust?

Not what answer we would give in a halaqah. Not what creed we would sign. But what our reflexes reveal. Whose approval becomes urgent. Which doors suddenly seem necessary. Which compromises become reasonable. Which camps become attractive. Which silences become excusable. Which loyalties become negotiable.

[PAUSE]

That is why the phrase **nakhsha an tusibana da'irah** is so psychologically honest. The people in the verse do not present themselves as villains. They present themselves as afraid. They sound practical. Strategic. Cautious. Concerned about what may happen.

And precisely there the Quran exposes the disease.

Because fear is one of the easiest places for the nafs to baptize betrayal as prudence. We tell ourselves we are only being realistic, only reading the room, only securing the future, only avoiding unnecessary harm. But underneath that language may be a deeper confession: I am no longer convinced that staying firm with Allah is the safest place to stand.

[PAUSE]

That is a devastating realization, but also a saving one. It means the cure is not pretending not to fear. The cure is retraining fear until it returns us to Allah instead of pushing us away from Him.

### Theme 2: The Diseased Heart Often Moves Faster Than the Healthy Heart

There is something else deeply revealing here: **yusari'una fihim**.

They rush.

We often imagine the spiritually compromised person as sluggish. He is lazy in worship, slow to repent, hesitant in obedience. Sometimes that is true. But this passage shows another profile entirely: the diseased heart can be extremely active. Extremely responsive. Extremely quick.

[PAUSE]

It moves fast wherever self-protection is at stake.

It knows whom to text, whom to flatter, where to reposition, how to signal, when to soften its stance, where to attach itself, which line to cross quietly, and which loyalty to dilute before anyone notices.

That means speed is not a proof of health. Activity is not a proof of sincerity. Strategic intelligence is not a proof of guidance.

Sometimes the healthy heart is slower because it is listening. It is checking its intention. It is asking whether this move comes from tawakkul or from panic, from principle or from insecurity, from faith or from hunger to survive at any moral cost.

[PAUSE]

The verse invites us to become suspicious of our own hurriedness wherever fear is doing the driving.

### Theme 3: Empty Oaths Cannot Carry a Heart That Has Already Chosen Another Camp

Verse 53 is one of the saddest scenes in the surah because it captures the collapse of performed sincerity.

These were people who had sworn emphatically. They had given assurances. They had spoken the right language. They had been able to say, in effect, "We are with you."

But the passage shows that once the interior loyalty has shifted, language cannot hold the structure up forever.

[PAUSE]

Sooner or later, Allah brings the moment that exposes the heart.

Then the community looks back in shock: **Were these the ones who swore so strongly?**

That line should humble all of us. It means religious speech can be loud while spiritual truth is thin. The right vocabulary can coexist with a diseased interior for a while. But not forever.

So the real work is not only to speak belonging. It is to build a heart whose loyalties do not change camps when the circle of hardship begins to close.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture

This passage begins with **awliya'** and ends with **khasirin**.

In between, it shows us the whole hidden chain.

Chosen allegiance.
Disease in the heart.
Hastened movement toward false shelter.
Fear of a hostile turn.
Allah's unexpected opening.
Exposure of what was concealed.
Collapsed deeds.
Loss.

[PAUSE]

So these ayahs are not only warning us about one historical group. They are revealing a recurring spiritual mechanism.

When fear outruns trust in Allah, the heart looks for another protector.
When another protector is chosen, loyalty shifts.
When loyalty shifts, speech becomes hollow.
When speech becomes hollow, deeds are emptied.
And when deeds are emptied, the person discovers too late that the refuge he ran toward could not save him.

### Questions to Carry

1. When I fear loss, humiliation, or instability, where does my heart run first for shelter?
2. What hurried loyalties or compromises in my life might actually be fear wearing the clothes of realism?
3. If Allah exposed what I have concealed in myself, would my outward claims of belonging still ring true?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs teach that the heart diseased by fear rushes toward false shelter, but Allah eventually exposes the loyalties it concealed and leaves empty oaths and hollow deeds in ruins.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, purify our hearts from the disease that makes us seek protection anywhere but with You. Do not let fear choose our loyalties before faith has spoken. Make us truthful in our belonging, firm in our trust, and protected from hidden hypocrisy. When the future feels uncertain, let our hearts run toward You first, and never let our deeds collapse because our interior chose another shelter. Ameen.
