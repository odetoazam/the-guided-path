# Will You Finally Stop?

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:90-92)

[ayah:5:90] يَـٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوٓا۟ إِنَّمَا ٱلْخَمْرُ وَٱلْمَيْسِرُ وَٱلْأَنصَابُ وَٱلْأَزْلَـٰمُ رِجْسٌ مِّنْ عَمَلِ ٱلشَّيْطَـٰنِ فَٱجْتَنِبُوهُ لَعَلَّكُمْ تُفْلِحُونَ

[ayah:5:91] إِنَّمَا يُرِيدُ ٱلشَّيْطَـٰنُ أَن يُوقِعَ بَيْنَكُمُ ٱلْعَدَٰوَةَ وَٱلْبَغْضَآءَ فِى ٱلْخَمْرِ وَٱلْمَيْسِرِ وَيَصُدَّكُمْ عَن ذِكْرِ ٱللَّهِ وَعَنِ ٱلصَّلَوٰةِ ۖ فَهَلْ أَنتُم مُّنتَهُونَ

[ayah:5:92] وَأَطِيعُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ وَأَطِيعُوا۟ ٱلرَّسُولَ وَٱحْذَرُوا۟ ۚ فَإِن تَوَلَّيْتُمْ فَٱعْلَمُوٓا۟ أَنَّمَا عَلَىٰ رَسُولِنَا ٱلْبَلَـٰغُ ٱلْمُبِينُ

*Ya ayyuha alladhina amanu innama al-khamru wa al-maysiru wa al-ansabu wa al-azlamu rijsun min 'amali al-shaytan fa-ijtanibuhu la'allakum tuflihun.*

*Innama yuridu al-shaytanu an yuqi'a baynakumu al-'adawata wa al-baghda'a fi al-khamri wa al-maysiri wa yasuddakum 'an dhikri Allahi wa 'ani al-salah. Fa-hal antum muntahun.*

*Wa ati'u Allaha wa ati'u al-rasula wa ihdharu. Fa-in tawallaytum fa'lamu annama 'ala rasulina al-balagh al-mubin.*

**O you who believe, indeed intoxicants, gambling, idols, and divining arrows are filth from the work of Satan, so avoid them that you may prosper. Satan only wants to sow among you enmity and hatred through intoxicants and gambling and to turn you away from the remembrance of Allah and from prayer. So will you finally stop? And obey Allah and obey the Messenger and beware. But if you turn away, then know that upon Our Messenger is only the clear conveyance.**

## Introduction

Some sins arrive wearing the face of ease.

They look social, ordinary, entertaining, almost harmless. They enter gatherings with laughter. They promise release, bonding, relief from pressure, a little thrill, a little forgetting, a little loosening of what feels too tight inside us. But what walks in smiling does not always leave us smiling. Some things do not simply pass through a person's evening. They pass through his judgment, his tongue, his relationships, his prayer, and eventually his nearness to Allah.

That is the world of this passage.

These ayahs do not speak in the language of private preference. They do not say merely that khamr and maysir are bad habits that carry some personal risk. They expose them as part of a shaytanic project. First the verse names them as defilement from the work of Satan. Then it pulls back the curtain on the strategy beneath them: enmity, hatred, distraction from Allah's remembrance, and distraction from salah. Then the sequence closes with a question sharp enough to shake a soul awake: **So will you finally stop?**

[PAUSE]

That final question matters because this passage is not only about substances or games of chance. It is about obedience becoming real at the moment Allah asks for surrender. There are times when the believer is not being asked to admire wisdom from afar. He is being asked to stop.

The hook of these ayahs is this: **Allah does not only forbid what poisons the body or drains the purse; He forbids what Satan uses to fracture the heart's bond with people and its bond with prayer.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Rijsun min 'amali al-shaytan**: The Passage Names the Moral Texture Before It Gives the Command

The first ayah opens with a strong restriction:

**innama**.

It is the language of narrowing and confining. The verse is not offering a casual description. It is fixing how these things must be seen.

Then it names four realities together: **al-khamr**, **al-maysir**, **al-ansab**, and **al-azlam**. Intoxicants, gambling, idolatrous altar-stones, and divining arrows. The list is striking because it gathers what modern people often split apart. Some are obviously "religious" corruptions. Some look like leisure. But the ayah teaches that what matters is not whether a thing appears sacred or recreational. What matters is whether it becomes an opening through which obedience is displaced and the soul is made available to Satan.

[PAUSE]

Then comes the verdict:

**rijsun min 'amali al-shaytan**.

Filth from the work of Satan.

The word **rijs** is not mild. It points to corruption, foulness, something morally contaminating rather than neutral. And it is not described as a random human invention alone. It is tied to **'amali al-shaytan**. The passage is training the believer to see a deeper causality beneath the surface object. There is an operation here. A strategy. A worker behind the work.

Then comes the command:

**fa-ijtanibuhu**.

So avoid it.

The verb is stronger than a suggestion not to overindulge. It carries the sense of putting something to the side, keeping distance, refusing nearness. The ayah does not say manage it carefully. It says create space between yourself and it.

And then the promise:

**la'allakum tuflihun**.

So that you may prosper.

That is crucial. The prohibition is not framed as deprivation for its own sake. Allah is not narrowing your life to make you smaller. He is removing what blocks **falah**.

### 2. **An yuqi'a baynakumu al-'adawata wa al-baghda'a**: Satan Wants Social Fracture Before He Wants Theological Collapse

The next ayah begins again with **innama**, but this time it narrows Satan's intention:

**innama yuridu al-shaytanu**.

Satan only wants.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the mercies of revelation: it names the adversary's aim so you are not left interpreting your ruin as an accident.

Then:

**an yuqi'a baynakumu al-'adawata wa al-baghda'a**.

To cast between you enmity and hatred.

The verb **yuqi'a** gives a sense of dropping something into place, causing it to land, making it occur. Enmity and hatred are not presented here as purely spontaneous emotions. Satan wants to make them happen, to lodge them between people until community becomes abrasion.

And notice the pairing: **'adawah** and **baghda'**. Outward hostility and inward loathing. The verse is describing not only broken interactions but poisoned interiors. Something that starts with intoxication and gambling does not stay in the cup or on the table. It migrates into how people feel toward one another.

That alone is devastating, because many sins are justified on the grounds that they are "personal." But the Quran says these are not merely personal. They are socially corrosive.

### 3. **Wa yasuddakum 'an dhikri Allahi wa 'ani al-salah**: Social Damage Is Only Half the Story

Then the ayah goes deeper:

**wa yasuddakum 'an dhikri Allahi wa 'ani al-salah**.

And to turn you away from the remembrance of Allah and from prayer.

[PAUSE]

The verb **yasuddakum** is forceful. It is not mere distraction in the weak sense of a passing lapse. It is blocking, turning aside, preventing passage. Satan wants to make a barrier.

And what is the barrier erected against?

Not merely productivity.
Not merely family stability.
Not merely good reputation.

The barrier is raised against **dhikr Allah** and **al-salah**.

That is the heart of the matter. Revelation is teaching us that some behaviors are spiritually deadly not only because they produce visible chaos, but because they interfere with the two things that keep a human being inwardly alive: remembering Allah and standing before Him.

The sequence is also telling. First comes damage among people, then damage in the servant's tie to Allah. Social corruption and spiritual heedlessness feed each other. When the heart is pulled away from dhikr and salah, the soul becomes easier to inflame. When the soul is inflamed, it becomes easier to wound others. The cycle tightens.

### 4. **Fa-hal antum muntahun**: The Passage Climaxes in a Question About Obedience, Not Information

After exposing the whole strategy, the ayah does not continue with more argument. It asks:

**fa-hal antum muntahun**.

So are you ones who will stop?

[PAUSE]

This is one of the most searching questions in the surah.

The word **muntahun** is not simply "do you understand?" It is from the pattern of ceasing, desisting, coming to an end. The question does not test whether the believer has gathered data. It tests whether the believer will halt.

That is often the real spiritual threshold. Many of us already know more than enough about what harms us. What remains unresolved is not knowledge but ending.

Then the third ayah seals the matter:

**wa ati'u Allaha wa ati'u al-rasula wa ihdharu**.

Obey Allah and obey the Messenger and beware.

The repetition of **ati'u** matters. Allah and the Messenger are not collapsed into a vague religious feeling. Obedience is named with force and clarity. Then **ihdharu** adds alertness, caution, vigilance. Be on guard. Do not sleepwalk around what can undo you.

And if someone still turns away?

**fa-in tawallaytum fa'lamu annama 'ala rasulina al-balagh al-mubin**.

If you turn away, then know that upon Our Messenger is only the clear conveyance.

Meaning: the truth has been made plain. The Messenger's task is delivery, not coercion. Once revelation has arrived clearly, the unresolved matter is yours.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Some Sins Are Designed to Break More Than the Person Who Commits Them

Modern moral language often trains us to ask only one question: "Am I hurting anybody else?"

And even that question is usually answered narrowly. If the harm is not immediate, visible, and measurable, we reassure ourselves that the matter is private. My body. My money. My night. My coping mechanism. My entertainment.

But this passage refuses that flat way of seeing.

[PAUSE]

The ayah does not only speak about the person consuming or wagering. It speaks about what is being thrown **between you**. That phrase matters. Satan wants something lodged in the space between people. Suspicion. Friction. Unpredictability. Broken trust. Speech that becomes sharper, hands that become looser, tempers that become easier to ignite, homes that become harder to live in.

That means the believer must learn to see certain prohibited things as atmospheric, not only individual. They change the air around them. They enter friendships, marriages, homes, and gatherings. They normalize heedlessness. They multiply regret. They leave people sorting through fallout the next day that the sin promised would never arrive.

So one of the gifts of these ayahs is that they save us from a childish moral imagination. Not everything that feels voluntary is actually contained. Not everything that begins in pleasure ends in solitude. Some sins spread.

### Theme 2: Real Obedience Is Proven When Allah Asks You to End What You Still Desire

There is another reason this passage hits so hard. It does not only explain harm. It confronts desire.

The final question, **fa-hal antum muntahun**, lands after the evidence has been laid out. Satan wants enmity, hatred, forgetfulness, and neglected prayer. The matter has been clarified. But the question still remains because stopping is not only an intellectual act. It is a surrender of attachment.

[PAUSE]

That is where many of us recognize ourselves even beyond the specific case of khamr or maysir. We all have something we keep near even after we understand its cost. Something we call manageable though it keeps taking more from us than we admit. Something that weakens our dhikr, delays our salah, corrodes our relationships, or fills the inner world with a fog we have started calling normal.

In that sense, these ayahs become a mirror. They ask whether obedience is real only where it agrees with our inclinations, or whether it becomes real precisely when Allah tells us to stop where we still want to continue.

Then the passage answers despair before it even rises. It does not say, "Generate purity by yourself." It says obey Allah, obey the Messenger, beware. In other words, do not fight this battle as though it were merely self-improvement. Fight it as obedience, as vigilance, as loyalty to a clear word from your Lord.

That changes everything. A person can endure the pain of abstaining from a false comfort if he knows he is not merely losing something. He is answering Allah.

## Closing Synthesis

This passage moves with unmistakable force.

First the prohibited things are named and morally unmasked.
Then their hidden strategy is exposed.
Then the damage is shown on two axes: between people, and between the servant and Allah.
Then the ayah asks the only question left worth asking:
will you stop?

[PAUSE]

And finally it grounds the answer in obedience, vigilance, and the clarity of the Messenger's delivery.

That is the architecture. These ayahs do not leave us at the level of taboo. They teach us to see sin as strategy, harm as layered, and obedience as the point at which knowledge becomes real.

### Questions to Carry

1. What have I been calling "private" that may actually be changing the atmosphere of my relationships and my home?
2. Is there anything in my life that repeatedly weakens my dhikr or makes salah feel heavier, yet I still keep it near?
3. When Allah makes a harm plain, do I keep negotiating with desire, or do I hear the question: will you stop?
4. What would it look like for vigilance before Allah to become stronger than my appetite for temporary relief?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs teach that intoxicants and gambling are not mere personal excesses but shaytanic instruments that fracture human bonds and sever the heart from remembrance and prayer, so real faith appears in actually stopping when Allah commands it.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, show us harmful things as harmful before they root themselves in us. Keep us far from what poisons our hearts, disturbs our homes, weakens our remembrance, and delays our prayer. Make us people who stop when You command, who obey with vigilance, and who seek our relief in nearness to You rather than in what turns us away from You. Amin.
