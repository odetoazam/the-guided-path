# To Him Belongs the Dominion

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:40)

[ayah:5:40] أَلَمْ تَعْلَمْ أَنَّ ٱللَّهَ لَهُۥ مُلْكُ ٱلسَّمَـٰوَٰتِ وَٱلْأَرْضِ يُعَذِّبُ مَن يَشَآءُ وَيَغْفِرُ لِمَن يَشَآءُ ۗ وَٱللَّهُ عَلَىٰ كُلِّ شَىْءٍ قَدِيرٌ

*Alam ta'lam anna Allaha lahu mulku al-samawati wa al-ard. Yu'adhdhibu man yasha'u wa yaghfiru liman yasha'. Wa Allahu 'ala kulli shay'in qadir.*

**Do you not know that to Allah belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth? He punishes whom He wills and forgives whom He wills. And Allah is over all things competent.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

After a ruling, this ayah gives us the sky above the ruling.

The previous passage spoke about theft, consequence, repentance, and the possibility that Allah turns back to the one who mends after wrongdoing. This ayah now asks a question that pulls us out of narrow legal reading and into theology: do you not know that to Allah belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth?

That means the moral order is not self-standing. Punishment does not belong to human anger. Forgiveness does not belong to human softness. Both belong to the Lord of all dominion. He punishes whom He wills, and He forgives whom He wills. The same Allah who laid down consequence is the Allah who receives repentance, because both realities emerge from His ملك, not from competing moral instincts inside us.

This is why the ayah is so important after legal discourse. Law can become harsh when people forget Allah's sovereignty. It can also become sentimental when they forget it. But when the heart remembers that the heavens and the earth belong to Him, then punishment and forgiveness both fall back into their proper place: expressions of divine authority, wisdom, and power.

And the ayah is framed as a question because this is not meant to be new information only. It is meant to be recovering knowledge we should already live by. Do you not know? Then why do you keep behaving as if consequence or mercy were in your hands to define absolutely?

The hook of the ayah is this: **the believer can only understand judgment and mercy rightly when both are returned to the Allah whose dominion covers everything and whose power is never constrained by our narrow categories.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Alam ta'lam**: The Ayah Opens by Calling Us Back to What We Ought Already to Know

The verse begins with a question:

**Alam ta'lam** - do you not know?

This opening matters. The ayah is not introducing some strange hidden doctrine. It is summoning the listener back to an overlooked certainty. The kind of forgetfulness being corrected here is not lack of information alone. It is living as though known truth were absent.

[PAUSE]

That is spiritually piercing. Many distortions in our moral life do not come from ignorance in the simplest sense. They come from practical forgetfulness. We know Allah is sovereign, but we panic as though outcomes belong to us. We know mercy is His, but we despair as though our return is impossible. We know justice is His, but we judge like owners rather than servants.

So the verse begins by reopening a truth that should have governed our thinking already.

### 2. **Lahu mulku al-samawati wa al-ard**: Dominion Explains Why Both Punishment and Forgiveness Are His

Then comes the heart of the ayah:

**Lahu mulku al-samawati wa al-ard** - to Him belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth.

**Mulk** is not bare ownership. It is dominion, authority, sovereignty, right of disposal, lordship over the order of things.

[PAUSE]

This is why the verse does not mention punishment and forgiveness first. It mentions mulk first. The moral order is rooted in sovereign reality. Allah is not one actor inside the universe competing with other claims of power. The heavens and the earth belong to Him. That means every sentence, every mercy, every opening, every closure, every law, every exception, every accepted repentance stands inside His dominion.

Once that is understood, the rest of the ayah becomes clear. Punishment is not independent from mercy, and mercy is not weakness against justice. Both are acts of the One who owns all command.

### 3. **Yu'adhdhibu man yasha'u wa yaghfiru liman yasha'**: Severity and Mercy Are Spoken in One Breath

Then the verse says:

**He punishes whom He wills and forgives whom He wills.**

The pairing is profound. The ayah does not split these into rival realms. It places them side by side in one sentence under one sovereignty.

[PAUSE]

This matters because the human heart often wants to absolutize one side and flatten the other. Some souls are drawn to punishment because control and severity make them feel morally strong. Others are drawn to forgiveness because softness feels emotionally safer. The Qur'an refuses both mutilations. Allah punishes. Allah forgives. Both are true. Both are His.

And notice: the verse does not hand us a formula by which we master these realities. It leaves them in the language of His will. That preserves humility. We are not owners of divine mercy, nor owners of divine judgment. We are servants standing before both.

### 4. **Wallahu 'ala kulli shay'in qadir**: Nothing Limits What Allah Can Do With a Soul

Then the ayah ends:

**Wallahu 'ala kulli shay'in qadir** - and Allah is over all things competent.

This closing is not generic. It seals the meaning of the whole verse. If His dominion is total, then His power is total. No case is too complicated for Him to judge. No sinner is beyond His power to forgive if He wills. No tyrant is beyond His power to punish. No legal consequence, no spiritual restoration, no hidden state of the heart lies outside His competence.

[PAUSE]

That is both unsettling and comforting. Unsettling, because it means we cannot hide in some zone beyond divine reach. Comforting, because it means nothing in us is too ruined for Allah's power to address.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: We Misread Law When We Forget the Lord Behind It

One of the reasons people become confused by legal passages is that they isolate the ruling from the Lord who revealed it.

Then the law appears as bare severity, or bare structure, or social control, or abstraction detached from living theology. This ayah heals that fracture. It says: remember who owns the heavens and the earth. Remember whose dominion this ruling belongs to. Remember that the One who punishes and forgives is not a cold mechanism but Allah.

[PAUSE]

That does not make the ruling less serious. It makes it more intelligible. The law is not floating. It is anchored in mulk, wisdom, and power.

### Theme 2: The Soul Wants to Seize Mercy or Judgment as Its Own Property

Human beings often speak as though mercy and punishment were theirs to distribute in absolute terms.

We do this inwardly when we despair as though our sin is too large for Allah's forgiveness. We do it outwardly when we speak of others as though their case were permanently closed under our judgment. In both directions, we are acting as if we have stolen a portion of Allah's mulk.

[PAUSE]

This ayah restores scale. He punishes whom He wills. He forgives whom He wills. You are not the owner of either final verdict. Your task is taqwa, repentance, obedience, justice, and hope. The final dominion remains His.

There is relief in that. It means we are not asked to become miniature gods over ourselves or others. We are asked to return everything to Allah.

### Theme 3: Divine Power Means No Soul Is Outside Seriousness or Outside Hope

Because the ayah ends with **qadir**, it guards us from two opposite lies.

One lie says: Allah will punish, but perhaps this case is too tangled, too compromised, too lost for forgiveness to reach. The other says: Allah forgives, but perhaps His mercy means moral order is not really serious. The ayah destroys both. He is capable over all things.

[PAUSE]

That means every soul should feel both seriousness and hope. No one should feel safe in disobedience because Allah's power is real. No one should despair of return because Allah's power is real. The same ending holds both truths in place.

## Closing Synthesis

This brief ayah gives us the theology beneath both punishment and forgiveness.

It opens by summoning us back to what we should already know: Allah's dominion covers the heavens and the earth. Then it places punishment and forgiveness together under that dominion. Then it seals the whole matter by reminding us that Allah is powerful over all things.

So the architecture is simple and immense. Know His dominion. Then understand His judgment and His mercy. Then rest in the truth that nothing escapes His power.

[PAUSE]

Ask yourself:

- Where in my life have I been thinking about mercy or punishment as though they were detached from Allah's sovereign rule?
- Do I despair about myself or judge others as though I owned final verdicts?
- What known truth about Allah's dominion am I practically forgetting right now?
- How would my repentance, fear, and hope change if I really lived inside the knowledge that to Him belongs the heavens and the earth?

The distillation of the ayah is this: **Allah's dominion over everything is the foundation beneath both punishment and forgiveness, so the believer must return all final judgment, all hope, and all fear to Him alone.**

O Allah, teach us to know Your dominion the way we should know it, protect us from stealing judgment or despairing of mercy, and let our hearts stand before Your power with truthful fear and truthful hope.
