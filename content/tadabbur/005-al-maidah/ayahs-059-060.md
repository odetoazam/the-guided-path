# When Belief Becomes the Offense

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:59-60)

[ayah:5:59] قُلْ يَـٰٓأَهْلَ ٱلْكِتَـٰبِ هَلْ تَنقِمُونَ مِنَّآ إِلَّآ أَنْ ءَامَنَّا بِٱللَّهِ وَمَآ أُنزِلَ إِلَيْنَا وَمَآ أُنزِلَ مِن قَبْلُ وَأَنَّ أَكْثَرَكُمْ فَـٰسِقُونَ

[ayah:5:60] قُلْ هَلْ أُنَبِّئُكُم بِشَرٍّ مِّن ذَٰلِكَ مَثُوبَةً عِندَ ٱللَّهِ ۚ مَن لَّعَنَهُ ٱللَّهُ وَغَضِبَ عَلَيْهِ وَجَعَلَ مِنْهُمُ ٱلْقِرَدَةَ وَٱلْخَنَازِيرَ وَعَبَدَ ٱلطَّـٰغُوتَ ۚ أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ شَرٌّ مَّكَانًا وَأَضَلُّ عَن سَوَآءِ ٱلسَّبِيلِ

*Qul ya ahla al-kitabi hal tanqimuna minna illa an amanna bi-Allahi wa ma unzila ilayna wa ma unzila min qablu wa anna aktharakum fasiqun.*

*Qul hal unabbi'ukum bisharrin min dhalika mathubatan 'inda Allah. Man la'anahu Allahu wa ghadiba 'alayhi wa ja'ala minhum al-qiradata wa al-khanazira wa 'abada al-taghut. Ula'ika sharrun makanan wa adallu 'an sawa'i al-sabil.*

**Say, "O People of the Scripture, do you resent us except because we have believed in Allah and what was revealed to us and what was revealed before, and because most of you are defiantly disobedient?" Say, "Shall I inform you of what is worse than that as recompense with Allah? It is that of those whom Allah has cursed and with whom He became angry and made of them apes and pigs and slaves of Taghut. Those are worse in position and further astray from the sound way."**

## Introduction

There is a kind of hostility that hurts because it forces you to ask whether you have actually done something wrong.

When people dislike you, mock you, exclude you, or keep treating your commitment to Allah as though it were some defect, the nafs quickly starts searching for explanations. Maybe I was too visible. Maybe I should have softened the edges. Maybe the problem is not what they are seeing, but the fact that I am still showing it.

That is the pressure these ayahs answer.

The surah has just spoken about people who turn din and prayer into ridicule. Now it addresses them more directly and asks a devastating question: what exactly do you resent in us?

And the answer is even more devastating: you resent that we believe in Allah, that we believe in what was sent down to us, and that we believe in what was sent down before. In other words, the offense is not betrayal of revelation but fidelity to revelation in its fullness.

[PAUSE]

Then the passage does something the wounded believer desperately needs. It refuses the enemy's moral scale. It does not accept their dislike as proof of your disgrace. It turns the question back and says: shall I tell you what is actually worse in recompense with Allah?

That shift is everything.

Because one of the soul's deepest vulnerabilities is letting hostile people decide what counts as shameful. Once that happens, truth starts defending itself before false standards. These ayahs come to liberate the believer from that captivity. They do not deny that other people may resent him. They deny that resentment has the right to define reality.

The hook of the passage is this: **when belief itself becomes the offense, the believer must stop borrowing his moral scale from the offended.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Hal tanqimuna minna illa an amanna**: The Ayah Forces the Grievance into the Light

The first question is surgical:

**Hal tanqimuna minna** - do you resent us? do you hold something against us? do you find fault in us?

The word **tanqimuna** is stronger than mere disagreement. It carries blame, censure, resentful fault-finding. The ayah is not dealing with calm theological distance. It is dealing with grievance sharpened into hostility.

[PAUSE]

Then comes the word that exposes everything:

**illa** - except.

Do you resent us **except** for one thing? Meaning: strip away the noise. Name the real charge. Bring the grievance to its purest form.

And what is left?

**An amanna bi-Allahi** - that we have believed in Allah.

This is one of the Quran's most clarifying moments. Sometimes the resentment directed at believers is not because they were unjust, treacherous, or morally corrupt. Sometimes it is because their faith itself is intolerable to people who do not want that standard standing in front of them.

The ayah gives the believer enormous relief here. It tells him that not every accusation deserves internalization. Some hostility is really just a reaction to iman.

### 2. **Wa ma unzila ilayna wa ma unzila min qabl**: The Believer Is Faulted for Wholehearted Reception

Then the statement widens:

**Wa ma unzila ilayna wa ma unzila min qabl** - and what was revealed to us and what was revealed before.

This is a beautiful moral posture compressed into one line. The believers are not being presented as sectarian rejecters who honor only what reached them and dismiss all prior revelation. They believe in Allah's sending to them and before them. Their faith is expansive, receiving the continuity of revelation rather than narrowing it to tribal possession.

[PAUSE]

That matters because it reveals the irrationality of the grievance. The People of the Book are not being told that the Muslims despise previous scripture. Quite the opposite. The believers affirm the revelatory river in full.

So why the resentment?

Because the real problem is not that revelation is being dishonored. The real problem is that revelation is being honored too completely. A heart that wants selective religion will always feel threatened by full reception.

That is why the ayah concludes:

**Wa anna aktharakum fasiqun** - and that most of you are defiantly disobedient.

Notice the precision. It does not flatten every individual into one verdict. It says **most of you**. But it also refuses flattery. The issue is not lack of information. It is **fisq** - stepping outside the boundary, living in breach rather than covenantal fidelity.

The resentment, then, is not neutral. It comes from a moral condition.

### 3. **Hal unabbi'ukum bisharrin min dhalika mathubatan 'inda Allah**: The Scale Is Lifted from Social Reaction to Divine Recompense

The second ayah begins with another question:

**Hal unabbi'ukum** - shall I inform you?

This is not a defensive reply. It is a re-framing. The verse is about to take the whole conversation out of the court of social resentment and place it before Allah.

[PAUSE]

Then comes the crucial phrase:

**bisharrin min dhalika mathubatan 'inda Allah** - of what is worse than that in recompense with Allah.

That phrase **'inda Allah** is the turning point of the passage. Near Allah. With Allah. In Allah's reckoning.

Not in the gossip of communities.
Not in the politics of public opinion.
Not in the emotional reactions of offended people.

The ayah is saying: if you want to know what is truly disgraceful, stop using the scale of those who resent faith. Ask what something is worth before Allah.

And once that shift is made, the whole atmosphere changes. The believer no longer has to beg hostile people to certify his honor. The verdict that matters is elsewhere.

### 4. **Man la'anahu Allah ... wa 'abada al-taghut**: Revelation Does Not Trade in Insults; It Recalls Real Histories of Judgment

Then the passage names what is actually worse in Allah's scale:

**man la'anahu Allahu wa ghadiba 'alayhi** - those whom Allah cursed and was angry with.

These are not casual words. They name divine distance and divine displeasure, not mere human dislike. Then the ayah continues with historical judgments already known from earlier scriptural memory:

**wa ja'ala minhum al-qiradata wa al-khanazira** - and made of them apes and pigs,

and

**wa 'abada al-taghut** - and worshiped or served Taghut.

[PAUSE]

We need to sit with this carefully.

The ayah is not teaching believers to fling animal imagery around as free-floating insult. It is not handing the nafs a weapon of cruelty. It is recalling divinely judged histories and adding to them the most important theological criterion in the line: servitude to **Taghut**, false authority that is obeyed and followed against Allah.

That last phrase is crucial because it gathers the moral issue into its deepest form. The real degradation of a human being is not social embarrassment. It is falling under rebellion against Allah so deeply that one's loyalties, worship, and obedience are given elsewhere.

Then the ayah closes:

**Ula'ika sharrun makanan wa adallu 'an sawa'i al-sabil** - those are worse in position and further astray from the evenness of the path.

Again, this is scale-language. Position. Path. Placement. The verse is not arguing about who feels offended. It is clarifying who actually stands in the worse place before Allah.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Sometimes People Hate the Believer for the Very Thing That Saves Him

One of the hardest experiences in religious life is being treated as troublesome precisely because you are trying to stay faithful.

Not perfect.
Not self-righteous.
Not loud for the sake of loudness.

Simply unwilling to surrender the reality that Allah revealed.

[PAUSE]

And when that happens repeatedly, the soul starts negotiating with itself. Maybe I should make my faith less visible. Maybe I should present it more as a private preference than as truth. Maybe I should act slightly embarrassed by my obedience so that other people feel less indicted by it.

These ayahs are an act of rescue for that condition.

They teach us that sometimes the discomfort others feel around a believer is not evidence that the believer has gone wrong. Sometimes it is evidence that faith itself creates a contrast people do not want to sit beside. A life ordered by revelation can become a silent criticism of lives ordered by desire, selective obedience, or rebellion. And many people would rather resent the contrast than investigate the truth it reveals.

That does not give believers permission to become arrogant. It gives them permission to stop apologizing for iman itself.

### Theme 2: The Most Important Moral Skill Is Refusing False Scales

There is a deep psychological lesson here. Human beings are easily conquered by the scales of the people around them.

If a society mocks chastity, chastity starts feeling primitive.
If a culture mocks prayer, prayer starts feeling inconvenient.
If powerful people mock revelation, revelation starts looking intellectually embarrassing.

[PAUSE]

None of those reactions prove anything. They only prove that people are social creatures.

The Quran keeps saving us from that captivity by repeating the same move: bring the matter back **'inda Allah**.

What is this thing before Allah?
What does this path weigh in His scale?
What counts as honor or humiliation in His sight, not in the sight of people temporarily offended by truth?

This is one of the great liberations of taqwa. It does not make the believer insensitive to people. It makes him unenslaved by false verdicts.

And we need that desperately. Otherwise every age can re-educate us into embarrassment about the very revelation that came to free us.

### Theme 3: The Real Horror Is Taghut, Not Human Disapproval

Verse 60 contains heavy language, but it is moving toward one central theological warning: **wa 'abada al-taghut**.

The most terrifying thing in the passage is not being disliked by religious opponents. It is serving Taghut. It is handing obedience, reverence, and moral surrender to false authority in defiance of Allah.

[PAUSE]

That is where the whole section of Al-Ma'idah has been heading.

False alliances.
Mockery of din.
Resentment toward faith.
Then finally: servitude to Taghut.

The chain makes sense. Once a person stops measuring by Allah's scale, another scale will rule him. Once he no longer honors revelation as revelation, other authorities become eligible for devotion, fear, and obedience. Taghut is the culmination of moral displacement.

That should sober us. The opposite of iman is not always open atheism. Sometimes it is a life so reorganized around other authorities, other loyalties, and other fears that Allah's judgment no longer governs the center. The believer must therefore fear not merely being disliked, but being relocated inwardly under false mastery.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayahs begin by exposing the grievance:

Do you resent us for anything except belief in Allah and revelation?

Then they expose the fraud inside that grievance:

The offended do not get to decide what is actually shameful.

[PAUSE]

So the passage lifts the whole question to Allah's scale. It reminds the believer that faithfulness may provoke resentment without becoming blameworthy, and it reminds the critic that divine judgment is not rewritten by human disapproval. True disgrace is not wholehearted faith. True disgrace is curse, wrath, rebellion, and servitude to Taghut.

This is how the Quran heals a believer under pressure. It does not promise that others will approve. It gives something stronger than approval: a true measure.

### Questions to Carry

1. Where have I allowed other people's irritation with faith to make me suspicious of faith itself?
2. Do I secretly use social acceptance as a test of whether my obedience is "reasonable"?
3. What false scales still have power to name honor and shame inside me?
4. In what ways do I need to bring my life back under Allah's measure rather than the fear of being resented?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs teach that when people resent believers for iman itself, the believer must refuse their scale and remember that true honor and disgrace are decided only in Allah's reckoning, where servitude to Taghut is far worse than being disliked for faith.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, do not let us borrow our measure from those who resent Your light. Make iman beloved to us even when it is costly, and keep us from shrinking revelation to fit the comfort of others. Save us from every form of Taghut in belief, obedience, and fear. Let Your scale become so real in our hearts that no hostile judgment can rename what You have honored. Amin.
