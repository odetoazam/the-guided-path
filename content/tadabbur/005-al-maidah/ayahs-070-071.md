# They Thought There Would Be No Trial

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:70-71)

[ayah:5:70] لَقَدْ أَخَذْنَا مِيثَـٰقَ بَنِىٓ إِسْرَٰٓءِيلَ وَأَرْسَلْنَآ إِلَيْهِمْ رُسُلًا ۖ كُلَّمَا جَآءَهُمْ رَسُولٌۢ بِمَا لَا تَهْوَىٰٓ أَنفُسُهُمْ فَرِيقًا كَذَّبُوا۟ وَفَرِيقًا يَقْتُلُونَ

[ayah:5:71] وَحَسِبُوٓا۟ أَلَّا تَكُونَ فِتْنَةٌ فَعَمُوا۟ وَصَمُّوا۟ ثُمَّ تَابَ ٱللَّهُ عَلَيْهِمْ ثُمَّ عَمُوا۟ وَصَمُّوا۟ كَثِيرٌ مِّنْهُمْ ۚ وَٱللَّهُ بَصِيرٌۢ بِمَا يَعْمَلُونَ

*Laqad akhadhna mithaqa bani Isra'ila wa arsalna ilayhim rusulan. Kullama ja'ahum rasulun bima la tahwa anfusuhum fariqan kadhdhabu wa fariqan yaqtulun.*

*Wa hasibu alla takuna fitnatun fa'amu wa summu. Thumma taba Allahu 'alayhim thumma 'amu wa summu kathirun minhum. Wa Allahu basirun bima ya'malun.*

**We had certainly taken the covenant of the Children of Israel and sent to them messengers. Whenever there came to them a messenger with what their souls did not desire, a party of them they denied and another party they killed. And they thought there would be no trial, so they became blind and deaf. Then Allah turned to them in mercy; then many of them became blind and deaf again. And Allah is Seeing of what they do.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

One of the most dangerous spiritual states is not open rebellion by itself. It is rebellion combined with the confidence that nothing decisive will happen because of it.

If the soul can convince itself that disobedience carries no real trial, no unveiling, no consequence severe enough to interrupt comfort, then it becomes capable of almost anything. It can deny warnings, silence truth, mistreat messengers, and still move through life with the smugness of someone who believes the moral order of the world will never finally reach him.

That is the disease these two ayahs expose.

The passage begins with immense gifts: covenant taken, messengers sent. But then it shows a recurring reaction. Each time a messenger arrived with something the self did not desire, the response was not humble struggle. It was rejection. Sometimes denial. Sometimes murder. The issue was not lack of access to revelation. The issue was desire refusing to bend.

[PAUSE]

Then verse 71 gives us the inner sentence that made the whole cycle possible: **they thought there would be no fitnah**. No real testing. No real consequence. No exposure that would force truth upon them. That false confidence then becomes blindness and deafness, followed by an opening of tawbah, then another descent into the same blindness for many of them.

The passage is therefore not only history. It is anatomy. It shows how a covenant people can keep receiving truth and yet keep resisting it whenever desire remains the hidden judge of what will be allowed in. And it shows that one of the deepest roots of that resistance is the illusion of impunity.

The hook of the passage is this: **when the soul believes there will be no real trial for refusing truth, it soon loses the ability to see and hear truth at all.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Akhadhna mithaqa ... arsalna ilayhim rusulan**: The Passage Begins with Grace Before It Names Failure

The first verbs are all divine generosity:

**akhadhna mithaqa** - We took a covenant.

**arsalna ilayhim rusulan** - and We sent to them messengers.

[PAUSE]

This matters. The Quran does not begin the indictment in a vacuum. It begins with grace, responsibility, and repeated divine outreach. A **mithaq** is not a casual promise. It is a weighty binding covenant, a morally serious commitment. Then messengers are sent not once, but again and again.

So the coming failure cannot be blamed on neglect from above. Revelation came. Warning came. Guidance came. The relationship had already been initiated by Allah with generosity and seriousness.

That makes the later rejection heavier. To turn away after one warning is tragic. To keep turning away across a history of covenant and recurring prophetic visitations reveals something deeper in the soul.

### 2. **Kullama ja'ahum rasulun bima la tahwa anfusuhum**: Desire Becomes the Court in Which Revelation Is Judged

Then comes the recurring pattern:

**kullama ja'ahum rasulun** - every time a messenger came to them.

The phrase **kullama** matters. This is not a single accident. It is repetition. A historical habit. A stable moral reflex.

[PAUSE]

And what triggered the reaction?

**bima la tahwa anfusuhum** - with what their souls did not desire.

This is one of the most psychologically precise lines in the surah. The messengers are not first described as unclear, unproven, or absent. The problem is that what they brought crossed the grain of the lower self. Desire had already become a hidden tribunal. Revelation was permitted in only so far as it did not wound appetite.

That should shake us.

Because this remains one of the most common ways people reject truth without admitting it. They do not always say, "This is false." Sometimes the real movement is quieter: "This is not what I wanted. This is too costly. This touches a loyalty, appetite, pride, or habit I do not want disrupted."

Then the verse splits the response:

**fariqan kadhdhabu wa fariqan yaqtulun** - a group they denied, and a group they killed.

Some truths are dismissed. Others are violently silenced. But both grow from the same root: the self refusing to yield.

### 3. **Wa hasibu alla takuna fitnah**: The Most Dangerous Delusion Is the Delusion of No Consequence

Verse 71 begins with a sentence that explains everything:

**wa hasibu alla takuna fitnah** - and they thought there would be no fitnah.

They assumed no real trial would come of this. No exposing consequence. No destabilizing judgment. No spiritual cost weighty enough to force them awake.

[PAUSE]

This is an extraordinary diagnosis because it shows that corruption is not sustained by desire alone. It is sustained by false calculation. People persist in rejecting truth because they imagine the world can remain morally quiet while they do so.

That is the lie of impunity.

And then the consequence arrives at the level of perception itself:

**fa'amu wa summu** - so they became blind and deaf.

The punishment is fitting. They would not deal truthfully with what they were shown and told, so their ability to see and hear becomes spiritually dimmed. Not necessarily the loss of physical faculties, but the collapse of receptivity.

### 4. **Thumma taba Allahu 'alayhim thumma 'amu wa summu**: Mercy Reopened the Door, but Many Chose the Same Blindness Again

Then comes a deeply moving turn:

**thumma taba Allahu 'alayhim** - then Allah turned toward them in mercy.

Even after all that, the story does not have to end in sealed rejection. Allah reopens the door. He turns to them. He gives room for return.

[PAUSE]

But then:

**thumma 'amu wa summu kathirun minhum** - then many of them became blind and deaf again.

This is one of the saddest structures in the surah. Mercy came. Opened space came. A chance to return came. Yet many resumed the same pattern.

That teaches us that tawbah offered is not the same thing as tawbah received inwardly. A person can be granted another opening and still walk back into the same darkness if the deeper attachment to desire remains untouched.

Then the ayah closes:

**wa Allahu basirun bima ya'malun** - and Allah is Seeing of what they do.

The end of the passage returns us to reality. Nothing in the cycle - not denial, not violence, not repeated blindness, not even post-tawbah relapse - escapes the sight of Allah.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: We Often Judge Truth by Whether It Offends Our Nafs

These ayahs expose one of the oldest counterfeit methods of discernment: letting the nafs decide whether revelation will be accepted.

If the command aligns with desire, we call it beautiful.
If it opposes desire, we start searching for distance, reinterpretation, or dismissal.

[PAUSE]

That is not how a servant listens. That is how a consumer listens.

The passage teaches us that many refusals of truth are not failures of intellect at all. They are failures of surrender. The self hears what it does not want and immediately begins protecting its own preference.

Which means one of the hardest questions we can ask ourselves is not only, "Do I know what Allah says?" but, "What truths have I quietly sidelined because they contradict what my soul already wanted?"

### Theme 2: The Belief That "Nothing Will Happen" Is Itself Part of the Punishment

There is also a terrifying contemporary relevance to **wa hasibu alla takuna fitnah**.

People sin, distort, and refuse truth because they think the world will remain manageable.
Relationships will still work.
Religious identity will still stand.
Conscience will eventually quiet down.
No decisive rupture will come.

[PAUSE]

But this ayah teaches that the first fitnah may already be happening inside the very soul that thinks there is no fitnah. Blindness and deafness are not always spectacular external disasters. Sometimes they are the gradual loss of the capacity to be moved by truth.

That is one of the severest punishments a human being can undergo while still thinking he is fine.

### Theme 3: Mercy Repeatedly Opens the Door, But Repeated Return to Blindness Is Still Possible

The line **thumma taba Allahu 'alayhim** should keep us from despair.

Allah turns back to people even after patterns of grave refusal.
He reopens.
He gives space.
He does not close the door at the first rebellion.

[PAUSE]

And yet the next line should keep us from presumption:

many of them became blind and deaf again.

So the passage holds both truths at once.

Tawbah is real.
Repeated relapse into blindness is also real.

That is why repentance cannot remain only emotional. If the underlying alliance with desire is left untouched, the soul may use mercy as a way of postponing transformation rather than entering it.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayahs begin with covenant and messengers, then show a people repeatedly confronted by truth that crossed their desires.

Some denied.
Some killed.
Then they built the inner story that made such rebellion sustainable:

**there will be no real fitnah.**

[PAUSE]

From that sentence came blindness and deafness.
Then mercy came.
Then for many, blindness and deafness returned.

So the passage is teaching us that spiritual ruin is not only about loving the wrong thing. It is also about assuming that loving the wrong thing will carry no real consequence. And it is teaching us that Allah's mercy repeatedly reopens the door, but no reopening helps the soul that still insists on keeping desire as its hidden judge.

### Questions to Carry

1. Which truths do I find hardest to hear because they cross what my nafs already wants?
2. Where have I quietly acted as though no real fitnah will come from repeated disobedience?
3. Have there been merciful reopenings in my life that I treated as temporary relief rather than a real turning point?
4. What would it look like for me to stop letting desire function as the court that judges revelation?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs teach that when revelation is judged by the desires of the self and sin is treated as though it carries no real trial, the soul is punished with blindness and deafness - even though Allah may still mercifully reopen the door of return.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, do not let our desires sit in judgment over Your truth. Save us from the delusion that repeated refusal carries no real fitnah. If blindness or deafness has begun in us, open our sight and hearing before the darkness settles. And when You turn toward us in mercy, do not let us walk back into the same old blindness again. Amin.
