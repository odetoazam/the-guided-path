# Nothing Would Be Accepted From Them

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:36-37)

[ayah:5:36] إِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ لَوْ أَنَّ لَهُم مَّا فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ جَمِيعًا وَمِثْلَهُۥ مَعَهُۥ لِيَفْتَدُوا۟ بِهِۦ مِنْ عَذَابِ يَوْمِ ٱلْقِيَـٰمَةِ مَا تُقُبِّلَ مِنْهُمْ ۖ وَلَهُمْ عَذَابٌ أَلِيمٌ

[ayah:5:37] يُرِيدُونَ أَن يَخْرُجُوا۟ مِنَ ٱلنَّارِ وَمَا هُم بِخَـٰرِجِينَ مِنْهَا ۖ وَلَهُمْ عَذَابٌ مُّقِيمٌ

*Inna alladhina kafaru law anna lahum ma fi al-ardi jami'an wa mithlahu ma'ahu liyaftadu bihi min 'adhabi yawmi al-qiyamati ma tuqubbila minhum. Wa lahum 'adhabun alim.*

*Yuriduna an yakhruju mina al-nari wa ma hum bikharijina منها wa lahum 'adhabun muqim.*

**Indeed, those who disbelieve, if they had all that is in the earth entirely and the like of it with it to ransom themselves thereby from the punishment of the Day of Resurrection, it would not be accepted from them. And for them is a painful punishment. They will want to get out of the Fire, but they will not be those who get out from it, and for them is an abiding punishment.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

These two ayat take two of the deepest worldly instincts and strip them of their power.

The first instinct is: if the danger is real enough, maybe it can still be bought off. Maybe enough wealth, enough value, enough compensation, enough sacrifice of possessions can create a way out. The second instinct is: if escape is wanted intensely enough, perhaps a door will appear.

The Qur'an answers both. If the disbeliever possessed everything in the earth and another earthlike amount with it, and offered all of it as ransom from the punishment of the Day of Resurrection, it would not be accepted. Then the next ayah says they will want to get out of the Fire, but they will not get out of it.

That sequence is devastating. First the failure of price. Then the failure of desire.

The passage is not merely frightening; it is unveiling. It shows us what the Hereafter is like when worldly reflexes finally die. In this world, enough wealth can alter outcomes. Enough leverage can open doors. Enough bargaining can delay consequences. Enough movement can sometimes produce exit. But there comes a realm where truth is too exposed for any of that to work.

The hook of the passage is this: **the Hereafter is the place where what was never surrendered to Allah in life can no longer be exchanged for safety, and what the soul wants after judgment can no longer undo what it refused before judgment.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Law anna lahum ma fi al-ardi jami'an wa mithlahu ma'ahu**: The Ayah Expands the Imagined Ransom to the Edge of the World

The verse does not speak about a small offering. It builds an impossible scale.

**If they had all that is in the earth entirely, and the like of it with it.**

This is totality plus doubling. Everything in the earth, then its equivalent again.

[PAUSE]

The Qur'an is stretching the imagination until it snaps. If there were any imaginable ransom large enough, surely this would be it. Nothing is being left out. Not some wealth. Not enormous wealth. Total earthly possession, then more.

That is what makes the next line so severe:

**Liyaftadu bihi** - to ransom themselves by it.

The instinct behind **fida'** is deeply human. When punishment comes close enough, the self wants to pay something to preserve itself. It wants substitution. Exchange. A price.

The ayah lets that instinct reach its maximum fantasy before denying it completely. This is not because wealth is small. It is because the Hereafter is not for sale.

### 2. **Ma tuqubbila minhum**: At That Point, Even the Largest Offering Is Not Accepted

The line that follows is one of the hardest in the surah:

**Ma tuqubbila minhum** - it would not be accepted from them.

Notice the word **tuqubbila**. This is the language of acceptance.

[PAUSE]

That is striking because earlier in the surah and earlier in the human story, acceptance was the heart of the matter. An offering accepted. An offering not accepted. A life accepted. A path not accepted. Here too the issue is acceptance, but in a final and terrible form: there is no offering left that can be accepted now.

This tells us something profound about time in the spiritual life. There is an hour in which giving, surrendering, spending, repenting, believing, and returning are accepted as acts of nearness. Then there is an hour when the desire to offer arises only because consequence has fully arrived, and at that point the offering is no longer what it once could have been.

The tragedy is not merely that they lack enough wealth. The tragedy is that the age of accepted ransom is over.

### 3. **Yuriduna an yakhruju mina al-nar**: Desire Survives, But Power Does Not

Then the next ayah moves from wealth to will:

**Yuriduna an yakhruju mina al-nar** - they want to get out of the Fire.

This is one of the most haunting uses of **irada** in the Qur'an. The will is still alive. The desire is still intense. They want out. They do not want punishment. They do not consent to it. They long for release.

[PAUSE]

But the verse immediately says:

**Wa ma hum bikharijina منها** - and they are not ones who will come out of it.

The form is important. Not merely "they do not exit," but "they are not of those who exit." The possibility itself is denied for them in that state.

This is where worldly intuition dies. In dunya, strong desire often becomes movement, planning, escape, bargaining, re-entry, revision. In the Fire, wanting is no longer power. The self can want what it wants with total intensity and still remain unable to alter the sentence.

That is terrifying precisely because so much of our worldly life is built on the belief that if we want hard enough, we can still change the terms.

### 4. **'Adhabun alim ... 'adhabun muqim**: The Pain Is Not Only Sharp; It Stays

The first verse ends:

**Wa lahum 'adhabun alim** - and for them is a painful punishment.

Then the next ends:

**Wa lahum 'adhabun muqim** - and for them is an abiding punishment.

[PAUSE]

The pairing matters. **Alim** names the felt pain. **Muqim** names the staying, the settledness, the lack of departure. The punishment hurts, and it remains.

This two-part description is spiritually devastating because many people can imagine pain if only they can also imagine its ending. The Qur'an here refuses both false comforts: neither can it be bought off, nor can it simply be waited out through eventual exit.

So the passage closes every door the worldly ego loves to keep cracked open: price, leverage, and escape.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: We Spend Our Lives Training Ourselves to Believe That Enough Can Still Be Paid

One reason these ayat hit so hard is that they confront a habit deeply woven into worldly life.

Again and again, we learn that problems can be managed by cost. Fines can be paid. Losses can be offset. Reputation can be repaired with investment. Security can be purchased. Access can be bought. Pain can be numbed by spending more. Even moral failure can be disguised under displays of generosity or sacrifice when the soul is still protecting itself from actual surrender.

[PAUSE]

So by the time we hear an ayah like this, something in us still assumes that enough value should count for something.

But the Qur'an says there is a point where all of that collapses. Not because Allah undervalues what He created, but because the whole logic of ransom belongs to a world of partial concealment, mixed motives, and temporary leverage. When the Day of Resurrection fully unveils truth, what was withheld from Allah in life cannot simply be converted into price after the fact.

This is why the ayah should not merely terrify us. It should clarify us. It should ask: what am I still treating as if it could compensate later for what I refuse to surrender now?

### Theme 2: Wanting the Exit Too Late Is Not the Same as Wanting Allah Early Enough

The second ayah is painful because desire itself is still there.

They want to get out. They want release. They want distance from punishment. But wanting escape from the Fire is not the same thing as wanting Allah before the Fire.

[PAUSE]

That distinction is everything.

Many people assume that if the truth were fully visible, of course they would want salvation then. The ayah says: yes, they will want out then. The problem is not that desire disappears. The problem is that the time in which desire could take the form of faith, tawbah, surrender, and obedience has passed.

This makes the verse searching for us now. What do we want while wanting still has power? Do we want Allah, or merely the eventual avoidance of loss? Do we seek nearness while the road is open, or only imagine that if consequences became undeniable we would surely choose differently then?

The ayah tells us that there is a wanting that comes too late. That is one of the most frightening truths in the Qur'an.

### Theme 3: The Hereafter Is the End of Negotiation

Perhaps the deepest thread across both ayat is that the Hereafter is where negotiation ends.

No ransom. No accepted exchange. No successful exit. No revision of terms.

[PAUSE]

That is why the Qur'an keeps pressing us in this life toward taqwa, wasilah, repentance, and striving. Not because Allah wants to burden the servant unnecessarily, but because this life is the field in which the heart still has room to turn willingly. Dunya is the age of offer. Akhirah is the age of unveiling.

Once that is understood, these verses become a mercy even in their severity. They are telling us now, while doors still open, not to live as if everything can always be renegotiated later.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayat destroy the last illusions of leverage before Allah.

First, they imagine the largest possible ransom: everything in the earth and its equal besides, all offered to escape the punishment of the Day of Resurrection. It would not be accepted. Then they imagine the rawest possible desire: wanting to get out of the Fire itself. They will not get out. So the passage closes both the door of price and the door of escape, then seals the scene with painful punishment that remains.

The structure is exact. The worldly self trusts in what it owns and what it wants. The Qur'an shows that neither possession nor desire can save a soul once the time of accepted surrender has passed.

[PAUSE]

Ask yourself:

- What am I still secretly treating as if it could compensate later for what I am withholding from Allah now?
- Do I want Allah while wanting still has the power to obey, or only the idea of safety from consequence?
- Which worldly habits have trained me to assume that everything remains negotiable forever?
- What would change in my life if I truly believed that the age of accepted offering is now, not later?

The distillation of the passage is this: **when the Hereafter fully arrives, neither total wealth nor desperate desire can buy or force an escape; only what was surrendered to Allah while acceptance was still open truly matters.**

O Allah, do not let us reach the Day when we want release but can no longer choose the road to it, make us offer You our hearts and lives while acceptance is still open, and save us from the punishment that is painful and abiding.
