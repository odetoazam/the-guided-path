# They Took Your Religion as Play

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:57-58)

[ayah:5:57] يَـٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ لَا تَتَّخِذُوا۟ ٱلَّذِينَ ٱتَّخَذُوا۟ دِينَكُمْ هُزُوًا وَلَعِبًا مِّنَ ٱلَّذِينَ أُوتُوا۟ ٱلْكِتَـٰبَ مِن قَبْلِكُمْ وَٱلْكُفَّارَ أَوْلِيَآءَ ۚ وَٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ إِن كُنتُم مُّؤْمِنِينَ

[ayah:5:58] وَإِذَا نَادَيْتُمْ إِلَى ٱلصَّلَوٰةِ ٱتَّخَذُوهَا هُزُوًا وَلَعِبًا ۚ ذَٰلِكَ بِأَنَّهُمْ قَوْمٌ لَّا يَعْقِلُونَ

*Ya ayyuha alladhina amanu la tattakhidhu alladhina ittakhadhu dinakum huzuwan wa la'iban mina alladhina utu al-kitaba min qablikum wa al-kuffara awliya'. Wa ittaqu Allaha in kuntum mu'minin.*

*Wa idha nadaytum ila al-salati ittakhadhuha huzuwan wa la'iban. Dhalika bi-annahum qawmun la ya'qilun.*

**O you who have believed, do not take those who have taken your religion in ridicule and amusement from among those who were given the Scripture before you and the disbelievers as allies. And fear Allah, if you should truly be believers. And when you call to prayer, they take it in ridicule and amusement. That is because they are a people who do not use reason.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

Not every kind of disagreement wounds the same way.

There are people who disagree with you, oppose you, argue with you, even resent you, and yet still recognize that what you hold sacred is sacred to you. The relationship may remain difficult, but it stays inside the boundaries of seriousness. Something in them still understands that the things by which human beings stand before God are not toys.

Then there is another posture entirely.

It is the posture that cannot leave the sacred alone without turning it into a joke. It hears devotion and smirks. It sees worship and trivializes it. It watches human beings answer a call to prayer and interprets that movement not as surrender, not as awe, not as discipline, but as something amusing.

That is the world of these two ayahs.

And notice how exact the Quran is. It does not say only that such people are rude. It does not say only that they are unpleasant. It says: do not take them as **awliya'**. Do not hand them the place of intimate alliance, protective nearness, and trusted belonging. Why? Because mockery of the sacred is not a small issue of tone. It reveals a damaged way of seeing reality.

Then the second ayah narrows the lens. It moves from **your religion** as a whole to one visible sign within it: **the call to prayer**. As though to say: if you want proof of what their hearts are doing, look at what they do when the summons to stand before Allah enters the air.

The hook of the passage is this: **the one who laughs at what takes you to Allah has already shown you that he does not understand what a human life is for.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **La tattakhidhu alladhina ittakhadhu**: The Ayah Mirrors Their Taking Against Ours

The first thing to notice is the mirror in the wording:

**la tattakhidhu** - do not take.

Then immediately:

**alladhina ittakhadhu dinakum** - those who took your religion...

The same root appears on both sides: **a-kh-dh**, to take, adopt, seize into a role.

[PAUSE]

This is powerful. The ayah is not merely listing bad behavior and then separately giving social advice. It is staging a moral contrast through the repeated verb itself. They have already **taken** your din in one way: as an object of ridicule and play. Therefore, you are forbidden to **take** them in another way: as awliya'.

In other words, their posture toward revelation determines the boundary of your intimacy.

That is a hard lesson for modern people because we often imagine inner loyalty as detached from reverence. We think we can hand our trust, admiration, emotional dependence, and social nearness to anyone, regardless of what they do to the sacred, as though the heart can remain unaffected by that arrangement.

The Quran refuses that innocence. It teaches that what people do with your din matters. And if their settled posture is to reduce it, your heart is not meant to give them the place of loyal nearness.

### 2. **Dinakum huzuwan wa la'iban**: They Do Not Merely Reject; They Trivialize

Then look at the object of their mockery:

**dinakum** - your religion, your way of submission, the total path by which you organize life before Allah.

Not one custom. Not a cultural accessory. Not a hobby. **Din** is the whole ordering reality of servanthood.

[PAUSE]

And what do they do with it?

They take it as **huzuwan wa la'iban** - ridicule and play.

Those two words belong together, but they are not redundant. **Huzuw** carries mockery, derision, making something into an object of scorn. **La'ib** carries play, frivolous unseriousness, treating something weighty as though it were only entertainment or passing amusement.

Together they form a devastating portrait. These people do not simply remain unconvinced. They deform the moral object itself. They turn what should evoke seriousness into comedy, and what should humble a soul into a spectacle.

This matters because mockery is more corrosive than opposition. A direct enemy at least grants that something is serious enough to fight. The mocker tries to dissolve seriousness itself. He does not only reject the sacred; he makes it look silly in the eyes of others and, over time, even in the eyes of the weak-hearted believer.

[PAUSE]

That is why this boundary is protective. The believer is being told not to normalize nearness to people whose settled habit is to laugh at the path by which he reaches Allah.

### 3. **Wa idha nadaytum ila al-salah**: The Whole Din Is Condensed into One Audible Sign

The second ayah sharpens the accusation:

**Wa idha nadaytum ila al-salah** - and when you call to prayer.

This is a beautiful narrowing. The first ayah spoke about **dinakum**, the whole religion. The second moves to one of its clearest public signs: the call that gathers believers out of their scattered errands and turns them toward Allah.

[PAUSE]

The verb **nadaytum** gives us a public summons. Salah is not remaining hidden in the chest. It enters the air. It interrupts the market, the street, the routine of the day. It announces that there is something more important than whatever we were just doing.

And that is exactly what exposes the mocker.

**Ittakhadhuha huzuwan wa la'iban** - they take it in ridicule and play.

The repetition of the exact pair from the first ayah is not accidental. The Quran is showing that the mockery of the whole din was not theoretical. It becomes visible at the point of prayer. When the adhan or the call to salah appears, their inner estimate of worship comes spilling outward.

That should move us. Because salah is the most basic enactment of seriousness before Allah. If a person can only hear in it something comic, then the problem is not with the sound of the call. The problem is with the architecture of the heart that hears it.

### 4. **Dhalika bi-annahum qawmun la ya'qilun**: The Quran Treats Irreverence as a Failure of Understanding

Then comes the verdict:

**Dhalika bi-annahum qawmun la ya'qilun** - that is because they are a people who do not use reason.

This is remarkable. The passage could have ended by calling them rude, hostile, or insolent. Instead it diagnoses a deeper incapacity: they do not truly understand.

[PAUSE]

The Quran's point is not that intelligence means academic brilliance or social cleverness. A person can be sophisticated, witty, informed, and still fail at the most important perception. He can fail to grasp what prayer is, what worship is, what a revealed path is, what it means that human beings are being summoned to stand before their Lord.

So this is not merely a critique of manners. It is a critique of vision.

The one who sees salah only as a ritual to mock has misread existence itself. He has mistaken a ladder for a prop, a summons for a joke, a meeting with Allah for empty theater. The ayah is saying: do not entrust your inward nearness to people whose understanding has collapsed at that level.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Reverence Is One of the Conditions of Trust

These ayahs force us to ask a difficult question: can someone be safe for your heart if he has no reverence for what is most sacred to you?

Not safe in the shallow sense of politeness. Not safe in the transactional sense of usefulness. Safe in the deeper sense of influence, intimacy, admiration, and alliance.

[PAUSE]

The Quran's answer is no.

Because the people closest to your heart help shape what your heart finds normal. If mockery of worship becomes ordinary in your intimate environment, then seriousness before Allah begins to feel socially awkward. Salah starts to feel embarrassing. Sacred language starts to sound too intense. Gradually, without formally leaving the din, the heart begins apologizing for its own path.

That is why the prohibition is merciful. Allah is not making the believer harsher for the sake of harshness. He is protecting the tenderness of reverence. He is preserving the inner climate in which worship can still feel noble, weighty, and beloved.

### Theme 2: Prayer Reveals Whether a Person Understands Reality

Why does the passage zoom in on salah?

Because prayer is the daily contradiction of the world's illusions.

The world says: stay busy.
Prayer says: stop.

The world says: your worth is in productivity, motion, and visible output.
Prayer says: your first reality is servanthood.

The world says: keep bending around urgency.
Prayer says: interrupt urgency for the One who made time itself.

[PAUSE]

So when someone hears the call to prayer and responds with mockery, he is not only rejecting a community custom. He is reacting against a whole vision of existence. He cannot bear the claim that the human being was made to answer a summons higher than appetite, schedule, market, and ego.

That is why the ayah ends with **la ya'qilun**. Aql, in Quranic discourse, is not sterile cognition. It is an understanding that lets reality land on you correctly. These people fail at that. They misread one of the clearest signs of what a human being is for.

And this should also humble us from the other side. If salah itself has become light in our own hearts, if the adhan barely registers, if prayer feels like an inconvenience rather than a return, then we should not only blame outside mockery. We should ask what part of our own aql has gone dim.

### Theme 3: Mockery Is Dangerous Because It Makes the Heavy Feel Light

One of the most damaging powers of ridicule is that it changes the emotional temperature of truth.

Something that once felt solemn begins to feel awkward.
Something that once drew tears begins to feel performative.
Something that once called forth humility begins to feel socially exposed.

[PAUSE]

That is why derision is spiritually dangerous even when it comes dressed as humor. Humor can be harmless. But mockery of the sacred works by thinning out gravity. It makes the heavy feel light. It invites the soul to stand half a step outside its own devotion and begin watching itself through the eyes of the scorner.

Once that happens, worship starts losing its innocence.

These ayahs come to rescue that innocence. They tell the believer: you do not need to surrender your reverence to prove that you are mature, socially adaptable, or unbothered. You are allowed to guard the sanctity of what brings you near to Allah. In fact, you are commanded to.

## Closing Synthesis

The passage begins with a prohibition against taking certain people as **awliya'**, and it explains the prohibition by describing what those people have already taken your din to be.

That is the architecture:

They took your religion as mockery.
So do not take them into loyal nearness.

[PAUSE]

Then the second ayah proves the diagnosis through salah. When the call to prayer sounds, their hidden estimate of worship becomes audible. They laugh where they should have paused. They trivialize where they should have recognized a human being answering his Lord. And the Quran names this not as sophistication, but as failure to understand.

So these ayahs are not teaching fragility. They are teaching moral clarity.

They are teaching that the heart must know the difference between ordinary human disagreement and a posture that dissolves reverence itself. They are teaching that salah is not only an obligation we perform, but a sign by which hearts are revealed. And they are teaching that one of the mercies of taqwa is refusing to give intimate loyalty to those who laugh at the road that takes us to Allah.

### Questions to Carry

1. Have I allowed mockery of sacred things to feel more normal than reverence?
2. Do the people closest to me strengthen my seriousness in worship or slowly thin it out?
3. What does my own heart feel when the call to prayer interrupts my day?
4. Where have I confused usefulness or social ease with genuine safety for the soul?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs teach that people who reduce din and salah to mockery have already revealed a failure of reverence and understanding, so the believer must guard his inner loyalty from intimacy that makes the sacred feel small.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, protect our hearts from the slow corrosion of mockery. Keep din heavy in our sight and salah beloved in our limbs. Surround us with people who honor what You have made sacred, and do not let us seek closeness that teaches the soul to laugh at the road back to You. Give us aql that recognizes the call to prayer for what it is: Your mercy entering the noise of our day. Amin.
