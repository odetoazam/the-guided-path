# No Blame for What Has Passed

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:93)

[ayah:5:93] لَيْسَ عَلَى ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ وَعَمِلُوا۟ ٱلصَّـٰلِحَـٰتِ جُنَاحٌ فِيمَا طَعِمُوٓا۟ إِذَا مَا ٱتَّقَوا۟ وَّءَامَنُوا۟ وَعَمِلُوا۟ ٱلصَّـٰلِحَـٰتِ ثُمَّ ٱتَّقَوا۟ وَّءَامَنُوا۟ ثُمَّ ٱتَّقَوا۟ وَّأَحْسَنُوا۟ ۗ وَٱللَّهُ يُحِبُّ ٱلْمُحْسِنِينَ

*Laysa 'ala alladhina amanu wa 'amilu al-salihati junahun fima ta'imu idha ma ittaqaw wa amanu wa 'amilu al-salihati thumma ittaqaw wa amanu thumma ittaqaw wa ahsanu. Wa Allahu yuhibbu al-muhsinin.*

**There is no blame upon those who believe and do righteous deeds for what they consumed, so long as they are mindful of Allah, believe, and do righteous deeds, then remain mindful of Allah and believe, then remain mindful of Allah and excel in good. And Allah loves the doers of excellence.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

Sometimes a new command from Allah does not only change the future. It unsettles the past.

A person hears a prohibition and immediately another question rises in the chest: what about what came before? What about the years of habit, the gatherings, the things I took in when I had not yet been stopped? What about sincere believers who loved Allah but had lived inside a world where a thing had not yet been finally forbidden? Does everything now turn dark behind them?

This ayah arrives inside that human fear with extraordinary mercy.

It does not speak to the careless rebel trying to turn divine concession into a loophole. It speaks about **those who believe and do righteous deeds**. It lifts blame for what has already passed, then it refuses to leave the believer merely relieved. The verse turns mercy into movement. It answers anxiety about yesterday by building a ladder for tomorrow: taqwa, iman, righteous action, then taqwa and iman again, then taqwa and ihsan.

[PAUSE]

That is one of the most beautiful things about the Quran's moral architecture. When Allah opens relief, He does not open stagnation. He removes crushing blame from the past so the servant can walk more beautifully into the present.

The hook of the ayah is this: **Allah does not trap sincere believers inside what preceded a clarified command; He clears the past, then calls them upward through deepening taqwa until they reach ihsan.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Laysa ... junah**: The Ayah Begins by Lifting Weight

The verse opens with a negation:

**laysa 'ala alladhina amanu wa 'amilu al-salihati junah**.

There is no blame upon those who believe and do righteous deeds.

The word **junah** is moral burden, fault, something blameworthy resting on a person. So the ayah begins not by tightening pressure but by removing it. This is a verse of relief.

[PAUSE]

And notice who is described first: not people of empty affiliation, but **those who believe and do righteous deeds**. The verse addresses sincere believers whose lives are turned toward Allah, not people searching for technical excuses.

Then comes:

**fima ta'imu**.

In what they consumed.

The verb comes from **t-a-m**, to taste, eat, partake. The wording is broad enough to speak about what entered them, what passed through their life before the command was clarified. The concern is real, and the ayah answers it directly: there is no blame upon them regarding what has already been consumed, under the conditions the verse then unfolds.

### 2. **Idha ma ittaqaw wa amanu wa 'amilu al-salihat**: Mercy Is Given Inside an Ongoing Condition, Not as Moral Carelessness

The lifting of blame is not detached from spiritual seriousness. The ayah says:

**idha ma ittaqaw wa amanu wa 'amilu al-salihat**.

So long as they have taqwa, believe, and do righteous deeds.

[PAUSE]

That means the verse is not teaching indifference to command. It is teaching that what preceded prohibition does not stain the sincere servant who now stands before Allah with taqwa, iman, and righteous action.

The ordering matters. **Ittaqaw** comes first. Taqwa is the guarding posture, the inner watchfulness that keeps the servant from stepping carelessly into what angers Allah. Then **amanu**. Then **'amilu al-salihat**. Belief is not being treated as an inert label. It is bound to lived repair and right action.

So even in a verse of mercy, the Quran preserves moral seriousness. Relief is not laziness. Relief is given to those already walking toward Allah.

### 3. **Thumma ittaqaw wa amanu thumma ittaqaw wa ahsanu**: The Ayah Builds a Stairway Rather Than Repeating Itself Meaninglessly

Then comes the most striking feature of the verse:

**thumma ittaqaw wa amanu thumma ittaqaw wa ahsanu**.

Then they had taqwa and believed, then they had taqwa and excelled in good.

[PAUSE]

The repetition is too deliberate to be accidental filler. The ayah is not stuttering. It is ascending.

The particle **thumma** also matters. It does not simply chain phrases together in the same flat register. It signals progression, a later stage, a further movement. The believer is not merely being told once that taqwa and iman matter. He is being walked upward through renewed and deepened stages.

First: taqwa, iman, righteous action.
Then again: taqwa, iman.
Then finally: taqwa, ihsan.

This is beautiful because it shows that Allah's mercy toward what has passed is not the end of the story. It is the clearing of the road so the servant can rise. One level of obedience is not enough to satisfy the soul forever. Taqwa must be renewed. Iman must deepen. And eventually the path opens into **ihsan**: not merely avoiding blame, but beautifying one's response before Allah.

### 4. **Wa Allahu yuhibbu al-muhsinin**: The Ayah Ends Not at Pardon but at Love

The last line gathers the whole verse:

**wa Allahu yuhibbu al-muhsinin**.

And Allah loves the doers of excellence.

[PAUSE]

This ending is stunning. The ayah could have ended once blame was removed. That would already have been mercy. But it goes further. It leads the servant from fear about what has passed all the way to the possibility of becoming beloved.

That is one of the Quran's gentlest transformations. You begin worried that the past may condemn you. The verse answers that for sincere believers there is no blame in what preceded the command. But then it refuses to let you build your whole spirituality around mere acquittal. It says: rise higher. Become of the **muhsinin**. Live so beautifully that Allah loves the form your obedience has taken.

So the final emotional horizon of the verse is not simply relief. It is love.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Allah Does Not Weaponize Newly Clarified Guidance Against the Sincere Heart

There is a kind of fear that haunts religious growth. A person learns more, and with each new clarity he begins looking backward in panic. What about what I used to do? What about the stage when I had not yet understood? What about the things normal in my environment before Allah opened my eyes to them?

This ayah is a mercy for that fear.

[PAUSE]

It teaches that Allah is not waiting to use clarified guidance as a weapon against the sincere believer's past. The Lord who revealed the command also reveals the relief. If they are people of iman, righteous action, and real taqwa, then what came before is not treated as an unforgivable stain hanging perpetually over them.

That does not make the past meaningless. It makes divine guidance merciful. Allah is not like a cruel auditor searching for retroactive ruin. He is the Lord who clarifies, lifts blame, and then directs the servant upward.

This matters for many believers far beyond the immediate legal context of khamr. Many people delay nearness to Allah because shame about the past makes them feel that there is no clean way forward. This verse says the opposite. The way forward is exactly where Allah is meeting you. Come with taqwa. Come with iman. Come with righteous action. Do not let old fog become a reason to refuse present light.

### Theme 2: The Real Goal Is Not Merely to Leave Sin Behind but to Rise Into Ihsan

There is another subtle danger this verse saves us from. Sometimes when people hear a verse of relief, they build their whole spiritual horizon around being "technically no longer guilty." But the ayah itself refuses to stop there.

It repeats taqwa until you can feel that Allah is describing not a moment but a sustained life. Then it ends in **ahsanu** and **al-muhsinin**.

[PAUSE]

That means the believer should not think of divine mercy as only the cancellation of penalty. Mercy is also the invitation to beauty. You are not being brought out of blame merely to live at the moral minimum. You are being brought out so that your life can become marked by vigilance, sincerity, and excellence.

This changes how we understand repentance and post-sin life. The soul often imagines two options: either I am condemned by what I used to be, or I barely escape and spend the rest of my life relieved to have survived. The Quran offers something higher. You may leave behind what has passed, then grow until Allah loves the quality of your response.

That is the real dignity in this verse. It does not treat believers as permanently definable by what entered their bodies before the command was finalized. It treats them as creatures capable of rising, layer by layer, until they become people of ihsan.

## Closing Synthesis

This ayah begins by removing blame.

Then it names the conditions of sincere belonging: taqwa, iman, righteous action.
Then it repeats taqwa and iman.
Then it repeats taqwa once more and lifts the servant into ihsan.
And finally it ends not with mere safety, but with divine love for the muhsinin.

[PAUSE]

That is the architecture: relief, then ascent.

The passage teaches us that Allah's mercy does not freeze us at the point of pardon. It frees us from the crushing weight of what has passed so that we can move toward what is better. The past is not weaponized against the sincere heart. But the future is also not left vague. It is shaped into a path of deepening consciousness, strengthened faith, righteous action, and finally beautiful excellence before Allah.

### Questions to Carry

1. Do I let shame about what preceded guidance keep me from walking seriously with Allah now?
2. Have I reduced mercy to "not being punished," when Allah may actually be inviting me higher into ihsan?
3. Where in my life does taqwa need to become renewed and sustained rather than treated as a one-time emotional moment?
4. What would it look like for my story with Allah to be defined less by what has passed and more by the beauty of my response now?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**This ayah teaches that Allah lifts blame from what preceded clarified prohibition for sincere believers, then turns that mercy into a staircase of renewed taqwa, lived iman, righteous action, and finally ihsan beneath His love.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, do not let what has passed chain us away from what You are calling us to now. Lift from us the weight that You have lifted, renew taqwa in us again and again, deepen our iman, beautify our deeds, and raise us by Your mercy until we become of those whom You love. Amin.
