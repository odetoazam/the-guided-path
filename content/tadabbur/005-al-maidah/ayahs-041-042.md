# Do Not Let Them Grieve You

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:41-42)

[ayah:5:41] ۞ يَـٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلرَّسُولُ لَا يَحْزُنكَ ٱلَّذِينَ يُسَـٰرِعُونَ فِى ٱلْكُفْرِ مِنَ ٱلَّذِينَ قَالُوٓا۟ ءَامَنَّا بِأَفْوَٰهِهِمْ وَلَمْ تُؤْمِن قُلُوبُهُمْ ۛ وَمِنَ ٱلَّذِينَ هَادُوا۟ ۛ سَمَّـٰعُونَ لِلْكَذِبِ سَمَّـٰعُونَ لِقَوْمٍ ءَاخَرِينَ لَمْ يَأْتُوكَ ۖ يُحَرِّفُونَ ٱلْكَلِمَ مِنۢ بَعْدِ مَوَاضِعِهِۦ ۖ يَقُولُونَ إِنْ أُوتِيتُمْ هَـٰذَا فَخُذُوهُ وَإِن لَّمْ تُؤْتَوْهُ فَٱحْذَرُوا۟ ۚ وَمَن يُرِدِ ٱللَّهُ فِتْنَتَهُۥ فَلَن تَمْلِكَ لَهُۥ مِنَ ٱللَّهِ شَيْـًٔا ۚ أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ ٱلَّذِينَ لَمْ يُرِدِ ٱللَّهُ أَن يُطَهِّرَ قُلُوبَهُمْ ۚ لَهُمْ فِى ٱلدُّنْيَا خِزْىٌ ۖ وَلَهُمْ فِى ٱلْـَٔاخِرَةِ عَذَابٌ عَظِيمٌ

[ayah:5:42] سَمَّـٰعُونَ لِلْكَذِبِ أَكَّـٰلُونَ لِلسُّحْتِ ۚ فَإِن جَآءُوكَ فَٱحْكُم بَيْنَهُمْ أَوْ أَعْرِضْ عَنْهُمْ ۖ وَإِن تُعْرِضْ عَنْهُمْ فَلَن يَضُرُّوكَ شَيْـًٔا ۖ وَإِنْ حَكَمْتَ فَٱحْكُم بَيْنَهُم بِٱلْقِسْطِ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ يُحِبُّ ٱلْمُقْسِطِينَ

*Ya ayyuha al-rasulu la yahzunka alladhina yusari'una fi al-kufri mina alladhina qalu amanna bi-afwahihim wa lam tu'min qulubuhum wa mina alladhina hadu. Samma'una lil-kadhibi samma'una liqawmin akharina lam ya'tuka. Yuharrifuna al-kalima min ba'di mawadi'ihi. Yaquluna in utitum hadha fakhudhuhu wa in lam tu'tawhu fahdharu. Wa man yuridi Allahu fitnatahu falan tamlika lahu mina Allahi shay'a. Ula'ika alladhina lam yuridi Allahu an yutahhira qulubahum. Lahum fi al-dunya khizyun wa lahum fi al-akhirati 'adhabun 'azim.*

*Samma'una lil-kadhibi akkaluna lil-suhti. Fa in ja'uka fahkum baynahum aw a'rid 'anhum. Wa in tu'rid 'anhum falan yadurruka shay'a. Wa in hakamta fahkum baynahum bil-qist. Inna Allaha yuhibbu al-muqsitin.*

**O Messenger, do not let those grieve you who hasten into disbelief from among those who say, "We believe," with their mouths while their hearts have not believed, and from among the Jews - keen listeners to lies, keen listeners on behalf of other people who have not come to you. They distort words from their proper places. They say, "If you are given this, take it; but if you are not given it, then beware." And whoever Allah intends his trial - never will you possess for him anything against Allah. Those are the ones whose hearts Allah did not intend to purify. For them in this world is disgrace, and for them in the Hereafter is a tremendous punishment. Keen listeners to lies, devourers of illicit gain. So if they come to you, judge between them or turn away from them. And if you turn away from them, they will never harm you at all. But if you judge, then judge between them with justice. Indeed, Allah loves those who act justly.**

## Introduction

These two ayat are about corruption of hearing before corruption of judgment.

Allah begins not by telling the Messenger what to rule, but by telling him what not to let enter his heart: **do not let them grieve you**. That matters because the people being described are exhausting people. They rush toward disbelief. They speak iman with their mouths while their hearts stay elsewhere. They listen eagerly to lies. They listen on behalf of others. They distort words from their proper places. They approach judgment not to seek truth, but to shop for outcomes.

This is a remarkably modern moral scene. The problem is not lack of hearing in the literal sense. It is hearing that has already chosen its master. Some people listen only for what confirms the answer they want, serves the faction behind them, protects illicit gain, or lets them bend revelation around desire. By the time such a person reaches judgment, corruption has already entered much earlier at the level of reception.

And that is why the end of the passage is so powerful. If they come to you, you may judge or turn away. But if you do judge, then judge **with qist**. The people's hearing may be corrupt, their motives mixed, their appetite bent toward lies and illicit taking. Still, the Messenger is not permitted to mirror their distortion. He must answer corruption with justice.

The hook of these ayat is this: **when people come with hearts already trained to listen for lies and outcomes they prefer, the believer is commanded not to be broken by their corruption and, if he judges at all, to answer it with uncompromised justice.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **La yahzunka**: The Passage Begins by Protecting the Messenger's Heart

The first command is deeply tender:

**La yahzunka** - do not let them grieve you.

Before speaking about distortion, hypocrisy, lies, or illicit gain, Allah addresses the Messenger's interior. This is crucial. The corruption around him is real, but he is not to let their behavior become an inner weight that bends his mission.

[PAUSE]

This tells us something important about prophetic composure. Not every sorrow deserves residence in the heart. Some griefs must be returned to Allah precisely because the people producing them are committed to running toward corruption.

The verse is not making him indifferent. It is protecting him from being consumed by what he cannot purify by force.

### 2. **Yusari'una fi al-kufr**: They Do Not Merely Fall Into Corruption; They Hurry Toward It

Then the people are described as:

**Yusari'una fi al-kufr** - they hasten into disbelief.

This is stronger than saying they simply disbelieve. There is speed in it. Eagerness. Competition. A readiness to move quickly where truth should have slowed them.

[PAUSE]

The verse is showing us a moral psychology in which corruption is not reluctant. Some souls become agile in the direction of what darkens them. They are not dragged. They rush.

And this is sharpened by the next description:

**They said, "We believe," with their mouths, while their hearts did not believe.**

The mouth and the heart are split. The outer statement becomes available for use, while the inner chamber remains unconvinced. This is one of the most dangerous forms of religious corruption because the language of faith is retained even as its truth is withheld from the center of the self.

### 3. **Samma'una lil-kadhib ... yuharrifuna al-kalima min ba'di mawadi'ihi**: The Ear Is Corrupted Before the Tongue Distorts

Then comes a repeated word:

**Samma'una** - keen listeners.

The repetition is striking: listeners to lies, listeners for other people who have not even come to you themselves.

[PAUSE]

This is not passive hearing. It is eager, available, morally slanted hearing. The ear has become a servant of falsity. And because the hearing is already crooked, the next step follows naturally:

**Yuharrifuna al-kalima min ba'di mawadi'ihi** - they distort the word from after its proper places.

That phrase is devastating. The word has a place. Meaning has a place. Revelation has a rightful placement and integrity. Distortion happens when language is lifted out of that place and forced to serve another aim.

This teaches us that corruption of interpretation is rarely just intellectual error. Often it begins in desire. The person listens badly first, then reads badly, then speaks badly.

### 4. **In utitum hadha fakhudhuhu ... wa in lam tu'tawhu fahdharu**: They Approach Judgment as Outcome-Shoppers

Then the verse quotes them:

**If you are given this, take it; but if you are not given it, then beware.**

This is one of the clearest exposures of a corrupt religious posture in the Qur'an. They do not come to revelation asking, "What is true?" They come asking, "What serves us?" If the answer aligns with appetite or factional interest, accept it. If not, avoid it.

[PAUSE]

That is outcome-shopping, not submission.

And it is not confined to them. The same instinct lives wherever people approach scholars, texts, proofs, rulings, or religious communities looking not for truth but for endorsement. The heart has already decided; it merely wants sacred cover.

### 5. **Lam yuridi Allahu an yutahhira qulubahum**: The Passage Returns Again and Again to the Heart

Then the verse says:

**Those are the ones whose hearts Allah did not intend to purify.**

This is severe. But notice again where the ayah locates the real problem: **qulubahum** - their hearts.

[PAUSE]

The crisis is not merely false argument. It is impurity of reception. A heart that does not want purification will use even revelation as material for distortion.

That is why the passage ends with worldly disgrace and tremendous punishment. When the heart refuses cleansing, shame eventually reaches both worlds.

### 6. **Akkaluna lil-suht ... fahkum baynahum bil-qist**: Corrupt Appetite Is Answered by Just Judgment

The next ayah adds another diagnosis:

**Akkaluna lil-suht** - devourers of illicit gain.

This is stronger than simply "those who take." The صيغة intensifies appetite. They consume what is forbidden greedily, habitually, hungrily.

[PAUSE]

Then the legal instruction:

**If they come to you, judge between them or turn away from them ... but if you judge, then judge between them with qist.**

The passage does not require the Messenger to become entangled in every manipulative approach. He may turn away. But if he does judge, then there can be no distortion in response to distortion. The only answer is **qist** - equity, fairness, measured justice.

Then the beautiful ending:

**Inna Allaha yuhibbu al-muqsitin** - indeed Allah loves those who act justly.

After a passage full of corrupt listeners, distorted words, unpurified hearts, and illicit appetite, the final note is love - but love for those who hold fast to justice anyway.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Way We Listen Is Already a Moral Act

One of the deepest insights in these ayat is that hearing itself can be righteous or corrupt.

We often think the moral problem begins when someone lies, distorts, or judges wrongly. But the passage goes earlier. It keeps describing people as listeners - listeners to lies, listeners for others, listeners whose ears are already bent toward what serves corruption.

[PAUSE]

That means the ear is not neutral. We can train ourselves to listen for truth, or to listen only for what feeds the self we have already chosen. Once that corruption sets in, distortion of speech and judgment becomes almost inevitable.

This should make us examine our own reception. When truth comes, am I listening to be corrected, or only to confirm what I already want? When an answer from Allah arrives, do I hear it as a servant or as a consumer?

### Theme 2: Some Hearts Want Rulings, Not Purification

The line about unpurified hearts is one of the hardest in the passage because it exposes a terrible possibility: a person may keep engaging religious discourse while not actually wanting the cleansing that discourse is meant to produce.

He will still ask questions. Still search texts. Still cite positions. Still move among sacred words. But the aim is not taharah of the heart. The aim is self-protection, factional advantage, or appetite with scriptural cover.

[PAUSE]

That is why these people are described as saying, "If you are given this, take it; if not, beware." The ruling is acceptable only when it serves the self already enthroned.

This is a warning to every seeker. We can approach religion while secretly wanting permission more than purification.

### Theme 3: Justice Is Most Beautiful When Others Have Become Unjust

The ending of the passage is morally luminous because it places qist exactly where qist is hardest.

Not among clean-hearted people seeking truth sincerely. But among distorted listeners, manipulators, and devourers of illicit gain. In that atmosphere, the Messenger is told: if you judge, then judge with qist.

[PAUSE]

That is where justice becomes radiant. Anyone can like fairness when the room is easy. But Allah loves the **muqsitin** precisely because they keep equity intact where other people arrive already bent.

This is a profound lesson for us. The justice we owe is not contingent on the purity of the people in front of us. Their corruption does not license ours.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayat begin with grief and end with Allah's love for the just.

Between those two points, they map a whole ecosystem of corruption: people hurrying into disbelief, mouths speaking faith while hearts withhold it, ears trained to hear lies, words pulled from their proper places, rulings accepted only when they flatter desire, hearts left unpurified, and appetites feeding on illicit gain. Then the passage gives the Messenger his stance: do not let them grieve you; and if you judge, judge with qist.

So the architecture is exact. Corrupt hearing produces corrupt interpretation and corrupt appetite. But prophetic response remains uncorrupted: composure, freedom from manipulative pressure, and justice.

[PAUSE]

Ask yourself:

- What kind of listener am I becoming when truth reaches me?
- Do I seek rulings that purify me, or rulings that simply serve what I already prefer?
- Where have I been tempted to let other people's distortion justify some distortion in me?
- Can I keep justice intact even when the people in front of me are not clean in motive?

The distillation of the passage is this: **when hearts listen for lies and approach revelation only for favorable outcomes, the believer must not be broken by their corruption and, if he judges at all, must answer them with purified justice.**

O Allah, purify our hearing before we speak, purify our hearts before we seek rulings, protect us from listening only for what flatters desire, and make us among those You love because we hold fast to qist when others bend away from it.
