# Seek to Him the Wasilah

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:35)

[ayah:5:35] يَـٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ ٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ وَٱبْتَغُوٓا۟ إِلَيْهِ ٱلْوَسِيلَةَ وَجَـٰهِدُوا۟ فِى سَبِيلِهِۦ لَعَلَّكُمْ تُفْلِحُونَ

*Ya ayyuha alladhina amanu ittaqu Allaha wabtaghu ilayhi al-wasilata wa jahidu fi sabilihi la'allakum tuflihun.*

**O you who have believed, fear Allah, seek to Him the means of approach, and strive in His path so that you may succeed.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

Some ayat feel like an entire roadmap compressed into one breath. This is one of them.

After a section about corruption, punishment, repentance, and mercy, the surah turns directly to the believers and gives them a different kind of response. If you want to avoid the roads of fasad, if you want to live under Allah's mercy rather than His punishment, if you want to move toward Him rather than away, then here is the program: have taqwa of Allah, seek to Him the wasilah, and strive in His path.

The order matters. The ayah does not begin with motion. It begins with God-consciousness. It does not ask for striving before orientation. It asks first for a guarded heart facing Allah, then for intentional seeking of nearness, then for exertion in His path. Finally, it places the entire journey under a hopeful horizon: **la'allakum tuflihun** - so that you may succeed.

This makes the ayah both intimate and demanding. It does not leave falah as a vague wish. It ties it to a structured life: inward vigilance, purposeful nearness, and costly striving.

And the center of the verse is especially beautiful: **seek to Him the wasilah**. Not merely seek something from Him. Seek **to Him** the means by which you come nearer. The heart of the path is not self-improvement as an end in itself. It is approach.

The hook of the ayah is this: **real success is not reached by exertion alone, but by a life whose striving is born from taqwa and directed toward nearness to Allah.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Ittaqu Allah**: The Path Begins With a Guarded Heart

The first command is:

**Ittaqu Allah** - have taqwa of Allah.

That opening is decisive. Before the ayah speaks about seeking or striving, it places the believer inside taqwa. Taqwa is not mere fear in the narrow sense. It is protective awareness, a guardedness of the self before Allah, a refusal to move carelessly in a world where every movement has moral consequence.

[PAUSE]

This matters because striving without taqwa can become ego-project, activism without inward accountability, intensity without purification. The ayah begins where corruption is actually prevented: in a heart that remains awake to Allah before it becomes busy in the world.

The first task, then, is not simply to do more. It is to stand before Allah with enough reverent consciousness that whatever comes next is not severed from Him.

### 2. **Wabtaghu ilayhi al-wasilah**: Seek Not Only a Means, but a Nearness

Then comes the command that makes this verse unforgettable:

**Wabtaghu ilayhi al-wasilah** - and seek to Him the wasilah.

The verb **ibtaghu** is active and intentional. Seek. Pursue. Desire. Go looking. This is not passive drifting toward Allah. It is the language of deliberate wanting.

[PAUSE]

Then there is **ilayhi** - to Him.

This is the center of gravity in the verse. The wasilah is not an abstract spiritual technique. It is a means of approach **to Him**. The destination is Allah.

And **al-wasilah** itself carries the sense of a means of nearness, a way by which distance is crossed and approach becomes real. The ayah is teaching that closeness to Allah is not accidental. It is sought. And it is sought through what Allah Himself loves: obedience, remembrance, sincerity, repentance, du'a, sacrifice, truthful worship, clean intention, and all the works by which the servant comes nearer.

This is one of the most hopeful commands in the Qur'an because it assumes nearness is possible. Allah does not tell us merely to admire closeness from afar. He commands us to seek its means.

### 3. **Wa jahidu fi sabilihi**: Nearness Does Not Eliminate Striving; It Gives It a Direction

Then the ayah says:

**Wa jahidu fi sabilihi** - and strive in His path.

The verse does not stop at inward piety or devotional longing. It moves outward into exertion. **Jahidu** comes from a root of effort, strain, expenditure, costly engagement. The path to Allah is not maintained by desire alone. It requires labor.

[PAUSE]

But notice the qualifier:

**Fi sabilihi** - in His path.

This keeps striving from becoming self-referential. Not every intense effort is sacred. Not every sacrifice is nearness. The effort must belong to His path, be governed by His pleasure, shaped by His limits, and oriented toward His cause rather than the ego's hunger for significance.

So the verse gives us a complete structure. Taqwa guards the heart. Wasilah gives the direction of nearness. Jihad gives the cost-bearing motion. Each command protects the others from distortion.

### 4. **La'allakum tuflihun**: Falah Is Hopeful, Not Possessed

The ending is tender:

**La'allakum tuflihun** - so that you may succeed.

The ayah does not say success is automatically possessed. It leaves the believer in hopeful striving. **La'alla** keeps the heart from entitlement while still opening it to expectation.

[PAUSE]

And **falah** is richer than a thin idea of success. It carries flourishing, true attainment, arriving well, being saved from ruin and granted the good one was made for. It is success measured by Allah, not merely by visible outcomes.

That means the commands of the verse are not random acts of devotion. They are the architecture of a flourishing life. A person guarded by taqwa, seeking nearness, and striving in Allah's path is being led toward the only kind of success that survives death.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Many of Us Try to Strive Without First Asking Whether We Are Near

One of the quiet dangers in religious life is that activity can outpace approach.

A person can become very busy in what looks like Allah's path while the inward distance from Allah is quietly growing. He serves, studies, organizes, builds, debates, spends energy, yet does not pause long enough to ask whether the heart is still seeking Allah Himself or only the identity that comes from working hard.

[PAUSE]

This ayah protects us from that drift by refusing to let striving appear first. Before **jahidu**, it says **ittaqu** and **wabtaghu ilayhi al-wasilah**.

That means the believer must keep asking: what is bringing me nearer? What means of approach am I actually seeking? Is my effort in Allah's path still fed by nearness to Allah, or has it become a substitute for nearness?

This is a mercy, because Allah is not asking for exhausted motion detached from Him. He is asking for a life in which motion grows out of relationship.

### Theme 2: Nearness to Allah Is Not Passive; It Must Be Wanted

There is something searching in the command **wabtaghu**.

Seek. Pursue. Go after it.

The ayah assumes that closeness to Allah belongs to the kind of thing we must actively want. It will not simply appear because we admire it in theory. We may love the idea of being near to Allah while structuring our days around almost everything except the means of that nearness.

[PAUSE]

This is where the verse becomes uncomfortable in a good way. If I say I want Allah, but I am not seeking the wasilah to Him, then what I want may still be mostly the emotional image of closeness rather than the road itself.

The road, though, is made of concrete things: guarding the heart, obeying when it is hard, repenting early, remembering often, making du'a honestly, leaving what darkens the soul, staying with what Allah loves even when it costs. Nearness is not sentimental. It is pursued.

And yet there is deep comfort here too: Allah would not command us to seek nearness if He meant it to remain unreachable.

### Theme 3: Success Belongs to Those Whose Lives Are Ordered Around Allah

The end of the verse gathers everything under **falah**.

That matters because the world is full of rival definitions of success: being seen, winning arguments, building influence, staying comfortable, finishing goals, appearing effective. The ayah quietly rearranges all of that.

[PAUSE]

If taqwa is missing, the heart is exposed. If wasilah is missing, the direction is lost. If striving is missing, desire remains untested. So real success cannot be reduced to only one of these.

Success, then, is a life ordered around Allah. A guarded inward state. A consciously pursued nearness. A willingness to bear effort in His path. When those are present, falah becomes imaginable even if worldly measurements remain small. And when those are absent, worldly gain can hide a much deeper loss.

## Closing Synthesis

This ayah gives believers a whole path in three commands.

Have taqwa of Allah. Seek to Him the wasilah. Strive in His path. Then hold the heart beneath the hopeful horizon of falah.

The order is the wisdom. Taqwa guards the soul from drifting. Wasilah keeps the entire journey aimed at Allah Himself. Jihad turns inward seriousness into outward exertion. And falah reminds us that the point is not mere activity, but arriving well before Allah.

[PAUSE]

Ask yourself:

- Is my striving still born from taqwa, or has it become a substitute for it?
- What means of nearness to Allah am I actually seeking these days?
- In what area of my life do I want closeness to Allah without wanting the road that leads to it?
- Which definition of success has been quietly ruling me more than falah?

The distillation of the ayah is this: **the believer reaches real success only by guarding the heart with taqwa, intentionally seeking nearness to Allah, and then striving in His path from that place of approach.**

O Allah, place taqwa at the root of our lives, teach us the true means of approach to You, make our striving sincerely in Your path, and grant us the falah that remains beautiful when every lesser success has fallen away.
