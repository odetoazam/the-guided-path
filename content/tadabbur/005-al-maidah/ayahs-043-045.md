# With Them Is the Torah

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:43-45)

[ayah:5:43] وَكَيْفَ يُحَكِّمُونَكَ وَعِندَهُمُ ٱلتَّوْرَىٰةُ فِيهَا حُكْمُ ٱللَّهِ ثُمَّ يَتَوَلَّوْنَ مِنۢ بَعْدِ ذَٰلِكَ ۚ وَمَآ أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ بِٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ

[ayah:5:44] إِنَّآ أَنزَلْنَا ٱلتَّوْرَىٰةَ فِيهَا هُدًى وَنُورٌ ۚ يَحْكُمُ بِهَا ٱلنَّبِيُّونَ ٱلَّذِينَ أَسْلَمُوا۟ لِلَّذِينَ هَادُوا۟ وَٱلرَّبَّـٰنِيُّونَ وَٱلْأَحْبَارُ بِمَا ٱسْتُحْفِظُوا۟ مِن كِتَـٰبِ ٱللَّهِ وَكَانُوا۟ عَلَيْهِ شُهَدَآءَ ۚ فَلَا تَخْشَوُا۟ ٱلنَّاسَ وَٱخْشَوْنِ وَلَا تَشْتَرُوا۟ بِـَٔايَـٰتِى ثَمَنًا قَلِيلًا ۚ وَمَن لَّمْ يَحْكُم بِمَآ أَنزَلَ ٱللَّهُ فَأُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ هُمُ ٱلْكَـٰفِرُونَ

[ayah:5:45] وَكَتَبْنَا عَلَيْهِمْ فِيهَآ أَنَّ ٱلنَّفْسَ بِٱلنَّفْسِ وَٱلْعَيْنَ بِٱلْعَيْنِ وَٱلْأَنفَ بِٱلْأَنفِ وَٱلْأُذُنَ بِٱلْأُذُنِ وَٱلسِّنَّ بِٱلسِّنِّ وَٱلْجُرُوحَ قِصَاصٌ ۚ فَمَن تَصَدَّقَ بِهِۦ فَهُوَ كَفَّارَةٌ لَّهُۥ ۚ وَمَن لَّمْ يَحْكُم بِمَآ أَنزَلَ ٱللَّهُ فَأُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ هُمُ ٱلظَّـٰلِمُونَ

## Introduction

This passage is about people who do not reject revelation in the abstract. They reject it selectively.

They come asking for judgment while the Torah is already with them. The problem, then, is not the total absence of divine guidance. The problem is that revelation is welcomed only when it can be bent toward the answer already desired. So the ayah opens with a question almost heavy with amazement: **how do they make you their judge while the Torah is with them, and in it is the judgment of Allah?**

That opening matters because it exposes a religious disease deeper than ignorance. There is a kind of person who wants sacred legitimacy without sacred submission. He wants access to revelation while preserving the right to turn away when revelation refuses to flatter him. So the surah does not merely praise Torah here. It uses Torah to expose selective obedience.

Then the next ayah honors the Torah with extraordinary dignity: in it is guidance and light. Prophets judged by it. Rabbaniyyun and ahbar were entrusted with preserving it and witnessing over it. That means the issue was never that revelation was deficient. The issue was what human beings did with it when fear of people, appetite for small price, and reluctance to judge by Allah's descent entered the heart.

Then 5:45 makes the matter concrete. It names written law in the Torah itself: life for life, eye for eye, nose for nose, ear for ear, tooth for tooth, wounds in equal retaliation. This is not an abstract warning about revelation in general. The passage plants its feet in actual divine writing, then still opens mercy again by saying that whoever remits it as charity, it is expiation for him.

So the hook of the passage is this: **the greatest betrayal of revelation is not always denying that Allah spoke, but approaching His judgment while reserving the right to turn away the moment His word threatens your preferred outcome.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Wa kayfa yuhakkimunaka**: The Passage Opens in Moral Astonishment

The first word that arrests the listener is:

**Wa kayfa** - and how?

This is not neutral inquiry. It is moral astonishment. How can this be? How is this even being attempted with a straight face?

[PAUSE]

Then:

**Yuhakkimunaka** - they appoint you as judge, they seek your judgment.

So the question is not against judgment itself. It is against the contradiction in the people seeking it. They come to the Messenger as if they honor divine judgment while already sitting beside a revealed book they are unwilling to receive truthfully.

This matters because the Qur'an is teaching us that hypocrisy often appears not as rejection of religious process, but as manipulative use of it. A person may appear very interested in sacred verdicts while his deeper project is simply to shop for one that serves him.

### 2. **'Indahumu al-tawratu fiha hukmu Allah**: Revelation Was Already Present, and the Judgment Was Already Inside It

Then comes the decisive phrase:

**'Indahumu al-tawratu fiha hukmu Allah** - with them is the Torah; in it is the judgment of Allah.

The issue is not access. The issue is presence ignored.

[PAUSE]

And notice the precision: not merely "in it are teachings," but **hukmu Allah** - the judgment of Allah. The Torah is honored here as containing decisive divine command, not mere religious heritage to be admired culturally.

This makes the next clause devastating:

**Thumma yatawallawna min ba'di dhalik** - then they turn away after that.

The verb **yatawallawna** is not simple disagreement. It is turning away, withdrawing, refusing the direction of what has been put before them. That means the crisis is not misunderstanding alone. It is willful retreat from what was already sufficiently clear.

Then the verse lands:

**Wa ma ula'ika bil-mu'minin** - those are not believers.

Why? Because belief is not merely claiming reverence for revelation while reserving the right to abandon its judgment when it costs you something.

### 3. **Fiha hudan wa nur**: The Torah Is Described With the Same Dignity People Seek for Their Own Scriptures

Verse 44 then says:

**Inna anzalna al-tawrata fiha hudan wa nur** - We sent down the Torah; in it was guidance and light.

This is a remarkable honoring of the Torah. It is not spoken of as a dim relic. It is spoken of as divine descent containing both **huda** and **nur**.

[PAUSE]

Guidance tells you where to go. Light lets you see while going.

So the passage completely removes the excuse that the earlier turn-away happened because the book lacked clarity. The Torah had orientation in it and illumination in it. The real tragedy is not lack of revelation but lack of surrendered reception.

### 4. **Bima ustuhfizu ... wa kanu 'alayhi shuhada'**: Those Entrusted With Revelation Must Guard It and Bear Witness Over It

Then the passage names those who judged by it: prophets, rabbaniyyun, ahbar.

And it says they did so:

**Bima ustuhfizu min kitabi Allahi** - by what they were entrusted to preserve from the Book of Allah.

This is one of the deepest responsibilities in the passage. Revelation is not merely received; it is entrusted. To be given a divine book or divine teaching is to become a guardian of something not yours to edit.

[PAUSE]

Then:

**Wa kanu 'alayhi shuhada'** - and they were witnesses over it.

This means their relation to revelation is not possession but testimony. They stand under it and speak for it faithfully. They do not turn it into raw material for desire.

That is why the next command fits perfectly:

**Fala takhshawu al-nasa wakhshawni** - do not fear the people; fear Me.

Corruption in judgment often enters through fear of people. Revelation gets bent when hearts fear human reaction more than divine witness.

### 5. **Wa la tashtaru bi-ayati thamnan qalila**: Do Not Sell Revelation for Immediate Advantage

Then the passage names another corruption:

**Wa la tashtaru bi-ayati thamnan qalila** - do not purchase with My signs a small price.

This is one of the Qur'an's great diagnoses of selective obedience. A person sees revelation, sees what it requires, sees the cost of honoring it, then quietly trades it for something smaller: approval, safety, influence, factional gain, money, position, relief from discomfort.

[PAUSE]

The price is called **qalila** even if the world thinks it is large, because anything purchased at the cost of Allah's signs is small.

That line should stop us. Most betrayal of revelation does not happen because people think they are selling it for something tiny. They think the price is worth it. The ayah says: no. However large it looks now, it is still little.

### 6. **Wa katabna 'alayhim fiha**: Revelation Comes Down to Concrete Moral Writing

Then 5:45 shifts from principle to example:

**Wa katabna 'alayhim fiha** - and We wrote for them in it.

The wording is important. This is written law, not vague religious mood.

[PAUSE]

Then comes the chain:

**The life for the life, the eye for the eye, the nose for the nose, the ear for the ear, the tooth for the tooth, and wounds are qisas.**

The repetition is deliberate. It establishes measured equivalence rather than chaotic vengeance. This is divine law restraining rage into justice.

Then, beautifully:

**Faman tasaddaqa bihi fahuwa kaffaratun lahu** - whoever gives it up as charity, it is expiation for him.

So even within a passage stressing judgment by revelation, mercy reappears not as abolition of justice but as generous remission from the injured party. Law is not erased; mercy is dignified within it.

### 7. **Wa man lam yahkum bima anzala Allah**: The Crisis Is Not Merely Personal Weakness, But Refusal to Let Revelation Rule

Both 5:44 and 5:45 end with the same pattern:

**Whoever does not judge by what Allah has sent down...**

Then one ends with **al-kafirun**, the other with **al-zalimun**.

[PAUSE]

This repetition matters. The passage is not speaking about revelation as ornament. It is speaking about revelation as ruling criterion. The moral disaster lies in having Allah's judgment present, then preferring another source because it better serves fear, desire, or convenience.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Selective Obedience Still Wants Religious Legitimacy

One of the most sobering realities in this passage is that these people still come asking for judgment.

That means they still want sacred process. They still want the aura of divine legitimacy. They still want a ruling. What they do not want is unconditional submission to revelation itself.

[PAUSE]

This is deeply relevant because selective obedience rarely presents itself honestly. It does not say, "I reject Allah's judgment." It says, "I am still seeking what is right," while quietly keeping one hand on the right to walk away if the right answer proves costly.

That is why the passage begins in astonishment. The contradiction is not subtle once exposed.

### Theme 2: Fear of People and Appetite for Small Gain Corrupt Judgment More Than Lack of Knowledge

The commands in 5:44 are very revealing: do not fear people; fear Me. Do not buy My signs for a small price.

Why these commands here?

Because the passage is showing that revelation is often betrayed not mainly through intellectual inability, but through fear and appetite. People know more than they obey. They see more than they submit to. What bends them is not always lack of textual access. It is the pressure of human consequence and the seduction of immediate gain.

[PAUSE]

This is painful because it turns the mirror toward us. How often do we imagine our problem is uncertainty when in reality we are afraid of what obedience would cost? How often do we call the price "practical wisdom" when Allah calls it small?

### Theme 3: Justice Is Measured, and Mercy Inside Justice Is Also Real

The long chain in 5:45 could be heard only as severity if we were careless. But the passage is more balanced than that.

Qisas names proportion. It restrains injustice by making retaliation answer to measure. Then immediately the ayah opens the door to a higher generosity: if one remits it as charity, it becomes expiation for him.

[PAUSE]

That means the Qur'an is not trapped between harshness and softness. It gives measured justice first, then leaves room for moral beauty beyond bare equivalence. But the order matters. Mercy is meaningful when it emerges from strength and truth, not when it is confused with refusal to acknowledge what was done.

## Closing Synthesis

This passage begins in astonishment and ends in a warning against refusing revelation's judgment.

First it exposes the contradiction of people seeking the Messenger's judgment while the Torah is already with them and in it is Allah's judgment. Then it honors the Torah as guidance and light, names the prophets and learned custodians who judged by it, commands fear of Allah over fear of people, and forbids trading divine signs for a small price. Then it gives a concrete example of written judgment in qisas and still opens the door of expiating mercy through remission. And both movements close by warning against judging by anything other than what Allah has sent down.

[PAUSE]

The architecture is powerful. Revelation is present. Custody over it is real. Distortion enters through fear and appetite. Justice is written. Mercy is possible. But selective obedience remains one of the greatest betrayals of all.

Ask yourself:

- Where do I still want sacred legitimacy without sacred submission?
- What small price have I been tempted to take in exchange for clearer obedience to Allah?
- Do I fear people more than the witness of Allah when truth becomes costly?
- When I speak about mercy, am I preserving the integrity of justice or trying to avoid it?

The distillation of the passage is this: **the crisis is not that Allah's judgment was absent, but that people wanted to consult revelation while preserving the right to turn away from it, and revelation calls that fear-and-appetite-driven selectivity a betrayal of guidance and light.**

O Allah, make us people who receive Your judgment with submission rather than shopping for outcomes, free us from fear of people and the lure of small prices, and let us honor Your revealed guidance with justice, fidelity, and truthful mercy.
