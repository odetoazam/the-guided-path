# It All Belongs to Him

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:120)

[ayah:5:120] لِلَّهِ مُلْكُ ٱلسَّمَـٰوَٰتِ وَٱلْأَرْضِ وَمَا فِيهِنَّ ۚ وَهُوَ عَلَىٰ كُلِّ شَىْءٍ قَدِيرٌۢ

*Lillahi mulku al-samawati wa al-ardi wa ma fihinna. Wa huwa 'ala kulli shay'in qadir.*

**To Allah belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth and whatever is within them, and He is over all things fully able.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

Some endings do not introduce something new. They gather everything that came before and tell you what it was always really about.

That is how Al-Ma'idah ends.

This surah took us through vows, lawful and unlawful food, ablution, justice, covenant, testimony, expiation, hunting in consecration, sacred symbols, fabricated religion, warning, mercy, the table sent from heaven, the public clearing of Isa from false claims, and the Day when truth helps the truthful. It moved through law and theology, appetite and witness, earth and heaven, human speech and the unseen account before Allah.

And after all of that, the final ayah does something breathtakingly simple. It does not add one last rule. It does not reopen one more case. It returns every scattered thread to one absolute fact:

*Lillahi mulku al-samawati wa al-ardi wa ma fihinna.*

To Allah belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth and whatever is in them.

[PAUSE]

This is the kind of ending that settles the soul if we let it. Because much of our confusion comes from forgetting who truly owns the field in which all these struggles are happening. We argue, fear, desire, obey, betray, repent, testify, and tremble as though pieces of reality belonged independently to us. Then the Qur'an closes the surah by taking all of it out of our shaking hands and returning it to its Owner.

The hook of the ayah is this: **every command in the surah, every warning, every mercy, every courtroom, every meal, every prophet, every heavenward sign, and every final judgment rests on one foundation - Allah alone owns all dominion and has power over all things.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Ayah Begins with Exclusive Belonging: *Lillahi*

The first word that strikes the heart is not *mulk*. It is *lillah*.

To Allah belongs.

The structure places belonging first. Before telling you what belongs to Him, the ayah plants ownership in Allah at the opening of the sentence. This is not neutral description. It is reordering.

[PAUSE]

That matters because the whole surah has exposed human beings constantly acting as though ownership were theirs. People make halal and haram from themselves. They invent sacred categories. They manipulate testimony. They fear people more than Allah. They make claims about prophets that do not belong to them. They cling to appetite as though the body were self-owned. They treat signs as consumable without consequence. They narrate salvation as inherited label rather than surrendered truth.

Then the surah ends by saying, in effect: let us start over from reality.

To Allah belongs.

This is how the Quran repairs the soul. It does not only correct individual mistakes. It restores the underlying map.

### 2. *Mulk*: Not Mere Possession, But Dominion

Then comes the central word:

*mulk*.

Dominion. Kingship. Sovereign possession. Not ownership in the thin sense of holding an object, but ownership in the sense of true command, disposal, authority, and right.

[PAUSE]

This is why the ayah is such a powerful seal for Al-Ma'idah. Much of the surah was about authority.

Who decides what is lawful?
Who sets the terms of covenant?
Who receives worship?
Who owns the sacred months, the Ka'bah, the offerings, and the rites?
Who judges testimony and hidden hearts?
Who has the right to forgive, punish, or expose?

The answer was given piece by piece all the way through. Here the answer is gathered into one word:

*mulk*.

The whole field belongs to Allah's dominion.

So this is not a decorative closing statement. It is the hidden logic beneath the entire surah being named openly at the end.

### 3. The Sweep Is Total: Heavens, Earth, and Whatever Is Within Them

Then the ayah expands the horizon:

*al-samawati wa al-ard wa ma fihinna.*

The heavens and the earth and whatever is within them.

[PAUSE]

The heavens and the earth already form a comprehensive pair in Qur'anic language. They mark the full created order from above and below. But the ayah does not stop there. It adds:

*wa ma fihinna* - and whatever is in them.

This matters because human beings are experts at searching for tiny exceptions where they can still pretend independence. Perhaps the cosmos belongs to Allah in some grand poetic sense, but surely this corner is mine. My body. My grief. My reputation. My child. My wealth. My desire. My intellect. My platform. My tradition. My case.

The ayah does not leave such exits open.

Whatever is in them.

Every creature.
Every event.
Every law.
Every historical community.
Every reward.
Every punishment.
Every prophet.
Every disputed claim.
Every garden.
Every fire.
Every heart that trembles beneath command.

All of it sits inside the domain already named as His.

### 4. The Ayah Closes with Power: *Wa huwa 'ala kulli shay'in qadir*

Then the ending:

*Wa huwa 'ala kulli shay'in qadir.*

And He is over all things fully able.

[PAUSE]

The surah could have closed with ownership alone and that would already have been immense. But ownership is joined to power. Allah is not an owner whose dominion is symbolic, ceremonial, or challenged. He is *qadir* over all things.

The word *qadir* brings capacity, ability, effective power. Not only right, but execution. Not only claim, but realization.

That means every command in Al-Ma'idah comes from One fully able to legislate.
Every warning comes from One fully able to bring it.
Every mercy comes from One fully able to bestow it.
Every resurrection scene comes from One fully able to gather and judge.
Every impossible-seeming sign - including a table from heaven - falls easily beneath His power.
Every final reward promised to the truthful rests upon His capacity to fulfill it completely.

This last phrase makes the whole surah stand upright. Nothing in it was aspirational. Everything in it was spoken by the One who can actually do what He says.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Most Spiritual Anxiety Is Tied to False Ownership

A great deal of our inner unrest comes from acting like caretakers of things we actually treat as owners.

We say "my" so often that the word becomes metaphysical.
My future.
My children.
My image.
My body.
My gifts.
My projects.
My losses.
My timeline.

[PAUSE]

Of course Allah allows us stewardship, responsibility, and intimacy with what He places in our hands. But the soul becomes exhausted when stewardship quietly mutates into ownership. Because then every threat feels ultimate. Every delay feels like dispossession. Every change feels like violation. Every command from Allah feels like intrusion into territory we assumed was ours.

This final ayah is a mercy because it cuts underneath all of that. It reminds us that everything we fear losing was never self-owned in the first place. Everything we cling to is already inside a dominion that belongs to Allah.

That does not remove grief or effort. It removes illusion. And often illusion was the sharpest part of the pain.

### Theme 2: The Surah's Demands Become Beautiful Once We Remember Who Owns the Field

Al-Ma'idah is not a light surah.

It asks a lot.
It disciplines appetite.
It regulates speech.
It demands justice under hatred.
It corrects false religion.
It refuses self-made sacredness.
It exposes theological excess.
It places the servant before the Last Day.

[PAUSE]

If we hear all of that while still imagining ourselves as independent centers of authority, the surah can feel heavy in the wrong way. It can feel like imposition from outside.

But if the surah closes over us with this ayah and we really hear it, the whole emotional texture changes. Of course the One who owns dominion decides halal and haram. Of course the One who owns the heavens and earth has the right to define worship. Of course the One who owns all things may ask us to tell the truth, keep covenant, fear Him, and place every prophet where He placed them.

The commands become beautiful when ownership is remembered.

Because then obedience is no longer a negotiation between rival claims. It is reality aligning with reality.

### Theme 3: The Final Shelter of the Surah Is Not Law Alone, But Allah Himself

There is one more tenderness in this ending.

The surah does not leave us with ourselves.
It does not leave us with the burden of analysis.
It does not leave us staring only at our failures under divine law.

It leaves us with Allah.

[PAUSE]

That is the deepest shelter of any serious surah. Not that the law disappears, but that the law returns you to the Lawgiver. Not that the warnings vanish, but that the warnings bring you back beneath the One who owns all things. Not that the tests become unreal, but that the tests are now seen inside a dominion that never slips from His power.

So the final ayah is not only a doctrinal closure. It is a place to rest. If all dominion is His, then nothing in your life is orphaned. Nothing has slipped outside His reach. Nothing is beyond His capacity to command, heal, restrain, expose, forgive, or replace.

That is not softness. It is tawhid becoming shelter.

## Closing Synthesis

Al-Ma'idah ends by gathering its entire architecture into two truths.

First: the dominion of the heavens, the earth, and everything in them belongs to Allah.
Second: He is fully able over all things.

[PAUSE]

Those two truths explain the whole surah.

Why He may command.
Why He may forbid.
Why He may judge.
Why He may expose.
Why He may send a sign from heaven.
Why He may clear a prophet from false claims.
Why He may reward the truthful.
Why He may ask for your surrender in places you still wanted to control.

Because it all belongs to Him.

And that is finally not a threat to the believer's heart. It is the believer's relief. The world is not ownerless. Your story is not ownerless. The tests of this surah were not scattered under competing powers. They all unfolded under one dominion and one perfect ability.

### Questions to Carry

1. What in my life do I still treat as though it were ultimately mine rather than a trust inside Allah's dominion?
2. Which commands of Allah feel heavy to me partly because I have forgotten who truly owns the field in which they are given?
3. When I fear loss, exposure, or change, do I remember that what is being moved was never outside Allah's mulk?
4. What would become lighter in my heart if I really believed that He is over all things fully able?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**This final ayah gathers all of Al-Ma'idah into one stabilizing truth: every command, sign, prophet, courtroom, appetite, reward, and judgment rests inside Allah's absolute dominion, and His power over all things is complete.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, return our hearts to reality. Do not let us live inside the exhaustion of false ownership. Make us remember that our lives, our losses, our duties, our hopes, and our akhirah all rest inside Your mulk, and let certainty in Your complete power make us steadier, softer, and more surrendered before You. Amin.
