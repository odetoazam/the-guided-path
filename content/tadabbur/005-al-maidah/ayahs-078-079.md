# They Did Not Forbid the Wrong

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:78-79)

[ayah:5:78] لُعِنَ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ مِنۢ بَنِىٓ إِسْرَٰٓءِيلَ عَلَىٰ لِسَانِ دَاوُۥدَ وَعِيسَى ٱبْنِ مَرْيَمَ ۚ ذَٰلِكَ بِمَا عَصَوا۟ وَّكَانُوا۟ يَعْتَدُونَ

[ayah:5:79] كَانُوا۟ لَا يَتَنَاهَوْنَ عَن مُّنكَرٍ فَعَلُوهُ ۚ لَبِئْسَ مَا كَانُوا۟ يَفْعَلُونَ

*Lu'ina alladhina kafaru min bani Isra'ila 'ala lisani Dawuda wa 'Isa ibni Maryam. Dhalika bima 'asaw wa kanu ya'tadun.*

*Kanu la yatanahawna 'an munkarin fa'aluhu. Labi'sa ma kanu yaf'alun.*

**Cursed were those who disbelieved among the Children of Israel by the tongue of Dawud and Isa, son of Mary. That was because they disobeyed and used to transgress. They used not to forbid one another from wrongdoing that they committed. Evil indeed was what they used to do.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

Communities do not usually collapse only because some people commit sins.

Communities collapse when sin becomes ordinary enough that no one feels responsible to interrupt it.

At that point the problem is no longer only the original act. The problem is the atmosphere that forms around the act. Wrongdoing stops feeling like a rupture and starts feeling like a tolerated feature of the environment. People see it, name it privately, perhaps even dislike it inwardly, but they let it breathe. And once wrong is allowed to breathe socially, it multiplies faster than any one person's guilt.

That is the world of these two ayahs.

The passage does not merely say that some among Bani Isra'il fell into kufr and disobedience. It says a curse came upon them by the tongue of Dawud and Isa. Then it explains why: not only because they disobeyed and transgressed, but because they did not forbid one another from the munkar they were doing.

[PAUSE]

That is one of the most sobering social diagnoses in the Quran. Public wrong unchecked becomes shared guilt. Silence around manifest corruption is not morally empty. It helps build the conditions in which the corruption becomes normal, then durable, then identity-forming.

The hook of the passage is this: **a society is not only judged for the evil some of its members commit, but for the evil it learns to watch without resisting.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Lu'ina ... 'ala lisani Dawuda wa 'Isa**: The Curse Is Spoken Through Prophetic Tongues

The opening is severe:

**lu'ina** - they were cursed.

The passive form lands with heaviness. Divine rejection is being announced, but the ayah immediately adds:

**'ala lisani Dawuda wa 'Isa ibni Maryam** - on the tongue of Dawud and Isa, son of Mary.

[PAUSE]

That phrase matters. The condemnation did not come from reckless public outrage, nor from the ego of moralistic people enjoying denunciation. It came through prophetic tongues. That gives the judgment both authority and gravity. What is being named here is not social disapproval. It is morally weighty revelation articulated through messengers.

And notice the pairing: Dawud and Isa. Different times, different prophetic contexts, same moral disease recurring deeply enough that prophetic witness had to speak against it.

### 2. **Dhalika bima 'asaw wa kanu ya'tadun**: The Problem Is Both Refusal of Boundary and Aggressive Overstepping

Then the ayah explains:

**dhalika bima 'asaw** - that was because they disobeyed.

The root here is disobedience, refusal of what was commanded.

Then:

**wa kanu ya'tadun** - and they used to transgress.

[PAUSE]

This is more than personal weakness. **I'tida'** is overstepping, violating limits, moving beyond what Allah set. So the verse names two layers:

refusal to stay within command,
and active crossing beyond boundary.

In other words, they were not only failing to do the right. They were normalizing a posture of تجاوز, going past what should have stopped them.

That prepares us for the next ayah, because transgression on that scale rarely remains individual. It becomes communal texture.

### 3. **Kanu la yatanahawna**: Evil Deepens When People Stop Stopping One Another

Verse 79 gives the social mechanism:

**kanu la yatanahawna** - they used not to forbid one another.

The wording is important. This is mutual language. Not merely that formal leaders failed, though that matters elsewhere in the surah. Here the community itself is implicated. The people were not actively restraining each other.

[PAUSE]

And what were they failing to restrain?

**'an munkarin fa'aluhu** - from a wrongdoing they had done.

**Munkar** is what the moral fitrah and revelation both recognize as objectionable, ugly, deniable, not to be accepted. It is wrong that should not become familiar. Yet here it had become familiar enough to be committed openly while the network of mutual moral concern weakened or died.

This is a terrifying line because it teaches that one of the key signs of a dying conscience in a community is not only the presence of wrong, but the disappearance of meaningful protest against it.

### 4. **Labi'sa ma kanu yaf'alun**: The Verse Condemns the Doing and the Non-Forbidding Together

Then the closing verdict:

**labi'sa ma kanu yaf'alun** - evil indeed was what they used to do.

[PAUSE]

At first this sounds like a straightforward condemnation of the wrong action itself, and it is. But in the structure of the passage, "what they used to do" now includes the communal pattern just named: failing to forbid the munkar.

That is what gives the verse its severity. The ayah does not let moral passivity hide behind a claim of personal innocence. If wrong becomes communal habit because people stopped resisting it, that passivity has entered the field of "what they used to do."

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Moral Silence Is Not Neutral Once Wrong Becomes Visible

We often imagine guilt too individualistically.

I did not say it.
I did not take it.
I did not start it.
I was not the worst one there.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs complicate that comfort. Once munkar becomes visible and socially breathed, the refusal to resist it becomes part of the crisis. This does not mean every person is equally guilty in every situation or equally able to intervene in the same way. Wisdom, capacity, and risk all matter. But the ayah demolishes the fantasy that habitual communal silence is morally blank.

If a people get used to seeing wrong and simply adjusting around it, they are already being changed by it.

### Theme 2: Wrongdoing Spreads Through Familiarity More Than Through Argument

Most societies are not conquered by evil because evil first won a brilliant intellectual case. They are conquered because evil became familiar.

People saw it enough times that outrage dulled.
They adapted their expectations.
They stopped wanting the social cost of confrontation.
Eventually the abnormal began feeling inevitable.

[PAUSE]

That is exactly why **munkar** matters as a word. It names what should remain morally strange. The moment a community stops helping each other keep evil strange, the evil gains ground. Familiarity is one of shaytan's strongest strategies.

### Theme 3: Real Care for Others Includes Refusing to Let Them Settle Into Wrong

The ayah also teaches us something uncomfortable about love.

Many people think love means never making others uncomfortable. Never naming the hard thing. Never risking strain in the relationship.

[PAUSE]

But a community where nobody warns anyone for fear of discomfort is not a merciful community. It is a fragile community addicted to short-term peace at long-term moral cost.

Real care may require interruption. It may require saying, with wisdom and humility, "This is not okay. We cannot keep walking around this as though it were normal."

The goal is not harshness. The goal is to keep people from settling into what will wound them and everyone around them.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs begin with curse and end with the explanation of how that curse became deserved.

First: disobedience and transgression.
Then: a community that stopped forbidding munkar.

[PAUSE]

That is the architecture.

Wrong was committed.
Wrong was crossed into repeatedly.
Wrong was no longer seriously resisted.
Then prophetic denunciation named what the society had become.

So the passage teaches us that communal health depends not only on avoiding evil personally, but on keeping moral concern alive socially. Once we lose the courage, tenderness, and seriousness required to help one another resist visible wrong, we are already deeper inside the wrong than we think.

### Questions to Carry

1. What forms of munkar in my environment have started to feel too normal to challenge?
2. Where have I confused peacekeeping with moral responsibility?
3. Do I love people enough to help interrupt what is harming them, or do I mostly avoid discomfort?
4. What kind of community am I helping build through the things I resist - or quietly tolerate?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs teach that disobedience becomes socially catastrophic when a people stop forbidding visible wrong, because habitual silence allows munkar to pass from isolated act into communal atmosphere.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, do not let our hearts grow comfortable with munkar. Give us wisdom to recognize when silence is cowardice, and mercy enough to warn one another without arrogance. Keep wrong strange in our sight and obedience honorable in our communities. And do not let us help normalize what You hate by watching it without resistance. Amin.
