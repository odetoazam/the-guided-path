# You Are Human Among Those He Created

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:18)

[ayah:5:18] وَقَالَتِ ٱلْيَهُودُ وَٱلنَّصَـٰرَىٰ نَحْنُ أَبْنَـٰٓؤُا۟ ٱللَّهِ وَأَحِبَّـٰٓؤُهُۥ ۚ قُلْ فَلِمَ يُعَذِّبُكُم بِذُنُوبِكُم ۖ بَلْ أَنتُم بَشَرٌ مِّمَّنْ خَلَقَ ۚ يَغْفِرُ لِمَن يَشَآءُ وَيُعَذِّبُ مَن يَشَآءُ ۚ وَلِلَّهِ مُلْكُ ٱلسَّمَـٰوَٰتِ وَٱلْأَرْضِ وَمَا بَيْنَهُمَا ۖ وَإِلَيْهِ ٱلْمَصِيرُ

*Wa qalati al-yahudu wa al-nasara nahnu abna'u Allahi wa ahibba'uhu. Qul falima yu'adhdhibukum bidhunubikum. Bal antum basharun mimman khalaq. Yaghfiru liman yasha'u wa yu'adhdhibu man yasha'. Wa lillahi mulku al-samawati wa al-ardi wa ma baynahuma wa ilayhi al-masir.*

**And the Jews and the Christians said, "We are the children of Allah and His beloved ones." Say, "Then why does He punish you for your sins?" Rather, you are human beings from among those He created. He forgives whom He wills and punishes whom He wills. And to Allah belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth and whatever is between them, and to Him is the final return.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

This ayah is not only correcting theology. It is dismantling a temptation of the soul.

There is something in us that wants closeness to Allah without exposure to accountability. We want nearness as a badge, not as a burden. We want to be the exception, the favored ones, the people whose belonging itself guarantees safety. So the claim the verse quotes is not merely an old communal statement. It is a permanent human desire: *we are the beloved ones; ordinary standards do not apply to us in the same way.*

The Qur'an answers that temptation with astonishing brevity. If this claim were true, asks the verse, then why does He punish you for your sins? Then comes the demolition: **Rather, you are human beings from among those He created.**

That sentence does not humiliate humanity. It restores reality. You are not self-certified favorites who stand outside the moral order. You are created beings, dependent beings, answerable beings. Love of Allah is real, but it is not tribal immunity. Nearness to Allah is real, but it is not inherited exemption.

And then the verse widens its horizon. Forgiveness and punishment belong to Allah's will. Dominion belongs to Him. The final return is to Him. So no community, lineage, slogan, or religious self-image can seize the right to declare itself safe. The One who created decides, forgives, judges, and receives the return.

The hook of the ayah is this: **the Qur'an tears away every fantasy of spiritual exemption and returns us to the only safe posture before Allah: humble creaturehood under His mercy, judgment, sovereignty, and final return.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Nahnu abna'u Allahi wa ahibba'uhu**: The Soul Loves the Language of Specialness

The quoted claim is striking:

**Nahnu abna'u Allahi wa ahibba'uhu** - we are the children of Allah and His beloved ones.

Whatever the precise communal meaning of "children" in their rhetoric, the spiritual psychology of the claim is clear. It is a claim to exceptional closeness, special status, privileged affection. It says, in effect: our relation to Allah is not like that of ordinary people.

[PAUSE]

Then comes **ahibba'uhu** - His beloved ones. This is where the ayah cuts especially deep. The problem is not that love of Allah is false. Of course Allah loves. Of course there are those beloved to Him. The problem is when love is turned into a possession rather than a responsibility, into entitlement rather than trembling gratitude.

That is why this verse still lives inside us. The ego is always trying to convert blessings into guarantees. It takes a mercy and turns it into a certificate. It takes one sign of favor and builds a doctrine of immunity around it. "Because I belong to the right group." "Because I know the right language." "Because I have done certain things." "Because I am not like them." The form changes, but the disease is the same.

### 2. **Qul falima yu'adhdhibukum bidhunubikum**: One Honest Question Can Collapse a Whole False Theology

The Qur'an does not answer with a long philosophical treatise. It gives one question:

**Falima yu'adhdhibukum bidhunubikum?**

Then why does He punish you for your sins?

The force of the question is devastating. If your claim of guaranteed belovedness means exemption from accountability, then reality itself refutes you. The existence of punishment for sin already shows that the relationship cannot mean what you are pretending it means.

[PAUSE]

And notice the precision: **bidhunubikum** - for your sins.

The verse does not let the discussion remain abstract. It brings the claim down to moral fact. Sin still exists. Accountability still exists. Divine dealing with human wrongdoing still exists. So whatever nearness to Allah means, it cannot mean the suspension of truth and consequence.

This is an important correction for the religious self. Many of us do not say "we are Allah's children and beloved ones" in those words, but we can live with a softer version of the same delusion. We expect our affiliation, learning, service, or visible religiosity to function as a shield against serious self-reckoning. The ayah asks the same question of us: then what do you do with your sins?

### 3. **Bal antum basharun mimman khalaq**: Revelation Restores Us to the Shared Human Condition

Then comes one of the most leveling phrases in the surah:

**Bal antum basharun mimman khalaq.**

Rather, you are human beings from among those He created.

**Bal** is a turn, a correction, a redirection after false framing. The verse is not merely adding nuance to their claim. It is replacing the premise.

[PAUSE]

And what replaces it? **Bashar**.

Human beings. Flesh-bound, needy, limited, dependent, vulnerable, accountable. There is a profound mercy in this word because it returns us to reality. It strips us of the burden of self-made sacred exceptionalism and places us back where actual hope lives: among the created.

Then comes **mimman khalaq** - from among those He created.

That phrase completes the leveling. You are not outside the created order. You are in it. You are not above the law of dependence. You are under it. You are not beyond the reach of justice, mercy, command, or return. You are one of His creatures.

This is not degradation. This is liberation from delusion. The creature who knows he is a creature is finally in a position to ask, repent, fear, and hope truthfully.

### 4. **Yaghfiru liman yasha'u wa yu'adhdhibu man yasha' ... wa ilayhi al-masir**: Hope and Fear Are Returned to Allah Alone

The verse does not stop at leveling identity. It restores the whole matter to Allah:

**Yaghfiru liman yasha'u wa yu'adhdhibu man yasha'.**

He forgives whom He wills and punishes whom He wills.

This does not mean arbitrariness. It means ownership. Forgiveness is His. Judgment is His. No community monopolizes either. No one can obligate Allah with slogans of chosen status.

[PAUSE]

Then the ayah expands:

**Wa lillahi mulku al-samawati wa al-ardi wa ma baynahuma.**

To Allah belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth and whatever is between them.

The argument rises from one false human claim to the scale of the cosmos. The One before whom you make these claims is not a tribal god to be flattered into partiality. He owns the heavens, the earth, and everything in between.

And then the landing:

**Wa ilayhi al-masir** - and to Him is the final return.

Not to our self-descriptions. Not to our inherited myths about ourselves. Not to communal applause. To Him. The verse ends by relocating the whole human story into that unavoidable meeting.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Religious Ego Wants to Be Loved Without Being Judged

One of the hardest spiritual diseases to detect is not open rebellion. It is entitled religiosity.

Open rebellion at least knows it is outside the line. Entitled religiosity wants the comfort of being inside while refusing the vulnerability that being inside actually requires. It wants all the language of nearness with none of the trembling. It wants Allah's love as personal branding. It wants sacred identity without sacred honesty.

[PAUSE]

That is why this ayah is so unsettling. It reveals that a person can speak in the language of divine closeness while actually using that language to defend the ego from repentance.

This happens in subtle ways. We tell ourselves that our good commitments must surely outweigh the inner rot we are avoiding. We assume that because Allah has blessed us with some signs of guidance, He would not really confront us about certain cherished distortions. We hide behind a religious role, a pious environment, a doctrinal correctness, a family inheritance, or years of service. Somewhere inside, the claim forms: surely I am among the special ones.

But the ayah refuses to let love be used that way. It reminds us that belovedness in the sight of Allah never cancels answerability. If anything, real closeness makes a person more sensitive to his sins, not less. The more a servant knows Allah, the less he speaks like someone guaranteed. He hopes more intensely, yes, but he also stands more honestly.

### Theme 2: There Is Mercy in Being Returned to Creaturehood

At first, **bal antum basharun mimman khalaq** sounds like a stripping away. And it is. But it is also a rescue.

There is something exhausting about spiritual exceptionalism. Once you need to believe you are unlike other human beings, you must keep protecting the image. You cannot afford honest exposure. You cannot admit need too plainly. You cannot repent too visibly. You cannot descend into ordinary creaturely dependence, because your whole identity is built on being above it.

[PAUSE]

The Qur'an cuts that burden off at the root. You are human among those He created.

That means you get to need forgiveness like every other human being. You get to fear your sins like every other human being. You get to stand in hope without pretending to be exempt. You get to return to Allah not as a self-certified favorite, but as a servant.

And that is where tenderness becomes possible again. The creature can cry. The creature can ask. The creature can be broken and still be held. The creature can say, "My Lord, I have no claim except that I belong to the ones You created, and all of us survive only by Your mercy." That posture is far safer than the inflated certainty the verse dismantles.

### Theme 3: Final Return Destroys Every False Immunity

The last words of the ayah are among its most important:

**Wa ilayhi al-masir.**

To Him is the final return.

Every false identity can survive for a while in this world. Every community can tell itself flattering stories. Every ego can curate an image in which it remains exceptional. But return ends performance. Return ends selective memory. Return ends the comfort of being praised by those who share our illusions.

[PAUSE]

When the ayah says the final return is to Him, it is not only warning. It is also clarifying the standard. If I know I am returning to Allah, then I do not need to keep proving that I belong to some superior category among people. The goal is not to win the argument of belovedness in this life. The goal is to arrive before Allah in truth.

That changes the spiritual atmosphere completely. The question becomes not, "How can I reassure myself that I am special?" but, "How can I return sincerely, stripped of pretension, trusting only in Allah's mercy and fearing only His justice?"

That is the kind of human being this ayah is trying to make.

## Closing Synthesis

This verse begins with a claim of spiritual privilege and ends with the final return to Allah.

Between those two points, it dismantles one of the most dangerous illusions in religious life. The claim "we are His beloved ones" is met not with flattery, but with the question of sin and punishment. Then the ayah restores the speaker to reality: you are human beings from among those He created. Forgiveness and punishment remain with Allah alone. Dominion remains with Allah alone. And the end of the road is return to Allah alone.

So the architecture is severe and merciful at once. A false nearness is torn down so that real servanthood can begin. Entitlement is denied so that hope and fear can both be purified. Human beings are returned to creaturehood so that they can finally stand before their Lord truthfully.

[PAUSE]

Ask yourself:

- Where have I quietly turned blessings into guarantees about myself?
- Do I use religious identity to deepen repentance, or to avoid it?
- What would it mean for me to accept being simply one human being among those Allah created?
- If I truly remembered that to Him is the final return, which illusions about myself would become impossible to keep?

The distillation of the ayah is this: **Allah breaks the illusion of spiritual exemption by reminding us that we are created human beings, accountable for our sins, dependent on His forgiveness, subject to His judgment, and returning finally to Him alone.**

O Allah, save us from every form of religious entitlement, make us truthful about our sins, let us stand before You as humble servants rather than self-declared favorites, and return us to You with hearts that trust only in Your mercy and fear only Your justice.
