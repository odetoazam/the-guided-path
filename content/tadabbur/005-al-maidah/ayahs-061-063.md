# They Entered With It and Left With It

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:61-63)

[ayah:5:61] وَإِذَا جَآءُوكُمْ قَالُوٓا۟ ءَامَنَّا وَقَد دَّخَلُوا۟ بِٱلْكُفْرِ وَهُمْ قَدْ خَرَجُوا۟ بِهِۦ ۚ وَٱللَّهُ أَعْلَمُ بِمَا كَانُوا۟ يَكْتُمُونَ

[ayah:5:62] وَتَرَىٰ كَثِيرًا مِّنْهُمْ يُسَـٰرِعُونَ فِى ٱلْإِثْمِ وَٱلْعُدْوَٰنِ وَأَكْلِهِمُ ٱلسُّحْتَ ۚ لَبِئْسَ مَا كَانُوا۟ يَعْمَلُونَ

[ayah:5:63] لَوْلَا يَنْهَىٰهُمُ ٱلرَّبَّـٰنِيُّونَ وَٱلْأَحْبَارُ عَن قَوْلِهِمُ ٱلْإِثْمَ وَأَكْلِهِمُ ٱلسُّحْتَ ۚ لَبِئْسَ مَا كَانُوا۟ يَصْنَعُونَ

*Wa idha ja'ukum qalu amanna wa qad dakhalu bi-al-kufri wa hum qad kharaju bihi. Wa Allahu a'lamu bima kanu yaktumun.*

*Wa tara kathiran minhum yusari'una fi al-ithmi wa al-'udwani wa aklihimu al-suht. Labi'sa ma kanu ya'malun.*

*Lawla yanhahumu al-rabbaniyyuna wa al-ahbaru 'an qawlihimu al-ithma wa aklihimu al-suht. Labi'sa ma kanu yasna'un.*

**And when they come to you, they say, "We believe," while they have entered with disbelief and they have certainly left with it. And Allah is most knowing of what they were concealing. And you see many of them hastening into sin and aggression and their consumption of unlawful gain. Evil indeed is what they have been doing. Why do the rabbaniyyun and the scholars not forbid them from sinful speech and their consumption of unlawful gain? Evil indeed is what they have been crafting.**

## Introduction

One of the most painful kinds of corruption is the kind that knows how to sound religious.

It knows the right entrance line. It knows how to arrive with believable language. It knows how to stand near truth without surrendering to it. It knows how to keep one face for the gathering, another for the interior, and then a third for the systems of appetite and advantage that continue unchanged once the conversation is over.

That is where this passage begins.

People come saying, **We believe.** The sentence sounds clean. But the ayah opens their hidden ledger and tells us something chilling: they entered with kufr, and they left with it. The visit changed nothing. The profession changed nothing. The proximity changed nothing. The outside statement never became an inward crossing.

[PAUSE]

Then the passage widens. Hidden corruption never stays hidden for long. What began in the concealed interior spills out into observable habits: haste into sin, aggression, and the eating of unlawful gain. And then, more painfully still, the Quran asks why the people who should have restrained this corruption did not do so.

So the passage is not only about hypocrisy. It is also about ecosystems. It shows us how moral collapse moves from the hidden heart, to public behavior, to leadership failure. A diseased interior becomes a social order.

The hook of the passage is this: **a false "we believe" does not stay trapped in private; sooner or later it becomes a visible culture of sin, appetite, and silence.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Qalu amanna ... dakhalu bi-al-kufr ... kharaju bihi**: The Exterior Sentence Never Crossed the Interior Threshold

The first ayah gives us one of the sharpest portraits of false profession in the surah.

They say:

**Amanna** - we believe.

But then the ayah immediately counters their claim with two matched verbs:

**dakhalu** - they entered,

and

**kharaju** - they left.

[PAUSE]

This symmetry is devastating. They entered with kufr. They left with it. The whole interaction is framed as movement that never became transformation.

That is the point. You can pass through religious space without being changed by it. You can stand near a prophet, enter the company of believers, say the sentence of alignment, and yet carry the same darkness in and out because the crossing happened only with the body and the tongue, not with the heart.

The prepositional phrase deepens the warning:

**bi-al-kufr ... bihi** - with disbelief ... with it.

Kufr is not described here as an accidental residue or a passing doubt they were wrestling through honestly. It is baggage they knowingly carry through the whole encounter. The meeting with truth does not strip it off them because they do not come to be changed. They come to manage appearances.

Then the ayah closes:

**Wa Allahu a'lamu bima kanu yaktumun** - Allah knows best what they were concealing.

That line is mercy for believers who are often gaslit by surfaces. You do not need to solve every hidden heart. Allah knows what is being buried beneath the statement.

### 2. **Yusari'una fi al-ithmi wa al-'udwan**: Corruption Reveals Itself by What It Hurries Toward

Then verse 62 shifts from the concealed to the visible:

**Wa tara** - and you see.

The hidden interior is now producing an observable pattern. Many of them are:

**yusari'una** - rushing, hurrying, making speed toward something.

[PAUSE]

We saw this same verb earlier in the section when diseased hearts hurried into false loyalties. Here the same moral psychology returns in another form. Spiritual sickness is often known by its speed. The soul becomes quick wherever desire, advantage, and transgression are calling.

And what do they hasten into?

**fi al-ithm wa al-'udwan** - into sin and aggression.

**Ithm** names moral offense, guilt-bearing wrong. **'Udwan** adds trespass, overstepping, hostility, violating limits against others. So the verse is not describing a private inward weakness alone. It is describing wrongdoing that has become socially harmful.

Then comes the phrase that lands with unusual heaviness:

**wa aklihimu al-suht** - and their eating of unlawful gain.

The Quran often uses eating to describe wrongful consumption because it exposes how corruption is not merely ideological. It becomes nourishment. People start feeding themselves on what Allah has forbidden. **Suht** is not ordinary earnings. It is devouring what is filthy in law and destructive in moral consequence.

[PAUSE]

This is what false belief becomes in real life: not only theological distortion, but appetite trained to live on the forbidden.

### 3. **Lawla yanhahumu al-rabbaniyyun wa al-ahbar**: Leadership Is Judged Not Only for Personal Purity, but for Moral Restraint

Then the passage turns sharply:

**Lawla yanhahumu** - why do they not forbid them?

This is no longer aimed at the sinners directly. It is aimed at the religious leadership watching them.

**al-rabbaniyyun wa al-ahbar** - the rabbaniyyun and the scholars.

[PAUSE]

The wording is severe because it assumes something profound: knowledge creates responsibility. To know the boundaries of Allah and then fail to restrain public corruption is not a minor omission. It is part of the corruption story itself.

The first object of neglect is:

**'an qawlihimu al-ithma** - from their speaking of sin.

That matters. The leadership failure is not only about money. It begins with speech. Sinful language, false speech, corrupt talk, the rhetoric by which wrongdoing is normalized and circulated, should have been checked.

Then the verse repeats:

**wa aklihimu al-suht** - and their eating of unlawful gain.

So the leaders failed to restrain both the tongue and the appetite, both the public speech that spreads corruption and the economic practice that feeds on it.

### 4. **Labi'sa ma kanu ya'malun ... yasna'un**: The Passage Distinguishes Between Doing Wrong and Crafting a Corrupt Order

One of the most subtle details in this passage is the shift in closing verbs.

Verse 62 ends:

**labi'sa ma kanu ya'malun** - evil indeed is what they were doing.

Verse 63 ends:

**labi'sa ma kanu yasna'un** - evil indeed is what they were crafting or making.

[PAUSE]

That shift matters.

**Ya'malun** is broad: they are doing wrong. Their actions are evil.

But **yasna'un** suggests making, fashioning, producing something shaped by deliberate formation. When the scholars and spiritual leaders remain silent, they are not merely failing to interrupt isolated sins. They are helping craft an environment in which those sins can mature and multiply.

That is why the blame intensifies. Private wrongdoing is terrible. But institutional silence around wrongdoing builds a system.

The passage is teaching us that moral collapse has layers:

concealed disbelief,
visible haste into corruption,
then leadership silence that manufactures continuity.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Religious Language Can Become a Mask for an Unchanged Core

The sentence **We believe** is one of the most beautiful sentences a human being can utter.

Which is why it is so frightening to see it detached from truthfulness.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs warn us that it is possible to learn the language of faith before surrendering the self to faith. A person can master belonging signals, correct phrases, emotional timing, and external nearness to sacred people while the inner center remains untouched. He enters with kufr and leaves with it because his goal was not transformation but access, legitimacy, or cover.

This should make us suspicious first of ourselves, not only of others.

Do I use the language of iman as a genuine report of the heart?
Or as a way of managing how I am perceived?
Do my encounters with truth actually change what I carry in and out?

The passage teaches that Allah is not fooled by transit through sacred space. He is looking for crossing, not contact.

### Theme 2: Sin Becomes a Social Pattern When Appetite Starts Leading the Moral Imagination

Verse 62 is very modern in its psychology.

People hurry into sin, aggression, and unlawful consumption. They are not drifting slowly. They are moving quickly. That is what desire does once it stops being disciplined by taqwa. It becomes inventive, strategic, and urgent.

[PAUSE]

And notice the blend:

sin,
aggression,
unlawful gain.

This is how corruption usually works in lived communities. It is not one isolated vice. It is a braided system. Moral wrong feeds social harm, social harm feeds economic exploitation, and economic exploitation then finances the whole culture of wrongdoing.

The Quran's use of **eating** here is especially revealing. People do not only commit wrongs. They begin to feed on them. Their provision, prestige, and comfort become entangled with what Allah hates. At that point repentance feels expensive because renouncing sin would mean renouncing the very thing from which the self has been taking nourishment.

That is why haste is so dangerous. The faster we run into appetite, the harder it becomes to imagine living without it.

### Theme 3: Silent Leadership Does Not Remain Neutral

The final ayah should terrify anyone entrusted with influence.

The rabbaniyyun and the scholars are not condemned here for publicly approving corruption in explicit words. They are condemned for not forbidding it.

[PAUSE]

That means silence itself can become a form of participation.

Not every silence is sinful, of course. Wisdom matters. Timing matters. Capacity matters. But when leaders repeatedly watch speech rot, wealth become unlawful, and public corruption spread without meaningful restraint, they are no longer outside the story. They are helping shape the conditions in which the story continues.

This passage should therefore humble both teachers and communities.

Teachers must ask: what am I allowing to become normal because confrontation costs me comfort, access, or popularity?

Communities must ask: have we mistaken polished scholarship or spiritual branding for the harder work of real moral restraint?

The Quran does not flatter religious elites. It measures them by whether they help stop corruption before it becomes culture.

## Closing Synthesis

These three ayahs move with painful precision.

First, a false confession:

**We believe.**

But they entered with kufr and left with it.

[PAUSE]

Then the fruit appears in public:

haste into sin,
aggression,
and unlawful consumption.

Then the final layer is exposed:

the people who should have stopped this did not.

That is the architecture of moral decay. A heart remains unchanged by truth, its corruption becomes habitual behavior, and then silence from those with knowledge helps build a world in which that corruption can keep reproducing itself.

So the passage leaves us with a difficult mercy. It does not let us reduce spiritual failure to private hypocrisy alone. It forces us to see the whole chain. It asks whether our faith actually crosses the threshold of the self, whether our appetites are training us to rush into what Allah forbids, and whether our silences are helping craft what we later claim to lament.

### Questions to Carry

1. When I say words of faith, do they report a real inner crossing or only an outer alignment?
2. Where in my life do I notice unhealthy speed toward what I already know is wrong?
3. What forms of unlawful or morally compromised nourishment have I started treating as normal?
4. In what places have I been silent while corruption in speech or money was becoming ordinary?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs teach that false profession ripens into visible patterns of sin and unlawful consumption, and when people of knowledge fail to restrain that corruption, they help craft the very moral order they should have opposed.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, do not let our tongues outrun our hearts. Make iman a true crossing inside us, not a surface line we learn to say. Protect us from rushing toward sin, aggression, and any provision polluted by what You forbid. And if You give us knowledge or influence, do not let us hide inside silence while corruption spreads; make us among those who restrain wrong with wisdom, courage, and fear of You. Amin.
