# Do Not Exceed the Truth

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:76-77)

[ayah:5:76] قُلْ أَتَعْبُدُونَ مِن دُونِ ٱللَّهِ مَا لَا يَمْلِكُ لَكُمْ ضَرًّا وَلَا نَفْعًا ۚ وَٱللَّهُ هُوَ ٱلسَّمِيعُ ٱلْعَلِيمُ

[ayah:5:77] قُلْ يَـٰٓأَهْلَ ٱلْكِتَـٰبِ لَا تَغْلُوا۟ فِى دِينِكُمْ غَيْرَ ٱلْحَقِّ وَلَا تَتَّبِعُوٓا۟ أَهْوَآءَ قَوْمٍ قَدْ ضَلُّوا۟ مِن قَبْلُ وَأَضَلُّوا۟ كَثِيرًا وَضَلُّوا۟ عَن سَوَآءِ ٱلسَّبِيلِ

*Qul ata'buduna min duni Allahi ma la yamliku lakum darran wa la naf'an. Wa Allahu huwa al-sami'u al-'alim.*

*Qul ya ahla al-kitabi la taghlu fi dinikum ghayra al-haqqi wa la tattabi'u ahwa'a qawmin qad dallu min qablu wa adallu kathiran wa dallu 'an sawa'i al-sabil.*

**Say, "Do you worship besides Allah that which holds for you no power of harm or benefit, while Allah - He alone - is the All-Hearing, the All-Knowing?" Say, "O People of the Scripture, do not go to excess in your religion beyond the truth, and do not follow the desires of a people who had already gone astray before and led many astray and strayed from the soundness of the path."**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

One of the strangest things about the human being is that he can call excess love, call distortion reverence, and call deviation devotion.

The nafs is capable of doing this especially in religion. It does not always leave the sacred by mocking it. Sometimes it leaves the sacred by overstating it, overloading it, adding to it, or carrying it past the boundary Allah set while still imagining that such excess is piety. In that state, passion starts masquerading as holiness.

That is the crisis of these two ayahs.

The first ayah asks a blunt question: why worship what has no power to control harm or benefit for you? The second then explains one of the most common spiritual mechanisms behind such worship: **ghuluww** - going beyond the truth in religion, crossing the line from reverence into excess, and then letting inherited desires lead whole communities away.

[PAUSE]

So this passage is not only about idolatry in the crude sense. It is about a subtler human disease too: taking what should have remained within Allah's appointed place and pushing it beyond that place because desire wanted a more emotionally satisfying religion than truth would allow.

The hook of the passage is this: **you do not remain inside the truth merely by staying "religious"; you remain inside it only by refusing to let devotion outrun the boundaries Allah set.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Ata'buduna min duni Allahi ma la yamliku lakum darran wa la naf'an**: Worship Is Measured by Real Power, Not Sacred Feeling

The ayah opens as a question:

**ata'buduna** - do you worship?

Then immediately:

**min duni Allah** - apart from Allah, short of Allah, besides Allah.

[PAUSE]

This phrase is always destabilizing because it exposes how quickly the heart can relocate devotion. Something other than Allah begins receiving what belongs only to Him: dependence, reverence, ultimate petition, fear, hope, or sacred emotional centrality.

Then the test becomes brutally simple:

**ma la yamliku lakum darran wa la naf'an** - that which possesses for you neither harm nor benefit.

This is one of the Quran's great theological reductions. Strip the emotional aura away. Strip inherited sanctity away. Strip communal familiarity away. What actual sovereign capacity does this object of devotion have over your destiny? Can it truly control harm? Can it truly grant benefit?

The answer is no.

That is why worship here is shown to be irrational at its root. The soul has given highest devotion to what does not possess highest power.

### 2. **Wa Allahu huwa al-sami'u al-'alim**: The One Truly Worthy of Worship Is the One Who Fully Hears and Fully Knows

Then comes the counterweight:

**wa Allahu huwa al-sami'u al-'alim**.

Allah - He alone - is the All-Hearing, the All-Knowing.

[PAUSE]

The pronoun **huwa** adds emphasis. He, and none of these supposed alternatives, is the one defined by total hearing and total knowledge.

That matters because worship is not only about power in the abstract. It is also about relationship. The worshipped must hear the cry and know the interior. Allah does both perfectly. He hears what the false object cannot hear. He knows what the false refuge cannot know.

So the ayah is not merely demolishing false worship. It is redirecting the heart toward the One whose hearing and knowledge make worship meaningful in the first place.

### 3. **La taghlu fi dinikum ghayra al-haqq**: Religion Is Not Preserved by Emotional Intensity Alone

Then the second ayah names the disease more specifically:

**la taghlu fi dinikum** - do not go to excess in your religion.

The verb **taghlu** comes from going beyond the proper measure. Crossing the limit. Exceeding what should have remained in right proportion.

[PAUSE]

This is crucial because many people imagine religion is endangered only by neglect. The Quran says it can also be endangered by excess.

And not any excess whatsoever, but:

**ghayra al-haqq** - beyond the truth, outside the truth, not in keeping with the truth.

This phrase protects us from misunderstanding the verse as though all strong devotion were suspect. The problem is not intensity of faith. The problem is intensity that leaves the boundary of haqq.

That means sincerity is not enough by itself. Religious passion must still be disciplined by revealed truth.

### 4. **Wa la tattabi'u ahwa'a qawmin**: Excess Religion Is Often Just Desire Wearing Sacred Clothes

Then the ayah unveils the engine underneath the distortion:

**wa la tattabi'u ahwa'a qawmin** - do not follow the desires of a people.

Here is the shock: ghuluww is not presented as pure reverence gone slightly too far. It is linked to **ahwa'** - desires, inclinations, appetites, the lower self's preferred shape of religion.

[PAUSE]

That is devastatingly relevant.

We often think excess comes from "too much love." The ayah says it may also come from too much hawa. The self wants a form of religion that satisfies emotional hunger, communal pride, or inherited imagination more than it satisfies revealed truth. So it elevates, exaggerates, and overstates.

Then the verse sketches the tragic chain:

**qad dallu min qablu** - they had already gone astray before,

**wa adallu kathiran** - and they led many astray,

**wa dallu 'an sawa'i al-sabil** - and they strayed from the sound middle of the path.

This is not a private eccentricity. Desire-driven religious excess becomes a tradition. A people inherit it, transmit it, and multiply it.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Religious Excess Can Feel More Sacred Than Truth

One of the reasons ghuluww is so dangerous is that it often feels religiously richer than restraint.

Excess feels dramatic.
Excess feels emotionally charged.
Excess feels like intense devotion.

[PAUSE]

Restraint, by contrast, can feel plain. Staying exactly where Allah placed a matter may feel less exciting than enlarging it with reverent imagination. But the Quran trains us to distrust that instinct. The fact that a religious claim feels emotionally powerful does not make it true. Sometimes what feels most "spiritual" is simply the nafs enjoying a version of religion shaped by its own appetite.

That is why the ayah links ghuluww to hawa. It is teaching us that excess can be a form of self-service even while speaking sacred language.

### Theme 2: The Wrong Object of Worship Is Always Also the Wrong Estimate of Reality

The first ayah's question should keep humbling us:

Why worship what holds neither harm nor benefit?

[PAUSE]

This is not only a critique of old idolatry. It reaches every misplaced center in our lives. Anything we quietly treat as ultimate - approval, status, a person, a tradition over Allah's command, even a spiritually romanticized attachment - can begin receiving forms of devotion it cannot bear.

And every time that happens, reality is being misread. We are attributing weight where Allah did not place it and denying exclusive weight where He did.

So tawhid is not only a doctrine to recite. It is a constant correction of what we think actually controls benefit and harm.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayahs expose two sides of the same disease.

The first asks:

Why worship what possesses no real power over harm and benefit?

The second asks:

Why let religion itself be carried beyond the truth by desire?

[PAUSE]

Together they teach that deviation is not always secular rebellion. Sometimes it is sacred excess. Sometimes the soul does not walk away from religion; it drags religion away from haqq by following what feels spiritually satisfying rather than what Allah actually said.

That is why the cure is so exact:

Return worship to the One who truly hears and knows.
Refuse ghuluww.
Refuse to let hawa dress itself as reverence.
Stay with the truth, even when the truth feels less dramatic than excess.

### Questions to Carry

1. Where in my religious life am I tempted to treat emotional intensity as proof of truth?
2. What "sacred" attachments in my life may be quietly receiving a weight that belongs only to Allah?
3. Have I ever confused excess with love and restraint with weakness?
4. What desires might be trying to shape my religion while pretending to defend it?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs teach that false worship and religious excess both grow from misplacing ultimate weight, and that devotion remains true only when it stays within the boundaries of haqq rather than following desire beyond them.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, return all worship in our hearts to You alone. Do not let us seek harm, benefit, refuge, or sacred centrality anywhere short of You. Protect us from ghuluww, from desire dressed as devotion, and from every excess that crosses the truth while sounding holy. Make us lovers of haqq more than lovers of intensity. Amin.
