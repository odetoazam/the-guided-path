# What Judgment Do They Seek?

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:49-50)

[ayah:5:49] وَأَنِ ٱحْكُم بَيْنَهُم بِمَآ أَنزَلَ ٱللَّهُ وَلَا تَتَّبِعْ أَهْوَآءَهُمْ وَٱحْذَرْهُمْ أَن يَفْتِنُوكَ عَنۢ بَعْضِ مَآ أَنزَلَ ٱللَّهُ إِلَيْكَ ۖ فَإِن تَوَلَّوْا۟ فَٱعْلَمْ أَنَّمَا يُرِيدُ ٱللَّهُ أَن يُصِيبَهُم بِبَعْضِ ذُنُوبِهِمْ ۗ وَإِنَّ كَثِيرًا مِّنَ ٱلنَّاسِ لَفَـٰسِقُونَ

[ayah:5:50] أَفَحُكْمَ ٱلْجَـٰهِلِيَّةِ يَبْغُونَ ۚ وَمَنْ أَحْسَنُ مِنَ ٱللَّهِ حُكْمًا لِّقَوْمٍ يُوقِنُونَ

*Wa ani uhkum baynahum bima anzala Allahu wa la tattabi' ahwa'ahum wahdharhum an yaftinuka 'an ba'di ma anzala Allahu ilayk. Fa-in tawallaw fa'lam annama yuridu Allahu an yusibahum biba'di dhunubihim. Wa inna kathiran mina al-nasi la-fasiqun.*

*Afahukma al-jahiliyyati yabghun. Wa man ahsanu mina Allahi hukman liqawmin yuqinun.*

**And judge between them by what Allah has sent down, and do not follow their desires. And beware of them, lest they tempt you away from some of what Allah has sent down to you. But if they turn away, then know that Allah only intends to afflict them for some of their sins. And indeed, many of the people are defiantly disobedient. Then is it the judgment of jahiliyyah they seek? And who is better than Allah in judgment for a people who are certain?**

## Introduction

Very few temptations arrive by asking us to abandon the truth completely.

Most come with a softer voice.

Keep the principle, they say, but ease the edge. Keep the book, but do not let this particular part rule. Keep the sacred language, but make one exception for pressure, politics, desire, relationship, timing, optics, or fear. Keep almost all of what Allah sent down. Just not this part. Just not now. Just not here.

That is why this passage is so penetrating.

Allah does not only tell the Prophet to judge by what He revealed. He warns him not to be lured away from **some** of what He revealed. That word matters. The fitnah being described here is not merely open disbelief. It is selective softening. It is the attempt to keep revelation formally honored while reducing its actual authority one portion at a time.

Then the next ayah explodes the whole illusion by asking: **Is it the judgment of jahiliyyah they seek?**

Suddenly the matter becomes clear. There is no clean middle ground between the judgment of Allah and the judgment of jahiliyyah. If revelation is pushed aside, even partially, what comes forward is not neutral wisdom. It is another order of value, another way of deciding, another loyalty wearing the clothes of reason or necessity.

So this passage is not about a remote legal disagreement alone. It is about one of the oldest spiritual crises in human life: the desire to keep Allah's truth near enough to claim it, but far enough that it does not fully govern us.

The hook of the passage is this: **jahiliyyah begins whenever desire asks revelation to move aside, even if only for "some" of what Allah sent down.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Uhkum ... wa la tattabi' ... wahdharhum**: The Passage Moves From Command to Warning Without Pause

Verse 49 opens in direct command:

**Wa ani uhkum baynahum bima anzala Allah** - judge between them by what Allah has sent down.

The criterion is already settled. Judgment is not to be improvised from pressure, bargaining, inherited custom, or the strategic mood of the moment. It is to come from what Allah sent down.

[PAUSE]

Then immediately:

**Wa la tattabi' ahwa'ahum** - and do not follow their desires.

That pairing teaches us something severe and useful. The opposite of revealed judgment is not simply "another opinion." In the Quran's moral map, the rival force is often **hawa** - desire, inclination, appetite, what the self wants reality to yield to. So once the ayah tells the Prophet to judge by revelation, it at once warns him against the gravitational pull that tries to replace revelation.

Then the pressure intensifies:

**Wahdharhum** - beware them.

This is not mild caution. It is alertness. Watchfulness. Do not move through this encounter naively. Do not imagine that everyone seeking judgment is actually seeking submission.

The ayah is teaching us that some invitations to compromise do not look like hostility at first. They look like negotiation, moderation, pragmatism, or communal management. But Allah names the danger before it fully unfolds.

### 2. **An yaftinuka 'an ba'di ma anzala Allahu ilayk**: The Greatest Fitnah May Be the Removal of Only "Some"

Then comes the most unsettling phrase in the passage:

**An yaftinuka 'an ba'di ma anzala Allahu ilayk** - lest they tempt you away from some of what Allah has sent down to you.

The verb from **fitnah** here is powerful. Fitnah is not always blunt persecution. It can be seduction, pressure, manipulation, testing through attraction or fear, the gradual turning of someone away from firmness.

[PAUSE]

And what are they trying to move him from?

Not necessarily all of revelation.

**Ba'di ma anzala Allah** - some of what Allah sent down.

This is one of the sharpest diagnoses in the surah. The most dangerous corruption often does not begin by asking us to tear up the whole covenant. It begins by asking for one clause to be loosened, one command to be delayed, one ruling to be made socially painless, one obligation to be made negotiable.

That is why this phrase should make us tremble. A person may still love the language of Islam, still identify with revelation, still defend the book publicly, and yet be quietly bargaining away "some" of what Allah sent down whenever that part becomes expensive.

[PAUSE]

And once "some" becomes negotiable, the authority has already shifted. Because the deeper issue was never the size of the compromised part. The deeper issue was: who gets to decide what may be compromised?

### 3. **Fa-in tawallaw ... biba'di dhunubihim**: Turning Away Is Itself a Sign of Affliction

Then the ayah says:

**Fa-in tawallaw fa'lam** - if they turn away, then know.

This is not the language of surprise. Allah is teaching the Prophet how to interpret refusal. If they still refuse after being called to revealed judgment, do not imagine that revelation lost the argument. Understand what is happening beneath the refusal.

Then comes the explanation:

**Annama yuridu Allahu an yusibahum biba'di dhunubihim** - Allah only intends to afflict them for some of their sins.

The wording is sobering. Their turn-away is not morally isolated. It belongs to a prior history of wrongdoing. And the consequence named here is not necessarily total destruction at once. It is that they are struck for **some** of their sins.

[PAUSE]

Notice the mirror inside the verse.

They try to pull the Messenger away from **some** of revelation.
Allah afflicts them for **some** of their sins.

The symmetry is devastating. A people who start treating divine guidance partially may find that punishment itself begins touching them partially, in ways they do not even recognize.

And perhaps one of the worst forms of affliction is exactly this: being left in the condition of turning away while still imagining oneself intelligent, justified, or prudent.

Then the verse lands with a broader judgment:

**Wa inna kathiran mina al-nasi la-fasiqun** - indeed, many of the people are defiantly disobedient.

**Fisq** is departure, stepping outside the proper bounds. So the ayah is not describing a rare historical oddity. It is describing a common human pattern. Many people do not want no religion at all. They want religion that has exited its authority at the points where it challenges them most.

### 4. **Afahukma al-jahiliyyati yabghun**: Once Revelation Is Refused, the Rival Judge Is Named

Then verse 50 asks one of the most exposing questions in the surah:

**Afahukma al-jahiliyyati yabghun** - is it the judgment of jahiliyyah they seek?

The word **yabghun** means they seek, pursue, desire. So the ayah is not only naming what they end up with. It is naming what they actually want.

[PAUSE]

And what do they want? **Hukm al-jahiliyyah**.

Jahiliyyah here is not merely a date on a historical timeline. It is a moral order built on ignorance of Allah's measure, an order in which desire, tribal preference, image, force, inherited custom, and self-interest become the practical judge.

That is why this verse is so severe. It refuses the flattering myth that if we do not judge by what Allah sent down, we must be operating from some wiser neutrality. No. The ayah says the rival regime already has a name.

Then the closing question:

**Wa man ahsanu mina Allahi hukman liqawmin yuqinun** - who is better than Allah in judgment for a people who are certain?

The issue is not only law. It is certainty. A people of **yaqin** do not merely acknowledge that Allah judges. They are settled in the fact that no judgment could be wiser, truer, cleaner, or more mercifully exact than His.

[PAUSE]

So when certainty is weak, the soul starts shopping. But when certainty becomes real, the question of whether something better than Allah's judgment exists stops feeling intellectually open. It becomes spiritually absurd.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Slipperiest Compromise Is the One That Asks for "Just This Part"

Most of us would recognize open rejection more quickly than selective trimming.

If someone said, "I do not want Allah's guidance at all," the ugliness would be easier to see. But when the request is more refined, more culturally respectable, more strategically phrased, we become vulnerable. Keep the deen, but lower this demand. Keep the principle, but suspend it in this case. Keep the ideal, but let reality rule here. Keep obedience, but not where it will cost status, intimacy, profit, belonging, or peace with the crowd.

[PAUSE]

That is why **ba'di ma anzala Allah** is such a frightening phrase. It exposes the entry point of compromise.

Many people do not lose revelation by hating all of it. They lose it by designating small zones of exemption where the self remains sovereign. And those zones rarely stay small. Once the soul becomes used to deciding which parts of Allah's word may or may not fully apply, the throne has already shifted from submission to management.

This reaches far beyond formal law. We do it in speech, in modesty, in money, in family obligations, in public courage, in private temptation, in truth-telling, in repentance, in what we excuse for ourselves, and in what we label "complicated" only because obedience hurts.

[PAUSE]

The passage calls us back before that erosion becomes normal. Beware the moment you start loving the Quran in total but negotiating with it in detail. That is often where the fitnah begins.

### Theme 2: Jahiliyyah Is a Recurring Spiritual Logic, Not Only a Historical Era

Verse 50 is one of the great protections against romanticizing our own age.

We hear **jahiliyyah** and imagine a distant world of tribes, idols, vendettas, and desert custom. But the ayah's force is larger than chronology. Jahiliyyah is any moral order in which Allah's judgment is displaced by another ruling authority at the deepest level.

Sometimes that authority is tribe.
Sometimes it is state.
Sometimes it is market.
Sometimes it is social approval.
Sometimes it is ideology.
Sometimes it is the wounded ego.
Sometimes it is simply appetite with a polished vocabulary.

[PAUSE]

That means jahiliyyah can exist in very modern rooms.

It can exist in boardrooms where truth is bent for advantage.
It can exist in homes where Allah's boundaries are softened to protect comfort.
It can exist in religious spaces where prestige decides more than revelation.
It can exist inside our own hearts when we already know what Allah wants, but keep searching for a ruling that will leave our desires intact.

The point is not to turn the word into a slogan against others. The point is to let the ayah expose the logic wherever it appears. Jahiliyyah is not first "those people out there." It is the recurring temptation to prefer any judge over Allah when His judgment becomes costly.

### Theme 3: Yaqin Makes Allah's Judgment Feel Beautiful, Not Merely Obligatory

The final words are easy to rush past: **liqawmin yuqinun** - for a people who are certain.

That ending matters because it tells us why some hearts keep looking elsewhere. The problem is not always lack of exposure to evidence. Sometimes it is that certainty has not sunk deeply enough to make Allah's judgment feel like the obvious refuge of the soul.

[PAUSE]

When yaqin is alive, the heart knows several things at once.

It knows Allah knows reality better than we do.
It knows Allah is freer of self-interest than every human system.
It knows Allah sees what we cannot see.
It knows Allah's commands are not random intrusions but merciful placement.
It knows that surrendering to His judgment is not humiliation but safety.

So the ayah does not end by saying, "Who is better than Allah in judgment for clever people, informed people, strategic people, or powerful people?" It ends with certainty. Because once certainty settles, the question is answered from within before it is argued from without.

[PAUSE]

And perhaps this is one of the real tasks of tadabbur itself: not merely to prove that Allah's judgment is right, but to soften the heart until that rightness becomes something we can rest in.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture

The passage is built with remarkable sharpness.

It begins with **judgment**: judge by what Allah has sent down.
Then it names the rival force: do not follow desires.
Then it raises the alarm: beware them.
Then it exposes the method of corruption: being turned away from **some** of revelation.
Then it answers that "some" with another "some": Allah may afflict them for **some** of their sins.
Finally, it names the rival system in full: **hukm al-jahiliyyah**.

[PAUSE]

So the movement of the ayah is not random. It is diagnostic.

Compromise with revelation begins in partial concessions.
Partial concessions reveal a deeper authority shift.
That authority shift is not neutral.
It is jahiliyyah.
And the cure is not only legal compliance, but certainty that no judgment is better than Allah's.

### Questions to Carry

1. What "some" of Allah's guidance am I most tempted to treat as negotiable when it becomes costly?
2. Where in my life do I call something prudence, realism, or moderation when it is really hawa asking for authority?
3. What would it look like for my certainty in Allah's wisdom to become so deep that His judgment feels like refuge rather than restriction?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs teach that the road back to jahiliyyah begins the moment desire is allowed to bargain with even part of revelation, while certainty sees that no judgment could ever be better than Allah's.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, make us people who judge by what You sent down and who do not follow desire against Your truth. Protect us from the fitnah of partial compromise and from the blindness that calls jahiliyyah wisdom. Give us certainty that makes Your judgment beloved to our hearts, and do not let us be turned away from any part of what You revealed. Ameen.
