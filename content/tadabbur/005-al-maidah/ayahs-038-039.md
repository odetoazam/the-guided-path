# After Wronging, Then Mending

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:38-39)

[ayah:5:38] وَٱلسَّارِقُ وَٱلسَّارِقَةُ فَٱقْطَعُوٓا۟ أَيْدِيَهُمَا جَزَآءًۢ بِمَا كَسَبَا نَكَـٰلًا مِّنَ ٱللَّهِ ۗ وَٱللَّهُ عَزِيزٌ حَكِيمٌ

[ayah:5:39] فَمَن تَابَ مِنۢ بَعْدِ ظُلْمِهِۦ وَأَصْلَحَ فَإِنَّ ٱللَّهَ يَتُوبُ عَلَيْهِ ۗ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ غَفُورٌ رَّحِيمٌ

*Wa al-sariqu wa al-sariqatu faqta'u aydiyahuma jaza'an bima kasaba nakalan mina Allah. Wa Allahu 'azizun hakim.*

*Faman taba min ba'di zulmihi wa aslaha fa inna Allaha yatubu 'alayh. Inna Allaha ghafurun rahim.*

**As for the male thief and the female thief, cut off their hands as recompense for what they earned, as a deterrent from Allah. And Allah is Mighty, Wise. But whoever repents after his wrongdoing and makes amends, then indeed Allah turns to him in forgiveness. Indeed, Allah is Forgiving, Merciful.**

## Introduction

These two ayat again force us to hold together what the human ego prefers to split apart.

One ayah speaks with sharp public consequence. Theft is not treated as a clever survival tactic, a private lapse, or a morally weightless taking. It is named as wrongful earning, and its consequence is framed as recompense and deterrence from Allah. Then, immediately after, the surah turns to the person beyond the sentence and says: whoever repents after his wrongdoing and makes amends, Allah turns to him.

That pairing matters enormously. The Qur'an does not trivialize the violation of trust that theft introduces into communal life. But it also refuses to say that once a person has stolen, the story of his soul is over. Legal consequence and spiritual restoration are not the same question, and the surah teaches us not to confuse them.

There is also something striking in the wording of the second ayah. It does not say only "whoever repents." It says **repents after his wrongdoing and makes amends**. Tawbah here is not sentimental regret floating above reality. It faces the ظلم that was done and then turns toward إصلاح, toward setting right, repairing, mending.

So the hook of the pair is this: **Allah protects the moral order of the community with real consequence, yet still leaves the wrongdoer a road back through repentance and repair.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Al-sariqu wa al-sariqah**: The Ayah Names the Violator by the Act That Has Taken Shape

The verse begins by naming two forms of the same role:

**Al-sariqu wa al-sariqah** - the male thief and the female thief.

The language is direct. It identifies the person through the form of wrongdoing that has become manifest. This is not to reduce the entire human being to a single act forever; the next ayah will protect us from that mistake. But at the point of public judgment, the crime must be named clearly.

[PAUSE]

That clarity itself is a mercy to the community. Moral order cannot be preserved where violations are endlessly blurred, renamed, softened, or hidden under clever rhetoric. The Qur'an begins by speaking plainly.

### 2. **Jaza'an bima kasaba**: The Punishment Is Framed as Earned Recompense

Then comes the reasoning:

**Jaza'an bima kasaba** - as recompense for what they earned.

Again the Qur'an speaks in the language of measure. **Jaza'** is recompense, response fitting what was done. And **kasaba** is what they acquired, earned, gained through their own act.

[PAUSE]

This is morally important because theft itself is a distorted form of acquisition. The thief wants gain detached from right. So the verse answers that wrongful gain with recompense tied explicitly to what was earned.

The Qur'an is not describing random severity. It is showing that a hand used to violate trust in pursuit of illicit gain cannot be treated as though nothing morally weighty happened in the social body.

### 2b. The Conditions That Bound the Ruling — Why This Punishment Was Rare in Classical Practice

Before continuing, this is the second ayah in a row where the Qur'an names a severe public consequence — and the second in a row where the classical juridical framework needs to be visible, because the absence of that framework is what enables the ruling's misuse.

The four classical schools place an extensive set of conditions on the hand-amputation punishment that, taken together, made its application historically rare:

- **Niṣāb threshold:** The stolen item must reach a minimum value (the schools differ on the exact figure — roughly equivalent to 3 dirhams of silver in the Mālikī and Shāfiʿī schools, 10 dirhams in the Ḥanafī school). Petty theft is below the threshold and the hadd does not apply.
- **Ḥirz (secure custody):** The item must be taken from a place where it was reasonably protected — a locked house, a closed shop, a guarded enclosure. Items left in the open street, or in a place the thief had legitimate access to, do not trigger the hadd.
- **Full legal capacity:** The thief must be an adult, sane, not coerced, not under duress of starvation. A child, a person of diminished capacity, or someone driven by genuine necessity is excluded.
- **Two reliable witnesses or unforced confession:** Most schools require either two adult, just witnesses to the act, or a free, unforced confession. Circumstantial evidence is typically insufficient.
- **The "doubt averts the ḥudūd" principle:** Based on the Prophet's ﷺ instruction *"Idraʾū al-ḥudūda bi'l-shubuhāt"* — "Avert the prescribed punishments in case of doubt" — *any* reasonable ambiguity suspends the sentence. The ḥadd of theft is the most ḥadd-restrictive ḥadd in the Qur'an precisely because doubt-avoidance was applied to it most aggressively.
- **Suspension in famine and necessity:** The most cited precedent is ʿUmar ibn al-Khattāb's suspension of the amputation ḥadd during the year of famine (*ʿĀm al-Ramādah*), reasoning that a person stealing to eat in a society that has failed to feed him cannot meet the conditions of culpable theft. The principle generalizes: where the social conditions that the ruling presupposes (provision, opportunity, justice) have collapsed, the ruling is suspended.

The combined effect of these conditions, applied honestly, is that the hadd of theft was rarely carried out in classical Islamic societies. The ayah is the upper-bound deterrent for a fully proven, fully voluntary, fully non-necessitous violation of secured property. It was never read by the four schools as a routine punishment to be applied liberally.

This matters for the contemporary reader because regimes that publicize amputations as a marker of "Islamic justice" are almost always violating the conditions the classical jurists placed on this ayah — applying it without the niṣāb check, without rigorous evidentiary standards, without the doubt-averting principle, and most of all without first ensuring that the social conditions of provision and dignity are in place.

### 3. **Nakalan mina Allah**: Deterrence Is Part of Mercy

Then the verse says:

**Nakalan mina Allah** - as a deterrent from Allah.

**Nakal** is not merely pain. It is a consequence meant to warn, restrain, and prevent repetition. This means the punishment is not only backward-looking. It is also forward-looking. It protects the shared life of the community from becoming a place where property, vulnerability, and trust are casually violated.

[PAUSE]

That is why the verse ends:

**Wa Allahu 'azizun hakim** - and Allah is Mighty, Wise.

The pairing of these divine names matters. **'Aziz** tells us the law is not powerless, not weak before human manipulation. **Hakim** tells us that this severity is not arbitrary force. It is wise. The verse closes by teaching us how to read the ruling: with seriousness, but not with suspicion toward Allah's justice.

### 4. **Faman taba min ba'di zulmihi wa aslaha**: The Wrongdoer Is Not Abandoned if He Turns and Repairs

Then the next ayah makes a turn of immense mercy:

**Faman taba min ba'di zulmihi wa aslaha** - but whoever repents after his wrongdoing and makes amends.

The phrase **min ba'di zulmihi** is searching. It insists that tawbah not float above the name of the act. He repents after **his wrongdoing**. The ظلم is not denied, blurred, or dissolved in vague spirituality.

[PAUSE]

Then comes **wa aslaha** - and he makes amends, repairs, sets right.

This is one of the most important words in the passage. The opposite of corruption is not merely feeling bad. It is إصلاح, mending. What was bent must be straightened where possible. What was broken must be repaired where possible. What was violated must not simply be regretted inwardly; the person must turn toward restoration.

Then the promise:

**Fa inna Allaha yatubu 'alayh** - indeed Allah turns to him.

This is beautiful. The servant turns in tawbah, and Allah turns to him in mercy. Divine acceptance is not barred by the existence of past theft if there is real return and repair.

Then the ayah ends, again, with:

**Inna Allaha ghafurun rahim** - indeed Allah is Forgiving, Merciful.

The one who protected the community in the first verse is the same Lord who receives the repentant in the second.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Trust Is Part of the Moral Wealth of a Community

Theft is often discussed as loss of objects, but the Qur'an pushes us to see something wider.

When theft becomes normalized, what is lost is not only property. Trust erodes. Vulnerability increases. Fear enters ordinary transactions. Shared life becomes harder. The weak become easier prey. The community starts guarding itself against one another instead of living inside a field of relative safety.

[PAUSE]

That is why the verse's severity must be read at the scale of communal trust, not merely at the scale of an item taken. Moral order includes the invisible atmosphere by which people can live without constant violation. Protecting that atmosphere is a mercy.

### Theme 2: Tawbah Is Not Real Until It Wants Repair

The second ayah is a gift to the sinner because it tells him that his future with Allah is not closed. But it is also demanding because it defines the shape of real return.

Repentance comes **after his wrongdoing**, and it is joined with **islah**.

[PAUSE]

This means the heart cannot say, "I regret it," while quietly loving the gain it stole and resisting the hard work of repair. The Qur'an is teaching that spiritual restoration is not divorced from moral repair. If the act bent the world, tawbah reaches toward straightening what can still be straightened.

That is a merciful teaching, because it saves repentance from unreality. It turns tawbah from a mood into a truthful return.

### Theme 3: Allah Does Not Force Us to Choose Between Order and Mercy

Once more, the surah refuses a false choice.

Some people hear verses of legal severity and lose sight of Allah's mercy. Others hear verses of repentance and lose sight of moral order. These ayat insist that both remain visible at once.

[PAUSE]

The community is protected. The ruling stands. The wrong is named. The deterrent is real. And still the sinner is told: if you repent after your wrongdoing and make amends, Allah turns to you.

This is not contradiction. It is divine wholeness. Allah's law is wise enough to guard the shared life of people, and Allah's mercy is vast enough to receive the servant who truly returns.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayat show us both the seriousness of violating trust and the mercy that still waits after wrongdoing.

The first names the thief plainly, frames the ruling as recompense for what was earned, calls it a deterrent from Allah, and closes with Allah's might and wisdom. The second takes us beyond the sentence into the inner future of the wrongdoer: whoever repents after his wrongdoing and makes amends, Allah turns to him, for He is Forgiving and Merciful.

So the structure is exact. Wrong is named. Order is protected. The hand that violated trust is not treated lightly. But the soul is not declared unreachable. The one who stole is not locked forever inside the identity of theft if he turns, repairs, and returns to Allah.

[PAUSE]

Ask yourself:

- Do I understand how much communal trust depends on people not normalizing small wrongs of taking?
- When I repent, do I only feel regret, or do I also seek repair?
- Where have I been tempted to choose either moral seriousness or mercy, when Allah's ayah holds both together?
- What would honest islah look like in the places where I have benefited from what was not rightly mine?

The distillation of the pair is this: **Allah protects the shared trust of the community with real consequence, yet still opens the way back for the wrongdoer who repents after his ظلم and turns toward repair.**

O Allah, protect us from violating what You have made sacred between people, teach us to honor the trust woven into communal life, grant us truthful tawbah after every ظلم, and help us make real repair where we have broken what was not ours to break.
