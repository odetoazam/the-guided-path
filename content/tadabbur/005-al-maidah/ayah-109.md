# We Have No Knowledge

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:109)

[ayah:5:109] ۞ يَوْمَ يَجْمَعُ ٱللَّهُ ٱلرُّسُلَ فَيَقُولُ مَاذَآ أُجِبْتُمْ ۖ قَالُوا۟ لَا عِلْمَ لَنَآ ۖ إِنَّكَ أَنتَ عَلَّـٰمُ ٱلْغُيُوبِ

*Yawma yajma'u Allahu al-rusula fa-yaqulu madha ujibtum. Qalu la 'ilma lana. Innaka anta 'allamu al-ghuyub.*

**The Day Allah will gather the messengers and say, "What response were you given?" They will say, "We have no knowledge. Indeed, You are the Knower of the unseen."**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

There are moments in the Quran when scale changes so suddenly that the soul has to catch its breath.

You are in the world of procedure, testimony, oaths, and moral warning. Then all at once the horizon tears open: **the Day Allah gathers the messengers**.

Not a village. Not one nation. Not one disputed case. All the messengers. The entire prophetic history of humanity is brought into one assembly before Allah. And then comes a question so immense, and so stripped down, that it reaches through every age at once: **What response were you given?**

This is not a question about how eloquently they delivered. It is not a question about how much they suffered. It is not even phrased first as, "What did you say?" It is about what came back from humanity after revelation reached them.

And yet the answer of the messengers is astonishing. They do not begin listing nations, reactions, injuries, betrayals, followers, enemies, numbers, or outcomes. They say: **la 'ilma lana**. We have no knowledge.

The hook of the ayah is this: **on the Day when every mission is gathered before Allah, even the messengers refuse to claim mastery over the hidden reality of human response and return all unseen knowledge to Allah alone.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Yawma yajma'u Allahu al-rusula**: The Scene Begins With a Total Gathering

The ayah opens with a ظرف that throws us directly into the Day itself:

**yawma**.

The Day.

No explanation first. No cushioning. Just the arrival of time at its decisive form.

Then:

**yajma'u Allahu al-rusula**.

Allah gathers the messengers.

The root **j-m-'** is gathering, collecting, bringing dispersed things into one place. And here what is gathered is not ordinary humanity first, but the messengers themselves. The carriers of revelation across generations are assembled before the One who sent them.

[PAUSE]

That opening creates an overwhelming moral atmosphere. Every mission that unfolded separately in history is now viewed from above, together, in one divine gathering. Nuh, Ibrahim, Musa, Isa, Muhammad, and the many others named and unnamed. Their peoples were scattered by century, language, geography, and culture. But on that Day, all dispersion collapses. Revelation's entire human story is recollected before Allah.

That alone should humble us. We often live inside our own age as though it were self-contained, as though our debates and responses were isolated events. This ayah reminds us that every human answer to divine truth is part of one long history that will be gathered back to its source.

### 2. **Fa-yaqulu madha ujibtum**: The Question Is About Response

Then comes the question:

**madha ujibtum**.

What response were you given?

The verb is striking. It is passive: **ujibtum**. You were answered. You were responded to.

[PAUSE]

That matters. The question is not framed first around the messengers' performance, but around the reception their message met in the world. What came back toward you when you brought Allah's word? Acceptance? Rejection? mockery? partial obedience? distortion? violence? tears? indifference?

The passive form also creates a sobering shift in perspective. It reminds us that revelation is relational. A message from Allah enters history and demands an answer. No one is neutral forever. Humanity answers prophets, whether through faith, resistance, manipulation, delay, hostility, or surrender.

And notice how compressed the question is. No details. No names. No speeches. Just: what answer met you?

That compression is powerful because it reduces entire civilizational dramas to their essence. In the end, so much of human history can be described as the answer given to revelation.

### 3. **Qalu la 'ilma lana**: The Answer of the Messengers Is Humility, Not Self-Assertion

Then comes the reply:

**qalu la 'ilma lana**.

They say: we have no knowledge.

This is one of those Quranic moments that should stop us completely.

How do the messengers say they have no knowledge? Did they not know who believed and who rejected? Did they not witness responses, hostility, support, betrayal, and partial obedience?

The scholars explain this in different but related ways: compared to Allah's total knowledge, their knowledge is as nothing; they do not claim the inward truth of hearts or the final unseen reality of what people became; they refuse to speak from self-assurance in a scene where only Allah's encompassing knowledge is fitting.

[PAUSE]

And spiritually, that is the point. The greatest humans entrusted with revelation do not step into that Day boasting expertise over souls. They do not seize the register of hidden outcomes. They do not speak as owners of the final account.

They answer with adab.

They answer with scale.

They answer by knowing what belongs to them and what belongs only to Allah.

This is devastating for the ego, especially the religious ego. We love to imagine that if we had clearer vision, more learning, more authority, more proximity to sacred truth, we would be in a position to pronounce decisively on the inner reality of people and the meaning of outcomes. The messengers themselves refuse that posture on the Day of Gathering.

### 4. **Innaka anta 'allamu al-ghuyub**: Hidden Things Belong to the One Who Knows Them Intensively

Then the ayah names why:

**innaka anta 'allamu al-ghuyub**.

Indeed, You are the Knower of the unseen things.

The word **'allam** is not just **'alim**. It is intensified. Formed to indicate immense, comprehensive, repeated, all-encompassing knowledge. Not one unseen matter here and there, but the whole architecture of what lies beyond creaturely reach.

[PAUSE]

And **al-ghuyub** is plural. Not one hidden thing, but the unseen in its many layers: motives, sincerity, hypocrisy, fear, what a person understood, what they resisted, what they nearly accepted, what they concealed, what they became after the outward moment, the final truth of what every response meant before Allah.

So the answer of the messengers is not ignorance in the crude sense. It is recognition of proportion. They know what they witnessed. Allah knows what was hidden within it, behind it, beneath it, and after it.

That is why the ayah is so cleansing. It returns the deepest interpretive ownership of history to Allah. Human beings act. Prophets deliver. Communities respond. But the full unseen meaning of those responses belongs to the One who is **'allam al-ghuyub**.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Ayah Dismantles the Fantasy of Controlling Outcomes

Many of us live under a quiet religious fantasy.

We imagine that if we do the right work, speak the truth clearly enough, call people properly enough, parent carefully enough, teach wisely enough, lead faithfully enough, or struggle hard enough, then outcomes should eventually become readable and manageable. We want not only obedience, but interpretive control over what our obedience produces.

This ayah strikes that desire at the root.

[PAUSE]

The messengers are asked about response, yet they do not clutch at outcomes. They do not say, "Here is the full ledger. Here is what every response meant. Here is who was sincere, who was merely conforming, who will ultimately be vindicated, who was lost beneath the surface." They know the limits of entrusted responsibility.

That is a deep mercy for anyone carrying any form of da'wah, teaching, parenting, counseling, or moral labor. You are responsible to deliver truth faithfully. You are not Lord over what every soul finally did with it in the unseen chambers of its heart.

That does not reduce responsibility. It purifies it. It means we work seriously without worshipping outcome-ownership. We grieve, hope, invite, warn, and persist. But we do not pretend that the final inner account has been handed to us.

### Theme 2: Real Nearness to Revelation Produces Humility, Not Grandiosity

There is another disease this ayah treats.

Sometimes religious knowledge makes the self bigger instead of smaller. A person learns enough sacred language to become certain that he sees through everyone. He grows confident in narrating who is sincere, who is doomed, what this movement means, what that failure proves, what Allah must be doing inside history. He becomes the kind of person for whom the unseen begins to feel almost administratively accessible.

[PAUSE]

This ayah shatters that delusion by showing us the messengers themselves in the highest possible gathering. Their answer is humility. Their answer is restraint. Their answer is to name Allah as the one who knows hidden things exhaustively.

That means genuine proximity to revelation should make us more careful with claims, not less. More reverent before hidden realities, not more casual. More alert to the difference between what we witnessed and what only Allah knows.

If prophetic inheritance lives anywhere in us, one of its signs should be this adab with the unseen.

### Theme 3: Human History Is Ultimately the Story of Answers Given to Revelation

The question **madha ujibtum** also reorganizes how we see the world.

We often narrate history through power, economics, war, culture, technology, memory, identity, and trauma. All of these matter. But this ayah peels down to a more ultimate description: what answer was given to Allah's messengers?

[PAUSE]

This does not erase worldly complexity. It reveals what sits beneath it. At the deepest level, civilizations are not only producers and consumers, rulers and ruled, victors and defeated. They are answerers. They answer truth. They answer warning. They answer mercy. They answer command. They answer remembrance.

And so do we, personally. Every time revelation reaches us, there is an answer. It may be obedience. It may be postponement. It may be selective agreement. It may be tears. It may be argument. It may be performance without surrender. It may be love. The ayah teaches us to look at our lives through that terrifying simplicity: what answer am I giving?

## Closing Synthesis

This ayah gathers the whole prophetic story into one scene and then hands us one of the most humbling answers in the Quran.

Allah gathers the messengers.
He asks what response they received.
They do not seize interpretive ownership of souls.
They say they have no knowledge.
They return the unseen entirely to the One who knows it all.

[PAUSE]

That is the architecture of the verse: gathering, question, humility, divine omniscience.

And it leaves us with a purified way of standing in religion. Deliver truth seriously. Answer revelation honestly. Care about response deeply. But never confuse witness with omniscience, mission with mastery, or nearness to sacred truth with possession of the unseen.

### Questions to Carry

1. Where in my life have I started acting as though I own the final meaning of outcomes rather than serving faithfully within my limited responsibility?
2. Do I answer revelation with obedience, delay, defensiveness, performance, or surrender?
3. Has religious knowledge made me more humble before hidden realities or more confident in claiming what only Allah knows?
4. If my life were reduced to one question before Allah, what answer would it reveal I gave to His guidance?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**This ayah teaches that even the messengers refuse to claim mastery over the hidden truth of human response, returning all unseen knowledge of outcomes, hearts, and final meanings to Allah alone, the Knower of every unseen thing.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, make us truthful in the answers we give Your revelation, protect us from the arrogance of claiming what belongs to Your unseen knowledge alone, and let sacred knowledge make us humbler, more careful, and more surrendered before You. Amin.
