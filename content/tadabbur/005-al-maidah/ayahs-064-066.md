# His Hands Are Outstretched

## Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:64-66)

[ayah:5:64] وَقَالَتِ ٱلْيَهُودُ يَدُ ٱللَّهِ مَغْلُولَةٌ ۚ غُلَّتْ أَيْدِيهِمْ وَلُعِنُوا۟ بِمَا قَالُوا۟ ۘ بَلْ يَدَاهُ مَبْسُوطَتَانِ يُنفِقُ كَيْفَ يَشَآءُ ۚ وَلَيَزِيدَنَّ كَثِيرًا مِّنْهُم مَّآ أُنزِلَ إِلَيْكَ مِن رَّبِّكَ طُغْيَـٰنًا وَكُفْرًا ۚ وَأَلْقَيْنَا بَيْنَهُمُ ٱلْعَدَٰوَةَ وَٱلْبَغْضَآءَ إِلَىٰ يَوْمِ ٱلْقِيَـٰمَةِ ۚ كُلَّمَآ أَوْقَدُوا۟ نَارًا لِّلْحَرْبِ أَطْفَأَهَا ٱللَّهُ ۚ وَيَسْعَوْنَ فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ فَسَادًا ۚ وَٱللَّهُ لَا يُحِبُّ ٱلْمُفْسِدِينَ

[ayah:5:65] وَلَوْ أَنَّ أَهْلَ ٱلْكِتَـٰبِ ءَامَنُوا۟ وَٱتَّقَوْا۟ لَكَفَّرْنَا عَنْهُمْ سَيِّـَٔاتِهِمْ وَلَأَدْخَلْنَـٰهُمْ جَنَّـٰتِ ٱلنَّعِيمِ

[ayah:5:66] وَلَوْ أَنَّهُمْ أَقَامُوا۟ ٱلتَّوْرَىٰةَ وَٱلْإِنجِيلَ وَمَآ أُنزِلَ إِلَيْهِم مِّن رَّبِّهِمْ لَأَكَلُوا۟ مِن فَوْقِهِمْ وَمِن تَحْتِ أَرْجُلِهِم ۚ مِّنْهُمْ أُمَّةٌ مُّقْتَصِدَةٌ ۖ وَكَثِيرٌ مِّنْهُمْ سَآءَ مَا يَعْمَلُونَ

## Introduction

Sometimes the deepest corruption in a person's life is not first visible in his actions, but in what he has come to believe about Allah.

If he imagines Allah as withholding, constricted, ungenerous, reluctant to give, then that image quietly reshapes everything else. His worship hardens. His trust thins. His relationship to provision becomes anxious and grasping. Revelation itself begins to feel less like mercy and more like threat. And once the heart settles into that estimate of Allah, the moral life that follows is almost guaranteed to warp around it.

That is why these ayahs matter so much.

The passage has just exposed hidden kufr, public sin, unlawful gain, and the silence of leaders who should have restrained it. Now the surah goes even deeper and names a poisoned theological sentence: **The hand of Allah is chained.** It is a statement of divine constriction, a claim that Allah's giving is withheld, limited, or bound.

[PAUSE]

And the ayah responds with force. It does not merely reject the statement. It inverts it, answers it with the language of expansiveness, then shows what happens when revelation lands on hearts already committed to rebellion: for some it only increases transgression and disbelief. Yet even after all of that, the next two ayahs open a breathtaking door. If only they believed. If only they had taqwa. If only they upheld what had been given to them. Then sins would be covered, gardens opened, and earthly provision would pour from every direction.

So this passage is severe, but it is not closed. It diagnoses a twisted estimate of Allah, exposes its social fruits, and then reopens the possibility of healing through faith, taqwa, and actually living by revelation.

The hook of the passage is this: **when the heart thinks Allah is constricted, revelation feels heavy; when the heart finally knows His vastness, revelation becomes the road into abundance.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Yadu Allahi maghlulah ... bal yadahu mabsutatani**: The Passage Moves from Constriction to Overflow

The opening claim is ugly in its implication:

**Yadu Allahi maghlulah** - the hand of Allah is chained, tied up, constricted.

Without pushing beyond what the ayah itself states, we can say this much confidently: the claim attributes withholding and constriction to Allah's giving. It is a statement of divine stinginess or restraint in the moral imagination of the speaker.

[PAUSE]

Then comes one of the sharpest reversals in the surah:

**Ghullat aydihim** - chained are their hands.

The constriction they projected onto Allah is thrown back onto them. And then:

**wa lu'inu bima qalu** - and they were cursed for what they said.

The problem is not a harmless theological misphrasing. It is a moral-spiritual distortion serious enough to deserve repudiation.

Then the ayah opens wide:

**Bal yadahu mabsutatani** - rather, both His hands are outstretched.

Again, staying inside the ayah's own language, the contrast is unmistakable: not constricted, but expanded; not withheld, but open; not reluctant, but giving.

And the next phrase seals the meaning:

**yunfiqu kayfa yasha'** - He spends as He wills.

His giving is not under compulsion, scarcity, or external pressure. It flows from sovereign will. This is one of the most healing descriptions in the passage because it re-educates the heart about the Lord it is dealing with.

### 2. **Wa la-yazidanna ... tughyanan wa kufra**: Revelation Does Not Soften Every Heart; It Exposes the Direction Already Chosen

Then the ayah gives a sobering consequence:

**Wa la-yazidanna kathiran minhum ma unzila ilayka min rabbika tughyanan wa kufra**.

What has been sent down to you from your Lord will surely increase many of them in transgression and disbelief.

[PAUSE]

This is deeply revealing. Revelation is light, mercy, and guidance. But if a heart has already made rebellion its home, even more revelation can become fuel for further resistance. The problem is not in the revelation. The problem is in the posture of reception.

That is why the phrase **min rabbika** matters - from your Lord. What comes from the Lord should soften, guide, and restore. But when it lands on a self committed to denial, the self does not stay neutral. It hardens.

Then the social fruits return:

**wa alqayna baynahumu al-'adawata wa al-baghda'a** - We cast among them animosity and hatred.

The inward distortion about Allah does not remain abstract. It becomes a fractured social world. Hearts that misread the Giver begin tearing each other apart as well.

### 3. **Kullama awqadu naran lil-harb atfa'aha Allah**: Human Projects of Conflict Never Escape Divine Overrule

Then comes a vivid image:

**Kullama awqadu naran lil-harb atfa'aha Allah** - every time they kindle a fire for war, Allah extinguishes it.

This is one of those Quranic pictures that feels physical even before it becomes moral. A fire is lit. Heat rises. Intent gathers. Conflict is prepared. And then Allah puts it out.

[PAUSE]

The point is not that corruption lacks energy. It has plenty of energy. The point is that it does not own the outcome. Human beings can scheme, inflame, mobilize, and spread tension, but they still operate beneath divine mastery.

Then the ayah says:

**wa yas'awna fi al-ardi fasada** - and they strive in the earth spreading corruption.

Notice the contrast. They **strive**. They are active. Intentional. Restless. But the striving is toward **fasad**, not islah. Their motion is not creative healing; it is destructive disorder.

And then the verdict:

**wa Allahu la yuhibbu al-mufsidin** - Allah does not love the corrupters.

This brings us back to the heart of the section. What a tragedy: the earlier verse 5:54 gave us people whom Allah loves and who love Him. Here we meet a different profile entirely: people who spread fasad and are explicitly outside divine love in that state.

### 4. **Wa law anna ... amanu wa ittaqaw ... aqamu**: The Door Is Still Open, but It Requires More Than Identity

Then, unexpectedly, the passage opens.

**Wa law anna ahla al-kitabi amanu wa ittaqaw** - if only the People of the Book had believed and had taqwa.

Even after everything named above, the Quran does not close the door. It shows what could still happen:

**la-kaffarna 'anhum sayyi'atihim** - We would surely cover their misdeeds,

and

**wa la-adkhalnahum jannati al-na'im** - We would surely admit them into gardens of bliss.

[PAUSE]

Then verse 66 goes further:

**Wa law annahum aqamu al-tawrata wa al-injila wa ma unzila ilayhim min rabbihim** - if only they had upheld the Torah, the Gospel, and what was sent down to them from their Lord.

The word **aqamu** matters. Revelation is not merely to be possessed, cited, or honored ceremonially. It is to be established, upheld, made standing in life.

And if they had done that?

**La-akalu min fawqihim wa min tahti arjulihim** - they would have eaten from above them and from beneath their feet.

The image is abundance from every direction. Provision no longer pinched, but opened around them.

This answers the false claim of verse 64 with poetic justice. They spoke as though Allah's giving were bound. The Quran replies: if only you had truly lived by what He revealed, you would have been surrounded by His provision.

Then comes a final note of justice:

**minhum ummatun muqtasidah** - among them is a moderate, balanced community.

The surah refuses flattening. Not all are the same. Yet it also closes honestly:

**wa kathirun minhum sa'a ma ya'malun** - many of them, evil is what they do.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Many Moral Disorders Begin with a False Estimate of Allah

We often treat theology as though it lives far away from psychology and daily life. These ayahs do the opposite. They show that what a person believes about Allah's generosity, vastness, and way of giving will eventually appear in his emotions, ethics, and society.

[PAUSE]

If I think Allah is constricted, I become grasping.
If I think His provision is tight, I become anxious and suspicious.
If I think obedience only takes and never opens, revelation starts feeling like burden rather than mercy.

That is why correcting our image of Allah is not an abstract luxury. It is survival for the heart. So many forms of rebellion are, underneath themselves, arguments with Allah's generosity.

And the ayah answers that argument forcefully: **rather, both His hands are outstretched; He spends however He wills.**

The believer lives differently once that reality is trusted. Worship stops feeling like deprivation. Obedience stops feeling like exclusion from life. The heart learns that the One who commands is not trying to starve it, but to lead it into the right form of abundance.

### Theme 2: Revelation Magnifies the Direction the Heart Has Already Chosen

There is also a painful truth here: not everyone becomes softer when more truth arrives.

For some people, another verse is another chance.
For others, another verse is another occasion to harden.

[PAUSE]

That should make us humble every time we approach the Quran. The issue is not just how much revelation we have encountered. The issue is what sort of self is receiving it.

Have we come to be changed?
Or have we come to defend what we already want?

This ayah teaches that revelation is never inert. It exposes. It either heals receptivity or intensifies resistance. That is why we need du'a before knowledge, taqwa beneath study, and fear of becoming people who can gather more ayahs while drifting further into stubbornness.

### Theme 3: The Path to Provision Is Not Cynicism About Allah, but Establishing What He Revealed

Verse 66 is one of the passage's gentlest surprises.

After the severity of curse, hatred, conflict, and corruption, the Quran suddenly paints abundance:

from above them,
from beneath their feet.

[PAUSE]

This is not random prosperity language. It is a theological correction.

Those who think Allah is withholding are told that real abundance was sitting on the other side of faithful establishment all along. The Torah, the Gospel, and what was revealed from their Lord were not barriers to flourishing. They were the road to it.

That lesson reaches us too. Whenever we imagine that full obedience will make life unbearably narrow, these ayahs answer: perhaps what is narrowing life is not obedience, but the refusal to establish revelation deeply enough for its blessings to ripen.

Not every obedient life becomes wealthy in the simplistic sense. The Quran is not selling a crude transaction. But it is teaching that revelation and divine generosity are not enemies. The constricted heart imagines them that way. The healed heart learns otherwise.

## Closing Synthesis

The passage begins with a constricted sentence about Allah and ends with provision opening from every direction.

That is not accidental.

[PAUSE]

First the surah exposes a theology of divine withholding.
Then it answers with divine expansiveness.
Then it shows how revelation intensifies whatever posture the heart already chose.
Then it names the social consequences of rebellion: hatred, conflict, corruption.
And finally it opens the door back out: faith, taqwa, establishing revelation, forgiven sins, gardens, and abundance.

So these ayahs are teaching us how much depends on the heart's estimate of Allah. If we project constriction onto Him, we will read His revelation as deprivation and turn further into rebellion. But if we learn His vastness, then revelation stops looking like a cage and begins to look like the architecture of real life.

### Questions to Carry

1. In what ways have I quietly imagined Allah as more withholding than generous?
2. Does revelation soften me, or do I sometimes feel it making me more defensive and resistant?
3. Where in my life have I mistaken obedience for constriction rather than a road into opened provision?
4. What would it mean for me to truly "establish" what Allah revealed rather than merely admire it from a distance?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs teach that a constricted estimate of Allah breeds rebellion and corruption, while faith, taqwa, and truly establishing revelation reopen the heart to forgiveness, gardens, and provision from every direction.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, do not let us think of You with constricted thoughts. Expand our hearts with the certainty that Your giving is vast and wise. Make Your revelation a healing that softens us, not a proof we resist. Save us from corruption in the earth and from every false image of You that starves trust. And help us establish what You revealed so that we meet both Your forgiveness and the openings You know are best for us. Amin.
