# Work in the Open, But Know You Return Beyond Every Audience

## Surah At-Tawbah (9:105-106)

[ayah:9:105] وَقُلِ ٱعْمَلُوا۟ فَسَيَرَى ٱللَّهُ عَمَلَكُمْ وَرَسُولُهُۥ وَٱلْمُؤْمِنُونَ ۖ وَسَتُرَدُّونَ إِلَىٰ عَـٰلِمِ ٱلْغَيْبِ وَٱلشَّهَـٰدَةِ فَيُنَبِّئُكُم بِمَا كُنتُمْ تَعْمَلُونَ

[ayah:9:106] وَءَاخَرُونَ مُرْجَوْنَ لِأَمْرِ ٱللَّهِ إِمَّا يُعَذِّبُهُمْ وَإِمَّا يَتُوبُ عَلَيْهِمْ ۗ وَٱللَّهُ عَلِيمٌ حَكِيمٌ

*Wa quli i'malu fasayara Allahu 'amalakum wa rasuluhu wa al-mu'minun. Wa saturadduna ila 'alimi al-ghaybi wa al-shahadati fa-yunabbi'ukum bima kuntum ta'malun. Wa akharuna murjawna li-amri Allahi imma yu'adhdhibuhum wa imma yatubu 'alayhim. Wa Allahu 'alimun hakim.*

**And say: work, for Allah will see your work, and His Messenger, and the believers as well. Then you will be returned to the Knower of the unseen and the seen, and He will inform you of what you used to do. And others are left awaiting Allah's command: either He will punish them, or He will turn toward them. And Allah is Knowing, Wise.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

There is a kind of repentance that wants to stay verbal forever.

It wants the relief of being forgiven without the exposure of changed action. It wants the softness of spiritual language without the hard dignity of proof. It wants to say, "I have returned," while keeping life arranged almost exactly as it was before. These verses do not allow that. After confession, charity, purification, prophetic prayer, and tranquility, the next command is short and unsentimental: **work**.

But the passage also does something equally important. It refuses to let the community imagine that once visible deeds begin appearing, human beings now control the final verdict on every case. Some lives are still left in suspension. Some stories are not closed in public on our timetable. Some people are held under a deferment that belongs to Allah alone.

[PAUSE]

That tension is one of the great wisdoms of the passage. On one side, repentance must become visible. On the other side, visible change does not enthrone us as final judges. The surah therefore protects us from two opposite errors. It protects us from a private spirituality that never becomes accountable in action, and it protects us from a communal confidence that assumes every unresolved case has already been handed to us for decisive closure.

The tafsir tradition sharpens both sides. Al-Tabari reads **i'malu** as a command to those who confessed their sins to work for Allah in ways that please Him, obey His obligations, and leave disobedience behind. Ibn Kathir likewise treats the verse as a warning and a directive to the disobedient that their deeds will be seen. Then verse 106 shifts the scene. Ibn Kathir and others tie **wa akharuna murjawna li-amri Allah** to the three famous Companions whose case was delayed after Tabuk: Ka'b ibn Malik, Hilal ibn Umayyah, and Murarah ibn al-Rabi'. Al-Muyassar names them directly. Their deferment matters because it means not every penitent story follows the same timetable or the same outward pattern.

So these verses belong exactly where they stand. 9:102-104 just taught us that a mixed record can be opened by honest confession and embodied return. 9:105-106 now teaches us that confession is not the end of the road. The proof of return must enter action, and even then some matters remain under Allah's own postponement and wisdom.

[PAUSE]

That makes these verses painfully relevant to modern religious life. We live in cultures saturated with performance and explanation. It is easy to narrate growth, signal remorse, declare new intentions, and say the right language of healing. It is also easy for communities to overreact in the opposite direction, demanding immediate neat verdicts on people whose stories are still morally incomplete. The Qur'an gives us a better rhythm. Work. Be seen. Return to Allah's fuller knowledge. And know that some cases are still waiting under divine command.

The hook of the passage is this: **At-Tawbah teaches that repentance must become embodied enough to enter the field of witnessed action, yet every visible story still moves beyond every human audience to the Knower of the unseen and seen, and some cases are intentionally left unresolved before us so that we do not confuse communal witness with divine judgment.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **اعْمَلُوا**: The Imperative Replaces Endless Self-Narration with Embodied Proof

The first anchor is a simple but devastating Qur'anic tool: sometimes one imperative is enough to expose whole worlds of avoidance. The command is short, but it lands by cutting off all the safer substitutes we would rather offer.

That is what happens with **i'malu**.

[PAUSE]

Say: work.

The root **'a-m-l** is not about inward mood alone. It is deed, act, labor, what the person actually does. That matters because these verses come after a repentance sequence. If the surah wanted merely to prolong confession, it could have stayed with feeling, explanation, or reassurance. Instead it pivots to action. The command itself becomes the critique of every repentance that is content to remain verbal.

Translation can say "work," but the Arabic imperative carries more pressure than a generic encouragement toward productivity. It is not "consider some changes." It is not "continue to feel sincere." It is not "keep speaking of your return." It is: act.

This is why the verse is so spiritually disruptive. Many people are willing to offer Allah narratives of transformation long before they are willing to offer Him transformed conduct. The nafs often finds words cheaper than work. It can describe humility without becoming more obedient. It can describe regret without repairing what can be repaired. It can speak the language of growth while habits remain almost untouched. The imperative breaks through that fog.

[PAUSE]

Al-Tabari's gloss is important here because he interprets the command as working for Allah by what pleases Him, fulfilling obligations, and leaving disobedience. That keeps the verse from being flattened into generic activism. The issue is not simply "do something." The issue is obedience becoming visible in actual conduct.

And because the verse follows directly after the earlier repentant group, the command acquires even sharper meaning. Confession was not false. Charity was not false. Prophetic prayer was not false. But none of them is meant to become a new place of rest while life itself remains unchanged. Tawbah must continue moving.

This has a deep personal implication. The believer should beware of any stage in his return where he begins loving the feeling of repentance more than the cost of obedience. There is a sweetness in brokenness before Allah, and that sweetness is real. But the Qur'an does not let us build a home in the doorway. It says: now work.

That is also why 61:2-3 is such a sharp cross-Qur'anic companion. "Why do you say what you do not do? It is greatly hateful to Allah that you say what you do not do." The Qur'an is not suspicious of speech as such, but it is relentless against speech that substitutes for action. Likewise 2:177 moves righteousness beyond orientation and slogan into giving, prayer, covenant, patience, and embodied moral conduct. Qur'anic truth wants shape.

[PAUSE]

So what the Arabic reveals is not simply that believers should be active. It reveals that after failure, the next truthful sentence is no longer an explanation. It is a deed.

### 2. **فَسَيَرَى ... وَسَتُرَدُّونَ ... فَيُنَبِّئُكُمْ**: A Future-Verbal Chain Moves the Deed from Public Visibility to Final Disclosure

The second anchor uses a broader rhetorical feature: a chain of future verbs can stage successive circles of consequence. The action does not stay where it begins. It moves through widening fields until it lands in its full reckoning.

That is what happens in verse 105:

**fasayara Allahu 'amalakum wa rasuluhu wa al-mu'minun. wa saturadduna ila 'alimi al-ghaybi wa al-shahadah fa-yunabbi'ukum bima kuntum ta'malun.**

[PAUSE]

The first future verb is **fasayara**. Then you have **saturaddun**. Then **fa-yunabbi'ukum**. This is not accidental sequence. The deed enters sight, then return, then full informing.

Start with **fasayara Allahu 'amalakum wa rasuluhu wa al-mu'minun**. Allah will see your work, and His Messenger, and the believers. The order matters. Divine seeing comes first, then prophetic and communal seeing. Translation conveys the list, but the Arabic order keeps the hierarchy intact. The believer is being taught that action will become visible, but public visibility never outranks divine sight.

That is crucial because there are two equal and opposite temptations after repentance. One is to hide so completely that no accountable pattern ever emerges. The other is to overcorrect into audience management, making visible righteousness into a new performance project. The verse resists both. Yes, the work will be seen by the Messenger and the believers. But Allah is named first, so the visibility is not permission for riya'. It is accountability under divine priority.

Then the passage refuses to let the field stop there: **wa saturadduna ila 'alimi al-ghaybi wa al-shahadah**. You will be returned to the Knower of the unseen and the seen.

The phrase **'alim al-ghaybi wa al-shahadah** is itself a theological correction to human audience culture. What people see is never the whole matter. Allah knows both the visible deed and the invisible interior carried inside it. He knows what was displayed and what was hidden under the display. So the returning servant cannot rest merely in being seen doing better. The unseen must come with him too.

[PAUSE]

Then comes **fa-yunabbi'ukum**. He will fully inform you. The root **n-b-'** carries informing with significant news, disclosure of what matters. This is more than bare data retrieval. It is the final telling of the truth of what you were doing.

That means the verse stages a sobering movement. First your deeds enter worldly visibility. Then you are returned beyond all worldly audiences. Then the truth of those deeds is made fully known. Translation can tell you the sentence, but the Arabic verbal chain lets you feel the inevitability. Nothing remains where you placed it.

This becomes especially powerful in light of passages like 69:18, where no secret will remain hidden on the Day of presentation, and 86:9, "the Day when secrets are tested." Ibn Kathir explicitly links the seeing here to the Day of Resurrection as well. Al-Tabari, meanwhile, keeps the worldly visibility in view. Together they help us see that the verse bridges both worlds. Action is not only socially relevant; it is eschatologically durable.

This has a searching implication for the modern religious self. Many people are comfortable with being seen as changed if the unseen motives remain relatively unexamined. The verse says that outward work matters, yes, but not in a way that lets hidden motives retire from judgment. You are being returned to the One who knows both domains.

[PAUSE]

So what the Arabic reveals here is not merely a promise that good work will become known. It reveals a full arc: witnessed deed, unavoidable return, comprehensive disclosure. The field of action is therefore simultaneously public and profoundly beyond the public.

### 3. **مُرْجَوْنَ لِأَمْرِ اللَّهِ**: The Passive Participle Teaches Deliberate Suspension Under Divine Command

The third anchor turns on a form that is easy to under-hear in translation: the passive participle can name not only a condition but a withheld process. It can tell you that something has been placed into suspension by an authority beyond the person's control.

That is the force of **murjawna li-amri Allah**.

[PAUSE]

The root behind this expression is tied to delay, deferment, postponement, being left until a matter is decided. Al-Tabari notes the sense of being deferred or delayed for Allah's command and judgment. These are not people who have simply disappeared from moral concern. They are people whose case is not yet publicly settled.

The passive texture matters. They are not the ones deciding the timing of resolution. They are being left awaiting Allah's command. The matter has not been handed to them to close for themselves, nor to the community to close on its own terms. It hangs under divine postponement.

Then the verse intensifies the uncertainty: **imma yu'adhdhibuhum wa imma yatubu 'alayhim**. Either He will punish them, or He will turn toward them. The binary is given, but the decision is withheld from the audience.

That is spiritually unsettling, but it is meant to be. The verse is teaching that not every moral story arrives with immediate narrative closure. Some people are made to live in suspense, and some communities are made to watch without final pronouncement.

Ibn Kathir's identification of these deferred people with the three Companions left behind at Tabuk, Ka'b ibn Malik, Hilal ibn Umayyah, and Murarah ibn al-Rabi', gives the phrase its historical tenderness. They were not being lumped in with hardened hypocrites, but their matter was not immediately resolved in public either. Their suspension itself became part of their purification.

[PAUSE]

This is where translation can sound flatter than the Arabic. "Left awaiting Allah's command" may sound like a neutral administrative delay. But **murjawna** carries a more existential weight. It is deferred fate. It is a life under pause. It is the soul not being allowed to rush to its own acquittal.

And this matters because both individuals and communities hate suspense. We prefer quick verdicts. We want either clean restoration or clean condemnation. The passive participle resists that urge. Some matters remain in Allah's holding pattern because His wisdom is doing something through the delay itself.

So the Arabic reveals not only that these people were unresolved. It reveals that their unresolved state was itself part of the divine pedagogy.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Repentance Becomes Credible When It Starts Costing the Body, the Time, and the Public Self

One of the greatest confusions in contemporary religious life is that people often treat sincerity as though it can be proven almost entirely through self-description.

I know I changed. Allah knows my intention. My heart is softer now. I mean it differently this time. All of that may be true. But verse 105 introduces a harder mercy: **i'malu**. Work. Let your return become embodied enough to enter the world of deeds.

[PAUSE]

This is not because Allah only believes what is visible. Allah already knows the interior. It is because the servant often does not yet know the truth of his own return until it begins to take form under pressure. Work reveals to the self what intention alone may still leave pleasantly vague.

That is why the verse is so honest. After confession and charitable purification, the repentant believer is not allowed to build identity around the moment of remorse itself. He is moved onward into action. The body must now confirm what the tongue confessed.

This matters because repentance often feels most intense in its early emotional phase. A person is broken, humbled, reflective, spiritually awake. But that intensity can fade as soon as the work of obedience becomes ordinary again. The Qur'an seems to know that. It therefore ties repentance to sustained conduct, not to peak feeling alone.

Al-Tabari's reading keeps that practical edge alive: work in obedience to Allah, fulfill what He obligated, and leave what He forbade. In other words, the verse is not romanticizing post-failure spirituality. It is putting the repentant person back into the disciplined lanes of actual servanthood.

[PAUSE]

This is deeply important in a culture of self-expression. We are taught to value articulation, disclosure, and public testimony. There can be real benefit in honest speech. But Quranic repentance still insists on a further question: what now? What has become harder in your life because you returned to Allah? What routine has changed? What duty has been repaired? What relationship has been made more truthful? What hidden compromise has become materially less possible?

Without that, repentance can become another language-game of the self. The person feels morally serious because he has emotionally processed the failure and explained it well, while the architecture of life remains largely untouched. The verse does not despise words. It simply refuses to let them take the place of work.

There is another mercy here too. Visible work is not only for others. It is also for the returning servant's own stabilization. When repentance becomes embodied, the self gains a new footing. The limbs begin to teach the heart. New habits make old compromises harder to romanticize. Small faithful actions build a texture of truth that pure inward remorse often cannot sustain by itself.

[PAUSE]

This is why so many prophetic and Quranic paths of reform involve concrete obedience: prayer on time, charity, restitution, changed companionship, changed consumption, changed speech, changed priorities. The body is not an afterthought in moral recovery. It is one of the places where tawbah becomes believable.

And communities need to learn this too. We should not demand theatrical displays of moral change. But we should understand that real return eventually leaves traceable forms. If nothing in a person's conduct, patterns, loyalties, or obligations begins to shift, then language of repentance may still be living only in aspiration.

The verse therefore asks an uncomfortable but clarifying question: where is the work? Not because Allah needs evidence to know your heart, but because you may still be hiding from the full seriousness of your own return.

### Theme 2: Being Seen by Believers Matters, but It Must Never Replace Returning to the Knower of the Unseen

One of the most subtle gifts in verse 105 is that it teaches a healthy place for communal visibility without ever letting visibility become the final scale.

Allah says your work will be seen by Allah, His Messenger, and the believers. That means deeds are not imagined as purely private in the shallow modern sense. Religious life enters communal reality. People around us are affected by our integrity, inconsistency, reliability, and change. The believing community is not irrelevant to the moral life.

[PAUSE]

That is important because some people use "Allah knows my heart" to dodge every serious form of visible accountability. The verse does not permit that escape. If your return is real, the world around you will not remain untouched forever. The Messenger and the believers are named among those who will see the work.

But the order is just as important. Allah is named first, and the verse immediately carries us beyond human sight into return to **'alim al-ghaybi wa al-shahadah**. That prevents the other distortion: turning righteous visibility into a new audience project.

This is one of the great spiritual tensions of communal life. On the one hand, believers need to witness one another's deeds in enough measure that trust, discipline, and encouragement can exist. On the other hand, the heart is always in danger of shifting from answerability to performance. The verse solves this not by denying visibility, but by subordinating it.

You are seen, yes. But you are not finally measured by what human sight can hold.

[PAUSE]

This is where the passage becomes very contemporary. Many of us now live in layered audiences all the time. Some audiences are literal, some digital, some communal, some professional, some religious. We are constantly tempted to curate. That curation instinct can easily infect repentance too. I will improve in ways that are legible. I will produce the signs of change that calm the audience. I will become the kind of person the community can read quickly and favorably.

The verse interrupts that whole enterprise by reintroducing the unseen. Allah knows what was visible and what was hidden beneath the visible. He knows whether the visible change was genuine fruit or strategic management. He knows whether the deed grew from love of Him or fear of losing social standing.

That is why the phrase **fa-yunabbi'ukum bima kuntum ta'malun** is so sobering. We are not only returning to divine sight. We are returning to full informing. The truth of what we were doing will be disclosed. Not merely the external action, but its real meaning.

[PAUSE]

This should create a mature form of moral seriousness. We should care about how our lives affect the believers. We should not romanticize hidden spirituality that never becomes responsible in community. But neither should we let communal sight become our final court of appeal. Human audiences can be comforted too quickly or scandalized too quickly. Allah is neither deceived by our polish nor unjust to the complexity we carry.

This also helps communities themselves. Communities become unhealthy when they either refuse all visible accountability or confuse visible accountability with total knowledge. The passage guards against both. Yes, believers see. No, believers do not see everything. Yes, deeds matter in public. No, final disclosure remains with Allah.

It is a difficult balance, but it is one of the marks of spiritual adulthood: to be willing to be seen in real obedience without letting the seen layer become the whole religion.

### Theme 3: Some Souls Are Purified Through Delay, and Communities Must Learn the Discipline of Unfinished Verdicts

Verse 106 introduces one of the hardest disciplines in religion: living with a case that has not yet been publicly closed.

**Wa akharuna murjawna li-amri Allah.**

[PAUSE]

And others are deferred for Allah's command.

This is hard on the people deferred, and it is hard on the community watching them. The people deferred must live under uncertainty. The community must live without decisive closure. Neither side gets what human beings usually crave most: a neat, immediate ending.

The tafsir tradition's identification of these people with the three Companions left behind from Tabuk is deeply important. Their story, especially as it later unfolds around Ka'b ibn Malik, is one of the most emotionally exact stories in the Sunnah about purification through delay. They were not smooth hypocrites. They were not yet publicly cleared either. The waiting itself became part of the truthfulness Allah drew out of them.

[PAUSE]

That means delay is not always absence of mercy. Sometimes delay is the shape mercy takes when God is deepening a person's truth, stripping away premature reassurance, and making both the servant and the community feel that final verdict belongs to Him.

Modern people struggle with this badly. We prefer immediate labeling. We want to know whether someone is restored, disqualified, safe, unsafe, sincere, fake, in, out. We are impatient with morally unfinished stories because suspense is emotionally expensive. But verse 106 refuses that impatience. Some matters remain under Allah's command for a while.

This has direct pastoral significance. If every failure is met either with instant restoration or instant annihilation, communities lose a whole Quranic category: suspended moral seriousness under divine wisdom. Sometimes a person needs time not because the truth is unclear to Allah, but because the truth needs to ripen in human life under the pressure of waiting.

That waiting can expose whether a person's repentance was mainly hunger for reputation recovery or true submission to Allah's timing. It can expose whether a community wanted justice or merely narrative comfort. It can expose whether we trust Allah's wisdom when He does not satisfy our appetite for quick endings.

[PAUSE]

This does not mean passivity about clear evil. The verse is not a charter for moral laziness. It is a guardrail against overreaching verdicts in cases Allah has not yet handed to us in finished form. We act on what is clear. We acknowledge what is unresolved. We do not force closure to satisfy our own emotional need for certainty.

The divine names at the end matter here: **Allahu 'alimun hakim**. Allah is Knowing, Wise. Knowing means the hidden truth of the case is not in danger of being lost during the delay. Wise means the delay itself is not meaningless. Those two names are the theological ground for bearing unfinished verdicts without panic.

This is a painful mercy, but a mercy nonetheless. There are believers whose purification will not come through instant relief. It will come through staying under Allah's command long enough for the heart to become truer than it was when it first sought pardon.

[PAUSE]

And communities need to relearn how to stand in that space with reverence. Not all stories are ours to close. Not all morally serious situations are ready for final public language. Sometimes the most obedient communal posture is sober patience under Allah's knowledge and wisdom.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Passage

These verses are built on a pattern of **commanded embodiment followed by withheld closure**.

Verse 105 takes the repentance arc out of interior speech and moves it into work, sight, return, and disclosure. It builds a layered witness structure: visible deeds are seen by Allah, the Messenger, and the believers, yet then the whole matter is carried beyond that field into return to the Knower of the unseen and seen. Verse 106 then interrupts any assumption that visibility means immediate verdict by introducing another group held in deferment under Allah's command.

So the architecture is not merely linear. It first drives repentance outward into embodied public reality, then drives that public reality upward into divine knowledge, and finally reins in human verdicts by naming souls whose case remains suspended. The passage teaches that repentance must become visible, but visibility never gives us possession of final judgment.

### Questions to Carry

Where has your repentance remained mostly in language because work would expose whether it is really true?

Are you more concerned with being seen as changed, or with returning honestly to the Knower of the unseen and seen?

What deed in your life would count as real embodied proof that you have turned back to Allah in this matter?

Where have you demanded quick verdicts on someone else because suspense made you uncomfortable?

Can you distinguish between healthy communal accountability and the ego's hunger to control another person's final story?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These verses teach that repentance becomes true by entering witnessed action, yet every witnessed action still returns beyond human audiences to Allah's fuller knowledge, and some souls are intentionally left in suspended judgment so that we do not mistake visible change for final closure.**

### The Closing Invitation

Take this passage wherever your spiritual life has become overly verbal. Ask Allah to show you the work your repentance has been postponing. Then take one step that costs enough to be real. At the same time, let the verse humble your gaze toward others. Witness what is visible, yes, but do not seize a verdict Allah has not given you. Live with enough seriousness to act, enough honesty to be seen, and enough reverence to leave unfinished cases under the knowledge and wisdom of Allah.

### Du'a

O Allah, do not let our repentance remain trapped in speech.  
Give us deeds that prove our return to You with truth and steadiness.  
Protect us from performing righteousness for human eyes while neglecting the unseen.  
Return us to You as the Knower of all that was hidden and shown.  
Teach us patience with unfinished stories and humility before Your decrees.  
And make us people who work sincerely, wait reverently, and trust Your wisdom in every delayed verdict.
