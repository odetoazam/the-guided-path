# Do Not Be Impressed by What Is Already Turning into Their Punishment

## Surah At-Tawbah (9:55-57)

[ayah:9:55] فَلَا تُعْجِبْكَ أَمْوَٰلُهُمْ وَلَآ أَوْلَـٰدُهُمْ ۚ إِنَّمَا يُرِيدُ ٱللَّهُ لِيُعَذِّبَهُم بِهَا فِى ٱلْحَيَوٰةِ ٱلدُّنْيَا وَتَزْهَقَ أَنفُسُهُمْ وَهُمْ كَـٰفِرُونَ

[ayah:9:56] وَيَحْلِفُونَ بِٱللَّهِ إِنَّهُمْ لَمِنكُمْ وَمَا هُم مِّنكُمْ وَلَـٰكِنَّهُمْ قَوْمٌ يَفْرَقُونَ

[ayah:9:57] لَوْ يَجِدُونَ مَلْجَـًٔا أَوْ مَغَـٰرَٰتٍ أَوْ مُدَّخَلًا لَّوَلَّوْا۟ إِلَيْهِ وَهُمْ يَجْمَحُونَ

*Fa-la tu'jibka amwaluhum wa la awladuhum. Innama yuridu Allahu li-yu'adhdhibahum biha fi al-hayati al-dunya wa tazhaqa anfusuhum wa hum kafirun.*

*Wa yahlifuna bi-Allahi innahum la-minkum wa ma hum minkum wa lakinnahum qawmun yafraqun.*

*Law yajiduna malja'an aw magharatin aw muddakhalan la-wallaw ilayhi wa hum yajmahun.*

**So do not let their wealth or their children impress you. Allah only intends by them to punish them through these in the worldly life, and that their souls depart while they are disbelievers. They swear by Allah that they are surely among you, but they are not from you; rather, they are a people seized by fear. If they could find a refuge, or caves, or any place to slip into, they would turn to it at once, rushing wildly.**

## Introduction

One of the Qur'an's great mercies is that it teaches us not to envy what is actually ruining people.

These ayahs strip the glamour off hypocritical advantage. Wealth, children, apparent social embeddedness, and loud claims of belonging can all make a person look established, protected, even enviable. But Allah tells His Messenger not to be impressed. Why? Because what the eye reads as advantage may already be operating as punishment. The same wealth and family structure that look like signs of success may be the very medium through which the soul is being further trapped in dunya, hardened in disbelief, and led toward a worse ending.

[PAUSE]

Then the surah moves from their possessions to their emotional reality. They swear by Allah that they are from you. Outwardly, they want association with the believing body. But Allah says they are not from you. What drives them is not faith but fear. And the next verse paints that fear almost cinematically: if they could find a shelter, a cave, a hole to slip into, they would lunge toward it wildly.

This is such a powerful unveiling. Their problem is not only insincerity. It is that inwardly they are already fleeing. They may still stand near the believers in form, but their souls are leaning toward escape. What looked like belonging is actually panic kept in public clothing.

Ibn Kathir reads these verses as a complete exposure of the hypocrites' false glamour and inward terror. Al-Tabari preserves interpretive discussion around how wealth and children punish them, but all of the readings circle the same core truth: what looks like worldly proof can already be an instrument of divine exposure and misery. These ayahs do not merely criticize hypocrites. They retrain the believer's eye.

The hook of the passage is this: **At-Tawbah teaches that worldly appearance can hide spiritual collapse, and that what looks like enviable strength may in reality be a terrified soul being punished through the very things people admire.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Fa-la tu'jibka amwaluhum wa la awladuhum**: Do Not Be Captured by Appearances

Verse 55 opens:

**fa-la tu'jibka amwaluhum wa la awladuhum**

Do not let their wealth or their children impress you.

[PAUSE]

The verb **tu'jibka** comes from the root **'‑j‑b**, the field of amazement, admiration, being taken by something's apparent impressiveness. This is not merely "do not like it." It is deeper. Do not let it seize your evaluative gaze. Do not let your eye surrender judgment to outward shine.

And that matters because the two named objects, **amwal** and **awlad**, are the classic currencies of visible success in human society. Wealth suggests reach, options, insulation, leverage. Children suggest continuity, multiplication, legacy, social rootedness. The Qur'an names the two things people most easily use to score the human condition.

Then it negates admiration before offering explanation. That order matters. Revelation interrupts fascination quickly because fascination is one of the first doors by which false measures enter the heart. Once admiration hardens into envy, your scale has already started to drift away from Allah.

This echoes the Qur'anic training in 20:131, where the Messenger is told not to extend his eyes toward the splendor of worldly life given to different groups, because it is a flower of dunya by which they are tested. The eye is always in danger of reading bloom as proof.

So the ayah begins by disciplining the gaze. Before it explains the reality of their wealth and children, it first says: do not be taken in.

### 2. **Innama yuridu Allahu li-yu'adhdhibahum biha**: The Admired Thing Becomes the Medium of Torment

Then the verse explains the reason:

**innama yuridu Allahu li-yu'adhdhibahum biha**

Allah only intends to punish them through these things.

[PAUSE]

The particle **innama** restricts sharply. Only. Precisely. The verse is not giving wealth and children a neutral reading and then adding a cautionary note. It is relocating their meaning entirely in this case.

The verb **li-yu'adhdhibahum** from **'‑dh‑b** intensifies the shock. Punish them. Torment them. Cause suffering by means of them.

And then the small pronoun is devastating:

**biha**

through them, by them.

Not merely alongside them. Not after them. Through them.

This is one of the most important spiritual correctives in the Qur'an. Blessings are not self-interpreting. Wealth is not proof by itself. Children are not proof by themselves. Their truth depends on what they are doing to the soul holding them.

Al-Tabari preserves differing formulations about whether the punishment manifests in dunya by toil, anxiety, and calamity around these blessings, or whether the wording leans forward toward the final consequence. Al-Muyassar emphasizes the worldly angle: the weariness of acquisition, the preoccupation, the losses, the unredeemed pain. Ibn Kathir ties this verse to other places where Allah tells the Messenger not to be dazzled by worldly provision granted to those being tried. The interpretive shades differ, but together they yield a fuller picture: Allah may punish a person by making the thing he loves become his chain.

That should stop us. We often imagine punishment only as subtraction. The Qur'an reminds us that punishment can also come as possession. Not every increase is expansion. Some increases narrow the soul.

### 3. **Fi al-hayati al-dunya wa tazhaqa anfusuhum wa hum kafirun**: The Ending Reveals the Real Meaning of the Middle

The verse continues:

**fi al-hayati al-dunya wa tazhaqa anfusuhum wa hum kafirun**

in the worldly life, and that their souls depart while they are disbelievers.

[PAUSE]

The phrase **fi al-hayati al-dunya** matters because it keeps us from postponing the verse entirely to the afterlife. Something of this punishment is already unfolding now, in the life people can see and misread. The admired thing is not merely going to become a problem later. It may already be functioning as a trap in the present.

Then comes the closing darkness:

**wa tazhaqa anfusuhum**

and their souls depart.

The root **z‑h‑q** carries the sense of vanishing, draining away, departing with depletion. It is not a serene description of death. It feels spent, stripped, carried out without relief.

And then:

**wa hum kafirun**

while they are disbelievers.

This is the true unveiling of the whole picture. The worldly middle must be read in light of the final end. The question is not only: how impressive did their life look? It is: what did all of it prepare the soul for when it had to leave?

This is how the Qur'an repeatedly breaks the spell of surface success. In 23:55-56, Allah asks whether they think that because He extends wealth and sons to them, He is hastening good for them. No, but they do not perceive. Our passage belongs to that same education. Extension is not always approval. Sometimes extension is exposure stretched over time.

### 4. **Yahlifuna bi-Allahi innahum la-minkum ... wa ma hum minkum**: They Want the Cover of Belonging Without Its Truth

Verse 56 shifts from possessions to speech:

**wa yahlifuna bi-Allahi innahum la-minkum**

They swear by Allah that they are surely from you.

[PAUSE]

The oath matters. **Yahlifuna** from **h‑l‑f** signals insistence, anxious reinforcement, the need to verbally secure what is not inwardly secure. People usually swear hardest when the truth does not stand calmly on its own.

And the phrase **la-minkum** uses the emphatic lam. Surely from you. Definitely among you. The wording wants maximal association.

Then the verse cuts through it with one of the sharpest negations in the surah:

**wa ma hum minkum**

they are not from you.

Not: they are weak among you. Not: they are mixed up among you. Not: they are still sort of yours in a compromised way. The Qur'an severs the false claim cleanly.

Al-Tabari explains that they make these claims out of fear, not faith, wanting safety among the believers without belonging to their religion in truth. This is spiritually penetrating. It tells us that hypocrisy often covets the shelter of the community while refusing the sincerity that makes shelter meaningful.

The believer should sit with that. It is possible to want the reputation of Islamic belonging, the social insulation of Muslim identity, the beauty of public association with truth, and still not want the surrender that makes one truly "from" the people of faith.

### 5. **Qawmun yafraqun ... wa hum yajmahun**: Fear and Wild Flight Name Their Inner State

The verse then names the engine beneath the lie:

**wa lakinnahum qawmun yafraqun**

Rather, they are a people who fear.

[PAUSE]

The verb **yafraqun** from **f‑r‑q** here carries fright, dread, being seized by fear. Not reverent fear of Allah. Frightened self-preservation.

Then verse 57 turns that fear into motion:

**law yajiduna malja'an aw magharatin aw muddakhalan la-wallaw ilayhi wa hum yajmahun**

If they could find a refuge, or caves, or any place to slip into, they would turn to it while rushing wildly.

The sequence of nouns is powerful. **Malja'** is a refuge, a place of shelter. **Magharat** are caves, openings in mountains. **Muddakhal** is a place to enter secretly, to slip into, almost a hidden passage or burrowed access. Al-Tabari points out the texture of each: fortress, cave, tunnel-like concealment. The verse is piling up hiding places.

Then comes **yajmahun**, from **j‑m‑h**, to bolt, rush uncontrolled, like a startled animal no longer governable. Ibn Kathir emphasizes the panic here. If they could escape the pressure of standing near truth, they would hurl themselves toward escape.

That is the final x-ray. Their problem is not simply wrong doctrine stated coolly. It is terror. They are inwardly unmoored and desperate for anywhere that does not demand courageous nearness to Allah.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Not Everything That Looks Like Favor Is Favor

We need this correction constantly because almost every human environment trains the eye to read quickly and shallowly.

More money means more success.

More children means more blessing.

More reach means more worth.

More visible security means more divine favor.

The Qur'an says: slow down. You have not yet understood what you are looking at.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the hardest disciplines in spiritual life, because visible advantage is persuasive. It does not merely tempt greed. It tempts theology. We begin to assume that what is large must be blessed, what is multiplied must be approved, what is stable-looking must be safe.

But 9:55 refuses that whole reflex. Wealth and children can be mercy. Of course they can. The Qur'an elsewhere speaks of spouses, children, and provision as gifts, trusts, adornments, and means of gratitude. But here the same categories are stripped of automatic innocence. The issue is not the object. The issue is the relationship between the object and the heart.

This is why the verse is so useful for curing envy. Envy usually operates on partial information. I see the possession, not the burden. I see the polish, not the panic. I see the social fruit, not the spiritual rot. Allah, in mercy, tells His Messenger not to be impressed because the believer's eye must be saved from worshipping surfaces.

Ibn Kathir connects this ayah to 20:131, where the eye is warned not to stretch toward the flower of worldly life given to others as trial. That connection matters. The issue is not merely their wealth. The issue is your gaze. If your gaze is not trained by revelation, you will misread trial as triumph and punishment as prosperity.

And modern life intensifies this confusion because we now encounter curated surfaces constantly. Wealth is displayed without the anxiety it generates. Family image is displayed without the fractures beneath it. Success is displayed without the moral compromise that purchased it. Public belonging is displayed without the deadness of the private heart.

So the ayah becomes startlingly contemporary. Do not let their wealth or children impress you. Do not let metrics, aesthetics, volume, and continuity become your hidden creed. Do not assume that because something looks abundant it is healthy.

There is also a gentler side to this correction. It protects the believer from despair about his own smaller portion. If wealth and offspring are not self-interpreting, then your lesser visible share may not be deprivation in the way your nafs imagines. It may be protection. It may be a lighter test. It may be Allah sparing you a kind of entanglement you are not built to carry well.

This is where 18:46 is important: wealth and children are adornment of the life of this world, but enduring righteous deeds are better with your Lord in reward and hope. The Qur'an does not deny adornment. It simply refuses to mistake adornment for substance.

And that matters pastorally too. Some believers look at those with more and feel late, small, or forgotten. This passage tells them: do not reach conclusions from surfaces. What if the thing you envy is actually exhausting its owner, hardening his heart, and escorting him toward a darker end? What if your smaller, quieter life is easier to carry into the grave with iman intact?

The ayah is not anti-wealth or anti-family. It is anti-delusion. It wants us to stop treating outward expansion as a theological verdict.

[PAUSE]

That alone can save a heart from enormous unnecessary unrest.

### Theme 2: Allah May Punish a Person Through What He Refuses to Stop Worshipping

One of the most sobering lines in the passage is **li-yu'adhdhibahum biha**. Punish them through them.

We often imagine punishment as the loss of the idol. The Qur'an reminds us that punishment may also be the preservation of the idol while the soul decays around it.

[PAUSE]

What if the money stays, but it produces only fear, greed, fatigue, endless guarding, and no gratitude?

What if the children remain, but become a source of vanity, social competition, neglected tarbiyah, or distraction from Allah?

What if the platform remains, but grows into addiction to praise and terror of irrelevance?

What if the public role remains, but makes repentance harder because too much identity is now bound up in being seen a certain way?

This is punishment through possession.

Al-Muyassar emphasizes the worldly side of this punishment: strain in acquiring wealth, pains and calamities around it, and the fact that none of it is being converted into nearness to Allah. Al-Tabari preserves readings that sharpen both present and final dimensions. The point is not to reduce the verse to one mechanism, but to feel the breadth of the warning. Allah has many ways of making the beloved object reveal what the lover really serves.

This should make us ask a harder question about blessings than "Do I have them?" The better question is: what are they doing to me?

Are they softening me into gratitude?

Are they widening me into generosity?

Are they making obedience easier?

Are they making the akhirah more vivid?

Or are they tightening the fist, feeding the ego, amplifying fear of loss, and making trust in Allah more difficult?

If the latter, then the object may be functioning less as ni'mah and more as fitnah.

This is why the Qur'an says in 63:9 that wealth and children should not distract the believers from remembrance of Allah. The verse does not deny love. It denies enthronement. Once the blessing sits where only Allah should sit, the blessing begins to mutate spiritually.

There is something else deeply unsettling here: punishment through wealth and children is especially severe because the punished person may continue to feel publicly validated. Outward subtraction is at least easier to recognize as distress. But when the soul is decaying through the very things society applauds, self-deception becomes easier. Everyone around you may be confirming the illusion while your heart is quietly dying.

That is why the verse ends with death in kufr. The real horror is not merely that wealth became stressful or that family became complicated. The horror is that none of it brought the person home to Allah before the soul had to leave.

And that should create a beautiful kind of fear in us. Not fear of blessings, but fear of unexamined blessings. Fear of receiving things without asking what obligations came with them. Fear of increase without muraqabah. Fear of admiring what Allah may be using as an exposure.

The believer, then, must learn to respond to increase not first with self-congratulation but with trembling gratitude and honest audit. More is heavier. More is not a pure compliment. More can be mercy, but mercy only remains mercy when the heart stays under Allah.

### Theme 3: Hypocrisy Wants the Shelter of Belonging While Secretly Searching for a Way Out

Verses 56 and 57 reveal a form of hypocrisy that many communities know intimately: the person who wants to remain adjacent to truth for its safety, social capital, or protection, but whose soul is always leaning toward escape when obedience becomes costly.

[PAUSE]

They swear by Allah that they are from you.

That sentence alone reveals a desire. They want the category. They want to be counted. They want the advantage of public affiliation.

But Allah says: they are not from you. Why? Because belonging in the Qur'an is not built by oath, optics, vocabulary, or physical proximity. It is built by loyalty, surrender, courage, and willingness to remain near Allah when nearness becomes expensive.

Al-Tabari says they speak this way out of fear, wanting safety among the believers. Ibn Kathir also stresses fright and anxiety as the engine of their speech. That is spiritually helpful because it shows us that hypocrisy is often not driven only by intellectual confusion. It can be driven by cowardice that has learned religious language.

And then the surah intensifies the picture. If they could find a refuge, or a cave, or a hidden entry, they would rush there wildly. In other words: the public claim of belonging is not the heart's true direction. The heart's true direction is toward cover.

This is not limited to battle. It happens anywhere faith requires cost.

When truth risks reputation, some people begin looking for a cave.

When obedience threatens comfort, some people begin looking for a tunnel.

When solidarity with the believers carries social consequence, some people start rehearsing escape routes while still speaking the language of belonging.

This is why the final word **yajmahun** matters so much. It is not calm strategic departure. It is panicked flight. The soul is not merely unconvinced. It is already bolting inwardly.

That image should make us examine our own hidden movements.

When religion becomes demanding, what does my heart start searching for?

A loophole?

A hiding place?

A room where no one expects courage from me?

A version of Islam that leaves my attachments untouched?

The ayah teaches that direction matters as much as location. You can still be standing outwardly among the believers while your heart is already sprinting elsewhere.

This is also what makes hypocrisy so tragic. It lives close to the very people from whom it could have learned steadiness. It hears revelation. It sees courage. It watches sincerity. But instead of letting that nearness heal its fear, it uses nearness as cover while secretly seeking somewhere safer than obedience.

And there is a painful contemporary form of this too: wanting Muslim identity as familiarity, culture, aesthetics, family continuity, or moral branding, but not wanting Islam as surrender when surrender threatens appetite or image. The oath remains; the heart leans away.

Our passage does not let us settle for surface association. It asks: what are you really seeking when the pressure rises? Refuge in Allah, or refuge from Allah's demand?

That is the dividing line.

[PAUSE]

The people of iman may also fear. But their fear drives them toward Allah. The hypocrite fears in a way that drives him toward concealment from the cost of obedience. That difference is everything.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Passage

These three ayahs are arranged as a progressive exposure of false strength.

Verse 55 begins with the visible layer: wealth and children. The gaze is corrected before the reality is explained. Then the verse moves beneath the surface and reveals that the admired things are actually instruments of punishment and that the entire worldly picture must be read in light of the soul's final departure in disbelief.

Verse 56 then shifts from possessions to speech. The hypocrites swear that they belong to the believers, but Allah severs the claim and names the real engine under the oath: fear. So the passage moves from what they have to what they say to what truly drives them.

Verse 57 completes the unveiling by turning fear into motion. The surah shows us where that fear wants to go: toward any refuge, cave, tunnel, or hidden passage. The final image is not theological argument but physical flight. What looked like stability is exposed as panic.

So the architecture runs:

surface admiration -> divine reinterpretation -> final consequence -> false claim of belonging -> fear unveiled -> frantic search for escape

That sequence matters because it teaches us how nifaq works. It is not only false doctrine. It is a whole false ecology: admired surfaces, misread blessings, public religious speech, interior fear, and a heart secretly in motion away from costly truth.

### Questions to Carry

What kinds of visible success still make me spiritually careless in my evaluations of people?

Be specific. Is it money, family scale, influence, polish, stability, or public acceptance? The things you instinctively admire often reveal the metrics your heart is still tempted to worship.

What are my blessings doing to my soul right now?

Do they make remembrance easier or rarer? Do they increase gratitude, generosity, and obedience, or do they increase distraction, anxiety, and self-importance?

When obedience becomes costly, does my heart move toward Allah for refuge or begin searching for a place to hide from the demand?

This is one of the most important diagnostics in the passage. Direction matters more than outward location.

Have I ever wanted the social cover of belonging without the surrender that makes belonging true?

The verse is not only about others. It asks whether we love the identity of faith more than the actual demands of faith.

What do I envy that the Qur'an may be asking me to fear instead?

Sometimes the next step in purification is not getting what you desire, but learning to stop reading it as obvious good before Allah has taught you what it really is.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs teach us not to envy outward expansion or public belonging too quickly, because wealth, children, and social nearness can all become the scenery of punishment when the heart holding them is secretly ruled by fear and flight from Allah.**

### The Closing Invitation

The invitation of this passage is to ask Allah for corrected sight. Before envying anyone, pause and ask what their apparent blessing is doing to their soul. Before celebrating your own increase, ask whether it is making remembrance easier or harder. And when fear rises, do not let it drive you toward caves of avoidance, private tunnels of compromise, or religiously worded escape routes. Bring that fear to Allah until it becomes khashyah that deepens surrender rather than panic that deepens hypocrisy.

### Du'a

Allahumma, protect our eyes from being dazzled by what is only worldly bloom.

Do not let wealth, children, or status become punishments for us through heedlessness and pride.

Make our blessings ladders to gratitude, remembrance, and obedience.

Do not let us seek the cover of belonging while our hearts are fleeing from You.

When fear rises in us, turn it into refuge in You, not escape from Your command.

Give us truthful sight, truthful belonging, and a heart that runs only toward You.
