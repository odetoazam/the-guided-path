# Why Do You Fear Them When Allah Is More Worthy of Your Fear?

## Surah At-Tawbah (9:13-16)

[ayah:9:13] أَلَا تُقَـٰتِلُونَ قَوْمًا نَّكَثُوٓا۟ أَيْمَـٰنَهُمْ وَهَمُّوا۟ بِإِخْرَاجِ ٱلرَّسُولِ وَهُم بَدَءُوكُمْ أَوَّلَ مَرَّةٍ ۚ أَتَخْشَوْنَهُمْ ۚ فَٱللَّهُ أَحَقُّ أَن تَخْشَوْهُ إِن كُنتُم مُّؤْمِنِينَ

[ayah:9:15] وَيُذْهِبْ غَيْظَ قُلُوبِهِمْ ۗ وَيَتُوبُ ٱللَّهُ عَلَىٰ مَن يَشَآءُ ۗ وَٱللَّهُ عَلِيمٌ حَكِيمٌ

**Will you not fight a people who broke their oaths, intended to expel the Messenger, and were the first to begin against you? Do you fear them? ## Introduction

There are moments when hesitation looks humble but is actually misplaced fear.

That is the pressure of this passage. The believers are not being pushed toward reckless violence. They are being asked why they still tremble before people who already broke their oaths, already aimed at expelling the Messenger, and already initiated hostility. In other words, the question is not whether danger exists. The question is whether fear has remained properly ordered.

[PAUSE]

Then the ayahs do something profound. They do not describe fighting only as outward victory. They describe it as a place where Allah acts through the believers' hands, disgraces the aggressors, grants nusrah, heals the chests of wounded believers, and removes the inward rage left by prolonged injury. This is not romanticizing battle. It is revealing that when confrontation becomes necessary in Allah's path, the outcome is not only strategic. It is also moral and emotional.

And then the passage takes the matter deeper still. This is not only about defeating a hostile people. It is about exposing the believers themselves. Did you think you would simply be left alone without Allah bringing your striving into the open and showing who among you refuses hidden loyalties outside Allah, His Messenger, and the believers? So the confrontation is also a sieve. It reveals the order of fear, the truth of allegiance, and whether the heart still keeps a secret doorway to some other intimacy.

The hook of the passage is this: **At-Tawbah teaches that when fear of hostile people competes with fear of Allah, necessary struggle becomes a revelation of the heart, exposing both where our fear is ordered and where our hidden loyalties still live.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **A-takhshawnahum? Fa-Allahu ahaqqu an takhshawhu**: The Central Question Is Ordered Fear

Verse 13 opens with a challenge:

**ala tuqatiluna qawman nakathu aymanahum**.

Will you not fight a people who broke their oaths?

[PAUSE]

The verse immediately lists concrete grounds: they broke commitments, intended the Messenger's expulsion, and started the aggression first. So the Qur'an is not stirring emotion in a vacuum. It is recalling moral facts.

Then comes the piercing question:

**a-takhshawnahum?**

Do you fear them?

And the correction:

**fa-Allahu ahaqqu an takhshawhu in kuntum mu'minin**.

Allah has more right that you should fear Him, if you are believers.

The issue is not the total removal of human fear. It is its hierarchy. When fear of people starts displacing fear of Allah, discernment begins to warp.

Al-Tabari reads the verse as a direct divine incitement to confront oath-breakers whose hostility had already become manifest. But the striking part is where the incitement lands: not first on strategy, but on **khashyah**. The Qur'an does not ask merely whether they are dangerous. It asks whether your fear has become morally disordered. Ibn Kathir preserves the same force by tying the verse to those who had already broken covenant and sought the Messenger's expulsion, so hesitation here is not realism alone; it risks becoming a spiritual misplacement of dread.

### 2. **Yu'adhdhibhum Allahu bi-aydikum**: Allah Acts Through Believing Hands

Verse 14 says:

**qatiluhum yu'adhdhibhumu Allahu bi-aydikum**.

Fight them; Allah will punish them by your hands.

[PAUSE]

This is a delicate balance. The believers act, but the ayah attributes the real punishment to Allah. Human hands are means, not independent authors.

Then:

**wa yukhzihim wa yansurkum 'alayhim**.

He will disgrace them and grant you victory over them.

So the battlefield is not detached from divine action. Shame and victory alike are placed under Allah's governing hand.

And the sequence matters rhetorically. The verse does not give one result but a chain: **yu'adhdhibhum, yukhzihim, yansurkum, yashfi, yudhhib**. Five verbs opening five registers at once: punishment, disgrace, victory, healing, and removal of rage. The command to fight is therefore immediately unfolded into a map of what Allah is doing through that struggle. The believer is not being asked to stare only at collision. He is being taught to see divine action moving across outward, communal, and inward terrain at the same time.

### 3. **Wa yashfi sudura qawmin mu'minin / wa yudhhib ghayza qulubihim**: Allah Acknowledges Wounded Believing Hearts

Then the passage names something deeply human:

**wa yashfi sudura qawmin mu'minin**.

He will heal the chests of believing people.

[PAUSE]

And:

**wa yudhhib ghayza qulubihim**.

He will remove the rage of their hearts.

This is remarkable. Revelation does not pretend that aggression leaves believers inwardly untouched. Chests can be tight, hearts can burn with **ghayz**, deep anger and pressure. The ayah does not sanctify permanent bitterness, but it does acknowledge the wound.

And it teaches that Allah Himself can heal what prolonged harm lodged inside the believers.

### 4. **Wa yatubu Allahu 'ala man yasha'**: Even Here, the Door of Tawbah Stays Open

Then, after punishment, disgrace, victory, healing, and removal of rage, the ayah still says:

**wa yatubu Allahu 'ala man yasha'**.

Allah turns in mercy to whom He wills.

[PAUSE]

This keeps the passage from collapsing into a closed world of pure retribution. Even in the middle of a hard confrontation, Allah's tawbah remains possible for whom He wills.

So the same unit that commands fighting also refuses to imprison the future entirely inside present hostility.

### 5. **Wa lam yattakhidhu ... walijah**: Striving Reveals Hidden Inner Doors

Then verse 16 turns inward:

**am hasibtum an tutraku**.

Did you think you would be left alone?

[PAUSE]

Left without what? Without exposure. Without testing. Without the truth of the heart becoming visible.

Then:

**wa lam yattakhidhu min duni Allahi wa la rasulihi wa la al-mu'minina walijah**.

And that Allah had not yet made known those who do not take, apart from Allah, His Messenger, and the believers, an intimate inner confidant.

The word **walijah** is powerful. It is not merely a public friend. It is an inner entry, a private inside channel, a hidden intimate insertion into one's loyalties.

The ayah teaches that jihad does not only test courage. It exposes where our secret alignments remain.

Ibn Kathir glosses the verse in terms of testing that reveals pure intention from false intention, and al-Tabari's explanation of the trial makes the same movement: Allah does not leave believers unexposed, but brings forth who truly strives and who has kept an inside channel elsewhere. The force of **walijah** is stronger than casual companionship. It names the private ingress of influence, the inner access point through which some other allegiance keeps entering the heart.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Fear Has to Be Reordered Before Action Can Be Pure

We often think the main spiritual problem in hard moments is cowardice. The Qur'an goes deeper. The problem is disordered fear.

[PAUSE]

As long as the heart trembles before people more than it trembles before Allah, even outwardly correct action will be unstable. This is why the verse asks, **Do you fear them?** before commanding movement. The heart has to be named before the hand can be purified.

The word the verse uses is not accidental. It does not ask about any generic emotional reaction. It asks about **khashyah**, fear textured by perceived weight, consequence, and significance. That matters because many of us can tell ourselves our fear is only prudent realism when in fact we have quietly granted created things a moral largeness they do not deserve. The problem is not that people are harmless. The problem is that their size in the heart has exceeded their true place under Allah.

[PAUSE]

This is why the Qur'an phrases the correction the way it does: *Allah has more right that you should fear Him*. It does not deny that hostile people can terrify. It denies that their terror should outrank Allah in the hierarchy of the heart. 3:175 gives a parallel register: *It is only Satan who frightens his allies, so do not fear them, but fear Me if you are believers.* Both passages expose the same fracture. Human fear becomes spiritually corrupt when it starts editing obedience.

Al-Tabari's reading of verse 13 stays tethered to the actual moral case: these are people who broke their oaths, aimed at expelling the Messenger, and initiated hostility first. Ibn Kathir preserves the same context. That matters because the verse is not shaming the believers for noticing danger. It is confronting them for letting warranted danger swell into paralyzing authority over their response. There is a difference between seeing a threat and enthroning it.

This distinction reaches painfully into ordinary life. There are many places where fear of people quietly becomes the real god of the situation. We fear a spouse's anger more than Allah's claim over our speech. We fear professional exclusion more than Allah's command to be honest. We fear a family system's disapproval more than revelation's demand to stop protecting a lie. We fear a crowd's reaction more than the Lord who made the crowd. Then we rename that fear "wisdom," "timing," or "complexity."

But the Qur'an is not easily deceived by our vocabulary. It asks a humiliatingly direct question: *do you fear them?* The question is humiliating because it collapses our elaborate self-justifications into one exposed reality. This is not about whether the situation is complicated. It is about what is sitting on the throne of the heart while you face the complication.

That is why reordered fear is the condition for purified action. If you move while people remain larger than Allah in your heart, your movement will still be compromised. You may comply outwardly while bargaining inwardly. You may do the right thing resentfully. You may obey only where the social cost stays manageable. But once Allah becomes weightier than the thing frightening you, obedience stops feeling like a heroic exception and starts feeling like simple sanity.

The deeper mercy of the verse is that it does not merely rebuke fear. It rehouses it. It teaches the believer where awe belongs. And once awe is rehoused, people shrink back to creaturely size. They may still wound, threaten, scheme, and punish. But they no longer get to define the final terms of your obedience.

### Theme 2: Trials Expose Hidden Loyalties, Not Just Visible Behavior

Verse 16 should make us more honest about what tests are doing. We often imagine trials are only there to measure whether we will endure externally. But the Qur'an says they also reveal whether we have taken some **walijah**, some secret interior alliance outside the rightful circle of Allah, His Messenger, and the believers.

[PAUSE]

That means many crises are not only pressure. They are disclosure. They show us who we still quietly trust, where we still seek approval, and which inside doors we never fully closed.

The vocabulary here is mercilessly precise. The verse does not merely forbid public alliance. It forbids taking a **walijah** apart from Allah, His Messenger, and the believers. A **walijah** is an inside-entry point, an intimate channel, an inward access through which someone or something gets into the protected inner chamber of loyalty. It is a deeper word than acquaintance, softer friendship, or even strategic cooperation. It names intrusion into the interior.

That is why the verse should be read beside 3:118, where believers are told not to take **bitanah** from outside themselves, those who would not spare them corruption. The words are not identical, but they are close companions. **Bitanah** is the inner lining, the garment closest to the skin. **Walijah** is the inside way in. Together they expose how revelation thinks about allegiance: the greatest dangers are often not the loud enemies outside the wall, but the unexamined channels by which rival values keep entering within it.

Ibn Kathir reads verse 16 as one of the wisdoms of jihad: Allah tests the Muslims so the pure-hearted and false-hearted become manifest. Al-Tabari says the same in his own register, emphasizing that the believers are not left without ordeal but are sifted so their inward truth is made visible. The external struggle matters, but one of its deepest purposes is to bring hidden arrangements into the light. Allah does not only want to know whether you stood. He wants exposed what you were leaning on while you stood.

[PAUSE]

This becomes extremely uncomfortable when we stop imagining **walijah** only in political terms. A secret inner alliance can be a whole emotional system. It can be the approval of a social class you still refuse to disappoint. It can be an ideology whose moral instincts you trust more than revelation's. It can be a private addiction to praise. It can be the conviction that certain elite voices understand reality more deeply than Allah's Messenger. It can be a relationship whose permission you still require before you will fully submit to truth.

That is why tests can feel disproportionately exposing. They are not merely asking whether you can survive pain. They are revealing who gets the first consult in your heart when pain arrives. Whose voice becomes ultimate under pressure? Whose disapproval is catastrophic? Whose acceptance feels necessary? The answer to those questions often tells you where the **walijah** is.

The Qur'an returns to this theme in 60:1, where believers are warned not to take Allah's enemy and their enemy as intimate allies, extending to them affection while truth has already come. It also echoes in 33:10-11, where the confederate crisis shook believers until hearts reached throats and assumptions about Allah were exposed. Trials are not polite. They do not merely measure endurance. They uncover loyalties.

So verse 16 is not a side note after the emotional power of verses 13-15. It is the interpretive key. Necessary struggle is a sieve. It reveals who fears correctly, who heals correctly, and who still has an interior door cracked open to some rival sovereignty.

### Theme 3: Allah Does Not Shame Wounded Believers for Being Wounded, but He Refuses to Leave Them Ruled by Rage

One of the most arresting features of this passage is that it speaks openly about the inner aftermath of injury. *He will heal the chests of believing people. He will remove the rage of their hearts.* This is extraordinary language. The Qur'an is not pretending that aggression leaves the believers emotionally untouched. There is pain lodged in the chest. There is **ghayz** burning in the heart. Revelation names the wound without romanticizing it.

Al-Tabari preserves a historical concreteness here by relating the removal of rage to the believing allies of Khuza'ah, whose grief and anger had been sharpened by the aggression surrounding the treaty violations. Ibn Kathir likewise keeps the healing grounded in real injury rather than abstract spiritual mood. That matters because the Qur'an is not scolding the believers for having been wounded. It is acknowledging that prolonged betrayal and aggression produce inner compression that only Allah can finally relieve.

And yet the passage will not let rage become a permanent identity. The verb chain is instructive: **yansurkum**, **yashfi**, **yudhhib**. Victory is followed by healing, and healing is followed by the removal of rage. The point is not to enthrone grievance forever under a religious banner. The point is that Allah can move believers through necessary confrontation into a state where the wound no longer rules them. Rage is acknowledged, but it is not allowed to become sacred.

[PAUSE]

This is an urgently needed correction for religious people. Sometimes communities wounded by real injustice begin to organize their whole moral life around the wound. Memory becomes grievance-storage. Identity becomes an archive of what was done to us. Even correct causes can then be deformed by an inner addiction to rage, because rage gives clarity, energy, and a sense of innocence. The ego can feed on it for years while still speaking the language of justice.

But verse 15 says Allah removes the rage of their hearts. Not because the wrong never happened. Not because believers must be emotionally numb. But because a heart permanently ruled by **ghayz** is not a healed heart. It is still chained to the harm that entered it. The victory Allah promises is therefore not complete if the believers remain inwardly possessed by the very injury from which He delivered them.

This theme is mirrored elsewhere in the Qur'an in quieter forms. 8:10 speaks of divine aid bringing reassurance to hearts. 48:4 speaks of Allah sending sakinah into believers' hearts so faith increases with faith. These are not duplicates of 9:14-15, but they illuminate the same divine pattern: Allah does not only manage outward outcomes. He tends the interior condition of the believers so that struggle does not leave them spiritually deformed.

So a piercing question rises from this passage: what wounds in us have become more than wounds, have become identities, arguments, permissions, or ways of postponing surrender? We may be able to narrate every betrayal accurately and still not know how to ask Allah to remove the **ghayz** itself. The ayah teaches us that full deliverance is not only that the aggressor is checked. It is that the believer's chest is healed and his heart is no longer governed by burning.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Passage

This passage unfolds in three tightening turns. It begins by naming the moral case for confrontation and exposing the believers' fear-question directly: why fear them when Allah has more right to be feared? It then moves into a cascading chain of divine action through the believers' struggle, showing that punishment, disgrace, victory, healing, and the removal of rage are all part of one governed sequence. Finally, it turns the whole matter inward and upward by declaring that Allah does not leave believers untested; striving reveals who has kept no secret inner access point apart from Allah, His Messenger, and the believers. The architecture moves from reordered fear, to healed injury, to exposed allegiance.

### Questions to Carry

Who or what still becomes too large in your heart when obedience starts to cost you? Name the person, system, loss, or consequence that feels weightier than Allah in practice. Until that fear is named honestly, it will keep editing your obedience while pretending to be wisdom.

Where has rage settled into your chest in a way that now feels morally useful to you? Sit with whether a real wound has slowly become part of your identity. The passage does not shame you for being hurt, but it does ask whether you still want Allah to remove the burning itself.

What is the **walijah** in your life right now, the inside door through which a rival loyalty keeps entering? Do not answer too quickly. It may be a person, an ideology, a social world, a habit of needing approval, or a fear of exclusion that still gets the first and deepest consult inside your heart.

When you are tested, whose voice becomes decisive in you before revelation speaks? Trials do not only measure your endurance. They expose your inner chain of command.

Are you asking Allah only for outward victory over what opposes you, or also for inward healing from what that opposition has done to you? These ayahs promise more than triumph. They promise a chest healed enough to stop living from stored heat.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Allah uses necessary struggle to shrink created fears back to creaturely size, heal the burning inside wounded believers, and expose every hidden inner allegiance competing with Him.**

### The Closing Invitation

Bring these verses into the places where fear, injury, and loyalty are most tangled in you. Ask Allah not only to solve the conflict outside, but to reorder the heart inside it. Let Him show you what has become too large, what still burns, and what secret doorway remains open to a rival allegiance. Then ask for a courage deep enough to close that door and a healing deep enough to live without the fire that once ruled you.

### Du'a

O Allah, make Your weight greater in my heart than every created fear.  
Do not let people, loss, or pressure become larger to me than You.  
Heal the hurt that has settled in my chest and remove the rage that keeps trying to rule my heart.  
Expose every hidden **walijah** in me and close every inner door that competes with loyalty to You.  
Make my striving sincere, my allegiance clean, and my courage governed by faith.  
And let every trial drive me nearer to You, not deeper into fear, grievance, or divided loyalty.
