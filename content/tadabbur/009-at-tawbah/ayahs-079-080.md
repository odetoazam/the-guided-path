# They Mocked the Generous and the Poor Giver Alike

## Surah At-Tawbah (9:79-80)

[ayah:9:79] ٱلَّذِينَ يَلْمِزُونَ ٱلْمُطَّوِّعِينَ مِنَ ٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ فِى ٱلصَّدَقَـٰتِ وَٱلَّذِينَ لَا يَجِدُونَ إِلَّا جُهْدَهُمْ فَيَسْخَرُونَ مِنْهُمْ ۙ سَخِرَ ٱللَّهُ مِنْهُمْ وَلَهُمْ عَذَابٌ أَلِيمٌ

[ayah:9:80] ٱسْتَغْفِرْ لَهُمْ أَوْ لَا تَسْتَغْفِرْ لَهُمْ إِن تَسْتَغْفِرْ لَهُمْ سَبْعِينَ مَرَّةً فَلَن يَغْفِرَ ٱللَّهُ لَهُمْ ۚ ذَٰلَكَ بِأَنَّهُمْ كَفَرُوا۟ بِٱللَّهِ وَرَسُولِهِۦ ۗ وَٱللَّهُ لَا يَهْدِى ٱلْقَوْمَ ٱلْفَـٰسِقِينَ

*Alladhina yalmizuna al-mutawwi'ina mina al-mu'minina fi al-sadaqati wa alladhina la yajiduna illa juhdahum fa-yaskharuna minhum. Sakhira Allahu minhum wa lahum 'adhabun alim.*

*Istaghfir lahum aw la tastaghfir lahum. In tastaghfir lahum sab'ina marratan fa-lan yaghfira Allahu lahum. Dhalika bi-annahum kafaru bi-Allahi wa rasulihi wa Allahu la yahdi al-qawma al-fasiqin.*

**Those who mock the willing givers among the believers in matters of charity, and also those who find nothing to give except their utmost effort, and then ridicule them, Allah will mock them, and for them is a painful punishment. Ask forgiveness for them, or do not ask forgiveness for them; even if you ask forgiveness for them seventy times, Allah will never forgive them. That is because they disbelieved in Allah and His Messenger, and Allah does not guide the defiantly disobedient people.**

## Introduction

Some hearts do not merely refuse goodness for themselves. They cannot bear goodness in others either.

That is the social ugliness these verses uncover. The hypocrites are not content to remain stingy. They also need to puncture the dignity of anyone who gives. If a believer gives much, they sneer that it must be performance. If a believer gives little, they sneer that it is too small to matter. Either way, the target is the same: sincerity must not be allowed to stand in public without being humiliated.

[PAUSE]

Classical tafsir keeps the scene concrete. Reports gathered by Ibn Kathir and al-Tabari place these verses in the Tabuk atmosphere, when contributions were being gathered and the hypocrites used the donation line as another theatre for contempt. Wealthy believers who gave substantially were accused of ostentation. Poor believers who came with only a little were ridiculed as if their poverty made their sincerity worthless. The mockery fell on both ends because the real enemy was never amount. It was truthful offering.

Then the passage does something heavier. It turns from the market of public ridicule to the sanctuary of prophetic intercession and says: even repeated pleas for these people will not alter the verdict. That does not shrink divine mercy. It reveals how far a soul can travel in rejection. There is a stage at which persistent mockery, disbelief, and refusal are no longer minor stains in an otherwise soft heart. They have become residence.

The hook of the passage is this: **At-Tawbah teaches that hypocrisy hates sincere giving at every social level, because sincere giving exposes what hypocrisy does not want to become, and that if this rejection hardens long enough even repeated prophetic pleading does not undo the verdict it has chosen.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **ٱلْمُطَّوِّعِينَ**: Voluntary Giving Named as Self-Offered Readiness

The first crucial word in the verse is:

**al-mutawwi'in**.

The willing volunteers.

[PAUSE]

To hear what the Arabic is doing, we need a small tool first. An **active participle** in Arabic names the doer of an action as a recognizable kind of person: not just someone who acted once, but someone standing in a posture of doing. Then we notice the form. This word comes from **Form V**, built on the root **ṭ-w-ʿ**, which circles around willing compliance, pliancy, and non-coerced responsiveness.

That matters because the verse is not merely calling them "givers." It is naming them as people who came forward willingly, beyond what stinginess or compulsion would have produced. Al-Tabari hears them as believers giving in charity what Allah had not forcibly extracted from them. The voluntary quality is part of the praise.

Translation with "the willing givers" gets near the sense, but the Arabic carries a deeper texture. **Al-mutawwi'in** suggests a soul that has made itself responsive to Allah. The hand gives because the heart had already softened into willing availability. This is why hypocrisy finds them unbearable. Their giving is not only financial. It is revealing. It makes inner readiness visible.

[PAUSE]

The root itself sharpens the contrast. **Ṭawʿ** is the opposite of dragged compliance. These believers are not being pushed to the front by pressure alone. They have offered themselves. So when the hypocrites sneer at them, they are not simply mocking a transaction. They are mocking visible willingness to respond to Allah.

One reason this matters spiritually is that many of us still imagine the highest form of religion as correct duty performed under strain. That matters. But the Qur'an also honors something more tender: the servant who begins to move with a degree of inward willingness. The hypocrite cannot stand that sight because it exposes how different his own interior is. He may mimic outward belonging, but he does not want to become pliant before God.

And notice how this word is placed socially: **mina al-mu'minin**. From among the believers. The verse is careful to tie voluntary giving to iman itself. This is not decorative generosity floating free from theology. It is faith becoming visible as responsive action.

### 2. **جُهْدَهُمْ**: Allah Sees the Strain Hidden Inside a Small Gift

Then the verse turns from the affluent volunteer to the one at the opposite end:

**la yajiduna illa juhdahum**.

They find nothing except their utmost effort.

[PAUSE]

Here the tool is different. We are looking at a **noun of effort and capacity**, from the root **j-h-d**, the same root field that carries exertion, strain, and what a person can draw from himself under pressure. The Qur'an could have named a tiny amount and stopped there. Instead it names the cost hidden inside the amount.

That is what translation cannot fully carry. "Their utmost effort" gives the general meaning, but the Arabic points our attention away from quantity and toward strain. This is not only "all they had." It is "what cost them their effort, what lay at the edge of their ability."

Al-Tabari explains the word here as their **taqah**, their capacity, what they could manage. That exegetical choice matters. Allah is training the community not to read charity by the market's scale but by the giver's burden. The poor man's handful may contain more truth than the wealthy man's abundance because its ratio of cost is known only to God.

[PAUSE]

This creates one of the most beautiful social corrections in the surah. The hypocrites are looking at appearances. Revelation is looking at hidden cost. They see "little." Allah sees "juhd." They see a gift too small to impress. Allah sees a servant who reached to the edge of what he could bear.

That difference should change how we read each other. In worship, service, charity, and repentance, some of the most precious acts look unimpressive from outside because the observer cannot see what they cost. A quiet return to Allah after years of addiction may look small from a distance and feel like **juhd** from inside. A few dollars from a poor believer may look negligible to a spectator and feel like blood from the hand that gave it.

This word also protects the dignity of the poor. The verse does not say, "Those who had almost nothing and gave almost nothing." It names their effort, not their embarrassment. The Qur'an refuses to let poverty strip moral beauty from sacrifice. The poor believer is not pitied in the verse. He is honored.

### 3. **لَنْ**: The Door of Forgiveness Is Not Manipulated by Repetition

Then the second ayah turns abruptly and the key word becomes a particle:

**lan**.

**Fa-lan yaghfira Allahu lahum**.

Allah will never forgive them.

[PAUSE]

Particles in Arabic matter because they shape the whole atmosphere of a sentence. **Lan** is not the same as a simple negation. It is a forceful negation of what is being spoken about ahead. Here it seals the future result: no matter how the sentence is turned in human hope, this path does not lead to forgiveness.

The verse builds toward that closure with a sound pattern first. **Istaghfir lahum aw la tastaghfir lahum in tastaghfir lahum...** The repeated **gh-f-r** sound circles and circles, as though the door of forgiveness is being knocked from every possible grammatical angle. Ask forgiveness for them. Or do not ask. Even if you ask again and again. Then the chain closes with **fa-lan yaghfira Allahu lahum**. The same root remains, but the possibility has been shut.

That sonic closure is something translation rarely lets us feel. English gives you the meaning of the ruling. Arabic lets you hear the pleading build and then stop.

As for **sab'ina marratan**, classical tafsir is very clear that seventy here is not an invitation to arithmetic experimentation. Ibn Kathir and al-Tabari both preserve the Arab usage of such a number for emphatic abundance. The point is not that seventy-one might work. The point is that even multiplied intercession does not reverse a verdict rooted in chosen disbelief.

[PAUSE]

And that is why the verse's second half is so sobering. The issue is not that Allah has become ungenerous. The issue is named plainly: **bi-annahum kafaru bi-Allahi wa rasulihi**. They disbelieved in Allah and His Messenger. This is not a passing weakness being treated as final apostasy. It is a settled moral station.

The particle **lan** therefore teaches something spiritually serious. Divine mercy is vast, but it is not a tool by which a soul can indefinitely mock truth, reject truth, and still assume that external affiliation or others' pleading will erase what it has chosen to become. There is a point where repetition does not equal repentance.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Hypocrisy Attacks Sincerity at Both Ends of the Social Scale

One of the most psychologically revealing things in this passage is that hypocrisy mocks the rich giver and the poor giver alike. That means its scale is fundamentally dishonest. It is not trying to measure charity fairly and happening to be harsh. It is attacking sincerity wherever sincerity appears.

[PAUSE]

This is a pattern we still know. A wealthy believer gives substantially and people say it must be branding, image-management, tax strategy, or performative piety. A poor believer gives a little and people say it is sentimental, symbolic, too small to matter. At first those two judgments sound opposite. Spiritually they are identical. Both are attempts to keep sincere offering from retaining its dignity.

Why does the hypocritical heart do that? Because witnessed sincerity is threatening. It introduces an unwelcome comparison. It says, without speaking, that another way of being before Allah is possible. The mocker must either repent, admire, or diminish. Diminishing is usually easiest.

There is another Qur'anic echo here worth feeling. In 49:11 believers are forbidden from mocking one another because the one mocked may be better than the one mocking. That general command becomes particularly sharp in this passage because the people being mocked are not merely socially awkward or publicly vulnerable. They are in the middle of worship. The hypocrites turn somebody else's nearness to Allah into entertainment. That is why the sin feels so corrosive. It is not only rudeness. It is interference with the moral honor of obedience.

This is why 63:7 is such an important companion verse. There too hypocrisy reveals itself by trying to constrict the economy of generosity around the Messenger: "Do not spend on those who are with the Messenger of Allah." The issue is not just money. It is resentment at a believing atmosphere in which giving strengthens truth.

The Qur'an's response in 9:79 is devastating precisely because it refuses the mocker's scale. Allah does not say, "Actually the large donation was more useful" or "Actually the small donation had hidden strategic value." He goes deeper. He condemns the ridicule itself. He reveals that the social crime lies in treating sincere giving as a spectacle to puncture.

And there is a subtle distancing inside the verse as well. The mockers are spoken about in the third person. The passage does not honor their cynicism with intimate argument. It exposes them. That matters. Some forms of corruption are answered not by entering their performance but by naming them clearly before the community so nobody mistakes poison for insight. The verse is not fascinated with their commentary. It is interested in unmasking it.

[PAUSE]

That should make us more careful with our own tongues. Cynicism is often praised as discernment in modern life. We imagine that refusing to be moved by visible goodness proves maturity. But there is a fine line between discernment and a soul that no longer knows how to honor sincerity. If every generous act instantly produces suspicion in us, we should worry less about other people's hidden motives and more about our own inability to receive visible good without resentment.

There is also a communal lesson here. Believing communities are not protected only by correct theology. They are protected by an atmosphere in which sincere acts can survive without being routinely humiliated. If the generous are mocked as performative and the poor are mocked as pathetic, then only the hypocrite feels socially safe. The passage is teaching us that this atmosphere is itself a spiritual battlefield.

That also means the verse is not only speaking to the original mockers. It is speaking to the people around them who might have laughed, stayed silent, or quietly absorbed their scale. Hypocrisy often wins socially before it wins doctrinally. It teaches a room what to find embarrassing. It teaches a community which acts deserve eye-rolling. Once that atmosphere settles in, many sincere people stop offering what they could have offered because they do not want to become the next target. So protecting sincerity sometimes means refusing to become an audience for contempt.

The reports preserved in the tafsir make this painfully human. A rich donor and a poor donor both stood exposed before a community, and the hypocrites used both as opportunities for contempt. That means the disease is not class resentment alone. It is hostility to a world in which Allah can be loved from more than one economic station.

That is why this passage also stands so close to 2:264, where believers are warned not to nullify charity through reproach and injury. Even when a donation is materially useful, it can be morally wounded by the spirit surrounding it. Here the hypocrites attempt that injury from the outside. They try to drain meaning from the act by attaching mockery to it. The Qur'an answers by restoring the donor's honor and transferring humiliation to the mocker.

[PAUSE]

So the verse trains us in a counter-discipline: honor sincere effort where it appears, especially when its outward scale tempts the ego to trivialize it. If you cannot praise, at least do not poison. If you do not know the motive, do not use ignorance as permission to sneer. A community that learns that discipline protects not only donors, but its own moral future.

### Theme 2: Allah Measures Hidden Cost, Not Visible Amount

The word **juhdahum** should stay with us long after this passage ends. Allah did not frame the poor believer through smallness but through effort. That is a profound correction because human beings almost always read moral worth through visible quantity first.

[PAUSE]

We do it everywhere. We judge prayer by duration, service by publicity, sacrifice by optics, charity by numbers, grief by tears we can see, repentance by eloquence. But the Qur'an keeps redirecting us toward what only Allah sees easily: hidden cost, hidden willingness, hidden resistance overcome, hidden hunger still choosing to give.

This is why 2:261 and 2:271 are helpful companions. Charity is multiplied by Allah according to a scale far deeper than surface market math, and concealed giving is sometimes better for the giver's soul because it purifies intention. The Qur'an consistently retrains the eye away from public amount and toward divine valuation.

Another companion is 64:16: "So fear Allah as much as you are able." That verse does not lower the standard of sincerity. It locates sincerity inside real capacity. The servant is still answerable, but answerable within the truth of what he can actually carry. That is exactly the mercy hidden in **juhdahum**. Allah does not flatten the poor servant under the same outward metric as the affluent one and then call that justice. He reads the servant proportionately, truthfully, and without contempt.

That should humble both the affluent and the poor. The affluent should not assume largeness guarantees nobility. The poor should not assume smallness disqualifies beauty. Allah can accept and magnify either, because what He ultimately reads is not the size of the visible object but the truth carried within it.

[PAUSE]

This also has consequences beyond money. A person with abundant time may serve for hours while another barely steals fifteen minutes from a crushing schedule. A healthy worshipper may stand long in prayer while a sick believer prays briefly with enormous inward effort. A scholar may teach with ease while a beginner struggles to speak one truthful word. Human communities tend to notice the larger surface. Allah notices the hidden **juhd**.

There is deep tenderness in that. It means no one is spiritually locked out by having less. The poor believer in this verse is not told, "When you can finally give something meaningful, then you will matter." He matters already, because what he has brought is strain for Allah's sake. And strain offered honestly is never nothing in the divine scale.

This tenderness protects us from two opposite spiritual illnesses. The first is self-disqualification: "Because my offering is small, it is unworthy of Allah." The second is subtle vanity: "Because my offering is large, Allah must be especially pleased with me." Both mistakes come from staring at the object more than the heart. The Qur'an keeps moving our gaze back inward. The question is not merely what left your hand. The question is what had to happen inside you for that thing to leave your hand at all.

Al-Tabari's gloss of **juhd** as capacity is especially beautiful here because it means the verse is teaching proportion. Allah does not ask each servant for the same visible number. He asks for truth within the servant's actual range. That should remove both despair and arrogance. Despair says, "I have too little to matter." Arrogance says, "My greater amount must mean I matter more." The verse cuts both lies.

And this has implications for how we build communities of worship. If our communal culture only knows how to celebrate what is large, polished, and scalable, then we will eventually train the poor, shy, exhausted, sick, and frightened to assume that they have no meaningful place in the story of service. This verse refuses that training. It says the hidden sincerity of the one who gives through strain is fully legible to Allah even if the crowd barely notices.

[PAUSE]

One practical application is simple and difficult: do not despise your small truthful offerings. The prayer you managed in exhaustion, the apology you finally made, the little money you gave though fear was loud, the service you rendered though you had almost nothing left, the du'a you whispered in spiritual weakness, the lesson you taught though you felt unqualified. If it was **juhd** offered sincerely, it is not small in the only court that finally matters.

And just as importantly: do not despise the small truthful offerings of others. Communities become cruel when they only know how to celebrate spectacle. The Qur'an is building a community capable of recognizing worth hidden in strain.

### Theme 3: Mercy Is Vast, but It Is Not a Mechanism for Evading Chosen Rejection

Verse 80 is hard because it confronts a sentimental misunderstanding many people carry about mercy. We want mercy to mean that consequences can always be bypassed without actual return. The verse says otherwise.

[PAUSE]

The Messenger is told to seek forgiveness for them or not seek forgiveness for them; even multiplied pleading does not change the verdict. Why? Because the issue is not a minor lapse awaiting external repair. The issue is named: they disbelieved in Allah and His Messenger and settled into fisq. A heart can travel far enough in chosen rejection that borrowed pleading is no substitute for repentance.

This passage should be read beside 4:64, where seeking forgiveness around the Messenger is tied to living tawbah. There, intercession is surrounded by return. Here, intercession is severed from return. That contrast is everything. The Qur'an is not devaluing prophetic mercy. It is showing that prophetic mercy is not a bypass around a soul's determined refusal.

Another illuminating parallel is 63:5: when the hypocrites are told to come so the Messenger can seek forgiveness for them, they turn their heads and persist in arrogance. That verse lets us see the inner posture behind warnings like this one. The problem is not that forgiveness is scarce. The problem is that some hearts resist the very path by which forgiveness would be sought. They want the cover of mercy without the humility of return.

The later verse 9:84 intensifies this same horizon by forbidding prayer over certain hypocrites or standing at their graves. So 9:80 is not an isolated shock line. It belongs to a larger At-Tawbah pattern in which the surah keeps saying: do not confuse public belonging with inward truth. There are points at which the mask no longer protects the wearer from what he has become.

[PAUSE]

This should produce fear, but not despair. Fear, because a person can normalize mockery, contempt, and rejection for so long that the possibility of return itself begins feeling unwelcome. Not despair, because the verse is not addressed to the penitent. It is addressed to the case of hardened nifaq. The soul that still trembles before this warning is already not identical with the soul being described by it.

That distinction matters especially because the Qur'an also tells us in 39:53 not to despair of Allah's mercy. We do not honor 39:53 by emptying 9:80 of its seriousness, and we do not honor 9:80 by turning 39:53 into a lie. The reconciliation is moral, not mathematical. Mercy remains open to the one who returns. This warning falls on the soul that keeps choosing concealment, contempt, and refusal. The door is vast, but it is not entered by mocking the door.

There is also a social mercy in this harshness. If the community imagines that mockery of sincerity, contempt for sacrifice, and public corrosion of trust can all continue indefinitely while nothing decisive is ever named, then evil becomes soft and survivable in the public conscience. Verse 80 refuses that softness. It says there are trajectories that do not end lightly.

That warning is itself a gift. A community that still names hardening as hardening has not fully surrendered to it. The verse is severe, but severity here is serving rescue. It is trying to interrupt the long slide by which ridicule first becomes habit, then posture, then creed, then destiny.

Ibn Kathir's note on the rhetorical use of seventy is helpful because it removes false loopholes. The verse is not asking us to play numerical games with mercy. It is forcing us to hear finality where the heart prefers endless postponement. Some doors remain open to the one who repents. But the one who keeps choosing rejection cannot assume that other people's love will indefinitely outvote his own hardening.

[PAUSE]

That is a sobering mercy for us too. It reminds us not to outsource our return to the righteousness of people around us. A pious family, a righteous teacher, a prophetic legacy, a community's du'a, even the Messenger's pleading in the revealed context are not licenses to remain spiritually unserious. Mercy is invitation, not manipulation.

So the right response to this verse is not coldness toward sinners. It is seriousness about what repeated contempt for truth can do to a soul. We should become humbler, more urgent in our own repentance, more protective of sincere acts in public, and less casual about the kinds of habits that slowly convert the heart into a place where return no longer feels attractive.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Passage

These two verses are built on a pattern of **moral inversion under prophetic distance**. Human mockery falls downward on both ends of sincere giving: the abundant volunteer and the poor believer whose gift is mostly strain. Then the same root of ridicule returns upward in reversal: **sakhira Allahu minhum**. The mockers who tried to strip visible sincerity of dignity are themselves placed under divine humiliation. At the same time, the passage keeps them at a distance. They are exposed in the third person, while the only direct imperative is addressed to the Prophet: **istaghfir lahum aw la tastaghfir lahum**. That creates a second structural mirror. They are socially near enough to be discussed around the Messenger, yet spiritually distant enough that even prophetic pleading does not pull them back across the line they chose. So the architecture is not a simple sequence of charity and then forgiveness. It is a double reversal framed by pronoun distance: ridicule reversed upon the ridiculers, and presumed nearness to the Prophet reversed into exclusion from the benefit of his intercession.

The unit also hinges on scale. The hypocrites mismeasure both charity and mercy. They mismeasure charity by visible amount and mismeasure mercy as something still available without inward return. Revelation corrects both scales at once.

### Questions to Carry

How do you react when you see other believers giving themselves to Allah in ways that expose your own comfort?

Do you have a habit of treating visible sincerity with irony so you do not have to let it judge you?

Where in your life have you been tempted to despise a small offering, in yourself or someone else, because you were reading by quantity instead of hidden cost?

Have you quietly assumed that the righteousness of people around you can compensate for a hardness you are not urgently trying to repent from?

What would change if you really believed that Allah sees **juhd** more clearly than the world sees size?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These verses teach that hypocrisy mocks sincere giving at every scale because sincerity exposes it, while Allah measures hidden effort truly and warns that mercy is not manipulated by a heart determined to remain in rejection.**

### The Closing Invitation

Take this passage into the places where cynicism has begun to feel intelligent. Ask Allah to restore your ability to honor sincere goodness without needing to puncture it. Ask Him also to rescue you from despising your own small truthful offerings when they are all you have. Then let verse 80 make you serious about return: do not rely on borrowed nearness, inherited goodness, or other people's prayers while leaving your own heart unexamined. Protect sincerity. Become sincere. Return while the heart still wants to return.

### Du'a

O Allah, protect my heart from mocking the people who give for Your sake.  
Do not let cynicism feel wiser to me than sincerity.  
Teach me to honor the hidden cost of small truthful offerings, in myself and in others.  
Make me among those who respond to You willingly and do not despise the little good they can still bring.  
Do not let repeated compromise harden my heart until even mercy feels distant to me.  
Keep me urgent in repentance, soft toward sincere believers, and honest before You.  
And let my small offerings be accepted because You know the effort inside them.
