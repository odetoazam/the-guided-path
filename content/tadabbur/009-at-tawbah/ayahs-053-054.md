# A Resentful Offering Does Not Rise to Allah

## Surah At-Tawbah (9:53-54)

[ayah:9:53] قُلْ أَنفِقُوا۟ طَوْعًا أَوْ كَرْهًا لَّن يُتَقَبَّلَ مِنكُمْ ۖ إِنَّكُمْ كُنتُمْ قَوْمًا فَـٰسِقِينَ

[ayah:9:54] وَمَا مَنَعَهُمْ أَن تُقْبَلَ مِنْهُمْ نَفَقَـٰتُهُمْ إِلَّآ أَنَّهُمْ كَفَرُوا۟ بِٱللَّهِ وَبِرَسُولِهِۦ وَلَا يَأْتُونَ ٱلصَّلَوٰةَ إِلَّا وَهُمْ كُسَالَىٰ وَلَا يُنفِقُونَ إِلَّا وَهُمْ كَـٰرِهُونَ

*Qul anfiqu ta'w'an aw karhan lan yutaqabbala minkum innakum kuntum qawman fasiqin.*

*Wa ma mana'ahum an tuqbala minhum nafaqatuhum illa annahum kafaru bi-Allahi wa bi-rasulihi wa la ya'tuna al-salata illa wa hum kusala wa la yunfiquna illa wa hum karihun.*

**Say: spend willingly or unwillingly, it will never be accepted from you, because you have been a defiantly disobedient people. And nothing prevented their expenditures from being accepted except that they disbelieved in Allah and His Messenger, they do not come to prayer except lazily, and they do not spend except while resentful.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

These ayahs are about one of the most frightening possibilities in worship: that the act happens, but it does not rise.

The hypocrites may still spend. Money may still leave the hand. Outwardly, something measurable has occurred. But the Qur'an pierces through the external transaction and asks the only question that finally matters: is it accepted? Here the answer is brutal. Spend willingly or unwillingly, it will not be accepted from you.

[PAUSE]

Then Allah explains why. The issue is not a technical flaw in the donation. It is a larger spiritual condition. They disbelieved in Allah and His Messenger. They come to prayer lazily. They spend while resentful. So the problem is not merely that they gave too little. The problem is that their entire relation to Allah is broken, heavy, unwilling, and out of love with worship.

These ayahs should sober anyone who thinks religious life can be maintained through external throughput alone. Acts are not accepted merely because they were performed. They rise by faith, sincerity, reverence, and a heart that has not turned obedience into a burden it resents.

The hook of the passage is this: **At-Tawbah teaches that Allah is not only looking at whether worship happens outwardly, but whether disbelief, laziness, and resentment have already emptied that worship of acceptance before it arrives.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Anfiqu taw'an aw karhan**: Outward Spending Alone Does Not Secure Acceptance

Verse 53 says:

**anfiqu taw'an aw karhan**.

Spend willingly or unwillingly.

[PAUSE]

The phrasing is startling. The ayah momentarily brackets the outward difference and goes straight to the divine verdict beyond it. Even if the act takes place, the essential issue still remains unresolved.

Al-Tabari makes the point brutally plain: spend however you like, in this expedition or elsewhere, in willingness or dislike, and it will not be accepted from you because the deeper condition is corrupt. The verse is not interested in being impressed by the mere exit of money from the hand. It is pronouncing on what the act means before Allah.

### 2. **Lan yutaqabbala minkum**: The Real Question Is Acceptance

Then:

**lan yutaqabbala minkum**.

It will never be accepted from you.

[PAUSE]

The Qur'an keeps lifting us from performance to acceptance. Human beings often stop at the visible deed. Revelation asks what Allah received of it.

That is why the passive form matters. The ayah does not say merely "you spent." It says it will not be **accepted**. Ibn Kathir's framing of these verses keeps the same emphasis: the issue is not the surface occurrence of spending but the divine refusal of reception.

### 3. **Innakum kuntum qawman fasiqin**: Persistent Fisq Empties the Act

Then:

**innakum kuntum qawman fasiqin**.

You have been a defiantly disobedient people.

[PAUSE]

The refusal of acceptance is rooted in a broader condition of rebellion and خروج from obedience. The act of spending does not repair that state when it is not accompanied by real return.

Al-Tabari glosses their **fisq** here as خروج from faith and obedience, not a minor flaw riding alongside otherwise sound worship. The spending is being judged from inside a larger fracture.

### 4. **Wa ma mana'ahum**: The Verse Explicitly Names What Blocked Acceptance

Verse 54 says:

**wa ma mana'ahum an tuqbala minhum nafaqatuhum**.

What prevented their expenditures from being accepted?

[PAUSE]

The verse answers directly because believers need to know what really blocks ascent.

Qurtubi's discussion is useful here because he slows down on the grammar and then on the spiritual diagnosis. The obstacle to acceptance is not some hidden technicality. It is their disbelief, their lazy relationship to prayer, and their hateful posture in spending.

### 5. **La ya'tuna al-salata illa wa hum kusala ... wa la yunfiquna illa wa hum karihun**: Laziness and Resentment Reveal the State of Worship

Then the reasons include:

**wa la ya'tuna al-salata illa wa hum kusala**.

They do not come to prayer except lazily.

And:

**wa la yunfiquna illa wa hum karihun**.

They do not spend except while resentful.

[PAUSE]

This is not merely about effort level. It is about the emotional and spiritual posture carried into worship. Salah is heavy. Spending is hated. The heart is not moving toward Allah in longing, reverence, or surrender.

Qurtubi, citing Ibn 'Abbas here, says the lazy one is the person who if he is with الجماعة he prays, and if alone he does not; he neither hopes the prayer's reward nor fears punishment for leaving it. That is a devastating description. Laziness here is not just low energy. It is a thin relation to Allah in which worship has stopped feeling consequential. And Qurtubi says of their spending that they treat it as **maghram**, a burdensome loss, whereas withholding it feels like gain. That is exactly why it is not accepted.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Worship Is Not Measured Only by Occurrence but by Reception

These ayahs teach us to fear a purely external religion. It is possible for the body to perform and the hand to release while the act remains spiritually empty.

[PAUSE]

That is why the salaf feared rejection even after doing the deed. They understood that acceptance is the true finish line.

Modern religious life can make this fear strangely difficult to sustain because we are surrounded by visible metrics. We can count donations, attendance, volunteer hours, recitation streaks, posts, projects, initiatives, and public signs of involvement. Outward throughput is everywhere measurable. And because it is measurable, the nafs finds it very easy to confuse measurement with worth.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs destroy that confusion in one stroke. Spend willingly or unwillingly, it will not be accepted from you. The terrifying thing is that the external act is not denied. It happened. The money left the hand. The religious form took place. But the act still did not ascend as accepted worship. This means a person can achieve visible religious output while standing before Allah with spiritual bankruptcy.

Al-Tabari's wording cuts especially deep here because he does not let the expenditure itself carry any moral romance. However you spend, if the larger relation to Allah is broken, the act is not received. Ibn Kathir's treatment moves in the same direction. The verse is announcing that Allah is not captive to our outward religious productivity.

The Qur'an returns to this unsettling distinction elsewhere. In 2:262-264, spending can be ruined by reminders of generosity and harm. In 47:38, some are called to spend in Allah's path yet withhold, and the withholding is shown to reveal a deeper poverty in the soul. In 107:4-6, prayer itself can be present and still belong to those heedless of it, showing off before people. The Book keeps insisting that acts do not become precious merely because they happened. Their inner truth matters.

[PAUSE]

That should create a healthy dread in the religious person. Not the dread that paralyzes action, but the dread that keeps action humble. A person can build an identity around being "someone who gives," "someone who serves," "someone who shows up," while never asking the more terrifying question: did Allah receive any of it? The ego hates that question because it destabilizes all visible capital. It means your religious resume may not be your spiritual reality.

This theme presses hardest on those of us who know how to maintain form while the heart is deteriorating. We may still donate because the community expects it. We may still pray because the schedule carries us. We may still keep enough outward practice to preserve identity and belonging. But a person can be slipping inwardly while the dashboard still looks respectable. That is why acceptance is such a frightening category. It moves the center of judgment where only Allah can see.

And this is mercy, not cruelty. Allah tells us this because He does not want us to be fooled by our own throughput. If He only counted visible acts, hypocrites could live and die mistaking motion for ascent. The warning protects the believer from that delusion. It teaches us to do the deed, yes, but then to keep begging for qabool as though the deed by itself guaranteed nothing.

### Theme 2: Resentment in Obedience Is a Serious Spiritual Warning

The phrase about spending while resentful should make us pause. A believer can struggle, yes. A believer can feel cost, yes. But when resentment settles into the act itself and becomes its defining interior weather, something dangerous is happening.

[PAUSE]

The heart is beginning to experience Allah's claim as intrusion rather than honor.

This distinction matters because the Qur'an does not ask believers to be emotionless. A person may feel the sting of sacrifice, the pull of attachment, the fatigue of prayer, the difficulty of giving up money he loves. That is not hypocrisy by itself. But these ayahs describe something heavier: **karihun**. The act is approached in a state of dislike, aversion, grievance. The heart is not merely feeling cost. It is leaning against Allah's right to ask it.

Qurtubi's language here is especially sharp. They consider the spending **maghram**, a burdensome loss, and imagine that keeping it back would be **maghnam**, gain. That inversion tells you everything. Once obedience begins to feel like unfair extraction and disobedience begins to feel like intelligent self-protection, the soul is already in dangerous territory.

[PAUSE]

Most people do not narrate this honestly to themselves. They say, "I'm just tired," "I need balance," "this is a lot right now," "I give enough already," "I don't want to be taken advantage of," "I have to think about my future." Some of those statements can be true in the right place. But the heart can also hide resentment inside them. It can turn Allah's claim into a burden story and then decorate that story with practical language.

This is why the pairing with lazy prayer is so important. The verse is not isolating one reluctant donation. It is describing a whole worship atmosphere. Prayer is entered sluggishly. Spending is done resentfully. Faith itself is cracked. The problem is not a single emotion. It is a settled posture toward Allah in which worship has become heavy because the heart is no longer lovingly aligned with the One being worshipped.

The Qur'an gives us the opposite picture elsewhere. In 76:8-9 the righteous feed others despite loving the food themselves and do so only for Allah's face, asking for neither reward nor thanks from people. In 3:92 believers are told they will not attain birr until they spend from what they love. These verses do not say sacrifice will feel costless. They say the cost can be carried inside love rather than inside grievance.

[PAUSE]

That is where this theme becomes very personal. What is the emotional weather of your obedience lately? When you pray, is the heart late even if the body is present? When you give, does something in you mutter that Allah is taking too much? When you obey in costly matters, do you feel honored to have been asked or quietly irritated that the command touched something you wanted to keep untouched?

Resentment in worship is spiritually dangerous because it trains the heart to experience Allah's rights as interference. If that posture deepens, a person can keep enough form to look religious while inwardly becoming allergic to surrender. The verse names that danger before it hardens completely.

### Theme 3: Lazy Prayer and Bitter Spending Belong to the Same Spiritual Climate

Verse 54 is striking because it does not list random faults. It gathers disbelief, lazy prayer, and resentful spending into one coherent atmosphere. The point is not only that they did several bad things. The point is that these things belong together.

[PAUSE]

Qurtubi's citation from Ibn 'Abbas clarifies the laziness of prayer in a revealing way: the hypocrite prays when seen and neglects it when alone. He neither hopes reward nor fears punishment. This means **kusala** is not simply physical sluggishness. It is worship without awakened stakes. Prayer has become a social movement rather than a living meeting with Allah.

Once that happens, spending is almost guaranteed to sour as well. If prayer is no longer a place of nearness, then money spent for Allah will not feel like privilege. It will feel like depletion. If the standing before Allah is hollow, the giving for Allah will also hollow out. That is why the verse moves from disbelief to lazy prayer to hateful spending. These are not disconnected symptoms. They are one climate.

The Qur'an makes this climate visible in other places too. In 4:142 the hypocrites stand for prayer sluggishly, only to be seen by people, remembering Allah but little. In 63:9-10 wealth and children are named as distractions that can pull the heart away until death approaches and regret erupts. These passages together show that worship weakens as the soul's real attachments harden elsewhere. What weakens first is not always form. Often what weakens first is inward willingness.

[PAUSE]

This should make us more careful in self-diagnosis. We often isolate our religious problems. "My prayer is weak, but my heart is okay." "I'm struggling financially, so of course giving feels heavy." "I still do the acts, so things cannot be that serious." But the verse suggests that when multiple obediences begin to feel heavy in the same way, a deeper interior climate may be changing. The issue may not be one act. The issue may be the heart's relation to Allah across acts.

The mercy of this passage is that it shows us the pattern while return is still possible. If prayer has become sluggish and giving has become bitter, do not only fix the schedule and the budget. Ask what has happened to faith, awe, hope, and fear in the heart. Ask why Allah's rights are no longer tasting the same. The verse is not merely shaming the hypocrites. It is teaching believers how to recognize the beginnings of worship collapse before the shell remains and the life disappears.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Passage

These two ayahs move from verdict to diagnosis. First, the verdict comes down with frightening finality: spend willingly or unwillingly, it will not be accepted. Then the second ayah pulls back the veil and explains the blocked ascent: disbelief in Allah and His Messenger, sluggish prayer, and resentful spending. The structure matters because it teaches that rejection is not arbitrary. It has an inner logic. A broken relation to Allah creates a climate in which worship still happens outwardly but no longer rises inwardly.

### Questions to Carry

Am I satisfied merely that the deed happened, or do I still ache to know whether Allah accepted it? Sit with how rarely you may ask for qabool compared to how often you count outward completion.

Where has laziness begun to characterize my prayer in a way that says something deeper than tiredness? Be honest about whether the body is arriving while the heart has quietly stopped expecting meeting, reward, and consequence.

When I give, do I experience Allah's claim over my wealth as an honor or as a burden I grudgingly tolerate? Listen carefully to the inner tone. It often reveals more than the amount.

Have multiple acts of worship begun to feel heavy in the same spiritual way at once? If so, what does that suggest about the climate of my heart rather than about each act in isolation?

What would change in my religious life if I feared rejection almost as much as I fear omission? This passage asks for that kind of seriousness.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Allah is not only looking at whether worship happened, but at whether disbelief, laziness, and resentment hollowed it out before it could reach the station of acceptance.**

### The Closing Invitation

Take these ayahs into your next prayer and your next act of giving. Do not only ask Allah to help you complete them. Ask Him to let them rise. Ask Him to rescue you from a religious life built on visible throughput and inward erosion. And if you find resentment, dullness, or heaviness settling across multiple acts, do not manage the symptoms only. Return to the heart before the shell of worship survives without its life.

### Du'a

O Allah, do not let my worship become a shell that moves without rising.  
Grant me concern for acceptance greater than my concern for appearance.  
Rescue my prayer from laziness and my giving from resentment.  
Do not let my heart experience Your rights as intrusion or burden.  
Make faith, hope, fear, and love alive beneath every act I offer You.  
And accept from me what is small, purify what is mixed, and return me before my worship becomes hollow.
