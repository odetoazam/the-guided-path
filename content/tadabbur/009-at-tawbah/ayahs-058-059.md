# If Your Contentment Depends on Your Share, You Have Not Yet Learned Charity

## Surah At-Tawbah (9:58-59)

[ayah:9:58] وَمِنْهُم مَّن يَلْمِزُكَ فِى ٱلصَّدَقَـٰتِ فَإِنْ أُعْطُوا۟ مِنْهَا رَضُوا۟ وَإِن لَّمْ يُعْطَوْا۟ مِنْهَآ إِذَا هُمْ يَسْخَطُونَ

[ayah:9:59] وَلَوْ أَنَّهُمْ رَضُوا۟ مَآ ءَاتَىٰهُمُ ٱللَّهُ وَرَسُولُهُۥ وَقَالُوا۟ حَسْبُنَا ٱللَّهُ سَيُؤْتِينَا ٱللَّهُ مِن فَضْلِهِۦ وَرَسُولُهُۥٓ إِنَّآ إِلَى ٱللَّهِ رَٰغِبُونَ

*Wa minhum man yalmizuka fi al-sadaqati fa-in u'tu minha radu wa in lam yu'taw minha idha hum yaskhatun.*

*Wa law annahum radu ma atahumu Allahu wa rasuluhu wa qalu hasbuna Allah sayu'tina Allahu min fadlihi wa rasuluhu inna ila Allahi raghibun.*

**And among them are those who criticize you regarding the charities. If they are given from them, they are pleased; but if they are not given from them, at once they are angry. If only they had been content with what Allah and His Messenger gave them, and had said, "Allah is enough for us. Allah will give us from His bounty, and so will His Messenger. Surely we are turning our desire toward Allah."**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

These ayahs reveal a kind of religiosity whose real god is distribution.

The hypocrite may speak the language of fairness, principle, or moral concern. But the Qur'an tests the claim with one devastating measure: what happens to his face when he gets a share, and what happens when he does not? If he is given, he is pleased. If he is not, he is suddenly angry. The issue is exposed. The complaint was never first about Allah's right, or communal justice, or purified concern for the weak. It was about the self's relation to its portion.

[PAUSE]

Then the next verse offers one of the most beautiful alternatives in the surah. They should have been content with what Allah and His Messenger gave. They should have said: Allah is enough for us. Allah will give us from His bounty. And we are those who desire Allah. That is astonishing. The cure for greed here is not merely "be less materialistic." It is to learn where desire should ultimately face.

This means contentment is not just emotional calm. It is theological orientation. When desire is really directed to Allah, one's reaction to share, portion, and withholding is transformed. But when the heart remains self-centered, even charity can become a mirror exposing greed dressed as grievance.

Ibn Kathir says plainly that the critics here were not speaking in defense of religion but out of appetite for more. Al-Tabari explains **yalmizuka** as fault-finding and stabbing criticism against the Prophet in the matter of alms. Al-Muyassar reads verse 59 as the healthy alternative: to say Allah is sufficient, to hope for His bounty, and to desire an opening from Him rather than to be trapped inside the share of the moment. Together they show the passage doing two things at once: exposing corrupted desire and then healing desire.

The hook of the passage is this: **At-Tawbah teaches that our response to distribution reveals whether our satisfaction rests in Allah or in our portion, and that real contentment is born when desire itself is turned back toward Him.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Yalmizuka fi al-sadaqat**: The Complaint Targets Sacred Distribution

Verse 58 says:

**wa minhum man yalmizuka fi al-sadaqat**

Among them are those who criticize you regarding the charities.

[PAUSE]

The verb **yalmizuka** comes from the root **l‑m‑z**, a field of sniping criticism, taunting fault-finding, piercing someone verbally with belittling insinuation. This is not the language of a sincere person seeking clarification from the Messenger. It is the language of someone circling a sacred institution with corrosive suspicion.

And the object of the criticism matters:

**fi al-sadaqat**

regarding the charities, the alms.

The root **s‑d‑q** is the same field from which truthfulness and sincerity emerge. Sadaqah is not just money changing hands. It is wealth made truthful through surrender to Allah's command. So the hypocrisy is doubled here: they turn even the arena of purification into a theater for grievance.

Ibn Kathir points out the moral ugliness sharply: they question the Prophet's integrity in distribution even though their own motives are what deserve suspicion. The ayah is therefore not only about money. It is about what happens when the ego enters sacred distribution and tries to bend it back toward itself.

This also matters because complaints around money can sound morally elevated very quickly. The language of justice is easy to borrow. The ayah does not deny that questions of allocation can matter. It simply insists that the questioner himself be examined.

### 2. **Fa-in u'tu minha radu**: Satisfaction Is Tied to Receipt

Then the verse shows how the heart is tested:

**fa-in u'tu minha radu**

If they are given from it, they are pleased.

[PAUSE]

The passive **u'tu** is telling. The verse does not name the giver first. It focuses on the fact of receipt. If they are given. If something lands in their direction. The center of gravity is the self receiving.

Then:

**radu**

they are pleased, content, satisfied.

The root **r‑d‑w** carries pleasure, acceptance, contentment, willing assent. In the Qur'an, it is one of the noblest states when directed properly: Allah being pleased with His servants, and believers being pleased with Him. Here that same field is profaned. Their so-called contentment is not rooted in Allah's wisdom. It is rooted in possession.

This is spiritually devastating because it teaches us how counterfeit contentment works. Counterfeit contentment is cheerful only while the self is being fed. It looks calm. It sounds grateful. But it is structurally conditional.

So the معيار, the hidden criterion, is exposed. Not truth. Not fairness as such. Not purified concern for the needy. The criterion is: did I get mine?

### 3. **Wa in lam yu'taw minha idha hum yaskhatun**: Withholding Immediately Reveals the Heart

Then the verse flips the scene:

**wa in lam yu'taw minha idha hum yaskhatun**

If they are not given from it, at once they are angry.

[PAUSE]

Again the passive construction matters. Not given. The event is enough. The self has met deprivation, and the hidden god is exposed.

The phrase **idha hum** is one of those Qur'anic turns that makes the exposure feel sudden and visible. Suddenly they are. Immediately they are. The mask does not take long to fall.

And the chosen verb is not mild irritation:

**yaskhatun**

they become angry, resentful, bitterly displeased.

The root **s‑kh‑t** is the opposite field to **rida**. That pairing is deliberate. Pleased when fed, angry when denied. The entire religion of the nafs is condensed into two verbs. My رضا follows my portion. My سخط follows its absence.

That is why this ayah is so universal. It is about charity in its immediate setting, but the pattern reaches much further. Give the ego recognition, access, credit, influence, closeness, attention, and it settles. Withhold those, and suddenly the language of principle becomes heat.

The verse does not say all unhappiness at withholding is hypocrisy. Human need is real. Injustice is real. But it does tell us that sudden emotional volatility around portions can reveal more about our worship than our speeches do.

### 4. **Law annahum radu ma atahumu Allahu wa rasuluhu**: The Healed Posture Begins with Contentment

Verse 59 opens with a longing construction:

**wa law annahum radu ma atahumu Allahu wa rasuluhu**

If only they had been content with what Allah and His Messenger gave them.

[PAUSE]

The Qur'an now models the alternative. It does not first say: if only they had argued more politely. It does not first say: if only they had concealed their complaint better. It goes straight to the heart of the illness: contentment.

And the wording is beautiful:

**ma atahumu Allahu wa rasuluhu**

what Allah and His Messenger gave them.

The gift is framed inside revelation and prophetic trust, not random allocation. That matters because healing begins when the heart stops relating to portions as if they were detached from Allah's wise ordering.

Al-Tabari explains this as contentment with what Allah apportioned and what the Messenger distributed. So the verse is not separating divine decree from prophetic administration. It is teaching the believer to see both under one field of surrendered acceptance.

### 5. **Hasbuna Allah ... inna ila Allahi raghibun**: Sufficiency, Hope, and Desire Are Reordered

Then comes one of the most healing sequences in the surah:

**hasbuna Allah**

Allah is enough for us.

Then:

**sayu'tina Allahu min fadlihi wa rasuluhu**

Allah will give us from His bounty, and so will His Messenger.

And finally:

**inna ila Allahi raghibun**

Surely we are those who turn our desire toward Allah.

[PAUSE]

The noun **hasb** means sufficiency, enoughness, what adequately covers your need. This is not resignation. It is a declaration of theological sufficiency. My need is not finally trapped inside today's portion.

Then the future verb:

**sayu'tina**

He will give us.

Hope is preserved. The verse is not teaching deadened indifference. The believer may still hope, ask, await opening, and seek provision. But he does so without rage because his hope is not chained to immediate receipt.

And then the summit:

**raghibun**

those who desire, incline eagerly, direct longing.

The root **r‑gh‑b** carries desire, aspiration, yearning inclination. The verse does not kill desire. It sanctifies it. You do not become spiritually healthy by ceasing to want. You become healthy by learning where wanting should face.

That is why the line ends not with "we desire the share," but:

**ila Allahi**

toward Allah.

The direction of longing is the whole cure.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Grievance Often Reveals What the Heart Thinks It Deserves

These ayahs help us read our discontent more honestly. Not every complaint is fake. Not every objection is greed. There are real injustices, and Islam does not ask the oppressed to romanticize unfairness.

But many complaints, including very religious-sounding ones, are not first about principle. They are about entitlement wounded in public.

[PAUSE]

This is why the Qur'an gives such a blunt test:

If they are given, they are pleased.

If they are not given, they are angry.

In two short movements the ayah asks a devastating question: is your moral concern stable across gain and loss, or does it change its tone based on whether the self has been fed?

This matters because grievance is one of the easiest emotions to baptize. We can take a real ache of the ego and quickly translate it into the rhetoric of fairness, standards, values, integrity, transparency, or respect. And sometimes those categories are genuinely relevant. But the verse asks us to perform a harder audit:

Would I still sound this principled if my own portion were secure?

Would I still be this heated if my name had been included, if my role had been affirmed, if my access had been preserved, if my share had arrived?

That question does not solve every case, but it often exposes far more than we want it to.

Ibn Kathir says the hypocrites did not speak this way in defense of religion but to gain more for themselves. That line should stay with us. It means a person can sound as though he is defending moral order while in reality defending his appetite.

The human self hates this possibility, because we prefer to imagine that our strongest emotions are our most truthful ones. The Qur'an says not necessarily. Strong feeling may mean only that the ego has touched an unhealed claim.

This applies far beyond charity.

A colleague receives credit and suddenly we discover "principles" about process.

A sibling is favored and suddenly family ethics become our burning cause.

A community role goes elsewhere and suddenly we are full of concern for institutional health.

A friend receives ease, marriage, acceptance, provision, or visibility, and suddenly our speech grows more analytical, more skeptical, more burdened with nuanced critique.

Again, critique can be real. But the verse teaches that the self's relation to its share must always be interrogated.

That is why the passage is so psychologically merciful. It saves us from flattering ourselves too quickly. It teaches us to ask not only whether something was right or wrong, but why the issue burns as hot as it does inside us. Sometimes what has been injured is not justice but self-importance.

And if that is true, then the first repair is not argument. It is repentance.

[PAUSE]

Because a heart that mistakes entitlement for principle will eventually turn even sacred institutions into mirrors for its own hunger.

### Theme 2: Contentment Is Not Passivity; It Is a Form of Tawhid

The alternative verse is astonishing because it does not simply say, "Be quiet and accept your lot." It says:

**radu ma atahumu Allahu wa rasuluhu**

be content with what Allah and His Messenger gave.

**hasbuna Allah**

Allah is enough for us.

**sayu'tina Allahu min fadlihi**

Allah will give us from His bounty.

This is not deadness. It is a theology of sufficiency, hope, and surrender all at once.

[PAUSE]

Contentment in the Qur'an is not the absence of desire. It is the refusal to let desire dethrone Allah.

That is why this passage is really about tawhid. The hypocrite's emotional state is governed by portion. The healed believer's emotional state is governed by Allah.

If the portion comes, he thanks Allah.

If it does not come now, he still says Allah is enough.

If he needs more, he hopes in Allah's future bounty.

Do you see the difference? The locus of stability has shifted.

This is one reason **hasbuna Allah** is so powerful across the Qur'an. In 3:173 it is spoken under threat, when fear is being weaponized. Here it is spoken under deprivation, when dissatisfaction is being weaponized. In both cases the statement does the same work: it recenters the heart under Allah instead of under the circumstance.

Al-Tabari's reading keeps the verse beautifully balanced. The believer is content with what has been given, but he is also allowed to say Allah will yet give from His bounty. That means contentment does not kill hope. It purifies hope.

This is crucial because many people imagine spiritual maturity means wanting nothing. The Qur'an does not say that. The Qur'an says: want, but want under tawhid. Ask, but ask without resentment. Hope, but hope without making your current deprivation into a theological emergency. Let the heart remain stable enough that receipt does not intoxicate it and withholding does not unravel it.

This also exposes a subtle form of shirk in the emotional sense, not in formal creed. When the heart behaves as though its sufficiency depends on one created allocation, one created person, one created institution, or one created door opening, then the creature has been given a weight in the emotional economy that belongs only to Allah.

The verse heals that by restoring sufficiency:

Allah is enough.

Not because creatures do not matter.

But because no creature is the final owner of what sustains you.

This is why contentment is not weakness. It is one of the strongest forms of inner freedom. A content person can still work, ask, plan, negotiate, improve systems, and seek justice. But he does so without groveling emotionally before portions. His dignity remains intact because his heart is not kneeling before the share.

That kind of contentment is not easy. It requires repeated remembrance that Allah's خزائن, His treasuries, are not exhausted by today's distribution. It requires believing that the Messenger's administration is not separate from divine wisdom. And it requires deep surrender when the nafs is offended.

[PAUSE]

But when it settles, it produces a human being who cannot be reduced to whatever was or was not placed in his hand.

### Theme 3: Desire Must Be Redirected, Not Merely Suppressed

The most beautiful phrase in the passage may be the final one:

**inna ila Allahi raghibun**

Surely we are those who direct our longing toward Allah.

This is an extraordinary ending because it tells us the problem was never desire itself.

[PAUSE]

The hypocrites desired. Of course they desired. The angry person in verse 58 is not desireless. He is intensely desirous. The problem is that his desire is collapsed into portion. He wants through the share, for the share, around the share. His inner world is chained to distribution.

The Qur'an does not answer by saying: become numb. Desire nothing. Expect nothing. Care about nothing.

It answers by giving desire a qiblah.

Toward Allah.

That single reorientation changes everything.

When desire faces creation as ultimate, the heart becomes volatile. Every allocation feels final. Every withholding feels humiliating. Every comparison becomes combustible. Every institution starts to look like a gatekeeper of your worth.

But when desire faces Allah, created portions shrink back into proportion. They still matter, but they no longer define the soul's horizon. You can want and still remain free. You can hope and still remain soft. You can be denied and still remain dignified.

Al-Muyassar's comment is helpful here: they should have desired that Allah expand provision for them and free them from dependence on charity and on people's shares. In other words, even their need could have been turned upward. Instead of clinging downward in resentment, they could have faced upward in raghbah.

This has broad application in spiritual life. Many of our ugliest emotions are not products of too much desire but of misdirected desire.

We want recognition, but what we really need is acceptance with Allah.

We want a share in a system, but what we really need is trust in Allah's bounty.

We want inclusion, but what we really need is a heart not orphaned from divine sufficiency.

We want reassurance through visible allocation, while Allah is inviting us to a deeper reassurance: **hasbuna Allah**.

This is why suppressive spirituality often fails. If you merely tell the nafs, "Do not want," it will either become fake and repressed or erupt elsewhere. The Qur'an chooses a wiser path. It acknowledges longing, then teaches longing where to go.

That is spiritually transformative because once longing is redirected, even worldly things can be sought more cleanly. You can ask for provision without worshipping it. You can work for rightful distribution without being devoured by it. You can receive gladly without making receipt the proof of Allah's love. You can be denied without treating denial as abandonment.

And perhaps that is the deepest beauty of this passage: it takes one of the most humiliating human scenes, a person angry over his share, and answers it not only with rebuke but with a way back to nobility. Say Allah is enough. Hope in His bounty. Face your desire toward Him.

[PAUSE]

That is not less human than greed. It is more human, because it restores desire to the One who made desire in the first place.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Passage

These two ayahs are built as a contrast between corrupted desire and healed desire.

Verse 58 begins with public criticism in the sacred space of charity distribution. The complaint is then tested immediately, not by argument but by reaction: if they receive, they are pleased; if they are withheld from, they are angry. So the verse moves from speech to motive by exposing the emotional trigger beneath the criticism.

Verse 59 then rebuilds the same inner world in the opposite order. It starts with contentment, moves to the interpretation of the gift as coming from Allah and His Messenger, then teaches a speech-act of sufficiency, then opens future hope in divine bounty, and finally lands at the deepest layer: the direction of desire itself toward Allah.

So the structure is:

criticism -> receipt -> pleasure -> withholding -> anger

versus

contentment -> attribution to Allah -> sufficiency -> hopeful future gift -> longing toward Allah

That symmetry is the whole lesson. The Qur'an does not merely condemn a bad attitude. It shows us a disordered internal architecture and then gives us a healed internal architecture to replace it.

### Questions to Carry

When I become agitated over distribution, recognition, or allocation, what is actually being threatened in me?

Is it justice, or is it my portion? Is it principle, or is it my pride at not being included? The difference is spiritually enormous.

Would I still speak with the same moral intensity if my own share were secure?

This question may feel uncomfortable, but it is one of the most merciful diagnostics in the passage. It can separate wounded conscience from wounded entitlement.

Can I sincerely say **hasbuna Allah** when I am denied something I deeply wanted?

Not as a slogan, but as a lived truth. If that sentence feels impossible in deprivation, it may be showing you where created things have become too central in your emotional world.

What does my heart really mean by contentment?

Does my contentment collapse the moment the share changes, or has it become rooted in Allah enough that portions no longer own my inner weather?

Where is my deepest raghbah facing right now?

Toward Allah, or toward the created thing I think will finally settle me? The answer to that question explains much of our peace and much of our unrest.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs expose the heart that turns sacred distribution into grievance because its satisfaction is chained to its share, then heal that heart by reordering contentment, sufficiency, hope, and desire back toward Allah.**

### The Closing Invitation

The invitation of this passage is to examine every place where your emotional stability depends too heavily on what was placed in your hand. When you feel overlooked, under-credited, under-provided, or under-recognized, pause before constructing a moral case and ask what your heart is worshipping in that moment. Then practice the Qur'an's replacement liturgy: be content with what Allah has written for now, say **hasbuna Allah** until the sentence becomes real, hope in His bounty without rage, and keep turning the face of desire back toward Him. That is how grievance becomes ibadah instead of poison.

### Du'a

Allahumma, protect our hearts from making portions into idols.

Do not let our pleasure and anger rise and fall only with what we receive from the world.

Make us content with what You and Your Messenger have given, and make **hasbuna Allah** true in us when we feel denied.

Open for us from Your bounty without making us slaves to our share.

Turn our deepest raghbah toward You, so that what we seek in creation never dethrones the One we were made to desire most.
