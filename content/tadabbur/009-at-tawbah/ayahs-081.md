# They Chose Shade Over Striving and Forgot a Hotter Fire

## Surah At-Tawbah (9:81)

[ayah:9:81] فَرِحَ ٱلْمُخَلَّفُونَ بِمَقْعَدِهِمْ خِلَـٰفَ رَسُولِ ٱللَّهِ وَكَرِهُوٓا۟ أَن يُجَـٰهِدُوا۟ بِأَمْوَٰلِهِمْ وَأَنفُسِهِمْ فِى سَبِيلِ ٱللَّهِ وَقَالُوا۟ لَا تَنفِرُوا۟ فِى ٱلْحَرِّ ۗ قُلْ نَارُ جَهَنَّمَ أَشَدُّ حَرًّا ۚ لَّوْ كَانُوا۟ يَفْقَهُونَ

*Fariha al-mukhallafuna bi-maq'adihim khilafa rasuli Allahi wa karihu an yujahidu bi-amwalihim wa anfusihim fi sabili Allahi wa qalu la tanfiru fi al-harr. Qul naru Jahannama ashaddu harran law kanu yafqahun.*

**Those left behind rejoiced in their sitting back behind the Messenger of Allah, and they hated to strive with their wealth and their selves in the way of Allah. They said, "Do not go out in the heat." Say: the fire of Hell is far hotter, if only they understood.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

Tabuk was not a gentle summons. It came in punishing heat, at a long distance, against a formidable northern power, while Madinah's fruits were ripe and the body had many reasons to argue for postponement. The surah has already shown us excuses, hesitation, mockery, bargaining, and the instinct to remain near shade while the Messenger of Allah moved toward cost. Then this ayah gathers that whole climate into one devastating portrait.

These people did not merely fail to go. They were pleased not to go. Their sitting became a private celebration. Their aversion was not only toward battle in the abstract, but toward the very logic of spending wealth and self in Allah's path. Then they turned their reluctance into counsel. "Do not go out in the heat." Their fear wanted company.

[PAUSE]

That is why the verse feels so contemporary. The lower self rarely says, "I choose comfort over Allah." It speaks in a more respectable dialect. It says the conditions are bad, the moment is inconvenient, the obligation can wait, the body is strained, the timing is not wise, the costs are too high. The language sounds practical, but what it often protects is a heart that has already decided that ease is the truest good.

So revelation does not merely answer the sentence. It overturns the scale behind the sentence. You are worried about heat? Then weigh heat correctly. The fire of Hell is hotter. You are calculating short-term bodily discomfort as if it were the final reality. You have forgotten how to measure.

The hook of the passage is this: **At-Tawbah teaches that when the heart begins rejoicing in avoided obedience and preaching comfort as wisdom, it has not merely become weak; it has started losing the scale by which reality itself is supposed to be understood.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **ٱلْمُخَلَّفُونَ**: Passive Grammar and the Judgment of Being Made Rearward

The first major noun in the verse is not a neutral description:

**al-mukhallafun**.

Most translations render it as "those left behind." That is not wrong, but the Arabic is doing more than reporting a travel outcome.

[PAUSE]

This word is a **passive participle**. In Arabic, an active participle names the doer of an act: the one writing, striking, sitting, giving. A passive participle names the one the act has happened to: the written one, the struck one, the one made to remain, the one left to the rear. Translation often loses that distinction unless it adds extra words to force it back in.

So if the verse had used a more active shape, the stress would have fallen mainly on their own staying. But **mukhallaf** carries the sense of people who have been made rearward. The root **kh-l-f** circles around behind-ness, succession, coming after, remaining in the rear. Form II, **khallafa**, intensifies and causativizes: to cause someone to stay behind, to leave someone at the back, to render someone residual to the main movement. The passive participle seals that condition into a settled label.

Ibn Kathir explicitly links the word to those whom Allah left behind from Tabuk while the Messenger and the believers went forth. Al-Tabari's treatment also leaves room for hearing more than a bare logistical note. One way to sit with the grammar is this: they first chose the posture of hesitation, but then their hesitation hardened into a state described as being set back. Their preference becomes their sentence.

[PAUSE]

That is something English does not carry well. English hears an event. Arabic starts hinting at a moral condition. They are not only the people who happened not to go. They are now grammatically the people of rearwardness.

And notice the pronouns around them. They are not addressed as "you." The whole verse speaks **about** them in the third person: they rejoiced, they hated, they said. Then the Messenger is told, **qul**, "say." There is a distance in the architecture itself. Divine speech does not draw them into intimate direct moral address here; it exposes them as a case. That distance deepens the passive participle. They kept moving themselves outside the line of obedient motion until the language itself began treating them as people already set back from it.

There is a warning in that. Repeated compromise does not remain a series of isolated acts. It hardens into condition. Condition, if protected, becomes identity. The soul says, "I only stayed back this once." The Qur'an says: be careful, because if you keep choosing the rear, one day the rear will become your grammar.

### 2. **فَرِحَ / كَرِهُوا**: The Verse Leads with Emotion Before It Names a Single Physical Act

The ayah begins with two emotional verbs:

**fariha** and **karihu**.

They rejoiced. They hated.

[PAUSE]

That choice matters enormously. The verse could have started with bodies and roads. It could have begun by saying they sat, they refused, they did not march. Instead Allah begins inside the chest. Before the verse tells us what they did with their bodies, it tells us what had happened to their loves and dislikes.

That is already a spiritual diagnosis. Their crisis is not logistical first. It is affective first. Joy has migrated to the wrong place. Hatred has migrated to the wrong place. Once that happens, the outer actions become almost inevitable.

And even the sound of the words participates in the contrast. **Fariha** comes quickly and lightly on the tongue: **fa-ri-ha**. It has a swift, almost airy movement. Then **karihu** lands more heavily, rougher in the mouth, thicker with resistance. One word feels like relieved ease. The other carries the weight of inward aversion. The Qur'an lets you hear their moral climate before you fully analyze it.

[PAUSE]

That sonic shift is not ornamental. It mirrors the distortion itself. Ease comes lightly to them; costly obedience already feels heavy before it begins. The verse's music becomes a witness against their loves.

This is why the ayah is so penetrating. Not every failure proves hypocrisy. But when a person starts rejoicing at avoided obedience and hating the total claim of Allah's path, the interior disease is already advanced. A believer may stumble while still loving what Allah loves. He may fail while wishing he had not failed. He may struggle while still wanting the command. Here, by contrast, the emotional alignment itself has gone wrong.

Ibn Kathir's Tabuk framing sharpens the point historically, but the wording keeps the lesson universal. Some people miss good with grief. Others miss it with relief. Some find obedience hard yet still want it because Allah wants it. Others begin resenting the claim itself. The outward surface can temporarily overlap; the emotional verbs reveal the truth underneath.

[PAUSE]

That makes the verse a difficult mirror. What obligations do you not merely find difficult, but inwardly resent? Which missed obediences secretly make you exhale with satisfaction? What forms of cost cause the soul to say, "I'm glad that passed me by"? The ayah is teaching us to examine not only our movements, but our emotional signatures.

### 3. **يَفْقَهُونَ**: The Problem Is Not a Lack of Facts but a Lack of Penetrating Understanding

The verse ends with:

**law kanu yafqahun**.

If only they understood deeply.

[PAUSE]

This is exact. The Qur'an could have said **ya'lamun**, "if only they knew," which often names factual knowledge or possession of information. It could have said **ya'qilun**, "if only they reasoned," from the root of binding and restraining thought into sound judgment. But it says **yafqahun**.

In classical Arabic, **fiqh** is not bare data. It is understanding that penetrates into the inside of a matter. It is insight that gets beneath the first surface. So the problem is not that these men do not know the day is hot. They know very well what the sun feels like. Their failure is that the deeper meaning of the moment never opened up before them.

That is why the answer comes as **qul naru Jahannama ashaddu harran**. Their slogan is short and immediate: **al-harr**, the heat. A clipped bodily word. Revelation answers with the heavier phrase **naru Jahannama**. Even sonically, the response weighs more. **Al-harr** feels near, brief, and skin-level. **Jahannam** lands thicker and darker. The ayah does not deny temporary heat. It crushes the soul's shallow comparison by placing it next to a far heavier reality.

[PAUSE]

This is what translation cannot fully carry. English tells you both words refer to heat. Arabic lets you hear the difference between a passing temperature and a moral abyss.

So **yafqahun** becomes the perfect close. If the ayah had ended with **ya'lamun**, the cure might sound like a few more facts. If it had ended with **ya'qilun**, the stress might fall mainly on consistent reasoning. But **yafqahun** says the problem is shallowness. They felt the surface and never entered the meaning.

Al-Tabari, Ibn Kathir, and al-Muyassar all preserve the Tabuk setting, but the ending keeps the verse open beyond history. Every time temporary discomfort swells so large in our sight that final consequence becomes dim, we are living inside the same failure of fiqh. We may know the command and still not understand the moment.

That is why many of our compromises are not committed in a state of total ignorance. We know enough. We know the rule, the excuse, the cost, the bodily pressure. What we lack in the decisive moment is penetrating comprehension. Fiqh is what breaks the illusion that the first felt reality is the truest one.

[PAUSE]

And this has become painfully relevant in modern religious life. Many people know a great deal and still fear the wrong heat most. They know texts and remain ruled by embarrassment. They know doctrine and remain ruled by convenience. They know the Hereafter in theory and still let a brief worldly discomfort overrule the whole scale. In that moment the problem is not storage of information. The problem is the absence of fiqh.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Missing Obedience with Relief Is More Dangerous Than Missing It with Grief

One of the most important distinctions in this section of At-Tawbah is the difference between staying behind in pain and staying behind in pleasure. Not everyone who remained in Madinah was the same. Later in the passage, Allah speaks tenderly of those who came wanting to be carried, then turned away with tears because no provision could be found for them (9:92). Their bodies stayed, but their hearts had already gone. Here, by contrast, the hypocrites stay and rejoice.

[PAUSE]

That is a frightening distinction because outwardly both groups miss the expedition. But the Qur'an refuses surface reading. One absence is grief-stricken sincerity. The other is delighted avoidance. One heart aches because it could not obey. The other relaxes because it successfully escaped obedience.

This is not only a lesson about battle. It is a lesson about the inner life of every command. You may miss a night prayer because you truly collapsed from weakness and wake with regret. You may miss it because you quietly preferred the bed and then feel fine. You may fail to give because you genuinely had nothing. You may fail to give because spending threatened your comfort and then you rationalized the decision. The external result can look identical. The interior world can be opposite.

That is why the verb **fariha** is so dangerous. It tells us that one sign of disease is when the heart starts treating escaped obedience as gain. You are no longer merely weak. You are rearranging your joys. And once joy moves to the wrong place, argument soon follows to defend it.

This theme appears elsewhere in the Qur'an. In 48:11, the bedouins who stayed back come later with polished explanations, but the verse exposes that Allah knows what is in their hearts. In 9:44, true believers are described as the opposite type: they do not ask permission to be spared striving with wealth and selves. Their instinct is not to negotiate themselves out of cost. Their instinct is to belong to Allah's claim.

And this is where the passive participle becomes spiritually terrifying. You do not become **mukhallaf** all at once. First you make peace with relief at missed obedience. Then you defend that relief. Then you stop grieving what was lost. Then you start experiencing rearwardness as safety. Eventually a condition settles in which the language itself has to name you differently.

[PAUSE]

We should sit honestly with that. What do you secretly feel relieved to have escaped? An uncomfortable conversation? A duty toward family? A hard repentance? A financial obligation? A public loyalty? A difficult act of service? The soul often reveals itself not only by what it does, but by what makes it exhale with private satisfaction.

There is also mercy in naming this danger. If Allah did not expose it, many of us would continue mistaking relief for wisdom. We would say, "Alhamdulillah, I got out of that," without ever asking whether the thing we escaped was actually a divine opening that our appetite helped us lose.

The contrast with 9:92 is especially healing because it prevents despair. The ayah is not teaching that every missed obedience proves corruption. It is teaching that the emotional posture toward the missed obedience matters enormously. There are absences that Allah honors because they are soaked in longing. And there are absences that He exposes because they are soaked in relief.

There is a whole religion hidden in that difference. Some people carry real incapacity with wounded honesty before Allah. Others carry full capacity with a protected interior that simply does not want the cost. One says, "My Lord, had I been able, I would have gone." The other says, "I am grateful I found a way not to have to." Both may remain physically where they are. Only one remains morally alive.

And this is why grief after missed obedience can actually be one of the great mercies Allah leaves in a heart. We usually dislike grief and try to medicate it immediately. But there are griefs that prove life. The believer who weeps after missing a chance to obey has at least learned that his heart still knows what it lost. The person who feels no wound at all after escape should be more frightened than the person who feels pain. Spiritual pain is often a sign that spiritual nerve endings still work.

[PAUSE]

That can help us read our own lives differently. There are moments when we are embarrassed that an act of worship, generosity, service, or courage was too hard for us. Yet perhaps the most hopeful thing in that moment is precisely that we are embarrassed. Shame is not always toxic. Sometimes it is the soul's refusal to normalize its own shrinking. If you still feel grief when you miss what Allah called you to, then ask Him to turn that grief into renewed movement before it hardens into self-disgust or self-excuse.

[PAUSE]

So one practical discipline is this: when you miss something good, refuse to let the soul celebrate too quickly. Let yourself ask whether there should have been grief where there is now comfort. That question can save a heart from hardening under respectable circumstances.

### Theme 2: Comfort Becomes Most Dangerous When It Starts Preaching

The hypocrites do not keep their reasoning to themselves. They say, **la tanfiru fi al-harr**. Do not go out in the heat. Their private cowardice becomes public advice.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the ugliest transitions in spiritual life. A person first compromises because he wants ease. Then, instead of feeling the sting of compromise, he begins recruiting others into the same logic. Appetite wants company because company makes compromise feel like wisdom.

The Qur'an shows this pattern often. In 63:7 the hypocrites say, "Do not spend on those who are with the Messenger of Allah until they disperse." That, too, is not merely stinginess. It is stinginess becoming a policy. In 33:18-19, those who hinder others and only come reluctantly appear again as people whose reluctance is contagious. Hypocrisy rarely stays private for long. It tries to build an atmosphere.

That is why we should fear the move from "I do not want to obey" to "it is wiser if none of us obey right now." The first is weakness. The second is corruption beginning to organize itself.

We see this in quieter modern ways. A person is uneasy about giving generously, so he builds a whole language about financial realism and warns others not to be naive. A person is resistant to repentance, so he creates a philosophy of gradualism that always keeps the actual turn for later. A person dislikes principled speech, so he baptizes silence as maturity and teaches others that clarity is impractical. A person does not want the hardship of discipline, so he turns his resistance into a theory about balance, timing, emotional health, or strategic patience. Some of those words can be true in their place. But the nafs is skilled at hiding inside them.

And the ayah's own structure teaches us how this works. First came **fariha** and **karihu**. Only after the interior climate has been disordered do we hear **wa qalu**: "and they said." Speech is not the first stage of corruption; it is its export. By the time a person is persuading others not to move, he has already spent time privately rearranging the moral map inside himself.

[PAUSE]

This is why At-Tawbah is so sharp. It keeps exposing not only sin, but the vocabulary by which sin wants to become normal. Hypocrisy is not just a wrong act. It is a counter-language for reading sacrifice, risk, and timing.

And once a community starts speaking that language fluently, obedience begins feeling extreme while avoidance feels balanced. The ones still moving in Allah's path start looking reckless. The ones preserving themselves start looking wise. The moral map gets redrawn without anyone openly admitting that Allah's claim has been demoted.

That is one reason the Qur'an answers these slogans so forcefully. Revelation must interrupt the false atmosphere before it hardens into common sense. It must remind believers that the lower self is not only tempted to disobey; it is tempted to narrate disobedience beautifully.

There is also something sobering in the verse's third-person distance. They are spoken about, not invited into direct address. The Messenger is the one directly spoken to: **qul**. If a soul keeps converting appetite into guidance, one of the punishments is that revelation begins exposing it as a case rather than cultivating it as an eager addressee. That should make us tremble.

This also explains why hypocrisy is often socially elegant before it is spiritually obvious. The language it produces can sound measured, experienced, mature, and even caring. "Let's be reasonable." "This isn't the right time." "We need a healthier pace." "We shouldn't be extreme." None of those phrases are always false. But once a heart has become loyal to comfort, it will use even true words in false proportion. It will weaponize nuance against obedience.

[PAUSE]

That is why communities need people who can still hear when the moral temperature has quietly shifted. When real sacrifice starts being spoken of as recklessness, when real repentance starts being spoken of as instability, when real devotion starts being spoken of as unsustainable intensity, someone has to remember that the nafs often borrows the vocabulary of wisdom while draining wisdom of its substance. The verse trains that ear.

[PAUSE]

So guard your ears as carefully as your limbs. Ask not only, "What am I choosing?" but also, "What am I helping sound reasonable?" Sometimes the soul's deepest compromise first appears in the advice it offers others.

### Theme 3: Fiqh Is Learning to Weigh Temporary Heat Against Eternal Consequence

The ayah ends not by calling them uninformed, but by saying, **law kanu yafqahun**. If only they truly understood. That is a major correction. We often imagine fiqh as technical religious literacy. And it includes that. But in the Qur'an, fiqh also names penetrating comprehension, the kind of understanding that puts near and far in right proportion.

[PAUSE]

These men are not ignorant of the sun. They do not need a lecture on weather. Their problem is that the present has swollen beyond its proper size. Temporary heat has become more vivid to them than final fire. Immediate bodily discomfort has become more authoritative than the Hereafter. That is not merely weak resolve. It is shallow understanding.

The Qur'an repeats this reversal elsewhere. In 2:216, believers are told that something may be disliked while still being good for them, and something may be loved while still being bad for them. In 87:16-17, humanity is told that it prefers the lower life while the Hereafter is better and more enduring. In 61:10-11, Allah teaches a commerce that saves through belief and striving with wealth and selves. Again and again revelation interrupts the soul's shallow accounting.

This matters because many of our worst decisions are not made by people who know nothing. They are made by people who know much but weigh wrongly. We know enough to describe the hardship in front of us. We do not know enough, or do not remember enough, to place it beside what Allah says it is protecting or endangering.

[PAUSE]

One sign of increasing fiqh, then, is not merely that you can discuss religion well. It is that immediate sensations lose their tyranny over you. Heat remains hot, fatigue remains real, money remains beloved, embarrassment remains uncomfortable, but none of them get to define the horizon. They become data, not gods.

And that is deeply relevant outside Tabuk. The "heat" may now be social discomfort, professional cost, emotional exposure, financial sacrifice, or the inward burning of repentance itself. Telling the truth can feel hot. Ending a sinful attachment can feel hot. Returning money can feel hot. Apologizing can feel hot. Refusing a compromise can feel hot. But the Qur'an trains the believer not to let the first heat blind him to the greater one.

This does not produce numbness. It produces proportion. The believer is not asked to stop feeling pain. He is asked to stop granting pain final authority. That is part of what fiqh does inside a person. It restores scale.

Al-Muyassar's plain paraphrase is helpful here: if they knew that the Fire of Hell was hotter, they would not have spoken this way. That simplicity is worth keeping. A great deal of spiritual ruin can be traced to one basic failure: the soul speaks as if the unseen were less real than the immediate. The Qur'an comes to reverse that hierarchy.

And this is why the final word is so exact. **Yafqahun** says the soul needs more than information management. It needs a mode of understanding that breaks through surface appearance. Temporary heat presents itself as absolute. Fiqh breaks that illusion open. It says: this is real, but it is not ultimate; this hurts, but it is not the truest thing in the room.

[PAUSE]

Without that healing, even religion itself can remain shallowly possessed. You may know texts and still fear the wrong heat most. You may know the Hereafter in theory and still surrender to a momentary embarrassment as though it were final. In that moment the problem is not that revelation was absent. The problem is that revelation never became the deepest interpretive reality.

There is also a practical spiritual method hidden here. Fiqh grows when we repeatedly force the soul to compare the thing immediately in front of it with the larger horizon Allah named. Not by pretending the first thing is painless, but by refusing to let it become solitary. Put the awkward conversation beside the Hereafter. Put the financial sacrifice beside Allah's promise of provision and reward. Put the short-lived humiliation of repentance beside the long humiliation of meeting Allah with a defended sin. Put the heat beside the Fire.

[PAUSE]

This comparative habit is one of the ways the Qur'an tutors the heart. It keeps yoking present sensations to ultimate realities until the soul slowly becomes less naive. A childish soul experiences only what is nearest. A maturing soul begins to perceive consequence, trajectory, and divine meaning. That is why fiqh is so much deeper than literacy. It is the education of moral scale.

There is a reason this kind of understanding often comes late and painfully. The soul does not want proportion if proportion will strip its excuses of authority. It prefers urgency without horizon and emotion without interpretation. So fiqh is not just a gift of the mind. It is a surrender of the ego. It is the willingness to have revelation tell you that the thing screaming loudest in your body is not necessarily the thing that matters most in your destiny.

Seen this way, the verse is also a mercy for anyone trapped in the tyranny of the immediate. Allah is not only condemning a failed scale; He is teaching us how to recover one. Every time you pause before compromise and ask, "How large is this pain really? How long will it last? What larger reality am I forgetting?" you are already walking back toward fiqh. The hypocrites would not ask that question. The believer must keep learning to ask it.

And if you find that difficult, begin very simply. Take one repeated area of compromise in your life and narrate it with two vocabularies: the vocabulary of immediate heat and the vocabulary of ultimate consequence. Listen to how differently the soul sounds in each register. That practice alone can begin loosening the grip of the near.

The verse is teaching precisely that re-narration. Left to itself, the nafs will always tell the story in terms of present temperature. Revelation retells the same moment in terms of final meaning.

And whichever story you keep repeating will eventually become the one your heart believes.

That is why the Qur'an fights for the story before it even argues the rule.

If the story changes, obedience often changes with it.

And disobedience becomes harder to romanticize.

The scale is restored, and the fantasy loses color.

Excuses look smaller in that light.

And smaller excuses lose some of their spell.

So ask yourself a hard question: where has temporary heat become too large in your sight? Which present discomfort keeps persuading you to act as though long-term consequence were vague and negotiable? The answer may tell you where your fiqh is weakest, no matter how articulate you are elsewhere.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Passage

This verse is built as a descent from **hidden affection to public slogan**, then a sudden interruption from revelation. The first two major verbs are emotional: **fariha** and **karihu**. Before the ayah narrates a single physical act, it exposes misdirected joy and hatred. Then those emotions are fixed into condition through the passive participle **al-mukhallafun** and the image of a seat behind the Messenger. Only after interior appetite and settled posture are established does speech emerge: **wa qalu**. Their false theology of comfort is therefore shown to be the public overflow of a prior inward corruption.

Then the structure breaks. Their speech does not get the last word. **Qul** descends from above and replaces their scale with Allah's scale. Even the pronouns participate: they remain third-person subjects throughout, held at a distance, while the Messenger alone is directly addressed and authorized to answer. So the architecture itself performs the meaning of the verse: corrupted affection becomes contagious language, and prophetic speech comes to expose both.

### Questions to Carry

Where do you feel relieved to have escaped a costly obedience, and have you been honest enough to name that relief as spiritually dangerous rather than automatically wise?

What "heat" keeps looking decisive in your life right now: embarrassment, loss of comfort, financial strain, relational tension, tiredness, exposure, or the burning difficulty of repentance itself?

Have you ever turned your own hesitation into advice for others, making avoidance sound like balance, realism, or maturity?

When you weigh a hard command, what actually has more reality in your imagination: the present discomfort or the unseen consequence Allah keeps asking you to remember?

If Allah inspected the emotional texture of the obediences you miss, would He find grief, longing, and regret, or would He find quiet satisfaction that you got to keep your seat?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**This ayah teaches that the soul is in serious danger when joy and hatred become misaligned, rearwardness hardens into identity, and temporary heat is allowed to outweigh the deeper understanding that revelation calls fiqh.**

### The Closing Invitation

Take this verse into the places where your excuses sound most reasonable. Do not begin by asking whether the hardship is real; much of the time it is. Begin by asking whether the hardship has been allowed to become your ruler. Let the Qur'an teach you the difference between feeling the heat and bowing to it. Then ask Allah for the kind of fiqh that restores scale, so that temporary discomfort no longer gets to masquerade as final truth.

### Du'a

O Allah, do not let my heart celebrate the obediences I escape.  
Do not let my rearwardness settle into identity.  
Protect me from hating the costs of striving with my wealth and my self in Your path.  
When my nafs speaks in the language of comfort, expose its hidden cowardice to me.  
Give me fiqh that restores proportion, so the near discomfort never blinds me to the greater reality.  
And when obedience feels hot, keep my heart fixed on You until I can move again with honesty and trust.
