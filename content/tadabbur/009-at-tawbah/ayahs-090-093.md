# The Difference Between an Excuse and a Wound

## Surah At-Tawbah (9:90-93)

[ayah:9:90] وَجَآءَ ٱلْمُعَذِّرُونَ مِنَ ٱلْأَعْرَابِ لِيُؤْذَنَ لَهُمْ وَقَعَدَ ٱلَّذِينَ كَذَبُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ وَرَسُولَهُۥ ۚ سَيُصِيبُ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ مِنْهُمْ عَذَابٌ أَلِيمٌ

[ayah:9:91] لَّيْسَ عَلَى ٱلضُّعَفَآءِ وَلَا عَلَى ٱلْمَرْضَىٰ وَلَا عَلَى ٱلَّذِينَ لَا يَجِدُونَ مَا يُنفِقُونَ حَرَجٌ إِذَا نَصَحُوا۟ لِلَّهِ وَرَسُولِهِۦ ۚ مَا عَلَى ٱلْمُحْسِنِينَ مِن سَبِيلٍ ۚ وَٱللَّهُ غَفُورٌ رَّحِيمٌ

[ayah:9:92] وَلَا عَلَى ٱلَّذِينَ إِذَا مَآ أَتَوْكَ لِتَحْمِلَهُمْ قُلْتَ لَآ أَجِدُ مَآ أَحْمِلُكُمْ عَلَيْهِ تَوَلَّوا۟ وَّأَعْيُنُهُمْ تَفِيضُ مِنَ ٱلدَّمْعِ حَزَنًا أَلَّا يَجِدُوا۟ مَا يُنفِقُونَ

[ayah:9:93] إِنَّمَا ٱلسَّبِيلُ عَلَى ٱلَّذِينَ يَسْتَـْٔذِنُونَكَ وَهُمْ أَغْنِيَآءُ ۚ رَضُوا۟ بِأَن يَكُونُوا۟ مَعَ ٱلْخَوَالِفِ وَطَبَعَ ٱللَّهُ عَلَىٰ قُلُوبِهِمْ فَهُمْ لَا يَعْلَمُونَ

*Wa ja'a al-mu'adhdhiruna mina al-a'rabi li-yu'dhana lahum wa qa'ada alladhina kadhabu Allaha wa rasulahu. Sayusibu alladhina kafaru minhum 'adhabun alim.*

*Laysa 'ala al-du'afa'i wa la 'ala al-marda wa la 'ala alladhina la yajiduna ma yunfiquna harajun idha nasahu li-Allahi wa rasulihi. Ma 'ala al-muhsinina min sabil. Wa Allahu ghafurun rahim.*

*Wa la 'ala alladhina idha ma atawka li-tahmilahum qulta la ajidu ma ahmilukum 'alayhi tawallaw wa a'yunuhum tafidu mina al-dam'i hazanan alla yajidu ma yunfiqun.*

*Innama al-sabilu 'ala alladhina yasta'dhinunaka wa hum aghniya'. Radu bi-an yakunu ma'a al-khawalifi wa taba'a Allahu 'ala qulubihim fa-hum la ya'lamun.*

**And the excuse-makers from the Bedouins came so that permission might be given to them, while those who lied to Allah and His Messenger sat back. A painful punishment will strike those among them who disbelieved. There is no blame upon the weak, nor upon the sick, nor upon those who find nothing to spend, so long as they are sincere to Allah and His Messenger. There is no case against the doers of good; and Allah is Forgiving, Merciful. Nor upon those who came to you so that you might carry them, but you said, "I find nothing on which to carry you," so they turned back while their eyes overflowed with tears, grieving that they could find nothing to spend. Blame is only upon those who ask your permission while they are rich. They were content to be with those who stay behind, and Allah sealed their hearts, so they do not know.**

## Introduction

Not every person who remains behind is the same before Allah.

That is the moral precision of these verses. Outwardly, several groups do not go forth. Some Bedouins come with excuses. Some people simply stay seated and lie. Some are weak. Some are sick. Some genuinely have no means. Some come desperate to go, asking to be carried, and return weeping only because nothing can be found for them. If we judge only by the visible outcome, we collapse them into one category: absent. The Qur'an refuses that collapse.

[PAUSE]

In the Tabuk setting this matters even more. The expedition was difficult, hot, expensive, and exposed every hidden calculation in the soul. That is why At-Tawbah keeps distinguishing not merely between believers and hypocrites in the abstract, but between kinds of inner posture under pressure. Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir both preserve the scene as one in which excuses, incapacity, wealth, hesitation, and grief all stood side by side. Revelation enters that crowded social field and sorts it with painful fairness.

One group is named by falsehood. Another is cleared by sincerity. Another is remembered by tears. And blame is finally narrowed onto those who still had the means to go but wanted the language of innocence without the cost of obedience.

The hook of the passage is this: **At-Tawbah teaches that Allah does not judge by outward non-participation alone. He distinguishes between lie and limitation, between an excuse and a wound, and He places blame not on the unable but on the comfortable heart that borrows the appearance of inability.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **نَصَحُوا**: Sincerity in Arabic Is More Than a Private Feeling

The first decisive word in the passage appears in the condition that clears the weak, the sick, and the poor:

**idha nasahu li-Allahi wa rasulihi**.

If they were sincere to Allah and His Messenger.

[PAUSE]

To hear the force of this, we need a small tool first. A verb in Arabic does not only report that something happened. It often names the kind of relationship a person stood in. The root **n-s-h** is richer than the thin English word "advice." In Qur'anic and classical usage it carries sincerity, well-wishing, loyal counsel, and a form of purity in intention that is not mixed with betrayal.

So when the verse says **nasahu**, it is not merely checking whether these absent believers said the right things while staying home. It is naming the state of the heart. Their bond to Allah and His Messenger remained uncorrupted. Their absence did not conceal disloyalty. Their limitation did not become a shelter for double-mindedness.

Translation with "they were sincere" gives the central meaning, but the Arabic carries more texture than private emotion. **Nasaha** suggests a sincerity that is morally useful, loyal, and clean. It is sincerity that still wants the good of the cause even when it cannot physically take part in it. Al-Tabari explains this sincerity in terms of truthful religion and non-betrayal in their remaining behind. Ibn Kathir reads it through real excuse joined to loyal concern. The point is not only what they felt. The point is what kind of people they remained.

[PAUSE]

That matters because we often reduce sincerity to interior warmth. The Qur'an makes it more demanding. A person can be absent and still be present in **nush**. He can counsel, support, grieve, make du'a, refuse sabotage, and remain inwardly aligned with Allah's Messenger. That is why the passage can absolve them without trivializing jihad. It is not lowering the standard. It is recognizing a kind of participation the body could not complete.

There is also a quiet contrast hidden here with the liars of verse 90. They too have words. They too have explanations. But the Qur'an does not ask whether their excuse sounded polished. It asks whether sincerity remained intact. That is what translation alone can miss. The real opposite of hypocrisy here is not eloquence, and not even mere attendance. It is **nush**: a heart still clean in its loyalty to Allah and His Messenger.

### 2. **تَفِيضُ**: The Qur'an Makes Tears Move Before Our Eyes

Then the passage gives us one of its most tender images:

**wa a'yunuhum tafidu mina al-dam'**.

Their eyes overflow with tears.

[PAUSE]

The tool here is verbal aspect and root imagery. The verb **tafidu** comes from the root **f-y-d**, which carries the sense of overflowing, pouring out, or spilling beyond containment. And because it comes in the imperfect form, the scene is not frozen as a fact already filed away. It is set before us as movement: the eyes are overflowing.

That is why this line lands differently in Arabic than in a flat summary like "they were very sad." The Qur'an does not inform us about emotion from a distance. It lets grief travel across the face. It becomes visible, undeniable, bodily. Poverty is not relief for these people. Inability is not secretly what they wanted. Their tears prove that remaining behind hurt them.

The sound of the phrase serves the meaning too. **Tafidu mina al-dam'** moves with a kind of soft continuation, and then the line lingers on **hazanan**. The flow is not abrupt. It keeps spilling. That sonic smoothness matches the image of sustained grief better than any quick paraphrase can carry. The verse is teaching by picture and by sound that this is not staged disappointment. It is a wound that kept moving.

[PAUSE]

Classical tafsir preserves the human story beneath the image. These are the believers who came asking for mounts or provisions so they could join the campaign, and the Messenger told them he had nothing with which to carry them. Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir both preserve the basic frame: they turned back not with relief, but with tears because they had been shut out by circumstance, not by preference.

That is spiritually important because the Qur'an is granting tears evidentiary weight. The eye becomes a witness. A body can testify when the tongue is not enough. Their sincerity is not asserted abstractly and then left unshown. It is made visible in the overflow that incapacity produced in them.

There is also an absence worth noticing. These men are not mocked by the Qur'an for emotional weakness, and their tears are not treated as less manly than battle-readiness. In fact, the tears are part of why they are honored. They reveal that the heart had already gone where the body could not follow. Translation can tell you they cried. The Arabic lets you see the yearning spill over.

### 3. **إِنَّمَا**: Blame Is Not Scattered Broadly; It Is Narrowed Deliberately

Then verse 93 turns with a particle:

**innama al-sabilu 'ala alladhina yasta'dhinunaka wa hum aghniya'**.

Blame is only upon those who ask your permission while they are rich.

[PAUSE]

The tool here is the particle of restriction. **Innama** does not merely add emphasis. It narrows a ruling. It says the matter belongs here in particular, not everywhere people carelessly spread it. In Arabic, this kind of restriction can reshape the moral field of an entire passage.

That is exactly what happens here. After several verses in which many outwardly absent people have been mentioned, the Qur'an refuses indiscriminate suspicion. It does not leave blame floating over all who remained in Madinah. It gathers blame and places it precisely: on those who sought permission while possessing means.

Translation with "blame is only upon" is accurate, but the Arabic makes us feel the juridical tightening. The verse is not merely adding one more group to the list. It is correcting the scale on which the whole list has been read. If you heard verses 90 to 92 and concluded, "So anyone who stayed behind is morally suspect," **innama** arrives to stop you.

And look at what comes immediately after the restriction: **wa hum aghniya'**. They are rich. Then: **radu bi-an yakunu ma'a al-khawalif**. They were content to be with those who remain behind. The problem is not raw staying. The problem is affluent contentment with staying. The heart is not broken by inability. It is settled into comfort.

[PAUSE]

That is why this anchor matters so much for the whole passage. The Qur'an is not only clearing the innocent. It is teaching us how blame must be assigned if we want to judge like revelation rather than like gossip. **Innama** blocks lazy moral flattening. It says the believer is not permitted to turn incapacity, poverty, illness, and yearning into one blurred category. The blame has an address, and the verse writes it carefully.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Allah Judges Incapacity and Sincerity Together

One of the most beautiful acts of justice in this passage is that Allah does not judge by visible result alone. Some people did not go. That much is visible. But revelation refuses to stop there. It asks deeper questions. Was the person weak? Sick? Without means? Still loyal? Still truthful? Still pained by what he could not do? The passage will not let the public eye have the final word.

[PAUSE]

This is part of a larger Qur'anic pattern. In 2:286, Allah tells us that no soul is burdened beyond its capacity. In 48:17, blame is lifted from the blind, the lame, and the sick in the context of fighting. The divine standard is never a brutal demand for identical outward performance regardless of condition. It is a demand for truth within actual capacity.

That phrase matters: truth within actual capacity. Because sometimes we hear verses of striving and imagine that moral seriousness requires ignoring limits. The Qur'an does not ignore them. It reads them. It names them. It legislates around them. But it never makes limitation itself the measure of worth. The decisive matter is whether the heart remained in **nush** to Allah and His Messenger.

Al-Tabari's commentary makes this especially clear by defining the exempted groups through both their impediment and their sincerity. Ibn Kathir too does not present these verses as mere legal loopholes. He presents them as genuine excuses recognized by Allah when joined to truthful concern. That means the absolution here is not accidental leniency. It is principled divine fairness.

[PAUSE]

This should correct two opposite habits in us. The first is harshness toward others. We often see reduced outward action and assume reduced commitment. Somebody is less visible in service, less able to show up, less publicly productive, and we start writing moral stories we have no right to write. The second is harshness toward ourselves. Some servants assume that because they cannot do what others do, they have spiritually failed before even being weighed.

This passage dismantles both mistakes. It tells the observer: you do not know enough from outcome alone. And it tells the limited servant: Allah is not blind to your limitation, nor to your sincerity within it.

That is why verse 91 ends as it does: **ma 'ala al-muhsinina min sabil**. There is no case against the doers of good. That is a stunning phrase in this context. The weak, the sick, and the poor are not merely tolerated. If they remain sincere, they are counted among the **muhsinin**. The Qur'an can look at a believer who did not march and still call him a doer of good because his heart remained true under constraint.

[PAUSE]

That expands how we think about service in every age. A mother trapped by an obligation she cannot abandon, a sick person whose body has narrowed, a believer crushed by poverty, someone carrying a hidden disability, someone sincerely blocked by circumstances beyond choice. The religion is not asking them to pretend they are unbound. It is asking them not to lie, not to sabotage, not to harden, and not to let incapacity decay into inward betrayal.

This is where the passage becomes deeply merciful. Allah does not humiliate the unable by measuring them against an impossible outward scale. He asks whether they kept faith with Him inside their impossibility.

This also helps us read 4:95 more carefully, where those who sit back are not equal to those who strive, except for those with genuine harm. The Qur'an does preserve hierarchy where sacrifice differs, but it never weaponizes that hierarchy against the legitimately excused. In other words, distinction is not the same as contempt. Revelation can honor striving, preserve rank, and still protect the dignity of the one whose excuse is real. We usually lose one side or the other. Either we flatten all stations into sameness, or we turn difference into humiliation. The Qur'an does neither.

There is also a communal duty here. If revelation itself distinguishes incapacity from hypocrisy so carefully, then believing communities should be terrified of doing the opposite. We should be slow to accuse, slow to equate absence with indifference, and attentive to the many forms of hidden pain by which sincere people are kept from outward participation. The Qur'an models a justice more patient than suspicion.

That patience is not softness toward hypocrisy. It is accuracy before Allah. The same surah that speaks with blistering clarity about liars and permission-seekers also spends precious revelation clearing the weak, the sick, and the poor. That balance should become part of our own moral adab. We should be hard on deceit in ourselves, but careful with the unseen burdens of others. The person you are tempted to dismiss may be carrying a test that has already turned his private room into a battlefield.

### Theme 2: Tears Can Be a Form of Participation When the Heart Has Already Gone

Verse 92 is one of the Qur'an's great rehabilitations of frustrated desire for good. These believers do not reach the battlefield. But the passage refuses to let that be the whole story. They come. They ask. They are turned back by real lack. Then they weep because they could not spend and could not go.

[PAUSE]

That means desire matters. Yearning matters. Grief at being unable to obey matters. The world often counts only completed action. The Qur'an certainly values action, but it also reveals that uncompleted action can still disclose a great deal about the soul. These tears are not a substitute for jihad in every sense, but they are evidence that the heart was not sitting lazily at home.

This is why verse 9:46 is such a useful contrast. There, the surah says that if they had truly wished to go forth, they would have prepared for it. Here, by contrast, we are given people who did wish to go forth. Their inability is not inferred from rhetoric. It is shown in the pain of being unable to spend or find transport. The passage itself teaches us how to distinguish empty claim from costly desire.

The comparison with 9:81 is also striking. There, those left behind rejoice in staying back. Here, those left behind are broken by it. Outwardly, both groups remain. Inwardly, they stand at opposite poles. One absence is celebration. The other is sorrow. One reveals hypocrisy. The other reveals longing.

[PAUSE]

There is something spiritually arresting in that. Many people know what it is to want a good they cannot currently reach. They want to pray with greater presence but are battling exhaustion. They want to give but are frightened by poverty. They want to seek knowledge but are bound by care duties. They want to serve but illness keeps interrupting them. These verses do not tell such people that yearning is irrelevant until performance becomes visible. They tell them that Allah sees the wound of blocked obedience.

But we should be careful here too. The tears are honored because they sit inside a larger truthfulness. These believers came to the Prophet. They sought a way. They were refused only because no means existed. Then they wept. In other words, the grief followed real effort. It was not theatrical sadness over a duty they never tried to meet.

That distinction is crucial for us. It is easy to romanticize longing while avoiding the concrete steps obedience would have required. The Qur'an does not romanticize. It verifies. The yearning honored here is yearning that already pushed the body as far as it could go. Only then did impossibility stop it.

There is another tenderness in the verse's construction. The most intimate scene in the whole passage is the one addressed directly to the Prophet: **idha ma atawka ... qulta**. They came to you. You told them you had no means. Then they turned back in tears. The grammar draws us near to the Messenger precisely at the point where sincere inability is being honored. The poor, tearful believers occupy the center of the passage not as an afterthought, but as the place where the reader is brought closest to prophetic witness. Their grief is not peripheral to the moral argument. It is its warmest human center.

[PAUSE]

That is why the image is so healing for sincere believers who feel locked outside certain forms of service. Your blocked desire for Allah is not wasted with Him. If you have tried honestly, if you have sought the means, if what stopped you is real, then the ache itself is known. The Qur'an makes a place for that ache. It does not ridicule it. It records it.

And communities should learn from that record. We should stop treating the visibly active as the only spiritually serious people in the room. Sometimes the most intense longing belongs to the one sitting quietly at the edge, tears hidden, means unavailable, still aching to be included in what Allah loves. Revelation sees that person before the crowd does.

### Theme 3: Comfortable Hearts Love the Language of Inability

After clearing the sincere, the passage turns and narrows blame with great force: **innama al-sabilu**. Blame belongs here. On whom? Not on the weak. Not on the sick. Not on the poor. Not on the tearful who came and were turned away. Blame is on those who asked permission while rich.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the most penetrating diagnoses in At-Tawbah. The spiritually dangerous person is not only the openly defiant rebel. It is also the person with means who wants the moral protection usually given to the truly unable. He wants incapacity's innocence without incapacity's pain.

That is why the phrase **wa hum aghniya'** matters so much. Their wealth is not mentioned to shame wealth itself. It is mentioned to expose the dishonesty of their excuse. They had options. They had provision. They could have prepared. Nearby in 9:86 the surah already tells us that when they were called to believe and strive with the Messenger, the people of means asked leave to remain behind. Wealth, in this section of the surah, becomes the very thing that makes avoidance more blameworthy because it removes one of the obvious obstacles.

Then comes the deeper wound: **radu bi-an yakunu ma'a al-khawalif**. They were content to be with those remaining behind. This is not only external choice. It is emotional settlement. They are pleased with the lower station. They do not feel the tear of missing obedience. They feel the satisfaction of successful escape.

[PAUSE]

That is what makes this theme so relevant far beyond military context. Many of us know how to borrow the rhetoric of inability when the real issue is attachment to comfort. We say we are unable when what we mean is that the act would disturb arrangements we do not want disturbed. We say it is not the right time, the conditions are not ideal, our energy is too low, our schedule too full. Sometimes those are true limitations. Sometimes they are polished shelter for reluctance.

The passage gives us a test. Do you resemble the tearful believer or the contented avoider? When obedience is blocked, do you grieve it, seek alternatives, remain loyal, and wish the obstacle were gone? Or do you feel relief, search for legitimacy, and settle quickly into the company of those who are also staying back?

This is where 9:44 and 9:45 sharpen the lesson. The surah already said that those who truly believe in Allah and the Last Day do not keep asking permission to abstain from striving, while those who ask leave are the ones whose hearts doubt and waver. So 9:93 is not a random new rebuke. It is the concentrated conclusion of a profile At-Tawbah has been building all along.

And when the verse says they were content to be with **al-khawalif**, the phrase lands harder because it echoes the surrounding Tabuk section's repeated exposure of people who accepted the rear place as enough. The danger is not only that they physically remained. It is that the soul adapted itself downward. A person can keep negotiating with obedience until smaller and smaller forms of faithfulness begin to feel normal. At that point the greatest threat is no longer the missed act itself, but the satisfaction of no longer missing it.

And the ending is severe: **wa taba'a Allahu 'ala qulubihim fa-hum la ya'lamun**. Allah sealed their hearts, so they do not know. Notice the order. First contentment with staying. Then sealing. Then ignorance. When comfort is repeatedly chosen over costly obedience, ignorance is not merely lack of information. It becomes the fog produced by a sealed heart. A person can end up unable to recognize the ugliness of what he once negotiated.

That warning should not make sincere people despair. It should make comfortable people suspicious of themselves. Especially when we have means, education, influence, money, access, or time, we should fear the spiritual habit of clothing reluctance in respectable language. The verse does not despise weakness. It despises affluent avoidance pretending to be weakness.

One of the clearest self-tests is this: when a demanding act of obedience passes you by, do you feel a bruise or a release? The sincere blocked believer feels pain. The contented avoider feels safety. That emotional difference is not everything, but At-Tawbah teaches us that it is rarely nothing.

And if you no longer feel the difference, that numbness itself should frighten you deeply now.

There is mercy in that severity too. The Qur'an is trying to rescue us before contentment with smallness becomes identity. It is exposing the soul's favorite disguise. And once that disguise is exposed, we have a chance to stop speaking the language of inability where the real issue is that our comforts have become too precious to threaten.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Passage

These verses are built as a **ring of narrowed blame around a center of sincere grief**. The outer frame is verse 90 and verse 93: at the beginning, excuse-makers and liars are named; at the end, blame is explicitly restricted to those who sought permission while rich. Inside that frame, the middle opens twice with release: no blame on the weak, the sick, and the poor in verse 91, and no blame on the tearful seekers of provision in verse 92. The structure is not an incidental list. It is a deliberate moral sorting in which accusation sits on the outside and absolution occupies the center.

The passage also turns on direct address. Most groups are spoken about in the third person, but the middle scene suddenly becomes intimate: **idha ma atawka** ... **qulta**. "When they came to you ... you said." The closest point to the Prophet in the whole unit is not the wealthy permission-seeker but the poor believer turned back in tears. That is a structural revelation, not just a narrative detail. Nearness to the Messenger in these verses belongs to the wounded sincere, while the blameworthy are pushed back into a more distant profile.

### Questions to Carry

When you say, "I cannot," is it truly incapacity, or is it comfort defending itself?

What do your emotions reveal when obedience becomes costly: grief, longing, and loyal frustration, or relief masked as explanation?

Have you ever judged someone's outward absence without knowing the inward sincerity, illness, poverty, or hidden struggle Allah sees clearly?

Where in your life are you tempted to use respectable language to hide reluctance?

If Allah looked past your explanation and weighed only truth within capacity, what would He find?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These verses teach that Allah does not confuse inability with hypocrisy: He clears the sincere who are blocked by real limits, honors the tears of those whose hearts had already gone, and narrows blame onto the wealthy soul that borrows incapacity's language to protect its comfort.**

### The Closing Invitation

Take this passage into the places where your explanations have become too polished. Ask Allah to show you whether your "cannot" is a wound or a preference. If you are genuinely limited, let these verses comfort you: Allah sees what you wanted to do for Him and what stopped you. If you are comfortable and avoidant, let these verses unsettle you before that comfort hardens into contentment. And when you look at other believers, remember how carefully Allah distinguished them. Do not be cruder in judgment than revelation.

### Du'a

O Allah, make me truthful before You about my limits and my excuses.  
If I am genuinely unable, keep my heart sincere to You and Your Messenger.  
Let me grieve what I miss for Your sake instead of making peace with distance from You.  
Protect me from borrowing the language of weakness when the real issue is love of comfort.  
Do not let wealth, access, or ease become veils over my heart.  
Make me gentle in judging others, because I do not know the wounds You are carrying for them.  
And keep my heart unsealed, eager for obedience, and honest in every explanation I give.
