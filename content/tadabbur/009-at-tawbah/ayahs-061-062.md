# They Called Him "An Ear," But Allah Called That Mercy

## Surah At-Tawbah (9:61-62)

[ayah:9:61] وَمِنْهُمُ ٱلَّذِينَ يُؤْذُونَ ٱلنَّبِىَّ وَيَقُولُونَ هُوَ أُذُنٌ ۚ قُلْ أُذُنُ خَيْرٍ لَّكُمْ يُؤْمِنُ بِٱللَّهِ وَيُؤْمِنُ لِلْمُؤْمِنِينَ وَرَحْمَةٌ لِّلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ مِنكُمْ ۚ وَٱلَّذِينَ يُؤْذُونَ رَسُولَ ٱللَّهِ لَهُمْ عَذَابٌ أَلِيمٌ

[ayah:9:62] يَحْلِفُونَ بِٱللَّهِ لَكُمْ لِيُرْضُوكُمْ وَٱللَّهُ وَرَسُولُهُۥٓ أَحَقُّ أَن يُرْضُوهُ إِن كَانُوا۟ مُؤْمِنِينَ

*Wa minhum alladhina yu'dhuna al-nabiyya wa yaquluna huwa udhun. Qul udhunu khayrin lakum, yu'minu bi-Allahi wa yu'minu li-al-mu'minina wa rahmatun lilladhina amanu minkum. Wa alladhina yu'dhuna rasula Allahi lahum 'adhabun alim.*

*Yahlifuna bi-Allahi lakum li-yurdukum wa Allahu wa rasuluhu ahaqqu an yurduhu in kanu mu'minin.*

**And among them are those who hurt the Prophet and say, "He is all ear." Say: he is an ear of goodness for you. He believes in Allah, trusts the believers, and is a mercy for those among you who believe. And those who hurt the Messenger of Allah will have a painful punishment. They swear by Allah to you in order to please you, while Allah and His Messenger have more right that they should please Him, if they are truly believers.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

One of the ugliest habits of hypocrisy is that it turns mercy into a target.

The Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, listens. He hears people. He gives room. He does not respond with cynical hardness. So the hypocrites try to use that gentleness against him: he is all ear. Meaning: too open, too receptive, too easy to speak to. But Allah flips the insult and says: yes, an ear, but an ear of good for you.

[PAUSE]

That reversal is profound. The very quality they mocked is reclaimed as mercy. His listening is not gullibility. It is prophetic character. It is khayr for the believers. It makes communal life livable. It gives room for people to come near without being crushed by suspicion. But that mercy does not erase the reality that injuring the Messenger is grave. Those who wound him are still under painful punishment.

Then the next verse exposes the larger disease underneath all this oath-making and image management. They swear to please you. They are still governed by human reaction and social survival. But the ayah restores the believer's axis: Allah and His Messenger have more right to be pleased. The whole spiritual direction of life is corrected in one sentence.

Ibn Kathir reads the insult as the hypocrites mocking the Prophet's willingness to hear what is said to him and assuming that if they swore, he would simply accept their words. Al-Tabari similarly describes them belittling his readiness to listen and receive speech. But the Qur'an does something much more subtle than mere defense. It turns the very descriptor into praise, then grounds that praise in a whole prophetic moral architecture: faith in Allah, trusting reception toward the believers, and mercy for those who truly believe.

The hook of the passage is this: **At-Tawbah teaches that prophetic gentleness is not weakness but mercy, and that hypocrisy is exposed when pleasing people matters more than pleasing Allah and His Messenger.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Yu'dhuna al-nabiyya**: Hurt to the Prophet Is Named Clearly

Verse 61 opens:

**wa minhum alladhina yu'dhuna al-nabiyya**

Among them are those who hurt the Prophet.

[PAUSE]

The Qur'an does not trivialize this as banter, tone, or clumsy social friction. It names it as **adha**, injury, offense, harm. The root **a‑dh‑y** in Qur'anic usage often names a real wound, even when the means are verbal. This matters because religious communities can become careless about non-physical harm, especially when the harm is packaged as wit, realism, or "just saying what everyone knows." Allah does not speak that language here. He says: they hurt the Prophet.

And the fact that the object is **al-nabiyya** is also important. The verse first names his prophetic office before later saying **rasula Allah**. The wound is not only against a respected human being. It is against the one carrying revelation. Their words are not merely rude. They are misaligned with the station Allah gave him.

This should re-educate us in adab. Sometimes we treat verbal injury toward sacred figures, sacred knowledge, or people carrying religious responsibility as though seriousness itself were a lack of sophistication. The ayah says the opposite. Seriousness is accurate. The one mocking prophetic character is not edgy. He is doing adha.

### 2. **Huwa udhun**: They Mock Receptive Mercy as Weakness

Then the ayah quotes the insult:

**wa yaquluna huwa udhun**

They say: he is an ear.

[PAUSE]

On the surface the phrase is strikingly simple. An ear. But that simplicity is part of the insult. They reduce an entire prophetic mode of listening, patience, and careful social reception into a dismissive label. He is just an ear. He hears anything. He takes in too much. He is too easy to approach.

Al-Tabari notes the linguistic sense of a person characterized by hearing and ready acceptance. Ibn Kathir explains that they meant he lends his ear, he listens to whatever is said, and so they imagined they could manipulate that trait. The insult is built on a hard heart's inability to distinguish between mercy and credulity.

This pattern did not end with the hypocrites of Madinah. Hard hearts still do this. They interpret tenderness as weakness, receptivity as naivete, mercy as lack of discernment, and non-cynical presence as impracticality. People who only understand domination cannot recognize the strength required to listen without cruelty.

That is why the verse matters so much for spiritual formation. It teaches us that some of the qualities closest to prophetic character will look contemptible to people whose metric is hardness.

### 3. **Qul udhunu khayrin lakum**: The Insult Is Turned into Praise

Then comes one of the most elegant reversals in the surah:

**qul udhunu khayrin lakum**

Say: an ear of goodness for you.

[PAUSE]

Allah does not deny the وصف, the descriptor. He does not say, "No, he is not an ear." He says, in effect: yes, and what you call a weakness is actually your benefit.

That move is magnificent. It means the problem was not the attribute but your reading of the attribute.

The addition of **khayr** changes everything. Not merely an ear, but an ear of good, an ear characterized by خير, an ear whose receptive quality serves wellbeing rather than corruption.

And then:

**lakum**

for you.

Even the ones living under the shelter of his mercy are beneficiaries of what they mock. His listening is not only good abstractly. It is good for the community. It means people can approach him, be heard, return, explain, seek nearness, and not find the door slammed by suspicion at the first sign of imperfection.

This is deeply important for communities of faith. If every leader, teacher, parent, and elder interprets receptivity as weakness, communal life becomes unbearable. People stop coming close. Fear replaces trust. Only the polished survive near authority. The Prophet's character prevented that. His hearing was khayr.

There is a quiet echo here with 3:159, where Allah says it was by mercy from Him that the Prophet was gentle with them, and had he been harsh and hard-hearted, they would have dispersed from around him. Our passage shows the same truth from another angle: the softness they mock is exactly what keeps the believing body from shattering.

### 4. **Yu'minu bi-Allahi wa yu'minu li-al-mu'minin**: His Trust Is Ordered, Not Blind

Then the ayah explains why his hearing is good:

**yu'minu bi-Allahi wa yu'minu li-al-mu'minin**

He believes in Allah and gives trust to the believers.

[PAUSE]

The first clause is straightforward: **yu'minu bi-Allahi**. His entire posture is anchored in faith in Allah. That means his way of listening is not a personality quirk floating free of revelation. It is structured by certainty in his Lord.

Then the second clause shifts the preposition:

**yu'minu li-al-mu'minin**

He believes for the believers, or gives credence to the believers, trusts the believers.

That prepositional variation matters. The Qur'an did not simply repeat the first phrase. It is distinguishing between iman in Allah and a trusting, honoring reception toward the believers. His hearing is not indiscriminate credulity toward everyone. It is principled trust, حسن ظن, toward the people of faith.

Al-Tabari and others read this as accepting what is reported by the believers and dealing with them on the basis of trust. Ibn Kathir frames it as his knowing truth from falsehood and still embodying خير toward the community. This is crucial. The Prophet is not naive. He is merciful. Those are not the same thing.

Many people confuse skepticism with intelligence. The ayah destabilizes that assumption. There is a mode of trust that is not stupidity but prophetic adab. To live by permanent suspicion is not a mark of depth. It can be a mark of spiritual impoverishment.

### 5. **Rahmatun lilladhina amanu minkum ... ahaqqu an yurduhu**: Mercy Reorients the Entire Axis of Pleasing

The verse continues:

**wa rahmatun lilladhina amanu minkum**

and a mercy for those among you who believe.

Then verse 62 says:

**yahlifuna bi-Allahi lakum li-yurdukum**

They swear by Allah to you so that they may please you.

And then the correction:

**wa Allahu wa rasuluhu ahaqqu an yurduhu**

Allah and His Messenger have more right that they should please Him.

[PAUSE]

First, **rahmah**. The Prophet's listening is explicitly named mercy. The hypocrites saw only exploitable softness. Allah names mercy. This means communal ease, receptivity, hearing people out, and not weaponizing immediate suspicion are not soft skills floating at the edge of religion. They are part of prophetic rahmah.

Then the second verse takes us to the deeper root disease: people-pleasing. **Li-yurdukum** reveals their actual orientation. The oath is calibrated to human reception. How do we manage your reaction? How do we quiet the room? How do we preserve our image?

The ayah then restores the axis with **ahaqqu**, more rightful, more deserving, more proper by claim. Pleasing Allah and His Messenger is not one audience among many. It is the audience with prior right.

And the singular pronoun in **an yurduhu** after naming Allah and His Messenger carries a powerful unity of direction. The point is not grammatical curiosity for its own sake. The point is that the believer's axis of pleasing is one, not split into competing thrones.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Hard Hearts Often Misread Mercy as Weakness

Harsh hearts tend to misread gentleness because gentleness exposes what they have lost.

When a person only knows force, manipulation, control, guardedness, or suspicion, the one who listens mercifully will look unserious. The one who gives people room will look unsophisticated. The one who does not harden instantly will look vulnerable.

[PAUSE]

That is exactly what the hypocrites do here. They look at prophetic listening and interpret it through the only categories they trust: exploitable openness, over-receptivity, lack of caution. They cannot imagine that someone might hear fully and still be governed by revelation rather than by fear.

This happens constantly in ordinary life.

A parent listens carefully and is called too soft.

A teacher makes room for questions and is called weak.

A leader refuses to humiliate people publicly and is called naive.

A spouse does not weaponize every vulnerability and is called easy to take advantage of.

A friend keeps hearing you even after disappointment and is treated as though their mercy proves they do not understand what is happening.

The passage trains us not to join that misreading too quickly.

Why? Because some forms of gentleness are not the absence of strength but the fruit of greater strength. It is easier, in some ways, to become hard, suspicious, and closed. It is harder to remain principled without becoming cruel. It is harder to hear people without surrendering truth. It is harder to embody rahmah while still naming wrong as wrong.

The Prophet's hearing is precisely that. The ayah does not say he is an ear because he cannot tell truth from falsehood. Ibn Kathir explicitly rejects that reduction. Rather, his hearing is khayr because it is governed by iman in Allah and by ordered trust toward believers. Mercy here is not blindness. It is disciplined goodness.

This is a necessary correction in a time when cynicism is frequently mistaken for intelligence. Many people imagine that the more suspicious they become, the more realistic they are. But constant suspicion can also be spiritual damage. A heart unable to recognize or extend trust is not automatically mature. It may simply be wounded and then proud of the wound.

There is a related Qur'anic principle in 49:12, where excessive suspicion is forbidden. Not because discernment is unnecessary, but because suspicion can become a moral atmosphere that corrodes communal life. Our ayah shows the positive prophetic counterpart: a mode of listening and trusting that allows believers to breathe.

This matters practically for communities. If all mercy is mocked as weakness, eventually only harshness will be respected. And once harshness becomes the only respected register, religious spaces become places where people hide, posture, and flee inwardly rather than places where they can return, confess, learn, and heal.

The hypocrites wanted to weaponize the Prophet's mercy against him. Allah turned the weapon around and said: that very mercy is your good.

That should make us careful. Sometimes the very quality we are tempted to sneer at is the thing still keeping our world livable.

[PAUSE]

We should be deeply afraid of mocking what Allah Himself names khayr and rahmah.

### Theme 2: To Injure the Messenger Is to Reveal a Broken Relation to Mercy and Revelation

The verse begins with **yu'dhuna al-nabiyya** and ends with **walladhina yu'dhuna rasula Allahi lahum 'adhabun alim**. The repetition is not incidental. It frames the whole scene. What some would dismiss as commentary, sarcasm, or manageable slander is named twice as harm to the Messenger.

[PAUSE]

Why is that so serious?

Because the Messenger is not merely a personality inside the community. He is the vessel through whom Allah's mercy, guidance, law, and correction arrive. To wound him is not simply to hurt a private individual. It is to stand wrongly before the very channel by which Allah is turning people back to Himself.

That is why the verse moves from **al-nabiyy** to **rasul Allah**. The first names his prophetic station. The second names his belonging to Allah and his mission from Allah. The harm is not small because the station is not small.

And notice what exactly they are harming: not severity, not domination, not privilege, but mercy. That makes the offense spiritually revealing. The heart that is irritated by prophetic gentleness has a deeper problem than disagreement about style. It is uncomfortable with the very shape of divine mercy when that mercy comes embodied in a Messenger.

This should press on us today in a quieter but still real way. We may not stand among the hypocrites of Madinah, but we are still tested in our adab toward the Messenger. How do we speak about his character? How do we react when his sunnah confronts our instincts? Do we subtly prefer a version of strength that looks more like worldly hardness than prophetic mercy? Do we become impatient with what Allah chose to make beautiful in him?

The Qur'an elsewhere says in 33:57 that those who harm Allah and His Messenger are cursed in this world and the next. Our passage is part of that same moral world. Adha to the Messenger is never spiritually cheap.

There is also a personal dimension here. Many people struggle to accept mercy because mercy threatens their ego more deeply than law does. Law can be negotiated with outwardly. Mercy asks you to soften. Mercy asks you to trust. Mercy asks you to come close enough to be healed. Hard hearts often prefer distance because distance preserves control.

The hypocrites mock listening precisely because listening leaves room for return. If the Prophet hears, then doors remain open. If doors remain open, then excuses become harder to maintain. Mercy makes hypocrisy uncomfortable because mercy preserves possibility.

So the wound they inflict is also a confession. It confesses a hatred of the very atmosphere in which sincere return becomes possible.

That should sober us. A person can be bothered not only by truth's demands but by truth's mercy. And that may be even more revealing.

### Theme 3: Hypocrisy Is Governed by Human Approval; Faith Reorders the Throne

Verse 62 is devastating in its simplicity:

**yahlifuna bi-Allahi lakum li-yurdukum**

They swear by Allah to you so that they may please you.

This is the psychology of hypocrisy in one sentence. The oath is religious. The audience is human. The motive is image management.

[PAUSE]

They invoke Allah's name, but the real governing concern is social reception. How do we calm people down? How do we avoid consequences? How do we restore our standing? How do we make the room more comfortable for us?

This is not only about ancient hypocrites. It is a living spiritual disease. Many people still use religious language not because their hearts are centered on Allah, but because religious language helps manage human opinion. They say what will land well. They apologize where useful. They soften where strategic. They intensify where advantageous. But beneath it all the throne is occupied not by Allah's pleasure but by the crowd's.

The Qur'an answers with:

**wa Allahu wa rasuluhu ahaqqu an yurduhu**

Allah and His Messenger have more right that they should please Him.

That sentence restores the entire hierarchy of desire and fear.

Pleasing people has a place in Islam. Good character matters. Courtesy matters. reconciliation matters. But none of those sit on the throne. When pleasing people competes with pleasing Allah and His Messenger, the hierarchy must be clear or nifaq begins creeping in.

This is why people-pleasing is not always harmless softness. Sometimes it is a spiritual disorder. It can make a person betray truth slowly while still sounding agreeable. It can make him hide convictions, distort priorities, or calibrate religion around reception.

And perhaps the most dangerous form of people-pleasing is not dramatic compromise but constant inner governance by other people's reactions. We start asking of every matter: how will they take it? how will I look? how do I avoid disapproval? what arrangement keeps everyone comfortable with me? If that becomes the dominant filter, the soul is no longer facing Allah cleanly.

The verse heals this not by making people irrelevant, but by reordering rights. Allah and His Messenger are **ahaqq**. More rightful. More deserving. The believer does not stop caring about people, but he stops treating them as the final court.

This is liberating. A heart governed by human approval is never at rest. It is always adjusting, anticipating, editing itself, measuring the room. A heart governed by Allah's approval can still be gentle, wise, diplomatic, and careful, but it no longer kneels before fluctuating opinion.

There is a deep resonance here with 39:36: is Allah not sufficient for His servant? People threaten you with others besides Him, but if Allah is enough, the emotional tyranny of the crowd begins to loosen. Our verse makes the same movement in a communal register. They are still trying to stabilize life through pleasing people. The believer is taught to stabilize life through rightful orientation.

[PAUSE]

That is one of the clearest dividing lines between iman and nifaq: who actually sits on the throne of your approval-seeking.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Passage

These two ayahs move from a mockery of prophetic character to a correction of the believer's entire axis of approval.

Verse 61 begins with harm to the Prophet, then quotes the insult, then flips the insult into praise, then explains that praise through a three-part description: faith in Allah, trusting reception toward the believers, and mercy for them. Only after naming that beauty does the verse close by returning to the seriousness of harming the Messenger. So the first ayah is structured as:

harm -> insult -> reversal -> explanation -> warning

Verse 62 then moves underneath the specific insult and exposes the wider disease animating hypocritical behavior: swearing by Allah to please people. The verse closes by restoring the true hierarchy of who deserves to be pleased. So the second ayah is:

religious oath -> human audience -> hidden motive -> restored hierarchy

Together they form one unified lesson. The hypocrites misread mercy because their hearts are socially governed. They mock what serves the believers because they are still orbiting image and reaction. The Qur'an answers by defending prophetic mercy and then dethroning people from the center of the believer's approval-seeking.

### Questions to Carry

Have I ever mistaken gentleness, listening, or compassionate restraint for weakness simply because I was using worldly hardness as my measure of strength?

Sit with the kinds of people you instinctively respect. Do you honor prophetic mercy, or only forceful personalities? That answer shapes more of our religion than we think.

When I feel pressure, whose pleasure quietly governs my choices first?

Is it Allah's, or the room's? Is it revelation's, or the people whose disappointment I fear most? The verse is asking about the throne, not merely about one isolated action.

Do I use religious language at times to manage human perception rather than to stand truthfully before Allah?

This can happen subtly. We can sound pious while being driven mainly by reception, image repair, and social survival.

How do I respond to the Prophet's mercy when it confronts my instincts?

Do I love the prophetic way only when it aligns with my temperament, or do I also submit when his gentleness exposes the hardness I have normalized?

Am I building a heart that can please people responsibly without ever making them the ultimate court of approval?

That balance is difficult, but it is part of mature iman. Courtesy remains, but the throne belongs elsewhere.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs expose the heart that mocks prophetic mercy and swears religiously for social approval, then heal that disorder by naming the Prophet's listening as khayr and rahmah and returning the throne of pleasing to Allah and His Messenger.**

### The Closing Invitation

The invitation of this passage is to examine both how you read mercy and how you seek approval. Honor listening, receptivity, and gentleness when they are anchored in truth; do not let a hard world teach you to sneer at what Allah called good. And in moments of pressure, train your heart to ask first not, "How do I keep everyone settled?" but, "What would please Allah and His Messenger here?" That question does not make you harsher. It makes you freer, clearer, and more rightly aligned.

### Du'a

Allahumma, do not let our hearts mock what You named khayr and rahmah.

Make us people who honor the Prophet's character and learn strength through his mercy, not against it.

Protect us from seeking stability through the crowd's approval while neglecting what pleases You.

Let our words be truthful, our listening merciful, and our hearts governed by Your right above every other right.

Make pleasing You and Your Messenger sweeter to us than being accepted by people.
