# Do Not Let Their Oaths Purchase Your Pleasure

## Surah At-Tawbah (9:94-96)

[ayah:9:94] يَعْتَذِرُونَ إِلَيْكُمْ إِذَا رَجَعْتُمْ إِلَيْهِمْ ۚ قُل لَّا تَعْتَذِرُوا۟ لَن نُّؤْمِنَ لَكُمْ قَدْ نَبَّأَنَا ٱللَّهُ مِنْ أَخْبَارِكُمْ ۚ وَسَيَرَى ٱللَّهُ عَمَلَكُمْ وَرَسُولُهُۥ ثُمَّ تُرَدُّونَ إِلَىٰ عَـٰلِمِ ٱلْغَيْبِ وَٱلشَّهَـٰدَةِ فَيُنَبِّئُكُم بِمَا كُنتُمْ تَعْمَلُونَ

[ayah:9:95] سَيَحْلِفُونَ بِٱللَّهِ لَكُمْ إِذَا ٱنقَلَبْتُمْ إِلَيْهِمْ لِتُعْرِضُوا۟ عَنْهُمْ ۖ فَأَعْرِضُوا۟ عَنْهُمْ ۖ إِنَّهُمْ رِجْسٌ ۖ وَمَأْوَىٰهُمْ جَهَنَّمُ جَزَآءًۢ بِمَا كَانُوا۟ يَكْسِبُونَ

[ayah:9:96] يَحْلِفُونَ لَكُمْ لِتَرْضَوْا۟ عَنْهُمْ ۖ فَإِن تَرْضَوْا۟ عَنْهُمْ فَإِنَّ ٱللَّهَ لَا يَرْضَىٰ عَنِ ٱلْقَوْمِ ٱلْفَـٰسِقِينَ

*Sayahlifuna bi-Allahi lakum idha inqalabtum ilayhim li-tu'ridu 'anhum. Fa-a'ridu 'anhum. Innahum rijsun wa ma'wahum Jahannamu jaza'an bima kanu yaksibun.*

They will swear by Allah to you when you return to them so that you may leave them alone. So leave them alone. They are filth, and their refuge is Hell, as recompense for what they used to earn. They swear to you so that you may be pleased with them. But even if you are pleased with them, Allah is not pleased with the defiantly disobedient people.**

## Introduction

Some people do not return from exposure by repenting. They return by performing.

That is the atmosphere of these verses. The believers come back from Tabuk, carrying dust, effort, and the clarity that hardship often brings. The hypocrites, by contrast, have had time to prepare their strategy. They do not greet the returning believers with broken truth. They greet them with excuses, sworn statements, and a carefully staged hope: if we say enough, if we swear enough, if we sound pained enough, perhaps the pressure will lift and the community will let us back into moral comfort.

[PAUSE]

These verses are not mainly about clever speech. They are about the hunger to repair image without repairing the heart. Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir both preserve this frame clearly. The excuses are false. The oaths are strategic. The goal is not first Allah's forgiveness but social de-escalation: stop pressing us, stop regarding us as compromised, be satisfied with us again.

That is why the passage matters so deeply beyond Tabuk. Hypocrisy often survives through narrative control. It knows how to borrow religious language, sacred oaths, and emotional timing in order to make consequences feel excessive. It wants not necessarily to prove innocence, but to make confrontation feel rude and continued moral seriousness feel uncharitable.

The hook of the passage is this: **At-Tawbah teaches that hypocrisy often seeks rehabilitation through excuse, oath, and audience-management rather than repentance, but revelation trains believers to judge by what Allah knows, what deeds reveal, and whether Allah is actually pleased, not by how persuasive the performance sounds.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **يَعْتَذِرُونَ**: The Form of the Verb Already Tells You They Are Trying to Clear Themselves

The passage opens with:

**ya'tadhiruna ilaykum**.

They make excuses to you.

[PAUSE]

To hear the weight of this, we need a grammatical tool first. Arabic verb forms do not merely vary sound; they often add a layer of meaning. The root **'-dh-r** circles around excuse, justification, and removal of blame. When the verb comes in the form heard here, it carries the sense of seeking or producing an excuse for oneself. The action bends back toward self-exculpation.

That matters because the verse is not simply saying, "they speak to you." Nor is it yet granting the moral seriousness of true confession. The wording names an attempt to clear the self. The direction of the speech is already being exposed before we hear a single sentence from them.

Translation with "they apologize" can sound softer and morally cleaner than what is happening. Apology in English can include remorse. **Ya'tadhiruna** here is not primarily remorseful. In the tafsir tradition, especially with al-Tabari's emphasis on falsehood and vain pretexts, the word is functioning closer to excuse-manufacture than to repentance.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the Qur'an's great mercies. It does not let verbal formality hide moral direction. Two people may both speak after failure. One speaks to turn back to Allah. The other speaks to turn blame away from himself. The surface act looks similar. The wording helps us hear the difference.

That is why the response is so abrupt: **qul la ta'tadhiru**. Say: do not make excuses. Revelation interrupts the performance before it gathers emotional force. It refuses to grant the strategy more room than truth allows.

There is a further subtlety here. The excuses are offered **ilaykum**, to you, meaning to the returning believers. The real target is not only factual correction. It is relational repair on their terms. They want the audience back. That is crucial. Their speech is aimed not first heavenward but socially outward.

So the anchor teaches us to ask, whenever speech appears after wrongdoing: what direction is this speech moving in? Is it Godward confession, or audience-facing self-clearance? Arabic names that question inside the very first verb.

### 2. **قَدْ نَبَّأَنَا ... فَيُنَبِّئُكُم**: The Passage Answers Their Reports with a Higher Chain of Disclosure

Then verse 94 gives us a powerful repetition:

**qad nabba'ana Allahu min akhbarikum ... fa-yunabbi'ukum bima kuntum ta'malun**.

Allah has already informed us about your reports ... and He will inform you about what you used to do.

[PAUSE]

Here the tool is verbal pattern plus repeated root. The root **n-b-'** carries the sense of significant report, consequential news, not just any passing information. In the form used here, **nabba'a**, the verb becomes transitive and emphatic: to inform clearly, to disclose decisively, to make someone know something weighty.

That means the hypocrites are trying to manage the story through their own speech while the Qur'an answers by reminding them that revelation has already broken the monopoly of their version. **Qad nabba'ana Allahu**: Allah has already told us enough about you. Your narrative is not the first report in the room anymore.

And notice how the verse stages a whole chain of disclosure. First Allah has already informed the believers. Then: **wa sayara Allahu 'amalakum wa rasuluhu**. Allah and His Messenger will see your deeds. Then: **thumma turadduna ila 'alimi al-ghaybi wa al-shahadah**. You will be returned to the Knower of the unseen and the seen. Then again: **fa-yunabbi'ukum**. He will inform you.

[PAUSE]

That sequence matters. The hypocrites are operating at the level of reports and appearances. Revelation responds by lifting the matter through higher and higher courts of reality: divine disclosure now, divine and prophetic seeing in lived time, and final disclosure from the Knower of the unseen and the visible.

Translation gives the meaning, but the Arabic repetition lets you hear the overthrow of their image-management. They have **akhbar**, reports, stories about themselves. Allah has **naba'**, decisive informing. Their spin is not simply contradicted. It is engulfed by a more authoritative chain of disclosure.

Ibn Kathir and al-Muyassar both preserve this future-facing point: their deeds will still be seen. That means the verse is not trapping them in a past label if real repentance were to appear. But it is refusing to let present speech substitute for present proof. Deeds remain under sight.

There is also a spiritual reversal inside **fa-yunabbi'ukum bima kuntum ta'malun**. They were trying to tell others who they are. In the end Allah will tell them who they were by confronting them with what they actually did. That is sobering. Human beings often survive by narration. The Hereafter survives on disclosure.

So this anchor teaches us that the answer to manipulative speech is not mere suspicion for its own sake. It is relocation. Move the matter from self-report to divine report, from claim to deed, from audience management to the Knower of unseen and seen.

### 3. **لِتُعْرِضُوا / لِتَرْضَوْا**: The Two Purpose Clauses Reveal the Whole Strategy

The next anchor is not one isolated word, but two parallel purpose clauses:

**li-tu'ridu 'anhum**.

So that you will turn away from them.

**li-tardaw 'anhum**.

So that you will be pleased with them.

[PAUSE]

The tool here is the **lam of purpose**. In Arabic, this prefixed **li-** can tell you why something is being done. It exposes intended outcome. That means the passage is not only recording their oaths; it is diagnosing what the oaths are for.

And look at the order. First, verse 95: they swear so that you will turn away from them. They want pressure lifted. They want moral attention withdrawn. Then verse 96 intensifies the aim: they swear so that you will be pleased with them. First disengagement. Then approval. First end the discomfort. Then restore the relationship on favorable terms.

That sequence is psychologically exact. Many people who have done wrong do not initially ask for complete moral vindication. They ask for silence. They ask not to be pressed. They ask for space, softness, de-escalation, a lowering of the communal temperature. Once that succeeds, the next desire emerges: be satisfied with us again.

[PAUSE]

That is what translation can flatten. If you read both oaths merely as "they swore by Allah," you miss that the Qur'an is mapping a two-step strategy. The first oath seeks relief from confrontation. The second seeks restoration of approval.

Then revelation answers with stunning precision. **Fa-a'ridu 'anhum**. So turn away from them. The same root is returned, but not in the way they wanted. Their aim was to escape admonition by engineered softening. Allah commands a turning away that is itself judgment, not reconciliation. Then verse 96 delivers the deeper severance: even if you are pleased with them, Allah is not pleased with the defiantly disobedient.

That is why this anchor is so powerful. It teaches us not only to hear what people say after exposure, but to ask what outcome their words are trying to purchase. Relief? Distance from scrutiny? Recovered standing? Emotional absolution? The Qur'an is reading intention through grammar.

And that reading is spiritually liberating. The believer is no longer forced to collapse every oath into sincerity. He can ask, with calm moral intelligence: what is this speech trying to do to the community, and what verdict has revelation already placed on that aim?

## Part 2: Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Not Every Softening Is Tawbah

These verses are an education in one of the hardest distinctions in moral life: the difference between repentance and reputation repair.

[PAUSE]

Repentance is Godward before it is audience-facing. It begins with truth, not spin. It accepts exposure rather than resenting it. It is willing to bear shame if shame is what honesty now requires. Reputation repair, by contrast, is audience-managed. It wants consequences lowered quickly. It wants narrative control restored. It wants the emotional climate softened before truth has done its work.

That is why the first divine response in verse 94 is not, "let us hear them out sympathetically." It is, **la ta'tadhiru**. Do not make excuses. The passage does not deny that people can repent after serious failure. But it refuses to let excuse-performance impersonate repentance just because both use words after wrongdoing.

Al-Tabari's insistence that these are false justifications matters here. So does al-Muyassar's note that Allah has already exposed enough of their condition to make blind acceptance impossible. The issue is not that believers should never forgive. The issue is that forgiveness cannot be built on an agreed lie.

[PAUSE]

We need this distinction badly. Much of human life runs on strategic softening. A person harms someone, then works harder on tone than on truth. He wants the room to relax. He wants to sound sorrowful enough that no one keeps looking directly at the harm. He wants to be reabsorbed into ordinary emotional ease without the sharp, cleansing work of actual return.

Religious language can make this worse because it provides sacred tools for impression-management. Someone can swear by Allah, invoke humility, call attention to intentions, speak in the dialect of vulnerability, and still remain fundamentally committed to self-protection. The presence of pious words does not prove the presence of tawbah.

That does not mean believers become cynical people who deny all possibility of return. It means they become disciplined enough not to confuse early speech with finished healing. Real tawbah can speak, but it also accepts being seen, accepts changed behavior as necessary, and does not treat divine disclosure as the main problem.

[PAUSE]

This is why verse 94 keeps moving from excuses to deeds: **wa sayara Allahu 'amalakum wa rasuluhu**. Words are not irrelevant, but deeds remain the verifying field. In the Qur'an's moral grammar, the deed outlives the spin. The audience eventually matters less than the reality.

This is where 4:64 forms such a crucial contrast. There, people who wronged themselves come to the Messenger in a completely different posture. They do not arrive to manage perception. They arrive to seek Allah's forgiveness. The Messenger's role there is woven into a scene of actual return. Here, by contrast, the hypocrites approach after failure with excuses and oaths. Both passages involve speech after wrongdoing and both involve the Messenger, but they are morally opposite worlds. One speech bends upward toward tawbah. The other bends outward toward image control. The Qur'an teaches us to tell the difference.

That contrast is urgently relevant because many people want the emotional fruits of repentance without repentance's humiliations. They want relief, tenderness, resumed trust, and restored dignity while still editing the story so that their ego remains largely intact. But real tawbah usually includes a painful simplification. The self stops decorating the failure. It says, in effect: I did wrong. I need Allah. I do not get to control how true this looks while I am returning.

A practical test follows from this. When you fail, what do you want first: that people stop seeing the failure, or that Allah purify the failure's root in you? The answer often reveals whether you are leaning toward tawbah or toward reputation management.

One sign of truthful return is that it does not panic when it cannot immediately control other people's reaction. A person who is actually repenting may grieve the loss of trust, but he is willing to live under that loss for a while if that is what truth now requires. He is not bargaining: "I will admit enough only if the room warms up quickly." He is learning to let Allah heal the root before demanding that people heal the atmosphere.

And when others fail around you, the passage teaches patience without gullibility. Leave room for repentance, yes. But do not be so allergic to discomfort that you help false speech escape the judgment it was trying to outrun.

### Theme 2: Oaths Can Become a Currency for Buying Distance and Approval

The repeated **yahlifuna lakum** in verses 95 and 96 is deeply revealing. They swear by Allah, not because Allah has become the center of the matter, but because Allah's Name can be used to pressure the audience into a desired outcome.

[PAUSE]

That is a terrifying misuse of religion. The divine Name, which should humble the speaker, becomes part of the strategy by which the speaker tries to regain social ease. This is not ordinary lying. It is religiously armored lying.

That is why 63:2 is such an important companion verse: they have taken their oaths as a shield. The oath becomes protective technology. It is not used to surrender the self before Allah, but to manage how other people must now treat the speaker. Here in 9:95-96 the same dynamic appears in a more detailed form: the oath is meant to buy first disengagement and then pleasure.

And the believers are told something very clarifying: you do not owe an oath the result it was engineered to secure. An oath does not oblige you to call falsehood sincerity. An oath does not require you to grant premature approval. An oath does not erase what revelation already made known.

[PAUSE]

This is not a small lesson. Many people are trapped morally because they are too impressed by solemnity. If someone speaks with enough gravity, invokes Allah's Name, cries at the right moment, or sounds pained enough, they feel refusing him would be harsh. The Qur'an trains believers out of that reflex. Sacred language does not sanctify strategic intent.

There is also a communal dimension. If a community becomes easy to manipulate through pious performance, hypocrisy will learn exactly how to speak inside that vulnerability. It will swear when it needs to swear, cry when it needs to cry, soften when it needs to soften, and use the believers' hunger for harmony against the very truth that harmony was supposed to serve.

This is one reason unresolved wrong is so spiritually difficult for communities. Most groups do not merely love truth. They also love ease. They want meals to be peaceful again, gatherings to feel normal again, mutual glances to stop carrying weight. Hypocrisy knows that. So it often targets not only the conscience of the audience but the audience's fatigue. "Let us move on." "Do not drag this out." "You have heard my oath." "Why keep pressing?" Once the room starts preferring comfort to clarity, the liar gains a powerful ally: everyone else's exhaustion.

This is why verse 95's response is so important: **fa-a'ridu 'anhum**. Turn away from them. Not because truth no longer matters, but because their current speech is trying to purchase the wrong kind of resolution. Disengagement here is judgment, not reconciliation.

There is also wisdom in the fact that the verse does not command the believers to keep debating indefinitely. Once the strategy has been exposed, endless argument would only feed the performance further. Some people do not want clarification. They want a stage. They want enough back-and-forth to keep rearranging themselves in the audience's imagination. The Qur'an refuses to provide that stage. It names the strategy, gives the ruling, and denies the manipulator infinite conversational space.

That too is a mercy for communities. Not every controversy is healed by more airtime. Some are healed by refusing to let falsehood keep performing seriousness before tired hearts until fatigue starts sounding like the lasting truth before Allah in the end.

[PAUSE]

And then verse 96 goes deeper by shifting from external distance to internal approval. People often settle for less than vindication at first. They simply want the communal temperature lowered. But eventually most unrepented hearts still want more. They want to be thought well of again. They want clean emotional status.

The Qur'an therefore severs the link many of us instinctively make: if the community is satisfied, the matter must be settled. No. **Fa-in tardaw 'anhum fa-inna Allaha la yarda.** Even if you are pleased, Allah is not pleased. That line protects the believer from treating social comfort as an ultimate moral court.

So a hard but necessary discipline follows: do not let your discomfort with unresolved wrong make you cheaply purchasable by oaths, tone, and religious performance. Harmony is beautiful, but not when bought at the price of truth.

### Theme 3: Human Approval Can Be Restored While Divine Pleasure Is Still Absent

The final verse is one of the most liberating and sobering lines in the surah:

**fa-in tardaw 'anhum fa-inna Allaha la yarda 'ani al-qawmi al-fasiqin**.

Even if you are pleased with them, Allah is not pleased with the defiantly disobedient people.

[PAUSE]

This verse cuts a deep human confusion. We often think the restoration of relationship means the restoration of reality. If people like me again, trust me again, stop speaking of my failure, smile when I enter the room, and treat me as ordinary, then surely the matter has been healed. The Qur'an says: those are not the highest terms of judgment.

That is why the root **r-d-y** here is so powerful. It is a beautiful root elsewhere in the Qur'an. In 98:8, **radiya Allahu 'anhum wa radu 'anhu** names one of the highest states: Allah pleased with them, and they pleased with Him. But here the same root is used to expose a counterfeit equilibrium. Human pleasure may return while divine displeasure remains.

That is a frightening possibility, but also a merciful one. Frightening, because it means a person can successfully re-enter social comfort while still being spiritually unresolved. Merciful, because it frees believers from the crushing task of acting as final judges. You do not have to pretend your pleasure saves anyone. You do not have to carry the fiction that restored atmosphere equals restored standing before Allah.

[PAUSE]

Al-Tabari makes this especially clear: even if believers accept their excuses because they cannot see all that Allah sees, their acceptance does not benefit these hypocrites before Allah. That is not a contemptuous line. It is a metaphysical clarification. Human approval has limits.

This matters in almost every sphere of life. Public apologies, repaired reputations, resumed leadership, restored family ease, softened communal tone. Any of these can occur while the deepest matter remains unfinished. The Qur'an does not tell us that no restoration is possible. It tells us not to confuse restoration of atmosphere with restoration of divine pleasure.

And the closing word of the verse makes the standard even sharper: **al-qawmi al-fasiqin**. The root **f-s-q** carries the sense of stepping out, breaking beyond a boundary, leaving a proper enclosure. So the issue is not merely that Allah is emotionally displeased in some vague sense. The issue is that these people remain outside the boundary of obedience. Human approval cannot redraw the line they are still standing beyond. That is why the verse's logic is so unbending. Approval from inside the room does not erase the fact that a person may still be outside the line before Allah.

This should make us far more serious about what we actually seek when we say we want "closure." If closure means that the room feels easier while the soul remains outside obedience, then closure is a lie. If closure means truth has been faced, return has begun, and the line has been crossed back toward Allah, then it is mercy. The same social calm can accompany both situations, and the verse warns us not to mistake one for the other.

That distinction can save us from two opposite errors. One error is sentimentalism: if people feel better around me again, I assume the matter is healed. The other error is harsh absolutism: if the matter was ever serious, I assume no healing is possible. The Qur'an avoids both. It warns that human approval is not decisive, but it also keeps the decisive matter with Allah, which means the door of actual return is still His to open when the return is real. What it will not allow is the shortcut by which social ease pretends to be that return.

There is also an inward lesson here. Many of us are too eager to be pleased with ourselves once the social consequences ease. If no one is pressing anymore, if the embarrassment has passed, if others have moved on, we assume we may move on too. But the verse demands a more serious question: is Allah pleased? Has truth actually been faced? Has obedience actually been restored? Has the state of **fisq**, stepping outside the line of obedience, actually been abandoned?

[PAUSE]

This is where the passage becomes deeply cleansing. It relocates the whole moral drama. The important question is no longer, "How can I get them to think well of me again?" It becomes, "What would it mean to return to a state Allah is pleased with?" That shift is the beginning of sincerity.

And for the community, the verse offers a stable principle: do not let your relief at social closure become a theology. You may choose a certain outward dealing. You may lower a certain public tension. But never tell yourself that your emotional satisfaction settles what only Allah fully knows.

That is one of the reasons this line is so freeing. It protects kindness from becoming naivete, and it protects moral seriousness from becoming cruelty. You can refuse false closure without claiming omniscience, because the verse itself has already told you where the final pleasure and displeasure truly reside.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Passage

These verses are built on a pattern of **competing tribunals**. The hypocrites operate in the court of impression-management: excuse, oath, reduced pressure, restored approval. The Qur'an keeps lifting the case into higher courts that their performance cannot control. First, Allah has already informed the believers. Then Allah and His Messenger will see the deeds. Then there is return to the Knower of unseen and seen, who will disclose the truth fully. Their courtroom keeps shrinking while Allah's keeps widening.

The two purpose clauses in verses 95 and 96 also create a deliberate inner progression: first **li-tu'ridu 'anhum**, then **li-tardaw 'anhum**. First they want distance from censure. Then they want pleasure restored. The structure therefore is not a flat repetition of oaths. It is a two-stage map of manipulation answered by a two-stage refusal: **fa-a'ridu 'anhum** as judicial disengagement, and **fa-in tardaw ... fa-inna Allaha la yarda** as the severing of human pleasure from divine pleasure.

So the architecture is not simply "they speak, Allah responds." It is a contest between tribunals. Their language aims at the audience. Revelation keeps relocating the verdict above the audience until the audience itself is relativized.

### Questions to Carry

When you fail, do you first want truth, or do you first want the room to soften around you?

How often do you mistake solemn language, sacred oaths, or emotional intensity for actual repentance?

Have you ever been more eager for people's approval to return than for Allah's pleasure to return?

What speech in your life is aimed more at self-clearance than at Godward honesty?

If Allah weighed your current state apart from everyone's emotional reaction to you, what would still need repair?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These verses teach that hypocrisy often tries to buy social distance and restored approval through excuse and oath, but revelation lifts the matter above self-report and audience feeling until only Allah's knowledge, sight, and pleasure remain decisive.**

### The Closing Invitation

Take this passage into the places where you are tempted to repair image before repairing truth. Ask Allah to protect you from religiously flavored self-excuse, from using sacred language to get consequences lowered, and from settling for people's relief while His pleasure is still absent. And when others speak after failure, listen with mercy but judge with revelation: not every softening is tawbah, not every oath is sincerity, and not every restored atmosphere means the deepest matter has been healed.
