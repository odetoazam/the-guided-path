## INTRODUCTION

[ayah:24:35] ۞ ٱللَّهُ نُورُ ٱلسَّمَـٰوَٰتِ وَٱلْأَرْضِ ۚ مَثَلُ نُورِهِۦ كَمِشْكَوٰةٍ فِيهَا مِصْبَاحٌ ۖ ٱلْمِصْبَاحُ فِى زُجَاجَةٍ ۖ ٱلزُّجَاجَةُ كَأَنَّهَا كَوْكَبٌ دُرِّىٌّ يُوقَدُ مِن شَجَرَةٍ مُّبَـٰرَكَةٍ زَيْتُونَةٍ لَّا شَرْقِيَّةٍ وَلَا غَرْبِيَّةٍ يَكَادُ زَيْتُهَا يُضِىٓءُ وَلَوْ لَمْ تَمْسَسْهُ نَارٌ ۚ نُّورٌ عَلَىٰ نُورٍ ۗ يَهْدِى ٱللَّهُ لِنُورِهِۦ مَن يَشَآءُ ۚ وَيَضْرِبُ ٱللَّهُ ٱلْأَمْثَـٰلَ لِلنَّاسِ ۗ وَٱللَّهُ بِكُلِّ شَىْءٍ عَلِيمٌ

*Allāhu nūru s-samāwāti wa-l-arḍ. Mathalu nūrihi ka-mishkātin fīhā miṣbāḥ. Al-miṣbāḥu fī zujājah. Az-zujājatu ka-annahā kawkabun durriyyun yūqadu min shajaratin mubārakatin zaytūnatin lā sharqiyyatin wa-lā gharbiyyah. Yakādu zaytuhā yuḍī'u wa-law lam tamsas-hu nār. Nūrun 'alā nūr. Yahdī l-Lāhu li-nūrihi man yashā'. Wa-yaḍribu l-Lāhu l-amthāla li-n-nās. Wa-Llāhu bi-kulli shay'in 'alīm.*

Allah is the Light of the heavens and the earth. The likeness of His light is as a niche — inside it a lamp. The lamp inside a glass. The glass as though it were a pearlescent star — kindled from a blessed tree, an olive tree, neither of the east nor of the west. Its oil almost gives light even if no fire had touched it. Light upon light. Allah guides to His light whomever He wills. And Allah strikes parables for the people. And Allah is of all things All-Knowing.

---

Before we go anywhere: notice what this surah is named.

*An-Nur.* The Light.

Of all the subjects that occupy the 114 surahs of the Quran — resurrection, judgment, Paradise, Hellfire, the Prophet ﷺ, the heavens — Allah chose to name one of them after light. Not the source of light. Not the act of guiding. Just: Light.

And within that surah, there is one ayah — this one — that is the ayah the surah is named after. Everything in Surah An-Nur — the legal codes about public conduct, the protection of honor, the commands about lowering the gaze — leads to and flows from this verse. The laws are the structure. This verse is why the structure exists.

What it says, at its summit, before all the layered imagery: **Allah is the light of the heavens and the earth.**

Not "Allah created light." Not "Allah is like light." Not "Allah sends light." In Arabic, that statement is a *jumlah ismiyyah* — a nominal sentence, with no verb, expressing a permanent and essential reality. The way you would say "water is wet" — not something that became true or might become false. A simple, unbounded declaration of what IS.

From there, something unusual happens. Allah immediately qualifies it.

He says: **مَثَلُ نُورِهِۦ** — "the *likeness* of His light."

He just told us He IS the light. And then He says: "Let me give you a likeness of it." He's putting a flag on the door before we walk through. He is telling us, before the parable begins, that what follows is an analogy, not the reality itself. The image is going to be stunning. But He wants you to remember — from the first word — that the image is the pointing finger, not the moon.

And then He zooms in.

A niche in a wall. Inside it, a lamp. Inside the lamp, glass. The glass looking like a pearlescent star. The star kindled from a blessed tree — an olive tree that's neither eastern nor western. The oil of that tree on the edge of lighting itself without fire. Then: *nūrun 'alā nūr* — light upon light.

Five nested images, each one zooming toward a source that keeps receding.

**Here is what is stunning:** after all five layers, after the tree outside geography and the oil that almost ignites itself — Allah pulls back. He doesn't finish the zoom. He says: "Allah guides to His light whomever He wills."

We just traveled through an intricate, telescoping parable — and at the summit, we discover that the path didn't end where we expected. The light is not at the end of an arriving. It is bestowed.

Why? What is living inside يَكَادُ — *almost*? Why does the oil almost light itself but not quite? What is the hidden Kindler behind the passive verb? And what does a tree that belongs to no geography have to do with a light that cannot be bounded?

Stay with me.

## PART 1: THE LINGUISTIC JOURNEY

### I. مَثَلٌ — The Flag Before the Parable

In Arabic, the word *مَثَل* (mathal) carries several things at once: a likeness, a parable, an analogy, a proverb. But here it does something specific. It functions as a label that Allah places above what follows — before you enter the images, He tells you how to read them.

This is rarer than it sounds. In many places in the Quran, parables arrive without this label. Here Allah chose to say it explicitly: *mathal*. Likeness. What follows is not the reality; it is the closest the human mind can come to the reality using what it already knows.

Think about what that invitation requires of the listener. Every image that comes after is simultaneously two things: itself, and a pointer. The niche is a niche. But the niche is pointing at something else. The lamp is a lamp. But the lamp is pointing somewhere. You're supposed to travel through the image, not stop in it.

The Quranic tradition has a term for this kind of reading: *istidlāl al-ma'qūl bil-maḥsūs* — reasoning toward the unseen through what is sensed. Allah regularly uses the visible world as a launching pad toward an invisible reality. Not because the visible world is the subject. Because it is the most powerful set of tools the human mind already has.

A science class teaches about light. An astronomy lecture describes stars. A horticulture textbook analyzes olive trees. But those disciplines study the objects *as objects.* The Quran is doing something different: it is using those objects as *arrows.* Each arrow points somewhere that can't be described directly.

So when you enter this parable — when you look at the niche and the glass and the olive tree — you're not studying lamps. You're being trained to see what the lamp is pointing at. To go through the image, not to it.

And Allah doesn't just model this for us in this ayah. He names it at the end: **وَيَضْرِبُ ٱللَّهُ ٱلْأَمْثَـٰلَ لِلنَّاسِ** — "Allah strikes parables for the people."

The verb يَضْرِبُ deserves a moment. It's the same root as striking, hitting — the word you'd use for beating a drum or hammering a nail. Allah doesn't *offer* parables or *suggest* them. He *strikes* them. The parable lands with impact. It is not floating instruction; it is a blow of clarification. He strikes the image into your understanding.

Why parables at all? Because there is a reality — light, divine light, the light of guidance — that cannot be named directly. If Allah said only "My light is in you," the statement would be true and immediately evaporate. The mind needs a surface to land on. The parable creates the surface. It gives the truth somewhere to live inside the human mind's architecture of images.

So before we go one step further into the imagery: this whole parable is a gift from *عَلِيمٌ* — the All-Knowing. He knew how the human mind works. He knew it needed a niche and a glass and a pearlescent star to approach what "light of the heavens and the earth" actually means.

**That's what the مَثَل flag tells us.** We're not reading poetry. We're being guided through images that are themselves acts of divine knowledge about how we receive reality.

### II. يُوقَدُ — The Hidden Kindler

Now enter the parable.

There's a niche in a wall — *مِشْكَاة* (mishkāt). In the ancient Arab world, this was a specific architectural feature: a recessed alcove carved into a wall, designed not to hold something on display but to do something functional. A niche concentrates light. If you place a lamp on an open shelf, the light scatters in every direction — much of it lost. If you set it in a niche, the curved wall behind it reflects and directs. The light that would have dispersed backward is thrown forward instead. Nothing additional is done to the flame. The niche itself does the work of concentration.

Inside the mishkāt: a lamp. The lamp sits inside a glass vessel. The glass doesn't block the light — glass, in the ancient world, was one of the clearest materials that existed. It protects the flame from wind while being itself nearly transparent. But it does something beyond protection: it multiplies. Have you ever stood next to a candle inside a glass lantern? The glass catches the light and seems to glow from within. The glass becomes a second source.

Then the glass is compared to a pearlescent star — *كَوْكَبٌ دُرِّىٌّ* (kawkabun durriyyun). Not just a star. A star described with *durrī* — from *durr*, the Arabic word for pearl. A pearl-like radiance. The kind of light that doesn't just shine but glows with a layered luminescence. A pearl doesn't light up by reflection alone; it seems to absorb and then emanate. That's the glass vessel.

And then: this star is *kindled.*

The word is يُوقَدُ.

In Arabic, when you want to say "the lamp is lit" in the simplest way, you use Form I of the root وقد — *waqada* — simple ignition. But Allah didn't use Form I. He used Form IV: *awqada*. Form IV in Arabic is the causative form. It doesn't mean "to burn." It means "to *cause* to burn" — to make something ignite. The Form IV verb presupposes an actor. Something *causes* the kindling. The verb itself carries an implied hand.

And then Allah put the verb in the passive.

*Yūqadu.* It is being caused to kindle. By whom? Unstated.

This is the architecture of a theological statement. Form IV says: there is a Causer. Passive says: I will not name the Causer in this frame. The lamp is burning — caused to burn by an unnamed actor who chose not to appear in the sentence.

In a surah that opened with "Allah IS the light of the heavens and the earth" — Who do you think the unnamed Kindler is?

He is present before the parable begins. He is present at the end when He speaks of guiding whomever He wills. But inside the parable, at the moment of ignition, He steps back. The lamp is being kindled. The kindling is caused. The Causer is beyond the frame.

[PAUSE]

Think about what this does to the listener. We are inside a parable about light. We have watched the niche concentrate, the glass amplify, the oil saturate. And at the critical moment — when the question "how does it ignite?" demands an answer — the grammar itself answers with a shape we cannot fill. *Yūqadu.* Being kindled. That's all we're given.

We have a fire whose origin we are not shown.

We have a lamp in a niche in a surah called The Light, in a universe whose Lord is the Light of the heavens and the earth — and the grammar makes it simultaneously inevitable that He is the Kindler and impossible to name Him explicitly within the parable. The parable and the reality sit against each other like two panes of glass.

This is not accident. This is *'ilm* — knowledge. Allah's knowledge of how the human mind needs to be brought to a reality it cannot directly see.

### III. يَكَادُ يُضِيٓءُ — The Almost-Miracle, and What Fire Is Made Of

Now we've followed the zoom inward through the niche, the lamp, the glass, the star — and we arrive at the source of the oil. A blessed olive tree. And then: *lā sharqiyyatin wa-lā gharbiyyah* — neither eastern nor western.

Classical scholars explain this agriculturally: a tree that's not in shade — not behind a wall where morning (east) sun reaches it but afternoon (west) doesn't, or the reverse. A tree in an open clearing, turning all day in full sunlight. Such a tree produces the most potent, most luminous olive oil. Pure, saturated with light-energy.

But there's a deeper image here. East and west are not just solar directions. They are categories — spatial classifications that organize the world into regions of belonging. Everything in the physical world belongs somewhere: eastern, western, northern, southern, geographic. But this tree is *neither eastern nor western.* It belongs to no geography.

Something that belongs to no geography — that cannot be localized — is pointing at a light that also cannot be localized. The light of the heavens *and* the earth. Not concentrated in a spot. Everywhere and uncontainable.

The oil that comes from this tree that transcends geography — *that* is what's inside the lamp.

And now the most extraordinary line in the ayah.

**يَكَادُ زَيْتُهَا يُضِيٓءُ وَلَوْ لَمْ تَمْسَسْهُ نَارٌ**

*Its oil almost gives light — even if fire had not touched it.*

*Yakādu* — let's sit with this word. It's a verb of nearness. "Almost." "On the verge of." It describes something on the edge of happening but not quite. In Arabic grammar, it creates a condition of threshold — the subject is teetering between states.

The oil almost lights itself. Not quite. But almost.

And the verb it's almost doing: *yuḍī'u* — يُضِيٓءُ. This is also Form IV. Not "to shine" (Form I, intransitive) but "to cause light to happen" (Form IV, causative). The oil is almost performing the causation of light on its own. Almost making the fire unnecessary. Almost reaching a state where the external kindler isn't required.

Almost.

Now here is something that the Arabic morphological tradition confirms and that no translation has ever been able to carry. The word for fire in this ayah — نَارٌ — shares a root with نُور, the word for light. Both trace to the same Arabic root: ن-و-ر. Light and fire are not opposites in Arabic morphology. They are siblings. Fire is the denser, more volatile cousin of light, but they come from the same family.

So the fire that barely touches the oil — the *tamsashu nār* (تَمْسَسْهُ نَارٌ), "the fire that touches it" (and notice *tamsas* — the repeated *sīn* sounds, soft and deliberate, like the gentlest contact imaginable) — that fire is itself born from the root of the very light it kindles. The kindler and the kindled are, at the root level, the same thing.

[PAUSE]

What does يَكَادُ yūضِيٓءُ — "almost gives light without fire" — mean for the human being?

The classical scholars saw three things the parable could point to, and what's remarkable is that none of them is wrong:

Ibn Abbas and others read the niche as the believer's chest, the glass as their purified intellect, the oil as the natural human disposition (*fiṭrah*) — the pure inclination toward truth that every soul was sent into the world with. The *Prophet ﷺ* said: "Every child is born on fiṭrah."

Other scholars see the lamp as the Prophet ﷺ himself — the light of prophecy nested inside the niche of divine plan. The Quran calls him *sirājan munīrā* — "a lamp giving light" — in another surah.

Still others see the oil as the Quran itself — saturated with guidance, almost self-illuminating, requiring only the moment of genuine hearing for the light to cascade.

The composite is what's true. The Prophet ﷺ brings the Quran, which ignites the fiṭrah — the pre-existent capacity for light that every soul already carries. The three are nested, not competing. The oil is the fiṭrah. The fire is the revelation. The kindling is the moment of genuine contact. And the niche that concentrates it is the structure of sincere commitment that directs the light forward rather than letting it scatter.

Which is why يَكَادُ يُضِيٓءُ — *almost gives light without fire* — is the most human phrase in the ayah.

Because you know this state. You may be living in it right now. The oil is saturated. The capacity is fully formed. Everything in place. The niche is there, the glass is there, the tree was neither eastern nor western — and still. The fire has not touched. Not yet.

Almost.

[PAUSE — major]

## PART 2: THE THEMATIC DEPTHS

### Theme I: The Five-Layer Lantern — What You Are Already Made Of

Here is a question that is worth sitting with before we analyze anything.

Have you ever felt that someone else's faith looked different from yours? Not the rituals — the texture of it. You watch someone in prayer and their face looks like they're somewhere else entirely, somewhere you haven't been able to reach. Or you hear someone speak about their love of the Quran and you think: I believe the same things, I do the same acts, but when I try to describe what they're describing, there is... less. Something is present in them that I can't locate in myself.

Most people's first response to that gap is: *I need to try harder. I need more acts. More prayers. More knowledge.* The diagnosis is always the same — insufficient input.

The Light Verse doesn't agree with that diagnosis.

It doesn't describe a lamp that's missing fuel. It describes a lamp with everything in place — niche, glass, tree, oil — where the question is not whether the materials exist but whether the fire has touched.

Let's walk through the five layers and name what each one is.

The *mishkāt* — the niche — is concentration. It doesn't add to the flame; it focuses what's already there. In the human being, this is the capacity for *khushū'* — the inner posture that gathers rather than scatters. When you enter a state of prayer with genuine presence, the niche function is active. The light that would diffuse in ten directions is thrown forward. Nothing extra is required — just the architecture of genuine presence.

The glass — the crystal vessel that makes the glass glow like a pearl-star — is the purified intellect. The *'aql* that has been cleared enough to be transparent. It doesn't generate light on its own, but it holds and magnifies without blocking. When the intellect is used as a servant of truth rather than a guard of ego, it functions like glass: you see through it to the flame inside.

The oil — from the blessed tree, from neither east nor west — is the *fiṭrah.* The pre-loaded, pre-installed capacity for divine recognition. This is not earned or built. It is originary. Every human soul came into existence with a moment: *Alastu bi-rabbikum?* — "Am I not your Lord?" And every soul answered: *Balā* — yes. The covenant was made before birth. The oil is already in the lamp because that conversation already happened.

This is why يَكَادُ يُضِيٓءُ — almost lights itself — is possible. The oil is not depleted. The niche has not collapsed. The glass has not cracked. The tree from outside geography produced its harvest. The lamp is fully assembled.

What is missing?

The fire.

The *nafsiya* — the moment of genuine contact with revelation. Not exposure to it. Contact. The difference between hearing a *khutbah* and being broken by a single ayah at 3 AM. The difference between reading Quran as obligation and the moment when one line reaches through the text and touches you. *Tamsashu nār* — fire touching it — that's not a loud event. The verb تَمْسَسْ has those soft repeated *sīn* sounds. It's described as the gentlest contact. The fire barely touching.

And at that touch: *nūrun 'alā nūr.* Light upon light.

Not light where there was darkness. Light upon light — because the oil was already saturated with almost-light. The fire didn't import illumination from outside. It released what was always already there.

[PAUSE]

The practical implication is significant. If the fiṭrah is the oil — already present, not depleted — then the spiritual task is not the manufacture of raw material. You are not empty. You are not starting from zero. You are already made of oil from a blessed tree that grew outside the categories of east and west.

The question is not: how do I build more faith? The question is: what is blocking the fire from touching?

What is the *mishkāt* — the concentrating structure — that's been broken? The prayer that's rushed? The Quran recited without presence? The mind that knows the words but doesn't stay for them?

What is clouding the glass? Pride? The intellect used to defend rather than to receive?

The oil needs nothing added to it. It needs the fire to touch it. And the fire needs only to touch — barely — for the cascade to begin.

*Nūrun 'alā nūr.* Indefinite, both of them. No ceiling on the layering.

### Theme II: يَهْدِى مَن يَشَآءُ — The Parable Was Never the Path

After all of that — the niche, the star, the tree outside geography, the oil almost burning without fire, the light upon light — the ayah turns.

**يَهْدِى ٱللَّهُ لِنُورِهِۦ مَن يَشَآءُ**

*Allah guides to His light whomever He wills.*

If you've been reading the parable as instructions — as: *do these five things and you will have this light* — this line lands like unexpected cold water. The parable seemed to be describing a mechanism. A procedure. Five nested layers that together produce nūrun 'alā nūr.

And then: Allah guides to His light *whomever He wills.*

Not: whoever assembles the five layers correctly. Not: whoever is patient enough to find the blessed olive tree. Whomever He wills.

Let me say what this is not before saying what it is.

It is not fatalism. It is not: some people are preselected for light and others are not, and nothing you do matters. The surah that contains this ayah is full of commands — lower your gaze, guard your conduct, do not let corruption spread. These commands would be incoherent if nothing the human being does matters.

What it is: it is the correct orientation. The light is not at the end of a technique. It cannot be engineered. The parable was never the path — it was the description of the state of the one who receives guidance. The niche and the glass and the oil describe what the guided heart looks like from the inside. They are not a checklist for producing guidance.

Think about how the preposition works here. The verb is يَهْدِى — guides. And the destination is *li-nūrihi* — *to His light.* The *lā* indicates direction and purpose: the destination of genuine guidance is His light specifically. Not any light. Not self-generated illumination. Not the light of one's own reasoning, however sincere. The light that the heavens and the earth are already suffused with — *that* is where guidance leads.

You cannot guide yourself to it. You can prepare yourself for it. You can make yourself into the most finely constructed lamp possible — niche, glass, oil from the best tree — but the fire comes when He sends it. The kindling happens at *yūqadu* — that passive causative — where the Form IV presupposes an Actor and the passive conceals His name.

This is humbling, but it is not discouraging. It is the most clarifying thing in the ayah.

Because it means: the human task and the divine task are different. The human task is preparation — not manufacture of light, but the arrangement of the lamp. The divine task is kindling.

And here is what makes the *almost* bearable: يَكَادُ يُضِيٓءُ — the oil is almost there on its own. The distance between where the lamp already is and where it needs to be is *almost.* Not far. Not a complete reconstruction. Almost.

Which means the gap between you and nūrun 'alā nūr is the distance the fire needs to travel. And fire, in this ayah, barely touches (*tamsas*) — gently, quietly, the lightest contact — and that's enough.

**The asking is the arrangement of the lamp.** Every du'ā you make with genuine need — not recitation, genuine ache — is the oil presenting itself to be touched. Every moment of كَادَ — almost — every time you were on the edge of being cracked open by an ayah and then stepped back from the edge — that was the lamp fully arranged, waiting. The fire will touch it when He wills, for as many layers as He gives.

*Nūrun 'alā nūr.* Indefinite. No limit on the stacking.

## CLOSING SYNTHESIS

### The Architecture

Step back and look at the ayah as a whole.

It opens with a declaration: **ٱللَّهُ نُورُ ٱلسَّمَـٰوَٰتِ وَٱلْأَرْضِ** — nominal sentence, no verb, permanent reality. Allah *is* the light.

It ends with an attribute: **وَٱللَّهُ بِكُلِّ شَىْءٍ عَلِيمٌ** — Allah is of all things *'Alīm*. The intensive *fa'eel* pattern. Not عالم (one who knows) — عليم (one whose essence is knowing).

Between these two pillars lives the entire parable.

Why does the ayah close with عليم and not with نور? Why end on knowledge rather than on the attribute of light that opened the verse?

Because the parable was itself an act of divine knowledge. Every image was chosen with perfect understanding of how the human mind approaches the unseen. The niche because you needed architecture. The glass because you needed amplification. The tree outside geography because you needed to know the source transcends geography. The oil almost lighting itself because you needed to know how close you already are.

**He knew all of that about you. He knew what images you needed. He struck them, one after another, so that you could arrive at the edge of understanding what "Allah is the light of the heavens and the earth" actually means.**

That's عليم. Not just knowledge. The permanent, essential attribute of knowing — knowing you, knowing what you can hold, knowing which analogy would reach you. The parable closes with عليم because the parable proves it.

### The Architectural Shape

Declaration: **Allah IS the light** (before any image)
↓
Flag: **This is a مَثَل** — enter the images knowing they are images
↓
Zoom in: niche → lamp → glass → star → tree → neither east nor west → oil → almost
↓
Release: **Light upon light** — the cascade begins
↓
Ceiling removed: **Allah guides whomever He wills** — the zoom cannot complete itself
↓
Attribute: **عليم** — the parable was an act of knowing

The structure is a funnel that opens back out. You zoom in through five layers of physical imagery — getting closer and stranger at each level — and then the funnel opens again into divine sovereignty. You cannot arrive at the source by the route of the images. But the images were not wasted. They arranged the lamp. The kindling comes from beyond the frame.

### Questions to Carry

When you look at your own spiritual state — which layer of the lamp feels most compromised right now? Is it the niche (concentration, presence), the glass (the intellect used as barrier or as vessel), or the oil (the fiṭrah that may feel buried under years of noise)?

Where in your life are you living in يَكَادُ — almost? What is the territory of your almost? What would the fire touching it look like?

If the oil is the fiṭrah and the fiṭrah is already there — what are you still trying to manufacture that was never yours to build?

When you hear يَهْدِى مَن يَشَاء — does it feel like abandonment, or does it feel like relief? What's the difference between those two readings of the same phrase?

If the fire that barely touches is itself born from the root of light — what does that say about the moment of genuine hearing, genuine prayer, genuine ache before Allah?

### One-Sentence Distillation

You are already made of oil from a blessed tree that belongs to no geography — what remains is not manufacture, but the touch of fire from the One who kindled everything.

### The Closing Invitation

This ayah ends with يَضْرِبُ ٱللَّهُ ٱلْأَمْثَـٰلَ لِلنَّاسِ — *Allah strikes parables for the people.* Not for the scholars. Not for the spiritually advanced. For *al-nās* — the people. All of them. You.

The parable was made for you. The niche and the glass and the olive tree from outside geography were assembled in words because you needed a way into a reality that cannot be named directly. You were known, before you read this verse, to need the oil almost lighting itself. To need the gentle *tamsas* — the barely-touching fire. To need the cascade of *nūrun 'alā nūr* as an image that would make you feel how the stacking happens without end.

He struck this parable knowing you would read it. He closed it with *'Alīm* — the permanent, essential knowing — because the whole construction proves it.

The invitation is simple: arrange the lamp. Not manufacture light — arrange the conditions for receiving it. Repair the niche if it's broken. Clear the glass if it's clouded. Trust that the oil is already there. And then turn toward the One who said *yūqadu* in the passive, so you would know the kindling happens from beyond the frame of the analogy.

And ask. With the ache that يَكَادُ recognizes — the almost, standing on the edge, the hand almost on the handle.

He guides to His light whomever He wills.

Ask to be willed.

### Du'ā

*Yā Nūra s-samāwāti wa-l-arḍ — O Light of the heavens and the earth — You whose essence is light before any lamp was made, You who struck for us the parable of the niche and the glass so we could stand at the edge of understanding You — we come with our almost. With our oil present and our fire not yet touching. Arrange within us the conditions of Your guidance. Clear the glass. Concentrate the niche. And when You will, touch the oil — however lightly, however briefly — and let the cascade begin. You are of all things 'Alīm. You know what image it would take. You gave it to us.*

*Āmīn.*
