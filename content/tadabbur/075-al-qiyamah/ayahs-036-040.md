# The Body You Never Built

## Introduction

[ayah:75:36] أَيَحْسَبُ ٱلْإِنسَـٰنُ أَن يُتْرَكَ سُدًى
[ayah:75:37] أَلَمْ يَكُ نُطْفَةً مِّن مَّنِيٍّ يُمْنَىٰ
[ayah:75:38] ثُمَّ كَانَ عَلَقَةً فَخَلَقَ فَسَوَّىٰ
[ayah:75:39] فَجَعَلَ مِنْهُ ٱلزَّوْجَيْنِ ٱلذَّكَرَ وَٱلْأُنثَىٰ
[ayah:75:40] أَلَيْسَ ذَٰلِكَ بِقَـٰدِرٍ عَلَىٰٓ أَن يُحْيِىَ ٱلْمَوْتَىٰ

*Ayahsabu-l-insānu ay yutraka sudā. Alam yaku nuṭfatan min maniyyin yumnā. Thumma kāna ʿalaqatan fa-khalaqa fa-sawwā. Fa-jaʿala minhu-z-zawjayni-dh-dhakara wa-l-unthā. Alaysa dhālika bi-qādirin ʿalā ay yuḥyiya-l-mawtā.*

**Does man think that he will be left purposeless? Was he not a drop of emitted fluid? Then he became a clinging clot — and He created and proportioned. And made from him the two mates, the male and the female. Is not that Creator Able to give life to the dead?**

---

Surah Al-Qiyamah has been building a case for thirty-five ayahs.

It opened with a divine oath — not by a mountain or a star, but by the Day of Resurrection itself and the self-reproaching soul that already knows what's coming. Then it challenged the denier: *Does man think We will not reassemble his bones? Yes — We are able to proportion his very fingertips* [ayah:75:3] أَيَحْسَبُ ٱلْإِنسَـٰنُ أَلَّن نَّجْمَعَ عِظَامَهُ

[ayah:75:4] بَلَىٰ قَـٰدِرِينَ عَلَىٰٓ أَن نُّسَوِّىَ بَنَانَهُ

The argument was about the future — can Allah reassemble what decays?

Then the surah pivoted. It described the moment of death: when the soul reaches the collarbone, when the legs are brought together, when the dying person's household asks *"Who will cure him?"* and he knows the answer is no one. The present — how death arrives — occupied the middle of the surah.

Now, in its final five ayahs, the surah closes with its strongest argument. And the argument is not about the future and not about the present. It is about the past.

The opening asked: **can He do it again?**

The closing asks something more devastating: **were you ever yours to begin with?**

[PAUSE]

Listen to how the surah frames this. It doesn't open with evidence. It opens with an assumption — a comfortable, unexamined assumption that the human being carries like a wallet in a back pocket. And the five ayahs that follow will reach into that pocket and pull it out.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### I. The Word That Hangs in the Air — *Sudā*

There is a word at the end of ayah 36 that does more work than most sentences.

[ayah:75:36] أَيَحْسَبُ ٱلْإِنسَـٰنُ أَن يُتْرَكَ سُدًى

*Does man think that he will be left **sudā**?*

Let's talk about *sudā* — سُدًى.

In Arabic, when you want to describe a camel that has no rider, no harness, no direction — just wandering where it pleases, eating when it wants, sleeping when it wants, answerable to no one — the word for that camel is *sudā*. Free-ranging. Unattended. Purposeless in the most literal sense: nothing has been *applied* to it. No function assigned. No destination required.

Now notice what Allah is doing. He's not asking whether man *is* purposeless. He's asking whether man **thinks** he will be **left** that way.

The verb is *yaḥsabu* — يَحْسَبُ. Form I. Casual, reflexive assumption. This is not the verb you'd use for someone who has carefully reasoned their way to a philosophical position. This is the verb for the thing you've never actually thought about. You just... assumed it. The way you assume your wallet is in your pocket without checking. *Yaḥsabu* is the unexamined assumption. The thing you live by without ever having decided.

And the assumption is *yutraka* — يُتْرَكَ. Left. Abandoned. But notice: the verb is **passive**. *He will be left.* By whom?

[PAUSE]

The agent is unnamed. Arabic allows this — the passive voice conceals the doer. But the concealment here is not casual. There is only one being in the universe who could *leave* the human or *not leave* the human. Only one being whose attention determines whether a life has purpose or is just a camel wandering a desert. The passive voice forces you to fill in the blank yourself. And when you do — when you supply the answer — you've already conceded the argument.

If you can name who would have to leave you in order for you to be *sudā*, you've already admitted that **someone is not leaving you.**

[PAUSE]

And then there is the sound of the word itself. Say it: *sudā*. The *sīn* is a soft hiss. The *dāl* lands, and then the word opens into the trailing *alif maqṣūrah* — that unresolved vowel that just... hangs. The word doesn't close. It doesn't snap shut the way a strong final consonant would. It drifts. It wanders. It sounds like what it means: something left open, unresolved, uncollected.

The word sits at the end of the ayah the way purposelessness sits in a life. Quietly. Without a sharp enough edge to force you to deal with it.

Now — Imam al-Shafi'i, one of the greatest legal minds in Islamic history, built an entire argument on this single word. His reasoning: if Allah says the human being will *not* be left *sudā*, then the human being *must* be accountable. And if accountable, then there must be a framework — commands, prohibitions, a divine law — to be accountable *to*. From one word at the end of one ayah, al-Shafi'i derived the entire necessity of Shari'ah.

Al-Tabari confirms the reading: *sudā* means *lā yu'maru wa lā yunhā* — neither commanded nor prohibited. Not just aimless, but **unlegislated.** A life with no boundaries imposed from above.

And this is the assumption the Quran is about to dismantle — not through philosophy, not through abstract theology, but through something the listener already knows to be true about their own body.

[PAUSE]

### II. The Voice Shift — When the Human Disappears and Allah Arrives

Now watch what happens in the next three ayahs. Something shifts in the grammar that most translations cannot carry — and it is the architecture of the entire argument.

In Arabic, a sentence has a subject — the one doing the acting. And a sentence can have a passive subject — the one being acted upon. What makes these ayahs extraordinary is the pattern of who is acting and who is being acted upon.

Let me walk through it.

[ayah:75:37] أَلَمْ يَكُ نُطْفَةً مِّن مَّنِيٍّ يُمْنَىٰ

*Was he not a drop of fluid — emitted?*

The verb *yaku* — يَكُ — is the human being's verb. "Was he not...?" He was. He existed as a drop. But notice: the verb has been apocopated. The full form is *yakun* — يَكُنْ — but Arabic has clipped it to *yaku*, dropping the final *nūn*. The word is reduced. Shortened. Made small. And what is the human in this moment? A drop. The smallest, most insignificant form a human can take. The grammar mirrors the biology. The word shrinks to match the thing it describes.

And then: *yumnā* — يُمْنَىٰ. **Emitted.** Passive. The fluid doesn't emit itself. Even at the earliest conceivable stage — before there is a cell, before there is a heartbeat, before there is anything that could be called a person — the human being is already **not the agent of their own existence.** The passive voice starts here. At the very beginning.

[PAUSE]

Now ayah 38:

[ayah:75:38] ثُمَّ كَانَ عَلَقَةً فَخَلَقَ فَسَوَّىٰ

*Then he became a clinging clot — **and He created and proportioned.***

Stay with me. Something happens in the middle of this ayah that is easy to miss if you're reading quickly, and impossible to miss if you're reading in Arabic.

The first half: *thumma kāna ʿalaqah* — "then he was a clot." The subject is the human being. He *was* something. Still passive in posture — he didn't *become* a clot by effort; he just *was* one — but grammatically, the human is still the subject.

And then — *fa-khalaqa fa-sawwā*.

**He created. He proportioned.**

The subject has changed. Without announcement. Without transition. Without a single word to say "and then Allah..." — the sentence simply shifts from the human as subject to Allah as agent. One moment you are hearing about a clot. The next moment, the Creator is in the sentence.

And He arrives not with one verb but with two — placed in sequence, rapid, with only *fa* (so/then) between them.

The first: **khalaqa** — خَلَقَ. Form I. He created. This is the baseline verb of creation. Decisive, singular, complete. The خ (khā') is a guttural that starts in the back of the throat, and the ق (qāf) is the deepest sound in the Arabic mouth. The word itself sounds like something being brought into existence from depth.

The second: **fa-sawwā** — فَسَوَّىٰ. Form II.

Here's where we need to slow down.

In Arabic, verbs come in forms — patterns that reshape a root's meaning. Form I is the base: simple action. *Sawā* in Form I means to be level, to be equal. But Allah doesn't use Form I here. He uses Form II — *sawwā* — which takes that base meaning and **intensifies** it. Form II is the form of doing something thoroughly, meticulously, completely. Not just leveling — *proportioning*. Not just making equal — **perfecting every detail.**

Two verbs. One sentence. *Khalaqa* is the act of creation — the structure, the fact that something now exists. *Sawwā* is the art of creation — the proportioning, the balancing, the deliberate precision that makes every bone the right length, every organ the right size, every system integrated with every other system.

The design is the structure. The proportioning is where the care shows.

Think of a sculptor who casts a figure in bronze. That's *khalaqa* — the figure now exists. Then the sculptor spends weeks finishing the surface. Smoothing the brow. Adjusting the angle of the jaw. Ensuring the proportions carry weight from every viewing angle. That second phase — the one that takes longer, that requires more attention, that turns a shape into a form — that is *sawwā*. Form II. The verb that says: this was not mass-produced. This was **attended to.**

[PAUSE]

And you heard the sound shift. *Khalaqa* — choppy, strong consonants, decisive. *Fa-sawwā* — smoother, flowing, the double *wāw* almost humming as it moves into the open *alif*. The sound of the sentence changes when it moves from making to perfecting. As if the Arabic itself slows down when it reaches the stage that required patience.

### III. The Question That Cannot Be Answered — *Alaysa Dhālika bi-Qādirin*

[ayah:75:39] فَجَعَلَ مِنْهُ ٱلزَّوْجَيْنِ ٱلذَّكَرَ وَٱلْأُنثَىٰ

*And He made from him the two mates — the male and the female.*

The verb here is *jaʿala* — جَعَلَ — and it is not the same as *khalaqa*. *Khalaqa* is to bring something into being. *Jaʿala* is to **appoint** something — to designate, to assign it a role and identity. He didn't just create male and female bodies. He *made them into* male and female. He assigned the identity. He designated the role. From that same humble drop — *minhu*, from it — He appointed two kinds, and those two kinds now fill the earth.

The pronoun *minhu* — from him, from it — reaches back across three ayahs to the *nuṭfah*, the drop. The distance between "from it" and "the male and the female" is the distance between almost nothing and the entirety of human civilization. And the passage makes you hold both at once.

[PAUSE]

And then the closing:

[ayah:75:40] أَلَيْسَ ذَٰلِكَ بِقَـٰدِرٍ عَلَىٰٓ أَن يُحْيِىَ ٱلْمَوْتَىٰ

*Is not **that** [Creator] Able to give life to the dead?*

Three things to notice here. Three things that translations usually flatten into one.

**First:** the demonstrative — *dhālika* — ذَٰلِكَ. This is the **distal** demonstrative in Arabic. Not *hādhā* (this, close), but *dhālika* (that, far). Why distance? Because the demonstrative is pointing **back** — back through the whole creation sequence, back through proportioning and clots and drops and emission. *That One. The One who did all of that.* The word carries a sweep of the arm, a gesture that takes in the entire passage. Is not **that** One able?

**Second:** the word *qādirin* — قَادِرٍ. This is not a verb. It is an **active participle** — the *fāʿil* pattern. In Arabic, the active participle doesn't describe a single act. It describes a **state**. A *kātib* is not someone who wrote once — it's someone who is a writer. A *qādir* is not someone who was once able — it is someone who **is**, essentially and permanently, the Able One. The question is not "could He do this?" — a question about a moment. The question is "**is He not the kind of being for whom this is always possible?**"

The answer, of course, is yes. But the question is rhetorical — *istifhām taqrīrī* — a question whose purpose is not to ask but to confirm. To make the listener say the answer themselves.

**Third:** the surah's last verb — *yuḥyiya* — يُحْيِىَ. Form IV. The causative.

Form I of this root — *ḥayiya* — means "to live." But Allah doesn't use Form I. He uses Form IV — *aḥyā / yuḥyī* — which means "to **give** life." "To **cause** to live." The form encodes a theological truth that the English word "resurrect" cannot carry: life is not something the dead will *do*. It is something that will be *done to them*. By the same One who caused their first life from a drop they did not emit by their own will.

And notice: the surah's very last word is *al-mawtā* — ٱلْمَوْتَىٰ — **the dead**. The surah that is named "The Resurrection" ends on the word "the dead." The heavy *mīm* and *wāw*, the trailing *alif* — the word sits in the silence that follows it. And in that silence, the answer writes itself.

Now let's step back from the individual words and ask what the grammar of this passage has been doing as a whole.

In ayahs 36 and 37, every verb that applies to the human being is **passive**: *yutraka* (will be left), *yumnā* (emitted). The human never appears as agent. He is left. He is emitted. He simply *is* things — a drop, a clot — but he never *does* anything.

Then, in ayah 38, without announcement, Allah enters the sentence as the **active** agent: *khalaqa* (He created), *sawwā* (He proportioned), *jaʿala* (He made). Three consecutive active verbs. All divine. All Form I or Form II. All decisive.

The passage is grammatically divided: **the human being is always object. Allah is always subject.**

The human never once appears as the agent of their own creation. Not at the stage of the drop. Not at the stage of the clot. Not at the stage of proportioning. Not at the stage of diversification into male and female. At every stage, the human is passive and Allah is active.

This is not an accidental grammatical pattern. It **is** the argument. The proof that you will not be left *sudā* — purposeless, unattended, unaccountable — is that **you have never been unattended for a single moment of your existence.** The same hands that formed you from a drop, proportioned your bones, appointed your identity — those hands did not do all of that to then walk away.

The question *"Does man think he will be left sudā?"* is answered not by a counter-argument but by a **counter-history.** Your own history. Look at what was done to bring you here. Does the Artist abandon the painting?

[PAUSE]

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Comfort of *Sudā* — Why Purposelessness Feels Like Freedom

There is something deeply attractive about the *sudā* assumption.

Think about it honestly. If there is no ultimate accounting — if when you die, you simply stop, the way a candle goes out — then certain anxieties dissolve. The affair you're considering? No cosmic consequence. The money you're cutting corners on? No one tallying. The relationship you're neglecting? No record being kept.

*Sudā* is the assumption that underwrites a particular kind of modern peace. It's not nihilism — most people who live this way don't call themselves nihilists. They call themselves practical. Realistic. "I just focus on what's in front of me." They don't say "there is no God" — they say "I don't really think about it." That is *yaḥsabu*. Not a philosophy. An unexamined assumption. Something you carry without ever having decided to pick it up.

And it works. For a while. The camel wanders, eats, sleeps, and no one calls it to account. The absence of a rider feels like freedom.

[PAUSE]

But here's what the Quran does with this. It doesn't argue against *sudā* the way a philosopher would — with logical proofs for God's existence, or moral arguments for accountability. It argues **embryologically.**

Was he not a drop? Did he not become a clot? Was he not created and proportioned?

The argument is: **look at your own body.**

Your body is the evidence. Every cell that divided when you were a week old. Every bone that calcified to exactly the right length. Every neuron that wired itself into a network capable of consciousness. You didn't do any of that. You weren't even aware of it. You were, at every stage, the passive recipient of a creative act so meticulous that the Quran uses its most intensive verb form — Form II, *sawwā* — to describe it.

And the question the passage forces is this: **does the kind of being who does this — who takes a drop and turns it into a thinking, breathing, speaking person through a process of deliberate, stage-by-stage proportioning — does that being then leave the result unaccounted for?**

The sculptor doesn't smash the finished piece. The author doesn't burn the completed manuscript. The teacher who spent a year preparing a student doesn't say, on exam day, "Actually, no exam. Do whatever you want."

*Sudā* is the assumption that the exam was never coming. The Quran's answer is: **look at how much preparation went into you.** That level of preparation is not consistent with abandonment.

[PAUSE]

And there is something even deeper here. The comfort of *sudā* is a comfort that costs something — even if the person who holds it doesn't realize the price.

If there is no accounting, there is also no vindication. If no one is watching, then the injustice you suffered goes permanently unaddressed. The person who wronged you and prospered? *Sudā* means they got away with it. Forever. The person who sacrificed everything for a principle no one noticed? *Sudā* means it didn't matter. The mother who poured her life into raising a child who never thanked her? *Sudā* means the universe didn't keep score.

The denial of accountability doesn't just liberate the guilty. It **abandons the innocent.**

[PAUSE]

This is what Imam al-Shafi'i saw in *sudā*. If the human is not *sudā*, then accountability exists. And if accountability exists, then justice is guaranteed — not as a human project that might fail, but as a divine promise that cannot. The same God who proportioned your fingertips will proportion the scales.

Every passive verb in your creation is a receipt. And the One who authored those receipts does not lose them.

[PAUSE — major]

### Theme 2: The Argument the Body Already Knows

There is a reason the Quran chooses **this** evidence — embryological development — over every other possible proof for resurrection.

It could have pointed to the heavens. It does elsewhere. It could have pointed to the earth's revival after rain. It does that too. But here, in the surah that bears the name of the Resurrection itself, the closing argument is: **look at yourself.**

Why?

Because of all the proofs for resurrection, this is the one you cannot outsource. You can look at the stars and remain unmoved — they're far away, abstract, beautiful but impersonal. You can see rain revive dead land and think: "That's nature, not God." But you cannot look at your own body — the body that was assembled from a drop of fluid you did not will into existence, shaped through stages you did not supervise, proportioned with a precision you did not request — and say "I did that."

The passage is designed to make the argument **inescapable by making it personal.** Not "look at what God can do out there." Look at what God **did** do. To **you.** Right now. The body you're sitting in while you deny Him — that body is the proof.

[PAUSE]

And the argument has a specific logical structure that the classical scholars call *qiyās al-awlā* — reasoning from the lesser to the greater.

The harder thing — creating a conscious human being from a drop of fluid, through stages of clotting and forming and proportioning, and then diversifying that single origin into the two kinds that populate the earth — **has already been done.** You're the evidence. You're walking around in it.

The easier thing — doing it **again**, when the blueprint already exists, when the material has already been worked once — is what you're denying.

The Quran is not asking you to believe in something beyond your experience. It is asking you to be consistent with your own experience. If He could do the harder thing — and He did, because here you are — then the easier thing is not just possible. It is guaranteed.

This is why the closing question uses the active participle *qādirin* — قَادِرٍ — rather than a past-tense verb. The question is not "was He able?" — as if divine ability is something that comes and goes. The question is "**is He not the Able One**" — permanently, essentially, by nature? The answer to that question was written into your bones before you took your first breath.

[PAUSE]

And notice the specific progression the passage takes you through. It doesn't just say "God created you." It walks you through the stages:

**A drop** — *nuṭfah* — the smallest, most insignificant biological material imaginable. Something discarded, washed away, forgotten. You began as something forgettable.

**A clinging clot** — *ʿalaqah* — now there is adherence. Attachment. The material has found a wall to cling to. Something that was loose has become fixed. Something that was nothing has started becoming something.

**Created** — *khalaqa* — the decisive act. Existence is now a fact.

**Proportioned** — *sawwā* — not just existence, but precision. Not just a body, but **this** body. Every proportion deliberate. Every ratio intentional. Form II. Thoroughgoing. Meticulous.

**Male and female** — from one origin, two kinds. The diversity of the entire human race traced back to a single creative act from a single humble source.

The progression is from insignificance to precision to abundance. And at every stage, the human being is **passive** and the Creator is **active.**

[PAUSE]

Now here is the question the passage leaves you with — not stated explicitly, but hanging in every grammatical choice:

**If you were never the agent of your own creation — at any stage, in any way — on what basis do you assume you'll be the agent of your own ending?**

If you didn't author your beginning, you don't get to author whether there's a sequel.

[PAUSE — major]

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture You May Not Have Noticed

The surah opened, thirty-five ayahs ago, with these words:

[ayah:75:3] أَيَحْسَبُ ٱلْإِنسَـٰنُ أَلَّن نَّجْمَعَ عِظَامَهُ

*Does man think that We will not reassemble his bones?*

And now it closes:

[ayah:75:36] أَيَحْسَبُ ٱلْإِنسَـٰنُ أَن يُتْرَكَ سُدًى

*Does man think that he will be left purposeless?*

The same four opening words. أَيَحْسَبُ ٱلْإِنسَـٰنُ. The same human. The same unexamined assumption. But the direction has reversed.

In the opening, the question was about **divine power**: can He physically do it? Can He reassemble scattered bones? And the answer came immediately: *Yes — We are able to proportion his very fingertips.*

In the closing, the question is about **divine purpose**: would He leave His creation unaccounted for? And the answer comes not as a declaration but as a journey through the listener's own body — from drop to clot to creation to proportioning to male and female — ending in a question that answers itself.

The surah brackets the human denial between two questions. One about ability. One about intent. And together they dismantle the two walls behind which the denier hides: "He can't do it" and "He wouldn't bother."

[PAUSE]

And within these five closing ayahs themselves, there is a second architecture. The passage begins with *sudā* — purposelessness, abandonment, the assumption that no one is watching. And it ends with *yuḥyiya al-mawtā* — giving life to the dead. The journey of five ayahs is the journey from meaninglessness to resurrection. From the assumption that it's over to the certainty that it isn't.

And between these two endpoints, the passage takes you through your own creation — not as information, but as **evidence.** Every passive verb that described your coming into existence is a proof that you were never *sudā*. You were never unattended. You were never without a purpose or a Purposer. The body you're sitting in right now is the document that disproves the assumption you were carrying.

### Questions to Carry

Where in your life have you been living as though you are *sudā* — as though no one will ever read the exam you're writing?

If you stopped to really consider the body you inhabit — the proportioning you had no part in, the systems you didn't design, the consciousness you didn't request — what would that do to the way you treat tomorrow?

What is the difference between knowing that Allah created you and **feeling** that you were created — that every stage of your existence was attended to by Someone whose attention never lapses?

If the camel that wanders without a rider feels free, but the camel that serves a purpose arrives somewhere — which kind of freedom do you actually want?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The proof that you will not be left purposeless is written in the body that was never once yours to begin with.**

### The Closing Invitation

The next time you use your hand, flex it. Watch the tendons move under the skin. Watch the fingers close and open with a precision that no engineer has replicated. And then remember: *fa-sawwā.* He proportioned. Form II. Meticulous. Thoroughgoing. Deliberate.

That hand was not mass-produced. It was attended to. By Someone who attends to everything.

And the One who attended to your bones and your tendons and your fingertips — the One who took a drop of fluid and from it made male and female — is not the kind of being who walks away from what He made.

He is *qādir*. Always. Essentially. Permanently.

And He will bring you back.
