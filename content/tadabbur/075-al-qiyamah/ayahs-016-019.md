## Rebuilding the World

I need you to feel the strangeness of where we are before we look at a single word.

Surah Al-Qiyamah — *the Resurrection* — is one of the most vivid eschatological surahs in the entire Quran. It opens with Allah swearing two oaths: *by the Day of Standing*, and *by the self-reproaching soul*. Then it pulls you straight into the terror of that Day. The moon eclipsed. The sun and the moon joined together. The human being running, asking "Where is the escape?" And the answer: *there is no refuge — to your Lord, that Day, is the place of rest.* The face radiant, looking upon its Lord. The face darkened, knowing a back-breaking calamity is about to fall on it.

This is the world you're standing in. A surah about the end of everything.

And then — right in the middle of that vision — Allah stops.

He turns.

And He speaks to **one person**.

*"Do not move your tongue with it to hasten it."*

[PAUSE]

What just happened?

The surah was addressing humanity — all of us, the runners and the askers and the radiant and the darkened. And suddenly the camera pivots. The grammar turns. The address becomes singular, intimate, personal. Allah is no longer talking to the crowd at the Standing. He is talking to **Muhammad ﷺ** — here, now, in the moment that Jibreel is delivering this very ayah to him.

The Prophet ﷺ is receiving the words that describe the Day of Judgment. And as he receives them, he is trying — with the desperate eagerness of a man who does not want to lose a single syllable from his Lord — to move his lips along with Jibreel, to catch each word, to hold it, to memorize it before it can slip away. And mid-delivery, Allah pauses the revelation of the Day of Standing to correct his receiving of it.

Do you feel the vertigo of that?

The message interrupts itself to coach the Messenger on how to receive the message.

This is not a footnote. This is not a parenthetical that later compilers should have tucked away in a sira book. This is *in the Mushaf*. Allah preserved, forever, the moment of receiving as part of the thing received. The film has a moment where the director appears on camera — and that moment was kept in the final cut. On purpose. Between two ayahs about the terror of resurrection.

Stay with that for a breath before we go further. Because what we're about to walk through is not primarily about grammar. It's about what it means that the God of the universe interrupted the vision of the end of the world to tell one human being: *slow down. I've got this. Listen.*

## The Hook

Three times in these four ayahs, Allah says one word: *ʿalaynā*. **Upon Us.**

Upon Us is its gathering.
Upon Us is its recitation.
Upon Us is its clarification.

Three promises, stacked on top of each other, every one of them delivered from the throne to the tongue that was trembling to hold on. Each one begins with the same word, and that word — *ʿalaynā* — is fronted. Pushed to the beginning. In Arabic, when you move a word to the front that usually sits at the back, you are doing something. You are *loading* that word.

Allah is not saying "We will gather it." He is saying: **the gathering of it — falls upon Us.** The recitation of it — falls upon Us. The clarification of it — falls upon Us.

The Prophet's tongue can stop moving. Because three shoulders have just appeared under the weight it was trying to carry.

And we have to ask — what was the Prophet ﷺ so afraid of losing that Allah had to take the weight off him three times in four ayahs?

## PART 1: THE LINGUISTIC JOURNEY

### Element One: *Lā tuḥarrik* — the Form II of a moving tongue

Let me teach you something about Arabic before we look at this verb.

In Arabic, verbs live inside **patterns**. The same three-letter root can be cast into nine or ten different shapes, and each shape does something different to the meaning. Form I is the plain root — the simple action. Form II doubles the middle letter. And when you double the middle letter of an Arabic verb, you do one of a few things: you **intensify** the action, you make it **causative**, or — and this is the one I want you to feel — you make it **repeated, drawn-out, or thorough**.

Let me give you a simple example. *Kasara* in Form I means *he broke it.* One break. One clean snap. But *kassara* in Form II — with that doubled middle letter — means *he smashed it to pieces.* Not one break. Many. Thoroughgoing. The doubling of the middle letter *is* the doubling of the action.

Now watch what Allah does.

The root here is <span dir="rtl">ح-ر-ك</span> — ḥ-r-k — **to move**. Form I would be *ḥaraka* — *he moved it.* One movement. Simple. Clean.

But Allah does not say *lā taḥruk*. He says:

<div dir="rtl" style="font-size: 1.5em; text-align: center; margin: 1em 0">لَا تُحَرِّكْ</div>

*Lā tuḥarrik.* Form II. With the middle letter doubled. **Do not keep moving it back and forth.**

Notice something. Allah is not telling the Prophet ﷺ "don't move your tongue." He is telling him: *don't keep moving your tongue restlessly, repeatedly, in that rapid little trembling motion that you've started doing.* The form of the verb captures the exact kind of motion it is forbidding. Form II tells us this was not a single movement. This was a rapid, eager, repeated flicker — lips and tongue trying to match Jibreel syllable for syllable. The doubling of the middle letter of the verb **is** the doubling of the Prophet's lips. The grammar is echoing the gesture it is correcting.

[PAUSE]

Stay with what this tells you about the moment. The Prophet ﷺ was not casually listening. He was physically working with his mouth in time with the revelation. The angel spoke, and his lips moved, and his tongue moved, rapidly, anxiously, trying not to lose a single word. And Allah saw it. And Allah chose the one verb form in all of Arabic that would capture **not just the movement but the repetitiveness of the movement** — and used it in the negative.

*Do not keep doing that repeated little motion you are doing, right now, while I am saying this to you.*

One thing more. The word *bihi* — "with it" — appears twice in this one ayah. "Do not move **with it** your tongue, in order to hasten **with it**." The word "it" is the object of both verbs — both the tongue's moving and the Prophet's hastening. But look at what Allah has *not* done here.

He has not named what "it" is.

The grammar expects a referent. *With what?* But the revelation itself — the Quran — the thing that Jibreel is delivering in this very moment — is not named. It is just *hu*. *It.* Two times. The thing is too present to be named. It is so immediately here, in the Prophet's mouth, in his tongue, between him and Jibreel, that a name would create distance. Allah uses a bare pronoun because the Prophet knows exactly what "it" is. It is what is happening to him right now. This is the closest thing in language to pointing at something without words.

And then — and *then* — in the very next ayah, Allah gives it a name for the first time. And the name He gives it is stunning.

### Element Two: The Quran names itself — *wa-qurʾānahu*

The second ayah of this passage:

<div dir="rtl" style="font-size: 1.5em; text-align: center; margin: 1em 0">إِنَّ عَلَيْنَا جَمْعَهُۥ وَقُرْءَانَهُۥ</div>

*Inna ʿalaynā jamʿahu wa-qurʾānahu.*

"Indeed — upon Us is its gathering and its *qurʾān*."

I need you to notice what just happened, because almost every English translation hides this.

The word *qurʾānahu* — "its *qurʾān*" — looks identical to the name of the book we are reading. *Al-Qurʾān*. The Recitation. The name of the scripture. But in this ayah, that word is not functioning as a title. It is functioning as a **verbal noun** — a *masdar*. The root is <span dir="rtl">ق-ر-أ</span> — q-r-ʾ — *to recite*. The *fuʿlān* pattern turns a verb into an abstract noun of action. *Qurʾān* literally means *the act of recitation*, *the reciting of it*. The word that became the title of the entire revelation started as a common Arabic word for *recitation itself*.

And here — right here, at the moment of receiving — Allah uses that word in its raw, verbal sense. *Upon Us is its gathering and its being-recited.*

Let me slow down, because this is one of the quietest miracles in the Quran.

**The scripture is naming itself in the act of being delivered.** The Prophet ﷺ is being told not to move his tongue with *it*. And in the very next breath, he is told what *it* is: *qurʾān* — *the recitation itself*. Before the book exists as a book, before it has a binding, before it has a compiler, before it even has all of its ayahs — it has already named itself. And the name it chose for itself is not a noun meaning "the Word" or "the Book" or "the Message." It is a verbal noun meaning **the act of reciting**. The Quran is, at its deepest, not a thing. It is an act. It is what happens when it is being recited. It is the *qirāʾa*, not the *kitāb*.

Do you see what that does to how we hold this book?

You are not holding a text. You are holding the record of an act — an act of reciting that has been happening, uninterrupted, since Jibreel first said *iqraʾ* — *recite*. When you open the Mushaf, the Quran is not sitting still on the page waiting for you to read it. It is a verbal noun waiting to become a verb again. The name of the book tells you what to do with it. Say it out loud.

And that word — *qurʾān* — appears three times in this short passage. Twice as *qurʾānahu* and once as the verb *qaraʾnāhu* — "when **We** have recited it." [PAUSE] Notice who is the reciter in that ayah. Not the angel. Not the Prophet. **Allah.** *When We have recited it* — through Jibreel, to Muhammad — *then follow its recitation*. The royal plural at the moment of recitation is telling you something that will break open in a moment. The reciting of the Quran, at the source, is an act of **Allah** reciting. Jibreel is a conduit. Muhammad ﷺ is a conduit. Our tongues today are conduits. The original voice is the voice of the One who wrote it.

When a hafidh recites Quran in tarawih tonight, somewhere in the world, this is what is actually happening: the human tongue is the current station of an act that began with *qaraʾnāhu* — *We recited it*. The reciter is borrowing the verb. The action belongs to Allah.

### Element Three: The triple *ʿalaynā* — three promises, one weight lifted

Now we come to the architecture of the passage. Because once you see what the three *ʿalaynā*s are doing, you cannot unsee it.

Read the three promises out loud to yourself, slowly:

<div dir="rtl" style="font-size: 1.3em; text-align: right; margin: 1em 0">
إِنَّ عَلَيْنَا جَمْعَهُۥ وَقُرْءَانَهُۥ<br>
فَإِذَا قَرَأْنَـٰهُ فَٱتَّبِعْ قُرْءَانَهُۥ<br>
ثُمَّ إِنَّ عَلَيْنَا بَيَانَهُۥ
</div>

Three guarantees. Three times Allah says *ʿalaynā* — *upon Us* — with the preposition **fronted**, pushed to the front of the sentence for emphasis. In Arabic, you don't start with *ʿalā* unless you mean it. The default word order would put the subject first — *jamʿahu ʿalaynā* — "its gathering is upon Us." But Allah starts with *ʿalaynā*. **Upon Us — is its gathering.** Upon Us — is its clarification. The preposition is shouted before the noun. *We* — emphatic — take responsibility. Not you.

Three responsibilities, each taken off the Prophet's trembling tongue:

**One: *jamʿahu* — its gathering.** The root is *j-m-ʿ* — to collect, to assemble, to gather into one place. This is the word that will later be used for the compilation of the Mushaf by the companions after the Prophet's death. And Allah is saying: *its gathering is upon Us*. You, Muhammad, do not need to fear that ayahs will be lost. You do not need to move your tongue restlessly to memorize, because the gathering of these words into a whole is **My** work, not yours. The anxiety that is pushing your tongue — *I might forget, I might drop a word* — is an anxiety I am releasing you from, right now, inside the very revelation you are anxious about losing.

**Two: *qurʾānahu* — its recitation.** Upon Us is even the reciting of it. The fact that it reaches your mouth in a form that is recitable, with the pauses and the vowels and the rhythms intact — that is also on Us. You don't have to build the recitation. You receive it whole.

**Three: *bayānahu* — its clarification.** The root is *b-y-n* — clarity, explanation, the revealing of meaning. And note the word that comes before this one. Not *wa* — "and." But *thumma* — **"then."** *Thumma* in Arabic signals sequence and delay. It means *afterward*. It means: *this comes later*. Allah is not promising that the clarification will come with the recitation. He is promising that it will come **after** it.

[PAUSE]

Let that land.

The Prophet was eager not just to memorize but probably also to *understand*. And Allah is saying: Muhammad, the meaning will come. But not now. Not in this moment. First comes the receiving — the bare, preserved words. And *then* — later, in its own time, upon the occasions that call for it, through the subsequent ayahs that will explain earlier ayahs, through the sira of your life where each piece will find its home — *then* comes the clarification. And that clarification is also **upon Us**. Not upon you to figure out. Not upon your Companions to guess. The unfolding of what the Quran means is a process I am running, in time, and it too falls upon Me.

Three weights. Three shoulders. One Prophet released from carrying any of it.

And here is what the three taken together reveal. They are not random. They are a **lifecycle**. *Jamʿ* — the gathering of the words from the unseen into the audible. *Qurʾān* — the audible becoming the recitable. *Bayān* — the recitable becoming the understood. From the unseen → to the heard → to the recited → to the understood. Four stages, three promises, each one taken by Allah from the Messenger's shoulders.

And the Messenger's only job, between promise two and promise three, is one verb: *fa-ttabiʿ*. Form VIII. **Follow closely.** Listen to the reciting, then do what the reciting does. Not ahead of it. Not trying to keep up. Not trying to anticipate. Just follow.

### Bridge to the Thematic Depths

The grammar has shown us something now. A Form II verb that captured a trembling tongue. A verbal noun that was the scripture naming itself mid-delivery. Three promises stacked on a fronted preposition, pulling weight off the Prophet's ﷺ mouth in three directions at once. An order to follow that comes only after the One who speaks has finished speaking.

Now let's ask what this reveals not just about words — but about what it means to receive something from the Source.

## PART 2: THE THEMATIC DEPTHS

### Theme One: The eagerness that Allah had to slow down

I want you to think about a moment in your own life when you were trying not to lose something.

Maybe you were in a lecture by someone you deeply respected, and you were writing as fast as you could, afraid that the insight they were dropping would not return if you didn't catch it in the exact form they delivered it. Maybe you were in a conversation with a grandparent, and you could feel that they were telling you something they had never told anyone, and you wanted to remember the exact words. Maybe you were holding your child for the first time, and you wanted to memorize the feeling of their weight, and you were afraid the feeling would leak out before you could seal it in.

Do you remember the physical quality of that eagerness? How the body tightens? How the mouth sometimes starts moving along in silence, whispering back the words you are hearing, because the whispering feels like it will glue them in place?

[PAUSE]

This is what the Prophet ﷺ was doing.

And I want you to feel something that is very easy to miss. **Allah was not displeased with him for doing it.** Read the ayah again. There is no rebuke in it. There is no *why are you doing that*. There is no *don't you trust Me*. There is just a gentle, precise instruction: *don't move your tongue with it, don't hasten with it — I've got this.* The verb is an imperative, but it is not a command issued in anger. It is a command issued in **tenderness**. The way a parent says to a child clutching at a precious object: *you don't have to hold on so tight. I'm holding it too.*

The Prophet's ﷺ eagerness was not wrong. It was the eagerness of someone who loved what he was receiving and was terrified of losing it. That eagerness is the **mark of the lover**. Allah did not correct him for loving. He corrected him for carrying a weight that was never meant to be on his shoulders.

Now let me bring this home.

How much of your spiritual life is like this? How much of your relationship with the Quran, with your prayer, with your responsibilities to Allah, is you trembling to hold onto something you are terrified of losing — because you think it is on your shoulders to preserve it? You memorize ayahs and lose them and grieve. You start a dhikr routine and it slips. You make a promise to Allah in a moment of clarity and watch the clarity evaporate within hours, and you think: *it was mine to keep and I have failed to keep it.*

Listen to what Allah is saying to His Prophet in this ayah, because He is also saying it to you. **You are not the one holding this together.** You are not responsible for making sure the light does not go out. Your job is not to preserve. Your job is not even to fully understand in the moment. Your job is one word: *fa-ttabiʿ* — **follow**. Follow what is being recited in you. Follow what is being shown to you. Follow with a loose grip, not a clenched one, because the thing you are afraid of losing is being held by Someone whose hands are infinitely more capable than yours.

And here is the deepest part of this theme. The eagerness was not the problem. The **location** of the eagerness was. The Prophet ﷺ was trying to hold the revelation from below — through his tongue, his memory, his concentration, his fear of loss. Allah did not tell him to stop being eager. He told him to relocate the eagerness. Stop holding from below. **Be held from above.** *Upon Us is its gathering. Upon Us is its recitation. Upon Us is its clarification.* Three Divine shoulders under the weight. Your tongue can rest. Your listening cannot.

[PAUSE]

What would change, tomorrow, if you lived as if the things you love most in your deen were being held by Allah from above — not by you from below? What would your prayer feel like? What would your striving feel like? What would your grief over the losses of last year feel like, if you suddenly understood that the preservation of what mattered was never your job, and that Allah Himself will guarantee the *jamʿ*, the *qirāʾa*, and the *bayān* in the life He is slowly reciting through you?

### Theme Two: The interruption itself — when the speech breaks frame to address the speaker

There is a technique in cinema called *breaking the fourth wall*. The actor, mid-scene, turns to the camera and speaks directly to the audience. It shatters the illusion. It reminds you that the thing you are watching was made. That there is a person behind the lens. That the story is being *told* to you by someone who knows you are listening.

Almost no serious film does this. Because most films want you to forget the frame. They want the story to seem to arise from nothing, to exist independently, to be a world you are peering into. The fourth wall is almost never broken in reverent storytelling, because breaking it shatters the magic.

The Quran breaks the fourth wall all the time. And Surah Al-Qiyamah just did it, harder than almost anywhere else in the Book.

You are watching the Day of Judgment. The sun and moon joined. Faces radiant and darkened. The human being asking where the escape is. This is not a documentary. This is Allah describing, with rhetorical power, a reality that is coming. And right in the middle of that description — the camera turns. Allah stops describing the Day. He stops addressing the humanity that will face it. He speaks *to the Messenger who is receiving the description*. And He speaks about the act of receiving, the tongue, the eagerness, the guarantees.

And then — and this is the part that should make your head swim — after those four ayahs are delivered, the surah goes **back** to the Day of Judgment. Ayah 20 continues: *Kallā, bal tuḥibbūna l-ʿājilata* — *No! But you love the hastening one — the immediate world — and you abandon the Hereafter*. Back to the vision. Back to humanity. Back to the original scene.

Four ayahs in the middle of the Day of Judgment, addressed only to Muhammad ﷺ, about how he is holding his mouth right now.

Why did Allah preserve this?

Because He wanted you, reading it fourteen hundred years later, to know **that the Quran is not an impersonal text.** It is not a scroll that fell from the sky with its meanings sealed in. It is a conversation that included a moment when the One speaking turned to the one listening and said, *easy. I've got this.* And that moment is still in the Mushaf. Every time you read Surah Al-Qiyamah, you read, preserved forever, the moment of the Prophet's ﷺ eagerness and the moment of Allah's gentleness in return. You are not reading around the process of revelation. You are reading *inside* it.

And here is what that means for you.

Every time you read the Quran, you are not just reading what Allah said **about** things. You are sometimes reading what Allah said **to the one who was receiving it** — and by extension, what He is saying to you as the one receiving it now. When Allah told His Prophet *don't move your tongue to hasten it*, He was not only correcting a specific habit in a specific man. He was preserving a truth about how **anyone** should receive what comes from Him. Slow down. Don't try to grip. Don't race the source. Listen, and then follow.

The interruption is the teaching.

[PAUSE]

There is something else. Notice *when* in the surah the interruption comes. It comes in between **the terror of the Day** and **the indictment of the lovers of the immediate world**. That placement is not accidental. Allah is saying: before I tell you why humanity fails on that Day — *because they love the hastening life and abandon the Hereafter* — I will first tell My Messenger, *do not be among the hasteners even for the best of reasons*. Don't hasten with your tongue. Don't hasten with your understanding. Don't hasten with anything, Muhammad, even if what you are hastening toward is My own words. **The quality of the opposite of the Day of Judgment is not speed. It is following.**

The people of *al-ʿājila* — the immediate — lose the Hereafter because they rush. The Prophet, in the very same passage, is told not to rush — even toward revelation. The disease of the Day of Judgment is *ʿajal*. The medicine for the Messenger is *lā taʿjal*. The surah is not just describing the Day. It is coaching the one who will teach others how to avoid failing it.

And the coaching is this: **the antidote to the hastening life is a tongue that has learned to wait for Allah's voice before moving.**

That is why the interruption is there. Not as a parenthetical. As the *model*. In a surah about people who failed because they rushed, the Prophet is shown as the one who was taught, in real time, not to rush — even in the most sacred direction available to a human tongue.

## CLOSING SYNTHESIS

### The Architectural Reveal

Now we can see the architecture, and it is stunning.

Surah Al-Qiyamah is a surah about **hastening**. It opens with the vision of the Day that is hastening toward humanity. It describes the human running, asking where to escape, too late. It ends, in ayahs 20-21, with the indictment: *you love the hastening one and abandon the Hereafter*. The whole surah is a meditation on the danger of hastening — on being so in love with immediacy that you lose what is coming.

And placed **exactly in the middle** of that meditation — right at the fulcrum point of the surah — is a four-ayah passage in which Allah tells His Messenger, who is the most sacred human to ever live, *do not hasten, even toward My words.* *Lā taʿjal bihi.* Do not hasten with it.

The surah's diagnosis of humanity — they hasten — is placed in direct architectural conversation with the surah's instruction to the Prophet — do not hasten. And the conjunction between these two scenes is the three promises. *Upon Us is its gathering. Upon Us is its recitation. Upon Us is its clarification.* Three *ʿalaynā*s that say: the one who does not hasten can afford to wait, because the thing he is waiting for is guaranteed by the One whose shoulders can carry what human shoulders cannot.

The people who fail on the Day of Qiyamah fail because they tried to hold the immediate life from below. The Messenger who succeeds on that Day succeeds because he was taught to let Allah hold even the revelation from above. The surah is not two disconnected themes — it is one theme with the medicine placed in the wound.

The eagerness of the lover (*lā tuḥarrik bihi lisānak*) and the eagerness of the heedless (*tuḥibbūna l-ʿājilata*) look similar from the outside. Both are hastening. But one is corrected into stillness by Divine promises, and the other is condemned into loss by Divine indictment. The difference is not in the speed. The difference is in **what the tongue is moving toward, and who the hands are that are holding what the tongue is afraid of losing.**

### Bringing It Together

Let me trace what we've walked through.

The Form II of *ḥarraka* showed us the trembling eagerness of a tongue that loved what it was receiving. The absence of a referent for *bihi* showed us that the Quran was so immediately present in the Prophet's mouth that a name would have created distance. The naming of the Quran as *qurʾān* — a verbal noun, an act of recitation — showed us that the book we hold is actually the record of an ongoing act, and that act began with *qaraʾnāhu*: *We recited it*. The triple *ʿalaynā* showed us that the gathering, the recitation, and the clarification were all lifted off the Prophet's shoulders and placed on the Divine. And the one word left to him — *fa-ttabiʿ*, follow — showed us what remains for anyone who has been given this kind of a guarantee.

And the thematic work showed us two things. That Allah did not rebuke the eagerness — He relocated it. And that the entire surah is architected so that the Messenger's instruction on how *not* to hasten sits at the exact center of a warning to humanity about what happens when they *do*.

What rises from all of this is a single realization: **the Quran was given to a man who loved it so much he was trying to hold it with his mouth, and Allah responded by taking the holding into His own hands.**

That is how much Allah loves the lovers of His speech. He will not let them carry what they cannot carry, and He will not rebuke them for wanting to.

- What would it change, tomorrow, if you truly believed that the preservation of your dhikr, your Quran, your prayer, and your growth was not your responsibility but Allah's — and that your responsibility was only to *follow*?
- Which of the three promises do you most need to internalize right now — the *jamʿ* (that nothing good you do is being lost), the *qurʾān* (that what Allah wants said through you will be said), or the *bayān* (that the meaning of your life will be made clear in its own time)?
- When you read the Quran next, can you feel that you are reading inside a conversation — not outside a text? Can you hear, in these four ayahs, Allah turning to speak to the Messenger who was receiving the very words you are now reading?
- What would it feel like to stop racing the source?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The eagerness of the lover is not the problem — the location of the eagerness is, and Allah corrects it by taking the weight off the trembling tongue and placing it on His own shoulders three times in four ayahs.**

### The Closing Invitation

Tonight, when you pray — or when you open the Quran, or when you sit in any moment of trying to hold onto something you love for the sake of Allah — I invite you to do one thing. Loosen your grip. Not in the sense of caring less. In the sense of **trusting more**. Let your hands go soft around the thing you were afraid of losing. Say to yourself, quietly: *ʿalayhi jamʿuhu. ʿalayhi qirāʾatuhu. ʿalayhi bayānuhu.* Its gathering is upon Him. Its recitation is upon Him. Its clarification is upon Him. **My job is to follow.**

And feel how the shoulders drop. Feel how the tongue stops trembling. Feel how the eagerness, relocated from your strength to His promise, becomes a different kind of eagerness altogether — not the eagerness of fear, but the eagerness of someone who has just been told, by the One whose voice made the heavens: *I've got this. Listen.*

### Brief Du'a

O Allah, You who interrupted the vision of the end of the world to tell Your Messenger that the weight he was trembling to carry was on Your shoulders, not his — lift from our tongues the weight we were never meant to carry. Teach us to follow Your reciting instead of racing it. Grant us the eagerness of the lover without the terror of the loser. And when the clarification is slow in coming, let us remember that *bayān* was always on You, and always in Your time, not ours. Āmīn.
