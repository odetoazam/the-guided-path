# The Violence You Call Nightfall

## Ya-Sin 36:37-40

[ayah:36:37] وَآيَةٌ لَّهُمُ ٱللَّيْلُ نَسْلَخُ مِنْهُ ٱلنَّهَارَ فَإِذَا هُم مُّظْلِمُونَ

[ayah:36:38] وَٱلشَّمْسُ تَجْرِى لِمُسْتَقَرٍّ لَّهَا ۚ ذَٰلِكَ تَقْدِيرُ ٱلْعَزِيزِ ٱلْعَلِيمِ

[ayah:36:39] وَٱلْقَمَرَ قَدَّرْنَٰهُ مَنَازِلَ حَتَّىٰ عَادَ كَٱلْعُرْجُونِ ٱلْقَدِيمِ

[ayah:36:40] لَا ٱلشَّمْسُ يَنۢبَغِى لَهَآ أَن تُدْرِكَ ٱلْقَمَرَ وَلَا ٱللَّيْلُ سَابِقُ ٱلنَّهَارِ ۚ وَكُلٌّ فِى فَلَكٍ يَسْبَحُونَ

*And a sign for them is the night. We strip from it the day, and at once they are in darkness. And the sun runs to a resting place appointed for it. That is the decree of the Mighty, the Knowing. ## Introduction

Let's set the scene.

Ya-Sin. The surah the Prophet ﷺ called **the heart of the Quran**. And like a heart, it has chambers — and each one does a different kind of work.

The first chamber was a story. Three messengers came to a town, the town rejected them, a man ran from the far end of the city to plead with his people, and they killed him. When his soul entered Paradise, he said something extraordinary: "I wish my people could know." Even in death — advocacy. Even in bliss — concern for the very people who murdered him.

That was the human proof. What people do with signs when signs walk toward them in the form of other people.

Now the surah pivots. It turns the listener's face upward. Not toward a story anymore — toward the sky.

And the argument is different here. The parable asked: what did you do when the sign *came to you*? These ayahs ask something quieter and more devastating: what have you been doing with the signs that have been above you **every single night of your life**?

وَآيَةٌ لَّهُمُ ٱللَّيْلُ — *And a sign for them is the night.*

Notice the grammar. Not "a sign for *you*." For *them.* Third person. Allah is talking *about* the deniers here, not *to* them. They're not even worth addressing directly on this subject. The signs are above them and they don't look up. So Allah turns to those who will listen and says: let me show you what they're missing.

Now here's what we came for.

There is a word in this passage that should stop you. A word you would never expect for what it describes. Allah is about to tell you what happens when the sun sets — the most gentle, most beautiful, most photographed transition in nature — and **the word He chose belongs in a slaughterhouse.**

Let's find out why.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### I. The Skinning

In Arabic, every word carries the memory of its root. The root doesn't just give you meaning — it gives you a *world.* It gives you the physical, tactile, sensory universe in which that meaning was born.

Take the root *sin-lam-kha* — سلخ.

This is the word for **skinning an animal.** The butcher takes the hide and *salakha* it — strips it clean off the body underneath. You hear this word and your mind goes to a carcass. To a blade. To the violent separation of the outer layer from the thing it was protecting.

Now watch what Allah does with it.

نَسْلَخُ مِنْهُ ٱلنَّهَارَ — *We strip from it the day.*

[PAUSE]

*Naslakhu.* **We skin.** We flay. We pull the daylight off the night the way a butcher pulls a hide off a body.

Stay with this image. Don't rush past it. This is describing *nightfall.* The gentle dimming of the horizon. The slow turning of gold to amber to indigo. The thing you photograph from rooftops.

And Allah called it a **skinning.**

Notice the form. This is Form I — *fa'ala* — the simplest, most direct, most unmediated form of the verb. No intensification. No causation through an intermediary. Just: **We strip.** Allah Himself as the direct agent, performing the action without delegation.

And what is being stripped? *Al-nahaar* — the daylight. It's in the accusative case, the object receiving the action. The day isn't fading. The day isn't *going away.* The day is being **acted upon.** Something is being done *to* it. It is being removed — torn off — from the night underneath.

Now notice what's underneath.

The night isn't what *comes after* the day here. The night is what was there **all along.** The day was the skin. The light was the covering. Underneath — always — was darkness. And when the covering is stripped, what was always there is revealed.

[PAUSE]

Think about that for a moment. We live our whole lives assuming that light is the default. That the sun rises and the world is as it *should be.* That darkness is the interruption — the absence — the anomaly.

The Quran reverses this completely.

**Darkness is the default.** Light is the covering that Allah drapes over it. And every evening, He strips that covering away — and what you feel, what you sit inside, what surrounds you — is the raw, original, permanent state of things.

You're not watching the light leave. You're watching the *protection* being removed.

And then: فَإِذَا هُم مُّظْلِمُونَ — *and at once, they are in darkness.*

The word *fa-idha* — فَإِذَا — this is what Arabic grammarians call *idha al-fuja'iyyah*, the particle of **sudden surprise.** Not "then gradually they find themselves..." No. **At once.** Immediately. The covering is stripped and *instantly* — مُظْلِمُونَ — **they are darkened ones.**

That word: *mudhlimun.* مُظْلِمُونَ. It's a Form IV active participle — from the root *dha-lam-mim* — ظلم. This root gives us two realities that Arabic holds in the same hand: *dhulm* (ظُلْم) — injustice, wrongdoing, putting things where they don't belong — and *dhulma* (ظُلْمَة) — darkness. The language itself treats darkness and being-out-of-place as the same root condition. To be in the dark and to be in the wrong — Arabic says these are related.

But here the form is *mudhlimun* — Form IV participle — meaning "those who have entered into darkness." They don't produce the darkness. They don't cause it. They **enter** it. It swallows them whole. And the active participle makes this not a moment but a **state** — they *are* darkened ones. It's their condition. It's where they live.

And notice what's missing.

There is no description of their response. They don't react. They don't do anything. After the most violent verb in the passage — a skinning — the humans are just... there. Passive. In the dark. Standing still.

Remember this. We'll come back to it.

Here's the analogy that carries this.

Imagine you live in a house. It's warm, well-lit, comfortable. You've lived there your whole life. You think of the warmth and the light as *normal.* As the default state of things. You don't think about who maintains it — who pays the electricity, who insulates the walls, who laid the foundation.

Now imagine someone strips the walls off. Not slowly — *rips* them away. And suddenly you're standing outside. Exposed. Cold. In the dark. And you realize: the warmth was never yours. The light was never the default. Someone was maintaining all of it, and you never once looked at the walls and thought about the hands that built them.

**That is what happens every single evening.** Allah strips the day off the night, and for a few hours, you are supposed to feel what you actually are without the covering: vulnerable, dependent, exposed, small.

Night was never an inconvenience to be engineered away. It was a **daily lesson in who you are without His covering.**

### II. The Runners, the Stations, and the Cosmic Propriety

Now the passage moves from the stripping to the runners.

وَٱلشَّمْسُ تَجْرِى لِمُسْتَقَرٍّ لَّهَا — *And the sun runs to a resting place appointed for it.*

The verb: *tajri* — تَجْرِى. Form I. She *runs.* Not "moves." Not "proceeds." **Runs.** The same word used for a person sprinting, for water rushing in a riverbed. The sun is personified as a runner — urgent, purposeful, in motion.

And where is she running?

*Li-mustaqarrin laha* — لِمُسْتَقَرٍّ لَّهَا — to a *mustaqarr* appointed for her.

Let's sit with this word. *Mustaqarr* — مُسْتَقَرّ. This is a Form X noun — and Form X in Arabic carries the meaning of **seeking, requesting, considering.** The base root is *qaf-ra-ra* — قرر — which means to settle, to be stable, to find rest. Form I gives you simple settling. But Form X — *istaqarra* — means to **seek and find one's place of settlement.** A *mustaqarr* is not just any resting place. It's a resting place that has been **sought and arrived at deliberately.**

The sun isn't drifting toward an ending. She's running toward a destination she was designed to seek. Like a worker who works hard all day and then runs home — not because home is an afterthought, but because the whole day's work was oriented toward the return.

The classical scholars saw this destination differently, and what's remarkable is that each of them was seeing something real.

Al-Tabari understood the *mustaqarr* as the sun's **daily setting point** — each day she runs until she reaches her appointed place of rest, then returns. Ibn Kathir read it as a **fixed endpoint** — a destination at the end of time. Some companions, including Ibn Mas'ud, recited the variant reading *la mustaqarra laha* — "there is no resting place for her" — meaning she runs endlessly, without rest, a tireless servant. Each of these readings layers onto the others: daily obedience, cosmic journey, exhaustionless servitude. The sun runs without rest toward a rest she may never reach, and every running moment is an act of worship.

[PAUSE]

Then the divine signature:

ذَٰلِكَ تَقْدِيرُ ٱلْعَزِيزِ ٱلْعَلِيمِ — *That is the decree of the Mighty, the Knowing.*

*Taqdir* — تَقْدِيرُ. Form II verbal noun of *qaddara.* Not Form I *qadr* — a simple decree. **Form II: intensive, thoroughgoing, precise measuring.** This is not a rough estimate. This is engineering. Every sunrise, every orbit, every degree of inclination — *taqdir.* Calibrated.

And the two names that sign this calibration: *al-'Aziz al-'Alim* — ٱلْعَزِيزِ ٱلْعَلِيمِ. Might paired with Knowledge. Not just power — power guided by perfect information. These are both *fa'il* intensive adjective patterns, indicating permanent essential attributes — not momentary acts but the way Allah always is. The sun runs inside precision upheld by invincible knowledge.

Now the moon:

وَٱلْقَمَرَ قَدَّرْنَٰهُ مَنَازِلَ — *And the moon — We have determined for it phases.*

The same root — *qaddara* — but now the verb itself: **We precisely measured it.** Form II, first person plural, majestic. And what did He measure it into? *Manazil* — مَنَازِلَ — plural of *manzil*, from the root *nun-za-lam* — نزل — which means to descend, to alight, to take up a station. A *manzil* is a **stopping place on a journey** — the place a traveller rests for the night before continuing. Twenty-eight stations, one per night, each one precisely assigned. The moon doesn't wander. It moves from station to station like a traveller on a mapped route, alighting at each stop exactly on schedule.

Then the simile — **the only simile in the entire passage:**

حَتَّىٰ عَادَ كَٱلْعُرْجُونِ ٱلْقَدِيمِ — *until it returns like the old date stalk.*

*Al-'urjun.* ٱلْعُرْجُونِ. From the root *'ayn-ra-jim* — عرج — which means to be curved, to limp, to ascend by a winding path. An *'urjun* is the rachis of a date palm cluster — the curved stalk that holds the dates. After the harvest, when the dates have been picked and the stalk has dried for months, it becomes thin, yellowed, curved like a sliver — **exactly the shape of a waning crescent moon.**

*Al-qadim.* ٱلْقَدِيمِ. Not just "old" — **ancient.** The stalk that's been drying for seasons. Primordial in its thinness.

Now think about what just happened.

Allah is explaining the cosmos. The moon's twenty-eight-station journey across the sky. The mathematical precision of its waning. And the image He chose to make it land is something you could walk outside and pick up from the ground in a palm grove in 7th-century Hijaz. The curved, dried-out remnant of last season's date harvest.

**The entire universe explained through something you can hold in your hand.**

This is *Istidlal al-Ma'qul bil-Mahsus* — reasoning from the tangible to the rational — at its most intimate. Not a scientific lecture. Not an astronomical diagram. A dried date stalk. Because the Quran meets you where you are. It takes the cosmos and puts it in your palm.

[PAUSE]

Now the climax. Everything has been building toward this.

لَا ٱلشَّمْسُ يَنۢبَغِى لَهَآ أَن تُدْرِكَ ٱلْقَمَرَ — *It is not for the sun to overtake the moon.*

The word that unlocks this ayah: *yanbaghi* — يَنۢبَغِى.

In Arabic, there's a crucial difference between saying something **cannot** happen and saying something **is not befitting.** If you tell a child "you can't reach that shelf," you're describing a physical limitation. But if you tell a dignified elder "it wouldn't be appropriate for someone like you to do that" — you're describing something deeper. Not incapacity. **Propriety.** It's beneath them. It's not in their nature.

*Yanbaghi* is Form VII of the root *ba-gha-ya* — بغي. Form VII in Arabic is the **passive-reflexive** form — it means something that happens *to* the subject, something built into the subject's nature. *Inbaghaa* — it was befitting. *La yanbaghi* — it is *not* befitting.

Allah didn't say the sun **can't** overtake the moon. He said **it is not befitting for her to do so.** The impossibility isn't external — it's not a wall. It's *ontological.* It's built into what she is. The sun doesn't stay in her lane because someone is policing the boundary. She stays because **she was made in such a way that transgression is foreign to her nature.**

This is cosmic *adab* — propriety, respect for boundaries — encoded into the fabric of the universe.

And notice: *tudrika* — تُدْرِكَ — this is Form IV of *da-ra-ka* — درك — meaning not just "to reach" but **to cause oneself to catch up.** The causative form intensifies the negation: the sun cannot even *make herself* overtake the moon. It's not just that she doesn't — she can't even generate the will to try.

Then the parallel:

وَلَا ٱللَّيْلُ سَابِقُ ٱلنَّهَارِ — *nor does the night outstrip the day.*

*Sabiq* — سَابِقُ — is an active participle. Not a past tense verb ("the night did not outstrip") or an imperfect ("the night does not outstrip"). An active participle: **the night is not a preceder of the day.** It never has been. It never will be. This is its permanent identity — or rather, the permanent *negation* of an identity. The night has never been, is not now, and will never be the kind of thing that races ahead of the day. It's not even a possibility to negate. It's an identity that never existed.

[PAUSE]

And then — the final three words. The ones that change everything.

وَكُلٌّ فِى فَلَكٍ يَسْبَحُونَ — *And each, in an orbit, they swim.*

*Yasbahun.* يَسْبَحُونَ.

Two things are happening here, and both of them are extraordinary.

**First: the conjugation.** In Arabic grammar, non-rational things — objects, animals, celestial bodies — take the feminine singular or the feminine plural verb form. You would say *tasbahu* (she swims, using the singular for collective non-rational plurals) or *yasbahna* (they swim, feminine plural). But Allah used يَسْبَحُونَ — the *masculine rational plural.* The *waw al-jama'ah.* This is the form reserved for **conscious, thinking beings.**

The sun, the moon, the stars — they're being spoken about as if they are **persons.** Not objects hurtling through space. Not dead matter following physics. Persons. Conscious swimmers, each one deliberate, each one aware.

**Second: the root.**

The root of *yasbahun* is *sin-ba-ha* — سبح. To swim. To float. To move through a medium with grace.

Now consider: what is the most common word derived from this same root?

*Subhan.* سُبْحَان. Glory be to Allah.

*Tasbih.* تَسْبِيح. Glorification of Allah.

The celestial bodies' **swimming** comes from the same root as their **glorification.** Their movement through their orbits is not separate from their worship. It *is* their worship. **Every orbit is a prayer.** Every revolution is a *tasbih.* The sun doesn't swim in her orbit and *also* glorify Allah — the swimming **is** the glorification. The motion **is** the meaning.

[PAUSE]

Stay with this. Because it rewrites the entire sky.

When you look up at night and see the stars moving — slowly, silently, without deviation — you are not watching physics. You are watching *salah.* You are watching a congregation so vast it fills the sky, and every member of it is a swimmer, and every swimmer is a worshipper, and not one of them has ever missed a single prostration since the day they were created.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### I. The Dependence You've Deleted

Let's come home for a moment. To your life. To the world you live in.

When was the last time you experienced real darkness?

Not the dimmed lights of a bedroom. Not the city night with its orange glow bleeding up from the streets. Not the phone screen that lights your face the moment you feel the first flicker of discomfort.

**Real** darkness. The kind where you put your hand in front of your face and cannot see it. The kind where the world around you becomes pure sound, pure vulnerability, pure dependence on something other than your own eyes.

For almost all of human history, this was not a question. It was every single night. The sun set and you were *mudhlimun* — in darkness, full stop. No switch to flip. No lamp to reach for. You sat in it. You felt the weight of your own smallness. And in that sitting, something happened that no sermon could replicate: you learned, in your bones, that the light was not yours.

We have engineered that lesson out of existence.

We've built a civilisation that runs twenty-four hours. Artificial light has turned night into a slightly dimmer version of day. We eat at midnight. We scroll at 3 AM. We have effectively **deleted the experience of vulnerability that night was designed to teach.**

And here's the thing that should unsettle us: we deleted the experience, but we didn't delete the need. The dependence didn't go away when the lights came on. It just went underground. You are exactly as dependent on Allah at midnight under fluorescent lights as you would be sitting in a field under a moonless sky. The only difference is that the field would remind you.

The Quran doesn't say: *and at once, they lit their lamps.* It says: فَإِذَا هُم مُّظْلِمُونَ — *and at once they are in darkness.* Full stop. Darkness as destination. Darkness as state. The passage holds you there.

Think about the verb Allah chose. *Naslakhu* — **We strip.** The covering is ripped away.

Now ask: what if you experienced one sunset — just one — not as a photo opportunity, but as a *naslakh*? As a stripping. As the moment when Allah removes the protective layer of light and leaves you standing in your actual condition — small, dependent, unable to produce a single photon of the light you take for granted?

The premodern believer experienced this every evening. The *adhan* of Maghrib was not an interruption of their day. It was the recognition that **the covering has been stripped again.** That they were back in their raw state. That the only appropriate response to exposure is prayer.

We pray Maghrib too. But we pray it under fluorescent lights, surrounded by artificial day, insulated from the very vulnerability the prayer was designed to name.

[PAUSE]

Here's the practical question this ayah leaves you with: **What would change if you treated darkness as a teacher instead of a problem to solve?**

Not abolishing electricity. Not pretending modernity doesn't exist. But once — perhaps once a week, perhaps once a month — sitting in actual darkness for five minutes after Maghrib and letting your body remember what *mudhlimun* feels like. Letting the root meaning surface: the shared root between *dhulma* — darkness — and *dhulm* — being out of place. Feeling, in your chest, the truth that without Allah's covering of light, you are in the dark in every sense of the word.

The night is not broken daylight. It is the original state that Allah covers with a mercy called day. And the stripping of that mercy each evening is not a problem. It is a **sign** — *ayatun lahum* — for those willing to feel it.

### II. The Lane That Is Not a Cage

Here is the lie this generation lives inside.

**Freedom means the absence of limits.** The fewer boundaries you have, the freer you are. Every self-help book, every motivational speaker, every social media prophet says some version of the same thing: break through. Push past. Disrupt. Optimize. Refuse to be contained. The moment you accept a limit, you've lost.

"Live your truth." "No one can tell you who to be." "The only rules are the ones you set for yourself."

And then you look at the sky.

The sun — the most powerful visible object in your world, the source of all the light and warmth you depend on — **runs.** She doesn't drift. She doesn't meander. She *tajri* — she **sprints** toward a destination she didn't choose, through a path she didn't design, on a schedule she didn't set. And Allah calls this *taqdir* — precise engineering by the Mighty, the Knowing.

Is the sun oppressed?

The moon — the most beautiful visible object in your night sky — has been *qaddarnahu manazil* — **precisely measured into twenty-eight stations.** Each one assigned. Each one calibrated. The moon cannot skip a station. He cannot decide tonight he'd rather be full when he's supposed to be waning. He alights at each stop on schedule, like a traveller on a road he didn't build, visiting inns he didn't choose.

Is the moon diminished?

And then: لَا ٱلشَّمْسُ يَنۢبَغِى لَهَآ أَن تُدْرِكَ ٱلْقَمَرَ — *It is not befitting for the sun to overtake the moon.*

Not "she can't." Not "she's been forbidden." **It's not befitting.** It's not in her nature. It would be beneath her. The sun doesn't stay in her lane because there's a wall at the edge. She stays because her lane is her *identity.* Crossing it wouldn't be freedom — it would be self-violation.

[PAUSE]

Now look at the humans.

In the same passage, four ayahs apart, you have two groups: the celestial bodies and the people.

The celestial bodies are in constant motion. The sun runs. The moon moves through stations. Everything swims. *Yasbahun.* Active, deliberate, graceful, conscious.

The people? فَإِذَا هُم مُّظْلِمُونَ — they are just *there.* In the dark. Passive. Standing still. No verb of motion. No action. Just a state: darkened.

The entire cosmos is swimming in worship, and the only creature standing still is the one who was given the choice to swim.

Here's the paradox this passage makes you feel: **The creatures with no freedom are in perfect motion. The creature with total freedom is frozen.**

The sun can't leave her orbit, and she *runs.* The human can go anywhere, and he stands in the dark.

What if the orbit isn't the prison? What if the orbit is what *makes* the swimming possible?

A fish in the ocean swims. A fish on the shore flops. The difference isn't the fish — it's the medium. The water isn't confining the fish. The water is what makes the fish *what it is.* Take the fish out of the water in the name of freedom, and you haven't liberated it. You've killed it.

The sun swims — *tasbahu* — inside her orbit. The orbit is her water. The *taqdir* — the precise measuring, the limits — is not what holds her back. It is **what makes her swimming possible.**

And the word Allah chose for this swimming — *yasbahun* — shares a root with *tasbih.* Glorification. Worship. Their movement within their prescribed orbit is not obedience *despite* freedom. It *is* the worship. The limit is not the cost of the worship. The limit is **the form the worship takes.**

[PAUSE]

You know what this means for you.

The *shari'ah* — the prescribed way — is not a cage. It is an orbit. And the creature inside the orbit doesn't feel confined. She swims. She runs. She alights at her appointed stations. She does not experience the limit as oppression because **the limit is the shape of the worship.**

The question is not: *why does Allah limit you?*

The question is: *why are you the only creature in the cosmos who experiences the orbit as a cage instead of a home?*

The sun doesn't. The moon doesn't. The night doesn't. The stars don't. They were made with their limits built in — *la yanbaghi* — and they swim.

You were given the choice. And you stand still.

Not because you lack an orbit. But because you refuse to enter it.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture

Step back now. Look at these four ayahs as a single structure. Watch what moves.

**36:37** — **Violence.** نَسْلَخُ. The day skinned from the night. A butchery word for a gentle event. You are shaken. The passage begins by stripping your comfort — the way it strips the day from the night.

**36:38** — **Precision.** تَقْدِيرُ ٱلْعَزِيزِ ٱلْعَلِيمِ. Underneath the violence, you find engineering. The sun runs on a schedule. The decree is from the Mighty, the Knowing. You were shaken first — now you are shown the calibration underneath the shaking.

**36:39** — **Intimacy.** كَٱلْعُرْجُونِ ٱلْقَدِيمِ. After the cosmic precision, a dried date stalk from a palm grove. Allah brings the universe down to the size of something in your hand. He meets you where you are. The intimacy of a God who explains galaxies through agriculture.

**36:40** — **Worship.** وَكُلٌّ فِى فَلَكٍ يَسْبَحُونَ. And finally, the deepest truth. Everything — all of it — is worship. Every orbit is a prayer. Every movement is glorification. The swimming that is *tasbih.*

Violence → Precision → Intimacy → Worship.

This is how Allah teaches. He shakes you, then shows you the order underneath the shaking, then meets you where you are, then reveals the deepest truth of all: that the entire cosmos, from the most violent stripping to the gentlest curve, is in a state of worship.

And the sonic arc of the passage mirrors this movement. Listen: *naslakhu* — harsh, guttural, the *kha* scraping like the skinning it describes. *Taqdir* — measured, deliberate, each syllable placed. *Al-'urjun* — curved, earthy, the 'ayn bending like the stalk itself. *Yasbahun* — flowing, smooth, aquatic, the sound of something gliding through water without resistance.

From violence to flow. From scraping to swimming. The passage doesn't just describe cosmic motion. **It performs it.**

[PAUSE]

### Questions to Carry

When you look at the sky tonight, can you see them swimming — not as a metaphor, but as the Quran sees it? Conscious beings, each one a worshipper, each orbit a prayer?

When was the last time you let yourself sit in real darkness — not as a problem, but as a message? And what was the message?

If the sun runs eagerly toward her *mustaqarr* — her appointed rest — what is yours? Do you run toward it, or away from it?

When you feel the limits of the *shari'ah* as a cage, can you hear this passage asking: **since when does the swimmer resent the water?**

And the question underneath all the questions: the cosmos swims in worship. The sun runs. The moon alights. The night holds its place. **What are you doing?**

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Every creature in the cosmos is swimming in worship — this passage asks why you, the only one given the choice, are the only one standing still.**

### The Closing Invitation

Tonight, when Maghrib comes and the light begins to leave the sky, perhaps — for one evening — don't reach for the switch.

Let the *naslakh* happen. Let the covering be stripped. Let yourself sit in what the Quran calls *mudhlimun* — in the darkness — and feel the raw dependence underneath.

And then, when you stand for prayer, know that you are joining something. Not starting something. The sun is already running. The moon is already at its station. The stars are already swimming. The whole universe has been in *salah* since the day it was created.

Your prayer isn't the beginning of worship. It's the moment you finally enter the orbit that was always waiting for you.
