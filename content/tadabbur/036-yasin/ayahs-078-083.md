## INTRODUCTION

Someone picks up a bone from the desert floor. Old. Dry. Crumbled to the point where it barely holds its shape — the kind of bone where you can rub your fingers across it and it turns to powder between them. He walks up to the Prophet ﷺ, crushes the bone in his hand, lets the dust scatter in the wind, and asks: **"Who is going to bring THIS back to life?"**

The classical scholars identify this person as Ubayy ibn Khalaf — or in some narrations, al-ʿAs ibn Wa'il — one of the leading opponents of the Prophet ﷺ in Makkah. The gesture was not a sincere question. It was a demonstration. He wasn't asking for information. He was performing an argument. Look at this dust. Look at how nothing it is. And you're telling me that God will reassemble this? The question was meant to end the conversation.

And it did end a conversation — just not the one he intended.

Because Allah's response to the crumbled bone is not a single counter-argument. It is a three-proof cascade, each proof operating at a different scale, building from the personal to the natural to the cosmic — and then, past all three proofs, a statement so absolute that proof itself becomes unnecessary. The passage begins with a man holding dust and ends with the One who holds everything. And the journey between those two points is one of the most tightly constructed arguments in the Quran.

Here is what we are going to discover: Allah does not simply answer the question "who will bring bones back to life." He dismantles the *worldview* that made the question seem powerful. He shows that the question was never about bones. It was about a failure of memory, a failure of observation, and a failure of scale. And the Arabic marks each failure with surgical precision.

## PART 1: THE LINGUISTIC JOURNEY

### I. The Forgotten Origin — نَسِىَ خَلْقَهُۥ

وَضَرَبَ لَنَا مَثَلًا وَنَسِىَ خَلْقَهُۥ

*He struck for Us an example — and forgot his own creation.*

Before Allah records the man's question, He diagnoses the error. And the diagnosis is devastating in its simplicity.

The verb ضَرَبَ — *ḍaraba* — to strike, to put forward, to present. When used with مَثَلًا — an example, a parable — it means "he presented an argument." He performed a rhetorical act. He *struck* an example the way you strike a table for emphasis. The physicality of ضَرَبَ is deliberate — this man didn't just ask a question. He made a show. He crushed the bone. He scattered the dust. He performed.

And then: وَنَسِىَ خَلْقَهُۥ — and he forgot his own creation.

Two words that contain the entire refutation before the refutation even begins.

نَسِىَ — *nasiya* — from ن-س-ي — to forget. Not "he didn't know." Not "he was ignorant of." He *forgot*. The word implies that the knowledge was once available to him. He once knew — or should have known — that he himself was created. That the bones he holds were once part of a living person who was once a clot, who was once a drop, who was once nothing. He has this information. He simply forgot it. The arrogance of his question is not intellectual — it is a failure of *memory*.

And خَلْقَهُۥ — his own creation. The possessive pronoun هُ — his — makes this intensely personal. Not "creation" in the abstract. **His** creation. His own body. His own bones. The very hand that crushed the bone was itself once assembled from nothing. He is using created fingers to deny the possibility of creation.

[PAUSE]

Then the question:

قَالَ مَن يُحْىِ ٱلْعِظَـٰمَ وَهِىَ رَمِيمٌ

*He said: "Who will give life to the bones when they are crumbled?"*

The word رَمِيمٌ — *ramīm*. From the root ر-م-م — to decay, to crumble, to disintegrate. A *ramīm* is something that has passed beyond death into dissolution. Not merely dead — beyond dead. Reduced to dust. The word was chosen to maximize the apparent impossibility: not just bones, but *crumbled* bones. Not just dead, but erased. He is saying: the raw material is gone. There is nothing left to work with.

And وَهِىَ — "when they are." The circumstantial clause insists on the current state: right now, at this moment, in this condition. As though the current state of decay is the permanent and final truth about these bones.

This is how materialism always argues: it points at the current state of things and calls it the whole story. The bone is dust *now*. Therefore the bone was always meant to be dust. Therefore there is no going back. The present moment is promoted from observation to metaphysics.

### II. The First Proof: The One Who Started It — أَنشَأَ

قُلْ يُحْيِيهَا ٱلَّذِىٓ أَنشَأَهَآ أَوَّلَ مَرَّةٍ

*Say: "He will give them life who produced them the first time."*

The command قُلْ — *qul* — say. Directed at the Prophet ﷺ. The answer comes through him. And the verb in the answer is يُحْيِيهَا — He will give THEM life. The feminine pronoun هَا refers back to ٱلْعِظَـٰمَ — the bones. Allah accepts the terms of the question. You asked about bones? Fine. He will revive *them*. The specific thing you asked about.

But the proof is in what follows: ٱلَّذِىٓ أَنشَأَهَآ أَوَّلَ مَرَّةٍ — "the One who *ansha'a-hā* the first time."

أَنشَأَ — *ansha'a*. Form IV from the root ن-ش-أ. This is not a simple creation verb. Form IV — the causative — means "to bring into being, to originate, to produce from nothing." The difference between *khalaqa* (created) and *ansha'a* (originated/produced) is significant. *Khalaqa* is the general word for creation. *Ansha'a* carries the specific sense of *bringing something into existence that had no precedent*. It is the creation of what did not exist before in any form. There was no template. There was no raw material waiting to be shaped. There was nothing — and then there were bones.

And أَوَّلَ مَرَّةٍ — the first time. The phrase is precise. It says: there was a first time. The bones had a beginning. And the one who began them — from nothing, without precedent, without material — is still here. The argument is deceptively simple: if the first creation (from nothing) was possible, the second creation (from dust) is *easier*. You are asking about the harder thing as though the easier thing didn't already happen.

Then: وَهُوَ بِكُلِّ خَلْقٍ عَلِيمٌ — "and He is, of all creation, Knowing."

عَلِيمٌ — *ʿalīm* — the intensive *faʿīl* pattern. Not just *ʿālim* (one who knows) but *ʿalīm* (one whose knowledge is exhaustive, permanent, essential to His nature). And بِكُلِّ خَلْقٍ — of ALL creation. Every act of creating. Every created thing. The implication: He knows the blueprint. He knows where every particle of every bone in every grave has gone. The reassembly is not a puzzle for Him because He never lost the design.

### III. The Second Proof: Fire from Green Wood — The Paradox You Already Hold

ٱلَّذِى جَعَلَ لَكُم مِّنَ ٱلشَّجَرِ ٱلْأَخْضَرِ نَارًا فَإِذَآ أَنتُم مِّنْهُ تُوقِدُونَ

*[It is] He who made for you from the green tree, fire — and then from it you ignite.*

This is the proof that changes everything. Because it doesn't argue. It *shows*.

The first proof was logical: the first creation proves the possibility of the second. The third proof (coming next) is about cosmic scale. But this one — this is about something you hold in your hands every day and never think about.

ٱلشَّجَرِ ٱلْأَخْضَرِ — the green tree. *Al-akhḍar* — green. Alive. Wet. Full of water and sap and cellular structure. Green is the color of life in the desert. Green is what you see after rain. Green means: this thing is not dead. This thing is growing.

نَارًا — fire. The opposite of green. The opposite of wet. The destroyer of wood. The undoer of growth.

And Allah says: I placed fire *inside* the green tree. Not "I made fire after the tree dried out." Not "I brought fire from dead wood." From the GREEN tree. From the living, wet, growing thing. The Arabs knew this — certain trees, like the *markh* and the *ʿafār*, could be rubbed together while still green to produce fire. The fire was *in* the wood before the wood died. Life carried the seed of its opposite within it.

Now feel the analogy. You think bones turning to dust is an irreversible direction — life to death, wet to dry, organized to chaotic. But you also light fire from green wood every day. You extract the opposite from its opposite. You pull heat from moisture. You get burning from growing. If that doesn't strike you as a miracle requiring explanation, why does reassembly from dust?

The ayah is doing what the Quran calls *istidlāl al-maʿqūl bil-maḥsūs* — the core principle of this entire methodology. It takes something physical, observable, in your hands — fire from green wood — and makes it a stepping stone toward an unseen reality: resurrection from dust. The green tree is not the destination. It is the device. The real argument is: if opposites can already emerge from each other in your daily experience, then life emerging from death is not a category error. It is the same principle applied at a higher scale.

And notice: فَإِذَآ أَنتُم مِّنْهُ تُوقِدُونَ — "and then from it YOU ignite." The pronoun shifts to the second person. **You** — the one asking the question, the one holding the bone — you do this. You light fires from green wood. You participate in the emergence of opposites from each other. And you don't find it remarkable. You find *resurrection* remarkable, but you don't find *fire from living wood* remarkable. The passage is saying: you already believe in this principle. You just haven't noticed.

### IV. The Third Proof and the Names — ٱلْخَلَّـٰقُ ٱلْعَلِيمُ

أَوَلَيْسَ ٱلَّذِى خَلَقَ ٱلسَّمَـٰوَٰتِ وَٱلْأَرْضَ بِقَـٰدِرٍ عَلَىٰٓ أَن يَخْلُقَ مِثْلَهُم

*Is not He who created the heavens and the earth able to create the likes of them?*

The third proof is scale. The first was personal — your own creation. The second was natural — fire from green wood. The third is cosmic — the entire universe.

This is another rhetorical question — and the answer Allah provides Himself:

بَلَىٰ — Yes. He is.

And then two names: وَهُوَ ٱلْخَلَّـٰقُ ٱلْعَلِيمُ

ٱلْخَلَّـٰقُ — *al-Khallāq*. Not *al-Khāliq* (the Creator — active participle, *fāʿil* pattern). ٱلْخَلَّـٰقُ — the *faʿʿāl* intensive pattern. The one who creates constantly, repeatedly, abundantly. Creation is not something He did once. It is what He does — continuously, prolifically, without effort. The doubled lām in خَلَّـٰق carries this weight: this is not a single act of creation that is finished. This is a nature that creates without stopping.

And ٱلْعَلِيمُ — the Knowing. Already established in ayah 79 as عَلِيمٌ (indefinite). Now definite: THE Knowing. The al- makes it essential and exclusive. He is not just *knowledgeable* — He is THE one whose knowledge defines the concept.

The two names together — الخَلَّاق العَلِيم — answer the bone-crusher's question at its root. "Who will give life to crumbled bones?" The Khallāq — the one for whom creation is a constant activity, not a finished project. The ʿAlīm — the one who never lost the blueprint. The question assumed that creation is hard. These names say: creation is what He does. Endlessly. Knowingly. Without effort.

### V. Past All Proof — كُن فَيَكُونُ

إِنَّمَآ أَمْرُهُۥٓ إِذَآ أَرَادَ شَيْـًٔا أَن يَقُولَ لَهُۥ كُن فَيَكُونُ

*His command, when He intends a thing, is only that He says to it, "Be" — and it is.*

The passage has offered three proofs. Personal origin. Natural observation. Cosmic scale. Each one was sufficient on its own. Together, they are overwhelming. But now — past all three — the Quran says something that renders proof itself secondary.

إِنَّمَا — *innamā* — "it is only." This particle of restriction reduces everything to its essence. After all the proofs, the passage strips down to the simplest possible statement about divine power.

أَمْرُهُ — His command. His affair. His business. Not His effort. Not His struggle. His *amr* — the word that also means "matter, affair, thing." The creation of the universe and the resurrection of bones are all just His *amr*. His business.

أَرَادَ — He intended. From ر-و-د — to want, to will, to intend. Past tense in the conditional structure: when He has intended. The intention is already complete before the command is spoken. There is no gap between wanting and doing.

كُن — Be. One syllable. Two letters. The most concentrated expression of power in the Arabic language. An imperative verb from ك-و-ن — to be, to exist. Allah commands existence itself. Not "be made." Not "be assembled." Not "come together." Just: **Be.**

فَيَكُونُ — and it is. The *fā'* is immediate. The present tense يَكُونُ carries the vividness of something happening right now, before your eyes. Not "and it will be" — that would be future tense. "And it IS." The thing that didn't exist IS. The gap between command and existence is zero.

[PAUSE]

This ayah is the Quran's answer to every materialist framework that demands a mechanism. The bone-crusher wanted to know *how* — what process, what technology, what force could possibly reassemble dust into bones into life. And the Quran says: there is no process. There is a command. كُن. And the command is sufficient. Not because the question of mechanism is forbidden — the first three proofs engaged mechanism respectfully — but because the One doing the creating operates at a level where mechanism is optional. He can explain it to you — and He did, in three proofs. But He also wants you to know: He didn't need to.

### VI. The Closing — مَلَكُوتُ and the Return

فَسُبْحَـٰنَ ٱلَّذِى بِيَدِهِۦ مَلَكُوتُ كُلِّ شَىْءٍ وَإِلَيْهِ تُرْجَعُونَ

*So exalted is He in whose hand is the dominion of all things, and to Him you will be returned.*

سُبْحَـٰنَ — *subḥāna* — from the root س-ب-ح. Literally: to swim, to float, to move freely. When applied to Allah, *tasbīḥ* means to declare Him free, transcendent, far above any deficiency, any limitation, any inability that someone might attribute to Him. The bone-crusher attributed inability to Allah: "Who CAN do this?" And the surah closes: *subḥān* — He is far beyond the question.

مَلَكُوتُ — *malakūt* — dominion. But not the ordinary word for dominion (*mulk*). This is the *faʿalūt* pattern — an intensification. *Mulk* is kingship, sovereignty. *Malakūt* is absolute, total, all-encompassing sovereignty — the kind that includes not just the kingdom but the hidden, the inner, the unseen dimensions of everything. When the Quran says *malakūt*, it means: not just the surfaces of things. The depths. The interior. The hidden structure of reality itself.

بِيَدِهِ — in His hand. The image of holding. Everything — the bones, the fire, the heavens, the command — is held. And to hold something in your hand is to have complete power over it and complete intimacy with it. It is not distant ownership. It is held. Close.

And the last word of the surah: تُرْجَعُونَ — you will be returned. Passive voice. You will not return of your own accord. You will be *returned*. The action is done to you. And the destination is إِلَيْهِ — to HIM. The passage began with a man walking toward the Prophet holding a bone, asking a question. It ends with that same man — and all of us — being returned to the One who holds everything.

## PART 2: THE THEMATIC DEPTHS

### Theme 1: The Amnesia Beneath the Argument

Every sophisticated objection to God rests on a forgotten premise.

The bone-crusher's argument had a structure: (1) bones decay, (2) decay is irreversible, (3) therefore resurrection is impossible. It sounds airtight. It even *looks* airtight when you hold the dust in your hand and let it scatter. The evidence is visible. The conclusion is obvious. Case closed.

But the argument depends on something the arguer has forgotten: he was once nothing. The hand that held the bone was once cells that didn't exist. The brain that formulated the objection was once unformed matter. The entire system — the lungs that breathed while he asked the question, the vocal cords that vibrated, the tongue that shaped the words — all of it was assembled from raw material that was, at one point, exactly as "impossible" as the reassembly he's denying.

And the Quran's diagnosis is not "you are wrong." It is وَنَسِىَ خَلْقَهُ — "he forgot his own creation." The error is not intellectual. It is *amnesiac*. He has the data. He just can't access it. Because accessing it would require him to admit that he — the questioner, the performer, the one holding the demonstration — is himself the evidence for the thing he is denying.

This is not unique to Quraysh. This is the structure of every modern materialist objection that treats the current state of reality as self-explanatory. "The universe just IS." "Consciousness is just neurons firing." "Life is just chemistry." Each of these statements forgets its own premise. Each one takes the *result* of creation and uses it to deny the *process* of creation. You are using a created brain to argue that creation doesn't require a Creator. That is the amnesia the ayah names.

And the Quran does not shame the amnesia. It *reminds*. قُلْ — Say. Here is the reminder. The One who originated you the first time will do it again. Not because He owes you a proof. Because the proof is you.

### Theme 2: When Opposites Are Already Inside Each Other

The green tree proof is not just an argument. It is a lens.

We tend to think in categories that don't cross: life/death, wet/dry, growth/destruction, beginning/ending. These categories feel permanent. We organize our entire worldview around them. And when someone proposes that one category can become another — that the dead can become alive, that the ended can begin again — we reject it the same way we reject a category error in logic. It just doesn't work that way.

And then Allah points at a tree. A green, living, wet tree. And says: you pull fire from this. Every day. The opposite of water. The opposite of growth. The destroyer of wood. You reach into the living thing and extract the force that undoes living things — and you don't think about it. You don't pause when you light a fire and say, "How remarkable that the principle of destruction was embedded inside the principle of life." You just light the fire.

This is what the Quran does at its most powerful: it takes what you already know and shows you that you never saw it. The green tree is not new information. It is old information made visible.

And the lesson extends far beyond resurrection. If fire can live inside green wood — if the opposite can be embedded in its opposite, waiting for the right conditions to emerge — then what else is embedded in what you see?

Healing embedded in illness. The breakthrough embedded in the crisis. Growth embedded in the loss. Hope embedded in the grief. Not as metaphor. As structure. As the way Allah built the world. The Quran says: you already live in a universe where opposites emerge from each other. Resurrection is not the exception to how reality works. It is the *completion* of how reality works.

[PAUSE]

And this is why the passage closes not with another proof but with كُن فَيَكُونُ. Because after the proofs have shown you that the principle is already operative in your world — fire from green wood, your own body from nothing, the cosmos from emptiness — the Quran says: and if all of that still isn't enough, know this. He doesn't need the process. He doesn't need the mechanism. He doesn't need the wood or the seed or the embryo or the dust. He says Be. And it is.

The proofs are a kindness. They meet you where you are. They speak in the language of observation and logic. But the reality they point toward is beyond observation and logic. The reality is: the One who holds the malakūt of everything does not need your permission, your understanding, or your belief to bring you back. He needs one syllable.

And you will be returned to Him whether you bring a bone or not.

## CLOSING SYNTHESIS

### The Architecture

The six ayahs form a staircase that ascends in scale:

**78:** The man — holding dust, performing disbelief, forgetting himself.
**79:** Proof 1 — personal. Your own origin refutes your question.
**80:** Proof 2 — natural. The fire in the green tree refutes your categories.
**81:** Proof 3 — cosmic. The heavens and earth refute your sense of scale. And two divine names settle the matter.
**82:** Past proof — كُن فَيَكُونُ. The command that makes mechanism optional.
**83:** Closure — *subḥān*. The One who holds everything. And to Him you return.

Each step is wider than the last. From a hand holding a bone — to a body that was once created — to a tree producing fire — to the cosmos — to the command that created the cosmos — to the dominion that holds the command. The funnel opens outward until the bone that started the conversation is an infinitesimally small point in an infinitely large reality.

And the surah's last word — تُرْجَعُونَ — you will be returned — answers the first question not with argument but with *fact*. The man asked "who will bring bones back to life?" The surah ends: you will be returned. Not as a promise. Not as a threat. As the structure of reality. You will be returned the way water returns to the sea. The way the fire returns to the wood. The way everything returns to the hand that holds the malakūt of all things.

### Questions to Carry

What have you forgotten about your own creation that, if you remembered it, would change how you think about what is possible?

Where in your life are you treating the current state of things as the permanent truth — forgetting that the same God who created the current state can create a different one?

If fire already lives inside green wood — if the opposite is already embedded in its opposite — what might be embedded in the difficulty you are currently living through?

What would it mean to live in a universe where the Creator's relationship to creation is كُن فَيَكُونُ — not effort, not process, not mechanism, but command? How would that change what you ask for in prayer?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**A man held crumbled bone and asked who could bring it back to life; Allah answered: the One who made you from nothing, who put fire inside green wood, who built the cosmos, and who needs only one word — Be — and it is.**

### The Closing Invitation

The next time something feels impossible — irreversibly broken, permanently lost, categorically over — remember the green tree. The fire was already inside it. The opposite was already embedded. And the One who placed it there is the Khallāq — the one for whom creation is not a finished project but a continuous act. He is not done creating. He is not done bringing opposites out of each other. And He is not done with you.

### Du'a

*O Allah, You are the Khallāq, the ʿAlīm — the one who creates without stopping and knows without limit. Cure us of the amnesia that makes us forget our own origins while questioning Yours. Let us see the fire inside the green wood — let us see the proof that You placed in our daily lives but that we walk past without wonder. And when the scale of what we face exceeds our understanding, let us hear كُن — and trust that the distance between Your command and its fulfillment is nothing. In Your hand is the malakūt of all things, and to You we will be returned. Make that return the best thing that ever happens to us.*
