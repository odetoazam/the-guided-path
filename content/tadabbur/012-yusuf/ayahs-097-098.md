# They Asked Their Father to Seek Forgiveness, and He Carried Them Back to His Lord

## Surah Yusuf (12:97-98)

[ayah:12:97] قَالُوا۟ يَـٰٓأَبَانَا ٱسْتَغْفِرْ لَنَا ذُنُوبَنَآ إِنَّا كُنَّا خَـٰطِـِٔينَ

[ayah:12:98] قَالَ سَوْفَ أَسْتَغْفِرُ لَكُمْ رَبِّىٓ ۖ إِنَّهُۥ هُوَ ٱلْغَفُورُ ٱلرَّحِيمُ

*Qalu ya abana istaghfir lana dhunubana inna kunna khati'in.*

*Qala sawfa astaghfiru lakum rabbi, innahu huwa al-ghafuru ar-rahim.*

**They said, O our father, seek forgiveness for us for our sins; indeed, we have been wrong. He said, I will seek forgiveness for you from my Lord. Indeed, He alone is the All-Forgiving, the Most Merciful.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

The brothers have already been forgiven by Yusuf. But the surah is not finished with moral repair.

There is another face they must still turn toward: their father's. This is the man they made grieve for years, the man whose trust they broke, the man before whom they staged blood, tears, and false report. So the Quran gives us a small but unforgettable scene. The brothers speak to Ya'qub not with self-defense, not with excuse, not with narrative control. They say: ask forgiveness for us. We were wrong.

[PAUSE]

And Ya'qub, without centering his own injury, carries the request to Allah. He does not say, "After what you did to me?" He does not say, "You should have known better." He names his Lord, and he names the Lord's forgiveness and mercy.

The hook of the passage is this: **repentance is not complete when the truth is finally admitted; it deepens when the sinner learns to stand before the one they wounded and ask to be carried back to Allah.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Ya abana ... istaghfir lana**: The Return Comes Through the Father They Wronged

The brothers say:

**"O our father."**

That address matters. They do not bypass the relationship. They come back through the very bond they had violated. The surah has made us hear this father-son relationship under terrible strain for many ayahs, and now the sons finally speak from underneath it rather than around it.

Then they say:

**"Seek forgiveness for us."**

The verb **istaghfir** is not simply "pardon us." It is a request that he ask Allah to cover, forgive, and remove the burden of what they did. The brothers are not treating Ya'qub as the source of absolution. They are asking him to turn toward Allah on their behalf.

[PAUSE]

That is a mark of real moral awakening. The self that has been broken open no longer speaks as if the problem were only interpersonal embarrassment. It sees that sin has a vertical dimension. They harmed Yusuf. They shattered their father. But they also stand in need of Allah's maghfirah.

Then they say:

**"For our sins."**

The verse uses **dhunubana**. Not "our mistake" in a vague sense. Not "our situation." Not "what happened." The language has become morally plain.

And then:

**"Indeed, we were khati'in."**

This matters because the surah has already let them confess to Yusuf that they were **khati'in**. Now the same confession is repeated before Ya'qub. That repetition is itself part of the truthfulness. The wrong must be named where it was committed. It is not enough to admit it in the room that is easiest.

### 2. **Sawfa astaghfiru lakum rabbi**: Ya'qub Receives the Confession Without Performing Himself

Ya'qub answers:

**"I will seek forgiveness for you from my Lord."**

The first thing to notice is how little ego there is in the sentence. He does not magnify his own fatherhood. He does not dramatize his pain. He does not even say "from Allah" in the most general way. He says:

**"Rabbi"**.

My Lord.

[PAUSE]

That gives the answer a beautiful intimacy. The father receives their confession by returning to the Lord who has carried him through the whole surah. This is the same Ya'qub who poured his grief only to Allah and knew from Allah what others did not know. His answer now emerges from that same relationship.

Then there is **sawfa**.

The word does not flatten the response into an instant performance. Ya'qub does not make the scene about proving that he is generous on command. He promises that he will seek forgiveness for them. The request is accepted, but it is still carried with gravity. The matter is placed into time, prayer, and relationship with Allah rather than being consumed as one emotional gesture.

That is important. Mercy is present here, but it is not theatrical. Ya'qub's response is deep, not showy.

### 3. **Innahu huwa al-ghafuru ar-rahim**: The Final Word Is Not the Brothers' Wrong but Allah's Names

The answer closes:

**"Indeed, He alone is the All-Forgiving, the Most Merciful."**

The brothers' sentence ended on **khati'in**. Ya'qub's sentence ends on Allah's names.

[PAUSE]

That movement matters enormously. He does not deny their fault. But he refuses to let their fault be the final horizon of the exchange. The final horizon is Allah.

And the two names are perfectly fitted. **Al-Ghafur** addresses the sin itself: the covering, forgiving, removal of its burden. **Ar-Rahim** addresses what comes with that forgiveness: not merely legal release, but mercy that continues to hold and heal.

This is the spiritual intelligence of Ya'qub. He knows that if the sons are really to come home, they cannot remain trapped forever inside the vocabulary of what they were. They must be brought into the vocabulary of who Allah is.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Real Tawbah Learns to Face the Person It Harmed

Many people want repentance without relational humility.

They are willing to feel bad privately. They are willing to make a general statement to Allah. They are sometimes even willing to say, "I made mistakes." But they resist the smaller, harder act: going to the person they hurt and speaking plainly from beneath the truth.

[PAUSE]

The brothers do not get to stop at Yusuf's pardon. They still have to stand before Ya'qub. That teaches us something essential about tawbah. Wrongdoing rarely damages only one surface. Sin travels through rooms. It wounds vertically and horizontally. It strikes Allah's command and people's hearts together.

So repentance deepens when a person stops managing optics and begins repairing the actual fabric they tore. That may mean admitting what happened without polishing it. It may mean asking for du'a from the very person who had reason to withdraw from you. It may mean standing under shame without making the other person responsible for rescuing you from it immediately.

That is a hard mercy. But it is mercy nonetheless. Allah does not expose us only to crush us. He exposes us so we can walk back through the true doors of return.

### Theme 2: The Most Beautiful Human Mediation Is the One That Forwards You to Allah

Ya'qub's response is a model of spiritual parenthood, leadership, and injured mercy.

He does not make the brothers stay circling around him. He does not use their confession to secure emotional control. He does not turn their need into his platform. He carries them to Allah.

[PAUSE]

That is one of the purest things a human being can do for another. Whether as parent, teacher, spouse, friend, or elder, the best of us are not the ones who make people remain indefinitely dependent on our mood. The best of us are the ones who, after truth has been told, help others face Allah more honestly.

This is especially searching for anyone who has been wronged. Pain can create a hidden temptation to remain central. When someone finally bends, we may want them to stay bent toward us so that our injured self can keep drinking from the scene. Ya'qub shows a different nobility. He does not erase what happened. He simply refuses to become a wall when he can become a doorway.

And because he has suffered with Allah already, he is able to do that without trivializing the wound. His mercy is not cheap. It has been ripened by years of grief.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayahs are brief, but they complete a crucial moral arc. The brothers address their father directly, ask him to seek forgiveness for their sins, and confess openly that they were wrong. Ya'qub answers not by centering himself, but by promising to seek forgiveness for them from his Lord and by ending the exchange beneath Allah's names: **al-Ghafur, ar-Rahim**.

[PAUSE]

The movement is beautiful: the sons finally stop speaking around the wound, and the father refuses to let the wound become the final word. The confession is real, but it is not the destination. The destination is Allah.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with these ayahs is to ask: where in my life have I wanted a private repentance that never had to face the person I harmed? When someone finally admits wrong to me, do I know how to forward them toward Allah without turning their need into my throne? And when my own heart is ashamed, do I still believe there is a path from **khati'in** to **al-Ghafur ar-Rahim**?

[PAUSE]

Ya'qub teaches us here: **name the sin without euphemism, return through the relationship you wounded, and let the last word belong not to what you were, but to the Lord whose forgiveness and mercy are greater than your fall.**
