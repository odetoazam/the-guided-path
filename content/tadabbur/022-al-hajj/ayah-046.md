## Rebuilding the World

Before we enter this ayah, I need you to feel the scene it drops into.

Surah Al-Hajj is a surah that keeps widening the camera. It begins with an earthquake that undoes every human attachment — a nursing mother forgetting the baby at her breast — and from that opening shock, it keeps moving through tiers of witness. The cosmos as witness. Creation itself as witness. The dead earth that Allah brings back to life as witness. The ancient cities that collapsed as witness. Travelers passing those ruins as witnesses. And underneath every scene, one question is humming:

**Why doesn't any of this land?**

The caravans of Quraysh were not an ignorant people. They were some of the most traveled merchants on the Arabian peninsula. The trade routes to the Levant and to Yemen ran right through territories littered with the ruins of ancient civilizations — cities that had flourished and been flattened, wells that once served thousands now dry and abandoned, palaces with no people in them. The Quran names some of those sites directly. The people of ʿĀd. The people of Thamūd. Madyan. Their ruins were *on the road*. You could put your hand on the broken stone.

And here is the puzzle the Quran keeps returning to: the Quraysh walked past these ruins every single season. They took their camel caravans right through the shadow of the destruction of earlier peoples. They *saw* it all. And it changed nothing.

The ayah immediately before this one names the problem bluntly:

> "How many a city did We destroy while it was committing wrong — so it fell into ruin upon its very pillars! And (how many) an abandoned well, and a lofty palace!"
> — *Al-Hajj 22:45*

Then verse 46 arrives. And Allah asks a question that, if you hear it the right way, has a sting in it you cannot escape.

**"Have they not traveled through the earth — so that they might come to have hearts that reason, and ears that hear?"**

Stay with that for a moment. The grammar of the Arabic is asking about an expected outcome. Travel was *supposed* to do something to them. It was supposed to produce a specific inner organ — a *reasoning heart*. It was supposed to produce a specific kind of *ear*. And because the expected outcome did not appear, the ayah takes a diagnostic turn.

**It is not the eyes that go blind. It is the hearts that go blind — the hearts that are in the chests.**

That last clause — *the hearts that are in the chests* — is the strangest and most beautiful thing in this ayah. And it is where I want to take you. Because everyone already knows that hearts are in chests. So why does Allah say it?

What you are about to see is this: the Quran is going to reach into your body, put your hand on your own chest, and tell you that the organ of reason you thought was in your skull is actually right there, under your ribs. And when that organ fails, it does not fail by running out of information. It fails the way an eye fails.

It goes blind.

## Part 1 — The Linguistic Journey

### Opening the first door: *yaʿqilūna* — a reasoning you do with your body

I need to teach you one Arabic root before we enter the ayah properly, because the ayah will not open without it.

The word translated as "to reason" here is *yaʿqilūna*. The root is ʿ-q-l (ع-ق-ل). In every modern English translation, this gets rendered as some word from the vocabulary of the intellect — *understand*, *comprehend*, *reason*. And those are not wrong. But they are not where the word was born.

Here is where it was born.

In classical Arabic, an *ʿiqāl* was the rope the Bedouin tied around the foreleg of a kneeling camel to keep it from wandering off. You would fold the camel's leg, slip the rope around the knee, and cinch it. The camel then could not stand, and so could not walk away. The rope was called an *ʿiqāl*. The act of tying it was *ʿaqala*. And from that literal, physical, rope-around-the-leg meaning, the Arabs took the word and used it for what the mind does when it thinks clearly.

Because what does thinking well actually *do*? It ties things down. It binds this thought to that one. It binds cause to effect. It binds action to consequence. It binds the wandering self to the post of truth so that the self cannot drift off. A good reasoner is someone who has tied up his camel before leaving it at the well.

This is why the Prophet ﷺ, when a Bedouin asked him "should I tie my camel or put my trust in Allah?" famously said: *iʿqilhā wa-tawakkal* — "tie her, and then rely on Allah." The same root. Binding first, then release into trust. Tying is not the opposite of faith. It is the *precondition* of faith.

Now hold onto that. Notice what this does to our concept of thinking.

In the English-speaking imagination, reason is something abstract. It happens in the skull. It is the pure, disembodied activity of a logical mind. Plato's mind. Descartes's mind. A detached processor producing conclusions.

The Arabic imagination never saw it that way. To the Arabs, reason was a *physical act* of *restraint*. It was what you did with your hands when the camel wanted to wander. And when Allah tells you in this ayah that people should have developed "hearts by which to reason" — *qulūbun yaʿqilūna bihā* — He is telling you something the English loses entirely:

**Reason is a form of holding. The heart is the organ that holds.**

A mind that cannot bind thoughts together wanders like an un-tethered camel. It moves from pasture to pasture. It follows the most recent stimulus. It has no anchor. And the organ Allah is asking us to develop is not the one that *gathers* information. It is the one that *ties* the information down so it cannot drift away.

Have you felt that? Have you felt the difference between knowing something and having it *land*? Between reading about mortality a hundred times and one morning suddenly feeling, in your chest, that you will die? The first is accumulation. The second is *ʿaql*. The rope has gone around the leg. The truth is no longer wandering.

That is the organ the ayah says travel was supposed to build.

And that organ, Allah is telling us, is housed in a very specific place.

### Opening the second door: *taʿmā* — not ignorance, blindness

Notice what word Allah chooses for the failure.

He does not say the hearts become *ignorant*. He does not say they become *confused*. He does not say they become *weak* or *distracted* or *forgetful*. He says a very specific thing:

*taʿmā l-qulūbu* — the hearts **go blind**.

The verb is *ʿamiya* — to be blind. It is the same verb you would use for an eye that cannot see. And Allah uses it twice in the same ayah — *lā taʿmā l-abṣāru wa-lākin taʿmā l-qulūbu*. The first time about the eyes. The second time about the hearts. The exact same verb. The exact same failure. Two different subjects.

This is *enormous* if you sit with it.

Because the entire modern approach to spiritual and moral failure assumes it is an *information* problem. We assume that if people are acting badly, it is because they don't *know* better. So we make more content. More videos. More articles. More books. More explanations. We pour information into people on the assumption that the bottleneck is at the level of data.

The Quran here is saying something else. It is saying the bottleneck is not informational. It is *perceptual*.

Let me show you what I mean with an everyday image.

Imagine a man standing on a cliff edge. He cannot see. He is blind. You are beside him, and you are shouting every piece of information you can think of. "There is a cliff in front of you! The drop is two hundred feet! If you take one more step, you will fall! The ground beneath your foot is loose!" You pile on data. You repeat yourself. You quote safety statistics.

Now ask: does any of this *restore his sight*?

The man's problem is not that he is missing a fact. The man's problem is that the organ that would receive the fact is offline. His eye cannot see the cliff. Your words, no matter how true, cannot reach the faculty that processes cliff-information, because that faculty is broken at a level your words don't touch.

This is exactly what Allah is telling us is happening with the heart.

**The heart is the organ that *sees* spiritual reality. It does not *know* it. It *sees* it.**

When the heart is working, you do not need ten thousand reminders that Allah is watching you — you *feel watched*. When the heart is working, you do not need a logical proof that this world is passing — you *see* it passing. When the heart is working, standing in a graveyard is a complete argument for preparing for death. The graveyard does the teaching. The heart receives it as a direct image.

But when the heart has gone blind — when the *ʿaql*-organ has lost its perceptual function — you can stand in the same graveyard as an awakened saint and feel absolutely nothing. You can walk past the same ruined city the Prophet ﷺ warned about and feel nothing but an aesthetic interest in old stonework. You can hear the same ayah of the Quran recited a thousand times and not have one molecule of your self *move*.

The ayah before this one names ruined palaces and abandoned wells. The ayah after this one talks about people asking you to hasten the punishment. In between, Allah places this diagnosis: the failure is not that the proofs are inadequate. **The failure is that the receiving organ has lost its sight.**

And here's the subtle turn: notice what Allah does *not* say. He does not say *their hearts are unclean*. He does not say *their hearts are hard*, though that image appears elsewhere. He says they are *blind*. Blindness has a very specific grammar. You cannot *argue* a blind eye into seeing. You cannot *shame* a blind eye into seeing. You cannot even *educate* a blind eye into seeing. The only thing that cures blindness is an intervention at the level of the organ itself. The Arabic doctors of the Prophet's time knew this. There were treatments for clouded vision — oils, stones, procedures. But the procedure had to reach the *organ*. You had to touch the eye.

So if the blind organ is the heart, then the only intervention that can reach it is one that touches the heart directly. Information hovers in the intellect. Dhikr reaches the chest. Dua reaches the chest. Weeping reaches the chest. The Quran recited slowly at night, with your hand on your chest, reaches the chest.

Do you see why the Companions would weep during recitation? It was not because they were more emotional than us. It was because they understood that weeping is the eye's admission that it has seen something it could not un-see. It is the organ confirming that it is receiving.

And the one who never weeps over any ayah in a lifetime of recitation — what is the diagnosis? The ayah just told us.

Notice something — the ayah says *lā taʿmā l-abṣāru*. Literally: "the eyes do not go blind." But of course eyes *do* go blind. People lose their sight all the time. So what does "the eyes do not go blind" mean here?

It means this: even when eyes go blind, that is not the blindness that matters. That is not the blindness that condemns. Blind eyes cannot read a warning sign — but blind eyes were never the problem. The Prophet ﷺ had a companion, ʿAbdullah ibn Umm Maktūm, who was blind in exactly this physical sense. The Quran rebukes the Prophet ﷺ in Surah ʿAbasa for turning away from him — because this blind man's heart was fully *seeing*. He could not see the face of the Messenger of Allah, but he could see what the Messenger of Allah was *for*. His eyes were dark. His chest was luminous.

The ayah is saying: the functional eye is not the one on your face. The functional eye is under your ribs. And it is the *only* one whose blindness is a disaster.

### Opening the third door: *allatī fī ṣ-ṣudūri* — Why does Allah say the heart is in the chest?

Now we arrive at the strangest thing in this ayah. And this is where I want you to slow down, because this clause has been puzzled over by the greatest scholars in Islamic history, and they all sensed that Allah was doing something deliberate when He added these four words.

The clause is: *allatī fī ṣ-ṣudūri* — *the ones which are in the chests*.

Stop and hear the oddness of it. Allah has just said "hearts go blind." Everyone knows where hearts are. The listener who hears *qulūb* (hearts) does not need a physical locator. It would be as if someone said to you "my stomach is hurting — the one that is in my belly." You would pause. You would ask: *why did you just tell me where your stomach is? I already knew.*

Arabic rhetoric has a specific term for this kind of clause. It is called *taʾkīd bi-l-dhikr* — emphasis by mention. When the Quran adds a piece of information that the listener already possesses, it is almost never decorative. The information that "didn't need to be said" is the information the listener most needs to *feel*. Allah is not informing you that hearts are in chests. He is *returning* the heart *to* the chest, in your awareness, at a moment when it might have started floating away.

And this is where the classical scholars did some of their most beautiful work.

Let me give you the composite sketch — because on this clause, every major *mufassir* saw something real, and each of them was looking at the same jewel from a different angle.

**Al-Tabari's reading.** Al-Tabari saw the clause as a *taʾkīd* — a confirmation that prevents metaphor from dissolving into vagueness. He understood that the Quran had just spoken of the heart in a figurative register — *the hearts that reason*, *the hearts that go blind* — and that the human listener, hearing metaphors, naturally abstracts. The metaphorical heart becomes a concept. It becomes *consciousness* or *inner life* or *the soul*. It floats away from the body. And so Allah pins it back. He says: *the hearts — the ones in the chests*. The one organ, right there, with a shape and a weight and a heartbeat. Not the abstract heart of poetry. *That* one.

**The reading the grammarians noted from pre-Islamic Arab idiom.** In the speech of the Arabs, there were expressions that relocated organs for rhetorical effect. They would say of a cowardly man that his *heart* had slipped into his *feet*. They would say of a frightened man that his *soul* had risen to his *throat*. These were not literal claims; they were emotional compressions. And classical scholars noted something remarkable: when the Quran says "the hearts that are in the chests," it is *correcting the drift of the idiom*. It is insisting: the heart we are talking about has not slipped into the feet. It has not floated to the throat. It has not dissolved into abstract consciousness. It is *exactly where Allah put it*. The drift of the emotional idiom gets rebuked. The heart stays home.

**The reading closest to Fakhr al-Dīn al-Rāzī's line of thought.** Al-Rāzī noted that the Arabic word *qalb* itself has two families of meaning. One family is the physical organ — the muscle in the chest that beats. The other family is the faculty of perception and moral discernment — what we might call the inner life. He noted that these two meanings are in tension in ordinary speech. A person might refer to his *qalb* and mean either the pump or the perceiver. Al-Rāzī's reading: Allah says *allatī fī ṣ-ṣudūri* to fuse the two meanings into one. He is telling you that the moral-perceiving *qalb* is not a separate, ethereal organ somewhere else. It is *the same organ* as the physical one. The pump and the perceiver are the same piece of flesh. The organ Allah asks you to develop into a reasoning, seeing organ is the one you can touch right now with your hand.

**And the reading that emerges from all of them when you stack them.** The composite image is this: Allah is refusing to let the human split himself. The split between a "mind" that reasons and a "heart" that feels — the split between a cognitive processor in the skull and an emotional appendix in the chest — is a split the Quran does not recognize. *Reason and perception and moral vision are all housed in the same organ, and that organ is physical, located, embodied, and it is in your chest.*

Now put your hand on your chest. I am asking this seriously. If you are listening or reading in a quiet place, bring your palm to your sternum and feel the rhythm underneath.

**That organ — right there — is the one Allah is asking about.**

That is the organ that was supposed to be built by travel through ruined cities. That is the organ that reasons. That is the organ that can go blind. And when it goes blind, no amount of information poured into the skull will reach it, because it is not in the skull. It is two inches beneath your hand, in the dark of your ribs, beating in silence.

And here is the theological weight: the Quran is telling you, in anatomical terms so specific that they cannot be mistaken, that *you cannot escape your body on the way to Allah*. There is no spiritual path that bypasses the chest. There is no clever intellectual shortcut that reaches God through the mind without passing through the organ under your ribs. The door is there. You will either walk through it or you will stand outside it forever with your skull full of correct information and your chest still dark.

This is why, in the Sunnah, so much emphasis is placed on the embodied acts of worship. The chest in *sujūd*. The chest rising and falling in breath during *dhikr*. The chest as the place where the Prophet ﷺ was *opened* by Jibrīl as a child — because the surgery of the soul was performed on the chest, not the head. This is why the hadith about the piece of flesh — "there is a piece of flesh in the body, and if it is sound the whole body is sound, and if it is corrupt the whole body is corrupt — and it is the heart" — why this hadith names *flesh*. *Muḍghah*. A piece of actual meat. Not a metaphor. A muscle you could hold in your hand.

The Quran's anatomy is not embarrassed to be anatomy.

## Part 2 — The Thematic Depths

### Theme One: Blindness is not ignorance

We live in a civilization that runs on an unexamined assumption: if a person believes or does the wrong thing, it is because they have not been given enough information. So we intervene at the level of content. We write more. We publish more. We produce more lectures, more videos, more books, more threads, more reels. We assume that the bottleneck is upstream, at the level of data.

And then we watch — over and over — as the data arrives and changes nothing.

You have seen this yourself, I am certain of it. You have seen a family member about whom you have *known* for ten years that a certain habit is destroying their health, their marriage, or their soul. You have given them information. You have sat them down. You have cited evidence. You have provided links. You have maybe even printed things and put them on their fridge. And the data keeps bouncing off. They nod. They agree. And then they keep walking toward the same cliff.

The cliff is not hidden. They know it is there. They have been told about it. If you quiz them, they can tell you in detail what is wrong with the thing they are doing. And yet something about the knowledge does not become *sight*.

We keep diagnosing this as a *willpower* problem. As if the missing piece is *discipline*. But the ayah is telling us something different and more precise. It is saying the receiving organ is blind. And a blind organ does not fail by choice. It fails because the function of *seeing* — of having the truth actually *land* as an image in the inner eye — has stopped happening.

And now think about what this means for us when we apply it to ourselves.

When you find yourself reading yet another reminder about the grave — and feeling nothing — the problem is not that you haven't read enough about the grave. You have read about the grave hundreds of times. The problem is that the organ which was supposed to *see* the grave when you read about it is currently dark. When you hear yet another khutbah about the shortness of life and walk out of the masjid feeling exactly the same as when you walked in — the problem is not the khutbah. It is not even you being *distracted* in the khutbah. The problem is that the part of you that should have been *struck* by what you heard has temporarily lost its function as a striking surface.

And this changes what you do about it.

Because if blindness is the diagnosis, then more information is not the treatment. You do not hand a blind man a brighter light. You treat his eye.

What treats the eye of the heart? The Sunnah tells us. *Dhikr* treats it. The repetition of Allah's name, slowly, with attention to the breath, is an intervention at the level of the organ itself. It is the ophthalmologist's oil dropped into a clouded eye. *Tahajjud* treats it — not because night prayer gives you more information, but because the chest is most reachable in the silence of night. Sitting with the Quran and *stopping* at a single ayah, refusing to move forward until something has *landed*, treats it. Weeping treats it, when weeping comes — because weeping is the heart's confession that it has seen something. Even one *real* weeping over an ayah you have read a thousand times is worth more than a year of reading without seeing.

And reflection on death treats it. The Prophet ﷺ said *akthirū dhikra hādhimi l-ladhdhāt* — "remember frequently the destroyer of pleasures." He did not say *learn* about death. He did not say *acquire information about* death. He said *remember it* — bring it into the chest. Let it live under your ribs for a few minutes every day. Because death has a way, when it is truly brought into the chest, of reopening an eye that had gone blind.

What if you tried this, genuinely, just once? What if tomorrow morning you sat for five minutes, your hand on your chest, and said only this: *"I will die. I do not know when. It might be this week."* Not as a lecture to yourself. Not as a philosophical reflection. As something you are letting *sit* in the organ under your hand. And you repeated this until something, somewhere in the chest, actually registered that it was true.

That is the treatment the ayah is pointing toward. Not more content. Not more reading. An intervention at the *organ* — slow, local, embodied — that restores the sight of the heart.

### Theme Two: You cannot escape your body on the way to God

There is a temptation, especially for people of a certain intellectual cast, to treat Islam as a *conceptual* religion. To treat it as a set of propositions to be understood, a theology to be mastered, an ethics to be argued. This is a tempting frame because it protects the self. It allows you to engage with Islam from the safety of the skull, where nothing actually has to change.

And the frame is not entirely wrong. There *is* a conceptual dimension. There *is* theology. There *is* reasoning. The tradition honors it. It produced al-Ghazālī and Ibn Taymiyyah and al-Rāzī and a thousand others. You cannot walk far into Islam without your mind getting engaged.

But this ayah is drawing a line that prevents the mind from ever becoming the whole game. It is saying: no matter how much your intellect learns, the moment of *landing* — the moment when the knowledge becomes sight — happens *in the chest*, not in the skull. And if that moment does not happen, the intellect's whole inventory might as well be sand.

This is humbling in a specific way. It means you cannot *think* your way to a soft heart. You cannot *argue* yourself into *khushūʿ*. You cannot *reason* your way into that state where the Quran suddenly feels like it was written directly to you. The intellect can *prepare* the way. It can clear obstacles. It can defend the chest from doctrinal confusion. But it cannot enter the chest and do the work of the chest on its behalf.

This is why the masters of the path always insisted that the student must, at some point, *put the book down*. Not abandon the book. Not reject learning. But *put it down*, sit quietly, and let something under the ribs wake up. The book was the map. The map is not the territory. The territory is the organ Allah planted in your chest when you were still inside your mother.

There is a beautiful asymmetry in the ayah that I have been keeping in my pocket until now. Allah says *hearts by which to reason, or ears by which to hear*. Two organs for the processing of truth: the heart, and the ear. Notice what is missing: the *eye*. The eye does not appear in the positive list. The eye only appears in the *negative* list — "it is not the eyes that go blind." Why? Because in the Quranic diagnostic, *visual* information is almost never the bottleneck. The bottleneck is *auditory* and *cardiac* — the ear that receives the recitation and the heart that processes it. You do not need to *see* the Quran. You need to *hear* it and to *feel* it. The ear is the door to the heart. The heart is the room where the truth lives.

And this matches the original revelation pattern. The Prophet ﷺ did not *read* the Quran off a page when Jibrīl delivered it. He *heard* it. He received it aurally. The Companions *heard* it from him. The auditory channel ran directly into their chests. The Quran was always, primarily, a *heard* thing. And what it produces in you — when it produces anything — it produces through the ear into the chest.

Which means that one of the most direct treatments for a hardening heart is to *listen* to the Quran, with your hand on your chest, and to allow the sound to arrive somewhere it has not arrived in a long time. Not to study it. Not to parse it. To *hear* it as the first listeners heard it. Sound entering the ear, landing on the chest, waking something up.

There is an ʿIshā you could spend this way. There is a Fajr. There is a train ride, with earphones in and the Quran playing, where you stop trying to learn anything and simply *let it arrive*. The ayah is telling you that the place it needs to arrive is precisely the place under your hand, in the dark of your ribs.

Do not leave this ayah and go write a better essay about hearts. Leave this ayah and *sit*, for five minutes, with your hand on yours.

That is the practice this ayah is prescribing.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture

Now let me show you how the pieces of this ayah were built.

Look at what the ayah does with its structure. It opens with a question about *travel* — a question that invokes the eyes (you travel by seeing the road), the feet (you travel by walking), the map, the caravan, the horizon. *Have they not traveled through the earth?* That is an *outer sensing* question. Eyes on ruins. Feet on dust. The whole ordinary human apparatus for gathering information about the world.

But then the ayah pivots to what the travel was supposed to *produce*. And it does not name outer organs. It names *hearts* and *ears* — *qulūbun yaʿqilūna bihā aw ādhānun yasmaʿūna bihā*. It skips the eyes entirely. The outer sensing was only a delivery mechanism. The real work was being done by inner organs that the outer ones were supposed to feed.

And then, in the diagnostic turn, the ayah does something astonishing. It says: *lā taʿmā l-abṣāru wa-lākin taʿmā l-qulūbu*. It is not the eyes that go blind. It is the *hearts* that go blind. The outer organ — the one that was supposed to do the delivering, the one that saw the ruined palaces and the abandoned wells — is not where the blindness lives. The blindness lives *downstream*, in the organ that was supposed to *receive* what the eyes delivered.

Then the final clause: *allatī fī ṣ-ṣudūri* — the ones which are in the chests. The camera, which has been moving inward through the ayah from "the earth" to "hearts" to "blind hearts," now lands on a precise anatomical location: *the chest*. The ayah has traveled from the widest possible scene — the whole earth with its ruins — to the smallest, most intimate location a human being possesses: the space two inches beneath your own palm.

That is the architecture. The ayah opens on the horizon and closes on your sternum.

And the reason this architecture is the summit is that it enacts, in its own shape, the very thing it is about. The ayah takes the listener on a journey — from the widest possible data (the ruins of ancient cities) to the smallest possible receiving organ (the heart in the chest) — and it asks: *did the journey produce anything in you?* If the answer is no, if you read the ayah and felt nothing, then the ayah itself has just diagnosed you. The exact thing that was supposed to happen when the Quraysh traveled past the ruins — the widening eye turning into a landing in the chest — was supposed to happen *in you*, listening to this ayah.

The ayah is a test it administers to itself.

And the way out is not to try harder to understand it. The way out is to place your hand on the organ the ayah just named and stay there, quietly, until something arrives.

### Questions to Carry

- When was the last time an ayah of the Quran — any ayah — actually *landed* in your chest? Not just passed through your eyes or your ears, but struck something under your ribs? If you cannot remember, what does that tell you about where the blindness is?

- Think of a truth you have known, intellectually, for years — a truth about your own life, your own flaws, your own relationship with Allah. You can articulate it clearly. And yet nothing about how you live has shifted. Where is the gap? Is it really in the information? Or is it in the organ that was supposed to receive the information?

- When you sit with the Quran tomorrow, what would it mean to *stop* at the first ayah that does something to you — and refuse to move forward until something has landed? Could you do that just once this week?

- If reason is housed in the chest and not the skull, what does that say about the hours you spend in the skull that are supposed to count as spiritual work? What portion of your worship actually reaches the chest? What portion never leaves the head?

### One Sentence

**The organ Allah asks you to develop is not in your skull — it is under your hand, beating in the dark, and its worst failure is not ignorance but blindness.**

---

### The Closing Invitation

Do not leave this ayah and go write something clever about it. Do not leave this ayah and feel intellectually satisfied. The ayah has warned you, in its very architecture, that intellectual satisfaction in the skull is exactly the place where the heart goes to die.

Leave this ayah and *sit*. Leave it and put your hand on your chest. Leave it and spend five minutes — not ten, not a heroic hour, just five minutes — giving the organ Allah named the attention it has not received in a long time. Say one of Allah's names slowly, with your hand there. Read one ayah slowly, with your hand there. Ask for one thing, slowly, with your hand there.

The ayah has just told you where the door is. The door is under your palm right now.

### Duʿāʾ

*Yā man jaʿala l-qulūba fī ṣ-ṣudūri —* O You who placed the hearts in the chests, do not let mine go blind. When my eyes read Your words and my skull fills with correct conclusions but nothing beneath my ribs wakes up — reach into the organ You made and restore its sight. Tie my wandering thoughts to the post of Your truth. Let the ayahs I have heard a thousand times land, even once, where they were always meant to land. And when I place my hand on my chest and feel nothing, let that itself be enough for You to forgive me, and to return me.

*Āmīn.*
