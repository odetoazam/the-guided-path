# Who Gives Life to Bones

## The Ayah

## Rebuilding the World

A man walked up to the Prophet ﷺ with a bone in his hand.

The narrations identify him as Ubayy ibn Khalaf — or, in some reports, al-'As ibn Wa'il. He crumbled the bone between his fingers, let the dust scatter in the wind, and said: *Do you really claim that Allah will bring this back to life?*

It was not an honest question. It was a performance. The bone dust falling through his fingers was his proof. His argument was the most intuitive one the human mind can produce: things that decompose don't come back. Rot is a one-way street. Entropy wins. Once something crumbles into anonymity, it is gone.

This objection felt, to him, unanswerable.

And the Quran's response is not what you might expect. It does not begin with theology. It does not appeal to faith. It does not ask the man to simply believe. Instead, it does something extraordinary — it enters the man's own logic, uses his own experience as evidence against him, escalates through four increasingly devastating proofs, and arrives at a statement so total that the objection doesn't just fail — it becomes absurd.

Here is the first thing to notice: the Quran takes this man's argument seriously enough to dismantle it. It does not dismiss. It does not ridicule. It demolishes — with his own evidence.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. He Struck an Argument and Forgot His Own Creation

The opening is already devastating before the argument even begins.

**وَضَرَبَ لَنَا مَثَلًا وَنَسِىَ خَلْقَهُۥ**

*"And he struck for Us a comparison — and forgot his own creation."*

Two verbs. Two actions. And the second one undoes the first.

*Daraba lana mathalan* — the root ض ر ب means to strike, to hit. When you "strike a comparison" in Arabic, you're not gently offering an analogy. You're throwing it down. You're putting it on the table with force. This man wasn't musing. He was confronting. He struck his argument *for Us* — **لَنَا** — for Allah. He aimed it at the Creator of the heavens and the earth. The audacity is in the preposition.

And then: *wa nasiya khalqahu* — "and he forgot his own creation." The root ن س ي means to forget, and in the Quran, forgetting is never innocent. Forgetting in Quranic Arabic is a moral act. It's not that the information left his brain. It's that he chose not to look at the evidence that was closest to him.

What did he forget? *Khalqahu* — his own creation. The هُ (his) is ambiguous — it could mean "the creation of him" (how he was made) or "his act of creation" (what he produced). The dominant reading is the first: he forgot **how he was made.** He stands there holding crumbled bone dust, making an argument against resurrection, and doesn't notice that his own body — standing, breathing, arguing — is itself the answer to his question. He was once nothing. Less than bone dust. He was a drop of fluid that became a clot that became flesh that became a man who now stands before the Prophet and asks "who can bring bones to life?" — while being, himself, the walking proof.

And the word **رَمِيمٌ** — *ramim* — crumbled, decomposed. This is a *fa'il* pattern that functions as an adjective: not "crumbling" (active, in process) but "crumbled" (complete, finished). The bone isn't dying. It has already died. It is done. The *fa'il* pattern gives it finality. The man chose the most extreme case — not weakness, not illness, but complete decomposition — because he wanted his argument to be unanswerable.

The Quran lets him have his strongest case. And then answers it.

### 2. Four Proofs — Escalating

What follows is not one answer but four, each bigger than the last. And the structure of the escalation is itself the argument.

**Proof 1: The First Time (36:79)**

**قُلْ يُحْيِيهَا ٱلَّذِىٓ أَنشَأَهَآ أَوَّلَ مَرَّةٍ**

*"Say: He will give them life who produced them the first time."*

The verb is *ansha'a* — Form IV of ن ش أ, meaning to originate, to produce for the first time, to bring into being from nothing. Form IV is causative — He **caused** them to come into existence. And the phrase *awwala marratin* — "the first time" — is the crux of the argument.

The logic is devastatingly simple: bringing something back is easier than bringing it into being from nothing. If Allah produced you *the first time* — when there was no template, no prior model, no existing material — then doing it again, when the blueprint already exists, is not harder. It is easier. The man's argument assumed that resurrection was unprecedented. The answer points out that his own existence was more unprecedented.

This is what the Quran calls *istidlal al-ma'qul bil-mahsus* — reasoning from the sensible to the intelligible. The man can see himself. He knows he exists. He knows he didn't always exist. The conclusion is already in his experience. He just forgot to look.

**Proof 2: Fire from the Green Tree (36:80)**

**ٱلَّذِى جَعَلَ لَكُم مِّنَ ٱلشَّجَرِ ٱلْأَخْضَرِ نَارًا**

*"He who made for you from the green tree, fire."*

Now something shifts. The first proof was logical. This one is empirical — and paradoxical.

The word *al-akhdar* — green. The word *naran* — fire. Green wood is wet. It is alive. It should not burn. Fire is the opposite of moisture, the enemy of life. And yet: from the living green tree comes fire. The Arabs knew this. The *markh* and *'afar* trees of the Arabian Peninsula produce fire when their branches are rubbed together, even when green.

The argument is no longer about logic. It is about **what you have already seen Allah do with opposites.** He brings fire from water. He brings heat from life. If He already crosses the boundary between wet and dry, living and burning — why would the boundary between dead and alive be harder?

And notice the word order. In Arabic, the normal order would place the object (fire) closer to the verb. Instead, the Quran puts the source first: *min ash-shajari al-akhdari* — from the green tree — and delays the object — *naran* — fire. The green tree comes first. You see the living thing. And then: fire. The delay creates surprise. You were looking at life, and it produced its opposite.

**Proof 3: The Heavens and the Earth (36:81)**

**أَوَلَيْسَ ٱلَّذِى خَلَقَ ٱلسَّمَـٰوَٰتِ وَٱلْأَرْضَ بِقَـٰدِرٍ عَلَىٰٓ أَن يَخْلُقَ مِثْلَهُم**

*"Is not He who created the heavens and the earth capable of creating the like of them?"*

The question form — *awalaysa* — expects "yes, obviously." This is the Quran's version of the argument from scale. You're worried about one set of bones? Look up. Look at the scale of what He already maintains. The heavens and the earth — the entire cosmos — are His creation. And you doubt He can reassemble you?

But the real payload is in the answer: **بَلَىٰ وَهُوَ ٱلْخَلَّـٰقُ ٱلْعَلِيمُ** — "Yes indeed — and He is **al-Khallaq**, the All-Knowing."

*Al-Khallaq.* Not *al-Khaliq* (the Creator — active participle, *fa'il* pattern). **Al-Khallaq** — *fa''al* pattern. The intensified form. The one who creates ceaselessly, abundantly, overwhelmingly. *Khaliq* creates. *Khallaq* is creation itself in superlative mode — a Creator whose creating never pauses, never exhausts, never reaches a limit. The morphological form carries what no translation can: this is not a God who creates occasionally. This is a God whose nature **is** creation, intensively and perpetually.

And paired with *al-'Alim* — the All-Knowing. He doesn't just create abundantly. He creates knowingly. Every creation is deliberate, precise, informed. The pairing — *Khallaq* and *'Alim* — answers the man's objection from both directions: the power to create (Khallaq) and the knowledge of what was created (Alim). Can He find your scattered bones? He already knows exactly where every particle is.

**Proof 4: Kun Fayakun (36:82)**

**إِنَّمَآ أَمْرُهُۥٓ إِذَآ أَرَادَ شَيْـًٔا أَن يَقُولَ لَهُۥ كُن فَيَكُونُ**

*"His command, when He wills a thing, is only that He says to it: Be — and it is."*

The escalation is complete. The first proof used the man's own creation. The second used the natural world. The third used the cosmos. And now the fourth leaves all of creation behind and goes to the mechanism itself — to the point before physics, before matter, before process.

*Innama* — "only." This restriction particle strips away everything you imagined was necessary. You thought creation required time? Materials? Effort? Process? *Innama* — only. His command is **only** that He says *kun*.

*Kun fayakun* — "Be, and it is." Two words. The first is an imperative — a command, second person. The second is an indicative — a fact, third person. The command and its fulfillment exist in the same breath. There is no gap between intention and reality. There is no waiting. There is no process. The *fa* (then/and) that connects *kun* to *yakun* is the thinnest possible conjunction — it creates the appearance of sequence where there is, in truth, simultaneity.

And notice: *an yaqula lahu* — "that He says **to it**." The *lahu* — "to it" — means the thing is already being addressed before it exists. Allah speaks to the thing that does not yet exist, and the speaking creates it. The object of the sentence exists only because the sentence was spoken.

[PAUSE]

Let the four proofs sit together as an escalation:

1. Your own existence — you were once nothing, look at yourself
2. The green tree producing fire — you've already witnessed impossible reversals
3. The heavens and earth — the scale of what He already sustains dwarfs your objection
4. *Kun fayakun* — the mechanism bypasses everything you imagined was necessary

Each proof makes the previous one seem small. And each makes the man's crumbled bone seem smaller. By the time you reach *kun fayakun*, the bone dust is not even a footnote. The gap between crumbled bone and living body — which seemed infinite to the man — has been revealed as nothing compared to the gap between nonexistence and existence. And Allah crossed **that** gap effortlessly.

### 3. The Closing Verse — Malakut and Return

**فَسُبْحَـٰنَ ٱلَّذِى بِيَدِهِۦ مَلَكُوتُ كُلِّ شَىْءٍ وَإِلَيْهِ تُرْجَعُونَ**

*"So exalted is He in whose hand is the dominion of all things — and to Him you will be returned."*

The surah ends where it must end: at the hand that holds everything.

The word is **مَلَكُوت** — *malakut*. Not *mulk* (sovereignty, the kind a king has — political, territorial). *Malakut* — the *fa'alut* pattern, which Arabic uses for intensification and totality. *Malakut* is sovereignty so total that it encompasses not just rulership but the very **reality** of things. The unseen dimension. The kingdom behind the kingdom. The hand that holds not just the thrones of power but the atoms beneath them.

*Biyadihi* — "in His hand." Singular. One hand. And in it: *kulli shay'in* — every single thing. The singular hand holding everything is an image of effortless, total control. Not two hands straining to hold the world together. One hand. Effortless. Complete.

And the last word of the surah: **تُرْجَعُونَ** — *turja'un* — "you will be returned." Passive voice. You won't return on your own. You won't choose to come back. **You will be returned.** The passive removes your agency from the equation. The man who crumbled the bone thought he was making an argument about what cannot happen. The surah's final word tells him: it's not up to you. *You will be returned* — whether the bones agree or not.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Amnesia of the Arguer

*Wa nasiya khalqahu.* He forgot his own creation.

This is not a verse about a seventh-century pagan. This is a portrait of every human being who has ever looked at a difficulty and said "that's impossible" — while standing inside an impossibility.

You are reading these words with two eyes that process forty million data points per second, through a brain made of a hundred billion neurons, inside a body that was once a single cell, which was once nothing at all. Your existence is the most improbable event you've ever witnessed. And yet you take it for granted every single morning.

This is the Quran's diagnosis of the arguing mind: **it uses its own existence as a platform to deny what made it possible.** The man stands — using bones, muscles, lungs, a brain — and asks "who can bring bones to life?" His body is his answer. He just forgot to look down.

Modern culture has a particular version of this amnesia. We call it **naturalistic familiarity** — the tendency to treat what already exists as self-evident and what doesn't yet exist as impossible. Water flowing from a tap doesn't amaze you. But water flowing from a rock would. A heartbeat doesn't astonish you. But a stopped heart restarting would. The difference is not in the miracle. It's in how many times you've seen it.

The Quran's response is not "believe harder." It is "look closer." The first proof doesn't ask for faith. It asks for **attention.** *He who produced you the first time.* Don't look up. Look at yourself. The argument against resurrection requires the arguer to forget the evidence he is standing inside.

That is why al-Tabari keeps the rebuke so close to the denier himself: the problem is not lack of evidence but neglect of the nearest evidence. He paraphrases the ayah's force as a reminder that the one objecting was first brought out from nonexistence, then dares to treat re-creation as impossible. Ibn Kathir leans in the same direction when he gathers the reports around the crumbled bone and shows that the Quran answers the skeptic by returning him to his own beginning. The point is not merely that Allah can do it. The point is that the objector already knows, from his own body, that Allah has done something greater.

The Quran repeats this method elsewhere. In 17:49-51 the deniers ask almost the same question in a different voice: after becoming bones and fragments, will we really be raised as a new creation? There too the answer is not sentimental reassurance. It widens the frame until even becoming stone or iron would not escape the One who originated them the first time. And in 22:5 the Quran walks human beings back through their own stages — dust, drop, clinging form, shaped and unshaped flesh — so that resurrection is read not as a bizarre exception to existence but as the return of a pattern Allah has already inscribed into us.

Where in your life are you building elaborate arguments against something — while the counter-evidence is in your own body, your own history, your own experience?

You've survived things you thought would end you. You've been brought back from lows you thought were final. You've watched dead situations come to life — relationships restored, doors reopened, hope returning from places where you were certain it had decomposed beyond recognition. *Wa nasiya khalqahu.* You keep forgetting that the One who brought you through the first time is still the same One.

### Theme 2: The God Who Answers Arguments

There is something theologically remarkable about this passage that is easy to miss. Allah is **arguing.** Not commanding. Not threatening. Not asking for submission. **Arguing.**

A man with a bone dust in his hand posed a question. And the Creator of the heavens and the earth answered it. With evidence. With logic. With escalating proofs that meet the man on his own ground and then exceed it.

This matters because it reveals something about the kind of God who speaks in the Quran. This is not a deity who says "because I said so." This is a God who engages the human intellect — who considers a skeptic's objection worth answering, who believes that the truth is not threatened by the question but revealed by the answer.

The four proofs are not random. They follow the logic of the objection:

*You say bones can't come back? → But you came from nothing (your experience).*
*You say death is irreversible? → But you already see fire from green wood (your world).*
*You say this is too hard? → But the heavens and earth are harder (His scale).*
*You say there must be a process? → Kun fayakun (His mechanism bypasses process entirely).*

Each answer absorbs the objection and returns something larger. And the final answer — *kun fayakun* — doesn't just answer the question. It dissolves the category the question belongs to. The man was arguing about difficulty. *Kun fayakun* says: difficulty is a category that doesn't apply. There is no spectrum from easy to hard in Allah's creative act. There is only will. And will creates.

Al-Qurtubi, when reflecting on these verses and their parallels, repeatedly returns to this collapse of the category of difficulty. For human beings, restoring the scattered is harder than preserving the whole. For Allah, the One whose act does not depend on tools, fatigue, or gradual assembly, "first" and "again" do not divide ability the way they divide ours. Ibn Kathir makes the same movement when he pairs this passage with other resurrection proofs: the first origination, the creation of the heavens and the earth, and the strange emergence of fire from green wood all function together to show that the objection is not intellectual strength but misplaced scale.

That is why the Quran so often binds resurrection to first creation. In 30:27, "He is the One who begins creation, then repeats it, and that is easier for you to grasp," the phrase does not mean resurrection is literally hard for Allah and then easier. It means the human mind should at least admit, on its own terms, that repetition cannot be more unimaginable than origination. And in 75:3-4, when Allah says He is able not only to gather bones but to proportion even fingertips, the answer becomes intimate again. Resurrection is not mass production. It is precise return. Not just bodies raised, but identities restored in their most delicate signatures.

[PAUSE]

This is what the Quran models for every person who encounters doubt. Not suppression. Not avoidance. Not "don't ask questions." But: **go deeper into the question until the answer surfaces from inside it.** The answer to "who gives life to bones?" was always inside the man's own skeleton. He just needed someone to tell him to look.

The practical question is this: **what are you treating as impossible that is actually just unfamiliar?** What would change if you stopped arguing from what you haven't seen and started reasoning from what you have?

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Passage

The passage is built like an argument that keeps widening its circle until the objection disappears inside a much larger truth. It begins with one man, one mocking example, one handful of bone dust. Then it answers him with a four-step ascent: first his own first creation, then the paradox of fire emerging from green wood, then the vastness of the heavens and the earth, then the level beneath all created process in *kun fayakun*. The movement is from the intimate to the cosmic to the metaphysical, and each step makes the original objection feel smaller and smaller.

Then the closing verse lands not on a technical answer but on sovereignty: *fasubhana alladhi biyadihi malakutu kulli shay'in*. That matters. The argument is not merely "resurrection is possible." It is "resurrection belongs to the One in whose hand is the dominion of every single thing." The architecture teaches the heart to move from question, to evidence, to scale, to surrender. The bones are answered, but the soul is being trained.

### Questions to Carry

- What argument are you repeating about your future that quietly depends on forgetting your own beginning? Sit with the fact that Allah did not first meet you at your strongest state, but at your nonexistence. Sometimes hopelessness survives only because we keep staring at the bone dust and refusing to look at the first creation.

- In what area of life have you mistaken familiarity for explanation? Your breathing, your memory, your healing, your continued standing after grief all feel ordinary only because they are repeated. This passage asks whether your "ordinary" may already be the evidence you keep demanding in more dramatic form.

- When doubt rises, do you argue with yourself the way the Quran argues with this denier: by moving from the nearest sign to the wider sign to the highest truth? Or do you let the first wave of fear define reality? The passage trains us not to shame the question, but to outgrow its frame.

- What have you declared "too broken" for Allah to restore? A heart after sin, a relationship after damage, a life after wasted years, a community after fracture? These verses do not promise that every worldly loss returns in the shape you expect, but they do break the idol of finality that despair keeps worshipping.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The man crumbled a bone to disprove the Creator — and didn't notice he was using the Creator's hands to do it.**

### The Closing Invitation

The bone dust fell through his fingers and he thought he'd proven something. The Quran's response was not anger but patience. It took him by the hand and walked him from his own body to the green tree to the heavens to the word that precedes all of it.

The invitation is this: the next time something in your life feels permanently broken — a relationship, a hope, a chapter you thought was closed — remember the four proofs. You came from nothing once. Fire comes from green wood. The heavens are harder than you. And the One who will restore what was broken doesn't need a process to do it.

*Kun fayakun.*

### Du'a

Allahumma, You answered the denier with proofs from his own body, his own world, and Your own sovereignty.
Do not let us forget our first creation when fear tells us that return, repair, or resurrection is impossible.
When we stare only at what has crumbled, teach us to see the green tree, the fire within it, and the heavens above us.
Make us people who reason toward You instead of away from You, and who let doubt become a doorway to deeper certainty.
Ya Khallaq, Ya 'Alim, gather our scattered hearts before You gather our bones, and return us to You in faith, humility, and awe.
