# The One Who Wants the Immediate Gets Only What Allah Wills, but the Hereafter-Seeker Finds His Striving Thanked

## Surah Al-Isra (17:18-21)

[ayah:17:18] مَّن كَانَ يُرِيدُ ٱلْعَاجِلَةَ عَجَّلْنَا لَهُۥ فِيهَا مَا نَشَآءُ لِمَن نُّرِيدُ ثُمَّ جَعَلْنَا لَهُۥ جَهَنَّمَ يَصْلَىٰهَا مَذْمُومًا مَّدْحُورًا

[ayah:17:19] وَمَنْ أَرَادَ ٱلْـَٔاخِرَةَ وَسَعَىٰ لَهَا سَعْيَهَا وَهُوَ مُؤْمِنٌ فَأُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ كَانَ سَعْيُهُم مَّشْكُورًا

[ayah:17:20] كُلًّا نُّمِدُّ هَـٰٓؤُلَآءِ وَهَـٰٓؤُلَآءِ مِنْ عَطَآءِ رَبِّكَ ۚ وَمَا كَانَ عَطَآءُ رَبِّكَ مَحْظُورًا

## Introduction

One of the most dangerous confusions in human life is to mistake receipt for approval.

If I got it, I assume it was good.

If I rose, I assume I was right.

If doors opened, I assume Allah must be pleased.

[PAUSE]

These verses shatter that confusion. They tell us that the one seeking `al-'ajilah`, the immediate world, may indeed receive from it. But what he receives is only what Allah wills and only for whom He wills. Provision is real, access is real, visible success is real, and none of that by itself proves final favor. By contrast, the one who wants the Hereafter, strives for it in the way it deserves, and remains a believer finds something astonishing: his striving is `mashkura`, thanked, appreciated, received by Allah.

Then the passage explains why our surface judgments so often fail: Allah extends to both groups from His gift. Dunya distribution is not the final scoreboard. The scoreboard that matters is in the Akhirah, where the degrees are greater and the distinction greater still.

The hook of the passage is this: **Al-Isra teaches that what ultimately defines a life is not what it managed to obtain in the immediate world, but what it truly wanted, how it strove for it, and whether that striving was carried by faith toward the Hereafter.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Man kana yuridu al-'ajilah**: The Problem Is Not Merely Possessing the Dunya, but Wanting the Immediate as the Final Horizon

The verse begins:

**man kana yuridu al-'ajilah**

Whoever desires the immediate.

Not merely the world as one sphere of human responsibility, but `al-'ajilah` - the rushed, the near, the quick-arriving, the immediate gratification horizon.

[PAUSE]

This is subtle and important. The Quran is not condemning lawful work, provision, family, or human flourishing in this life. It is exposing an orientation in which the heart wants what comes fastest and nearest as though that were the real measure of life.

Then comes the divine qualification:

**'ajjalna lahu fiha ma nasha'u liman nurid**

We hasten for him in it whatever We will, for whom We intend.

This destroys entitlement. Even the dunya-seeker does not obtain the world on his own terms. He gets only what Allah wills, in the amount Allah wills, for the person Allah wills. The immediate world is not self-owned territory. It is still under divine administration.

Then the terrifying end:

**thumma ja'alna lahu Jahannama yaslaha madhmuman madhhura**

Then We appoint for him Jahannam, where he burns, blamed and banished.

`Madhmuman` means blameworthy, deserving censure. `Madhhura` carries being driven away, expelled, pushed off from mercy. The final result of living only for the immediate is not simply loss of a better option. It is disgrace.

### 2. **Wa sa'a laha sa'yaha wa huwa mu'min**: The Hereafter Is Not Reached by Wishfulness, but by Fitting Striving Carried by Faith

Then the contrasting path:

**wa man arada al-akhirah**

Whoever desires the Hereafter.

Desire is still present. Islam does not kill wanting. It reorders it.

[PAUSE]

But wanting alone is not enough:

**wa sa'a laha sa'yaha**

and strives for it with the striving due to it.

This is a powerful formulation. The Hereafter has a `sa'y` that belongs to it. It must be sought in the mode appropriate to it: obedience, repentance, sincerity, patience, worship, moral struggle, and the long work of inward reordering. You cannot want Akhirah with a dunya-shaped effort.

Then:

**wa huwa mu'min**

while he is a believer.

Faith is not an accessory added after effort. It is the living ground that makes the striving truly oriented.

Then comes one of the most tender words in this part of the surah:

**fa-ula'ika kana sa'yuhum mashkura**

their striving is appreciated, thanked.

Imagine that. Allah speaks of the believer's labor as `mashkur`. The One who needs nothing receives the servant's stumbling, sincere, imperfect effort with gratitude-language.

### 3. **Kullan numiddu ... wa la-al-akhiratu akbaru darajatin**: Dunya Distribution Is Wide, but It Is Not the Final Measure

Then the passage clarifies the worldly scene:

**kullan numiddu ha'ula'i wa ha'ula'i min 'ata'i rabbik**

Each group We extend - these and those - from the gift of your Lord.

[PAUSE]

This is the ayah that stops us from making simplistic conclusions. The dunya-seeker may be given. The Akhirah-seeker may also be given. Provision crosses categories. Allah's `ata'` is broad.

Then:

**wa ma kana 'ata'u rabbika mahzura**

The gift of your Lord is not restricted.

So worldly spread, resources, openings, and capacities are not themselves proof of closeness.

Then comes the interpretive key:

**unzur kayfa faddalna ba'dahum 'ala ba'd**

Look how We favored some over others.

Yes, there are visible gradations in the world. But then the ayah immediately corrects our scale:

**wa la-al-akhiratu akbaru darajatin wa akbaru tafdila**

The Hereafter is greater in degrees and greater in distinction.

The world's differentials are real. They are just not ultimate.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Deep Question Is Not "What Did You Get?" but "What Were You Living For?"

These verses bring moral clarity to a world obsessed with outcomes.

We are trained to ask: what did you achieve? what did you accumulate? what doors opened? what status did you reach?

[PAUSE]

The Quran asks a prior question: what did you actually want?

`Man kana yuridu al-'ajilah.`  
`Wa man arada al-akhirah.`

The axis is desire before result.

That is sobering, because two people can outwardly perform similar actions while inwardly moving toward opposite worlds. One can work hard because he wants to be seen, secured, praised, envied, or soothed by the immediate. Another can work hard because he wants Allah, wants the Akhirah, wants his striving to survive death.

The dunya often hides that distinction because both people may receive something. But the ayah tells us that distribution is not disclosure. Final disclosure belongs to the Hereafter.

### Theme 2: Allah Can Give the World Broadly Without Giving It the Meaning We Project Onto It

One of the great sources of fitnah is that Allah gives widely in the dunya.

These and those.

The righteous and the rebellious.

The grateful and the heedless.

[PAUSE]

That means dunya is not a clean mirror of rank.

It is a field of test.

This is why people become spiritually confused by wealth, fame, influence, talent, visibility, and access. They assume that if Allah gave it, it must mean He endorsed the whole inner orientation behind it. But the ayah refuses that reading.

He gives. Broadly.

Yet `wa la-al-akhiratu akbaru darajatin wa akbaru tafdila`.

So the believer learns to loosen his obsession with comparative worldly placement. Yes, Allah has favored some over others in visible things. The ayah itself tells you to look at that. But it tells you to look at it in order to be corrected, not seduced. The greater hierarchy has not yet appeared.

This is deeply freeing. It means you do not need the dunya to prove your worth. You need to know what you are seeking and whether your striving belongs to the Hereafter.

## Closing Synthesis

These verses rescue us from one of the oldest illusions: that immediate attainment tells us the truth about a life.

It does not.

[PAUSE]

Some are given because Allah willed to give them in this world.

Some are denied because Allah willed to deny them in this world.

Some who are given are moving toward loss.

Some who struggle quietly are moving toward a `sa'y mashkur`, a striving thanked by Allah.

So the passage teaches us to re-read our own lives.

Not first by asking what came quickly.

Not first by asking what others received.

Not first by asking whether our station looks impressive in the eyes of people.

But by asking:

What do I actually want?

Am I seeking `al-'ajilah` as my horizon?

Or do I want the Akhirah enough to strive for it with the striving it deserves?

[PAUSE]

Because one day the visible rankings of this world will feel very small.

The true degrees will appear.

And in that unveiling, one of the sweetest possibilities named in these ayahs will be this:

that a life of sincere striving, perhaps unimpressive to many eyes, is found by Allah to have been `mashkura`.
