# Say What Is Best, and Do Not Call Upon Those Who Are Themselves Seeking Nearness

## Surah Al-Isra (17:53-57)

## Introduction

Some passages correct the tongue before they correct the theology.

That might surprise us. We might expect a section that ends by demolishing false invocation to begin with a direct argument against shirk. But Allah begins somewhere nearer: tell My servants to say what is best.

The mouth is not a small matter in the Qur'an. Speech can become a doorway for mercy, or it can become the opening through which Shaytan enters a relationship and starts pulling people apart. So before the passage tells us not to call upon powerless beings besides Allah, it tells us not to hand Shaytan our conversations.

Then the frame widens. Your Lord knows you best. The Prophet is not sent as a `wakil` over people. Allah knows everyone in the heavens and the earth. Even prophets have ranks by Allah's favor, not by human manipulation. Dawud receives the Zabur. Then the command lands: call the ones you claimed besides Allah if you want. They do not own the removal of harm. In fact, those very beings are themselves seeking a way nearer to their Lord.

The passage is one sustained reordering of misplaced power.

Your tongue is not neutral.

The Prophet is not your controller.

The honored servants are not gods.

And Allah alone knows, owns, judges, and draws near whom He wills.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. `Yaqulu allati hiya ahsan`: The Qur'an Does Not Merely Command Speech; It Commands the Best Possible Speech

Allah says:

`wa-qul li-'ibadi yaqulu allati hiya ahsan`

Tell My servants to say that which is best.

The phrase is more precise than "say good things." It does not use merely `hasan`, good. It uses `ahsan`, the comparative and superlative form: better, best, most beautiful. The servant is not only asked to avoid the obviously ugly sentence. The servant is asked to choose the higher possible word among the available words.

[PAUSE]

That is a demanding ethic.

Sometimes there are many true things one could say, but only one of them is `ahsan`. Sometimes the harsh version is technically accurate while the merciful version is more beloved to Allah. Sometimes silence is not cowardice but the best possible speech because every available sentence would feed the wrong fire.

Al-Tabari reads the command as directing believers toward beautiful speech in their address, and Ibn Kathir connects it to avoiding the kind of speech through which Shaytan corrupts relations. The verse is not sentimental. It is strategic. The next sentence explains why.

### 2. `Yanzaghu baynahum`: Shaytan Works in the Space Between People

`Inna al-shaytana yanzaghu baynahum`

Satan stirs discord between them.

The verb `yanzaghu` carries the sense of prodding, poking, inciting, inserting disturbance. It is not always a full frontal attack. It can be a nudge into suspicion, a tone added to a sentence, an interpretation placed over someone's words, a memory inflamed at the wrong time.

And the location is essential:

`baynahum` - between them.

Shaytan loves the between-spaces. Between spouses. Between siblings. Between teacher and student. Between believer and believer. Between da'wah worker and da'wah worker. He does not need to destroy a person privately if he can turn two servants of Allah against each other through speech.

That is why the verse repeats his identity:

`inna al-shaytana kana li-l-insani 'aduwwan mubina`

Satan has always been a clear enemy to the human being.

The enemy is clear even when the tactic feels subtle. The relationship conflict may feel like personality, stress, principle, or "just telling the truth." But the Qur'an trains us to ask: is there a hand stirring the space between us?

### 3. `Rabbukum a'lamu bikum`: Allah's Knowledge Relieves Us From Playing Lord Over People

The next verse begins:

`rabbukum a'lamu bikum`

Your Lord knows you best.

That sentence arrives right after the speech command, and it quietly disciplines the heart behind speech. Many ugly words come from pretending we know people fully. We know their motives. We know their final state. We know exactly what they deserve. We know what their future with Allah must be.

The verse says: your Lord knows you best.

Then:

`in yasha' yarhamkum aw in yasha' yu'adhdhibkum`

If He wills, He will have mercy on you, or if He wills, He will punish you.

Mercy and punishment belong to Allah's will and knowledge. The Prophet himself is then told:

`wa-ma arsalnaka 'alayhim wakila`

We have not sent you over them as a trustee.

Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir both preserve the meaning that the Messenger's task is delivery, warning, and guidance by Allah's permission, not coercive management of people's final outcomes. If even the Prophet is not made a `wakil` over people in that sense, how much more should ordinary believers fear appointing themselves guardians over the souls of others?

### 4. `Faddalna ba'da al-nabiyyina 'ala ba'd`: Prophetic Rank Is Real, but It Is Allah's Favor, Not Human Possession

Verse 55 widens the scale:

`wa-rabbuka a'lamu biman fi al-samawati wa-al-ard`

Your Lord knows best whoever is in the heavens and the earth.

The same `a'lam` returns, but now the scope expands from "you" to all beings in the heavens and the earth. Then:

`wa-laqad faddalna ba'da al-nabiyyina 'ala ba'd`

We have certainly favored some prophets over others.

This is delicate. The Qur'an affirms real distinctions among prophets, but it places those distinctions inside Allah's knowledge and favor. Prophetic rank is not a tool for human boasting, factionalism, or false invocation. It is a divine apportioning.

Then the verse singles out:

`wa-atayna Dawuda Zabura`

And We gave Dawud the Zabur.

Dawud is honored by receiving revelation, but the very grammar keeps him in servanthood: We gave. He received. The gift is magnificent, but it does not make the gifted one independent of the Giver. This prepares the argument of verses 56-57 perfectly. Honored beings remain recipients.

### 5. `Fa-la yamlikuna kashfa al-durri ... yabtaghuna ila rabbihimu al-wasilah`: The Ones Invoked Are Themselves Seekers

Now the direct anti-shirk command comes:

`quli-d'u alladhina za'amtum min dunihi`

Say: call upon those you claimed besides Him.

The verb `za'amtum` matters. It names the claim as claim, not reality. They have asserted powers, relationships, and access that the invoked beings do not actually possess.

Then:

`fa-la yamlikuna kashfa al-durri 'ankum wa-la tahwila`

They do not possess the removal of harm from you nor its transfer.

`Yamlikun` is ownership language. They do not own `kashf`, uncovering or removing harm. Nor do they own `tahwil`, transferring it elsewhere or changing its location. The verse denies both total removal and redirection.

Then the next ayah overturns the whole psychology:

`ula'ika alladhina yad'una yabtaghuna ila rabbihimu al-wasilah`

Those whom they call upon are themselves seeking the means of nearness to their Lord.

The invoked are themselves worshippers. The ones treated as access-points are themselves seeking access. The ones imagined as dispensers of relief are themselves hoping for mercy and fearing punishment.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the Qur'an's most elegant reversals of religious misdirection. The problem is not that the invoked beings are necessarily evil. Some classical explanations mention angels, prophets, or righteous servants whom people wrongly invoked. The problem is precisely that their righteousness is being misread. They are not divine alternatives. They are the clearest proof of servanthood.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: A Tongue That Refuses `Ahsan` Can Become an Entry Point for Shaytan

Most people underestimate how spiritual speech is.

We tend to think of speech as expression: I am saying what I feel, what I think, what happened, what is true. The Qur'an treats speech as construction. Every sentence helps build a world between people. It can build safety, clarity, repentance, courage, tenderness, and justice. Or it can build suspicion, humiliation, defensiveness, revenge, and distance.

That is why Allah does not simply say, "Tell My servants not to lie." He says to say what is `ahsan`.

The modern world often rewards the opposite. Sharpness gets clicks. Sarcasm gets laughs. Cruel accuracy gets mistaken for courage. Public takedowns are framed as moral seriousness. Even in religious spaces, a person can hide spiritual ugliness behind truth-language. "I am just being honest." "Someone had to say it." "This is ghayrah for the deen."

But the ayah asks a harder question: was it `ahsan`?

[PAUSE]

Ibn Kathir's connection between speech and Shaytan's stirring is deeply practical. The issue is not manners as decoration. It is protection. When speech becomes needlessly harsh, ambiguous, mocking, or inflammatory, we are not merely being socially unpleasant. We may be giving Shaytan material to work with.

So the believer asks before speaking: will this help the truth arrive, or will it help Shaytan enter?

That question can save marriages, friendships, masjid boards, parent-child relationships, and online communities from fires that began with one sentence that did not need to be said that way.

### Theme 2: We Are Not Sent as Guardians Over People's Final States

`Rabbukum a'lamu bikum` is a mercy for anyone exhausted by judging people.

Allah knows you best.

Allah knows them best.

That does not cancel advice, da'wah, accountability, or public justice. The Prophet still warns. The Qur'an still commands. Wrong remains wrong. But the verse removes from us the fantasy of final control.

There is a difference between guiding someone and needing to own the outcome. There is a difference between warning someone and acting as though their destiny is in your hands. There is a difference between concern and spiritual domination.

The Prophet himself is told, `wa-ma arsalnaka 'alayhim wakila`. This echoes other Qur'anic reminders: in 88:21-22, the Prophet is told he is only a reminder, not a controller over them; in 6:107, he is told he has not been made a guardian over the mushrikin. The repeated pattern is not meant to reduce his mission. It clarifies it. Delivery is noble precisely because final hearts belong to Allah.

[PAUSE]

This is liberating for parents, teachers, community leaders, spouses, and anyone trying to help another soul. You are responsible to speak in the best way you can. You are responsible to advise with sincerity. You are responsible to model obedience. But you are not Allah.

When we forget that, our concern mutates. We become controlling, resentful, panicked, or manipulative. We start speaking worse because we feel outcomes slipping from our hands. But the passage already gave the order: say what is best, remember Shaytan enters between, and know that your Lord knows best.

The person who trusts Allah's knowledge can advise without becoming cruel.

### Theme 3: The Righteous Are Not Shortcuts Around Allah; They Are Witnesses of Need Before Allah

Verses 56-57 correct a religious mistake that often disguises itself as reverence.

People may not always call upon false objects because they despise Allah. Sometimes they do it because they admire the created being. An angel seems pure. A prophet seems near. A righteous servant seems beloved. So the heart begins to treat that created nearness as an independent power.

The Qur'an breaks this by showing the inner life of the invoked beings:

They seek wasilah.

They hope for mercy.

They fear punishment.

In other words, their greatness is servanthood.

Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir both transmit the interpretive frame that those invoked besides Allah may include beings like angels, 'Isa, his mother, or righteous servants. But the argument does not depend on dishonoring them. It honors them correctly. Their rank is real, but it is not lordship. Their nearness is real, but it is not independent possession. Their righteousness is real, but it points toward Allah, not away from Him.

[PAUSE]

This theme matters for how Muslims love prophets, scholars, saints, parents, teachers, and spiritual elders. Love is part of religion. Honoring the honored is part of adab. But the line is crossed when love turns a servant into a functional refuge besides Allah.

The most righteous people are not shortcuts around Allah.

They are witnesses teaching us how to need Allah.

If Dawud was given the Zabur, he was given it. If the prophets are favored, they are favored by Allah. If the angels are near, they are near by Allah's permission. If the righteous are beloved, their love is rooted in their dependence on Him.

So true reverence follows them in seeking the wasilah to Allah. False reverence calls upon them as though they possessed what only Allah owns.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Passage

The passage moves from the nearest arena of spiritual vulnerability to the widest correction of false worship. It begins with the tongue: say what is best because Shaytan stirs the spaces between people. It then moves to knowledge and authority: Allah knows you best, and even the Prophet is not a `wakil` over people's final states. It widens again to the heavens and the earth, prophetic favor, and Dawud's Zabur. Finally it lands on the decisive anti-shirk reversal: the beings invoked besides Allah do not own harm's removal or transfer, and they themselves are seeking nearness, mercy, and safety from their Lord. The architecture teaches that every created relationship must remain under Allah's knowledge, ownership, and lordship.

### Questions to Carry

Where have you settled for speech that is merely defensible when Allah asked you for what is `ahsan`? Think of one relationship where the best truthful wording would change the spiritual climate.

Who are you trying to be `wakil` over in a way Allah did not ask of you? Concern is noble, but control can become a hidden claim to lordship.

What created person or institution have you begun to treat as though it owns relief? Love the righteous, but do not ask from them what they themselves seek from Allah.

Does your reverence for honored servants make you more like them in seeking Allah, or does it distract you from the One they seek? The answer reveals whether love has remained tawhid or drifted into misdirection.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The servants of Allah guard their speech from Shaytan's openings, surrender people's outcomes to Allah's knowledge, and love the righteous not as powers besides Him but as fellow seekers of His nearness.**

### The Closing Invitation

Choose one conversation today and upgrade it to `ahsan`. Not just less harmful, but more beautiful, more truthful, more protective from Shaytan's entry. Then choose one burden you have been carrying as though you were responsible for someone's final state, and return it to Allah. Finally, renew your tawhid by asking Allah directly for the harm you need removed and the nearness you need granted.

### Du'a

Allahumma, make our tongues doors of mercy and not openings for Shaytan. Teach us to say what is best when our nafs wants what is sharpest. Free us from pretending to own people's hearts and outcomes. Let our love for Your prophets and righteous servants increase our servanthood, not confuse our worship. Grant us the wasilah to Your nearness, fill us with hope in Your mercy, protect us from Your punishment, and keep our du'a directed to You alone. Amin.
