# The Quran Guides to What Is Most Upright, but the Human Being Is Hasty With His Own Ruin

## Surah Al-Isra (17:9-11)

[ayah:17:9] إِنَّ هَـٰذَا ٱلْقُرْءَانَ يَهْدِى لِلَّتِى هِىَ أَقْوَمُ وَيُبَشِّرُ ٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ ٱلَّذِينَ يَعْمَلُونَ ٱلصَّـٰلِحَـٰتِ أَنَّ لَهُمْ أَجْرًا كَبِيرًا

[ayah:17:10] وَأَنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ لَا يُؤْمِنُونَ بِٱلْـَٔاخِرَةِ أَعْتَدْنَا لَهُمْ عَذَابًا أَلِيمًا

[ayah:17:11] وَيَدْعُ ٱلْإِنسَـٰنُ بِٱلشَّرِّ دُعَآءَهُۥ بِٱلْخَيْرِ ۖ وَكَانَ ٱلْإِنسَـٰنُ عَجُولًا

*Inna hadha al-qur'ana yahdi lillati hiya aqwam, wa yubashshiru al-mu'minina alladhina ya'maluna al-salihati anna lahum ajran kabira.*

*Wa anna alladhina la yu'minuna bi-al-akhirati a'tadna lahum 'adhaban alima.*

*Wa yad'u al-insanu bi-al-sharri du'a'ahu bi-al-khayr, wa kana al-insanu 'ajula.*

**Surely this Quran guides to that which is most upright, and gives glad tidings to the believers who do righteous deeds that they will have a great reward, and that those who do not believe in the Hereafter, We have prepared for them a painful punishment. And the human being calls for evil as he calls for good, and the human being has always been hasty.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

After showing us the rise and fall of whole peoples, the surah now brings everything into the present tense.

What is the guide now?

What is the thing in our hands that can keep us from repeating the same corrupt cycles?

[PAUSE]

The answer is: this Quran.

But the passage does not only praise the Quran. It also diagnoses the human being who receives it. The Quran guides to what is `aqwam`, most upright, most sound, most rightly established. Yet the human being is `ajul`, hasty, impulsive, impatient with process, dangerously quick to want what will later wound him. So the problem is not merely that we need revelation. It is that revelation is addressing creatures who often rush against their own healing.

The hook of the passage is this: **Al-Isra teaches that the Quran offers the straightest measure for belief, action, and life itself, but the human being often resists that guidance not through lack of intelligence but through haste, short-range desire, and a failure to see beyond the immediate moment.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Yahdi lillati hiya aqwam**: The Quran Does Not Merely Guide to Something Good, but to What Is Most Upright

The ayah begins:

**inna hadha al-qur'ana yahdi**

Surely this Quran guides.

That statement alone would have been enough to establish the honor of the Book. But the verse does not stop there.

It says:

**lillati hiya aqwam**

to that which is most upright.

[PAUSE]

`Aqwam` comes from the root of standing upright, being straight, rightly set, properly established. It is not only morally "better." It is more structurally sound. More balanced. More capable of bearing weight without collapse.

That means the Quran is not only offering isolated good ideas. It is guiding us into the most rightly ordered way of standing before Allah, before ourselves, before other people, before time, wealth, desire, suffering, and power.

Many paths can feel effective in the short term. Many ideologies can produce energy. Many desires can feel like relief. But the Quran claims something more radical: it guides not just to what is attractive or temporarily useful, but to what is `aqwam` - the way that actually stands.

The surah has just shown us communities that rose in arrogance and then fell under consequence. Here we are told why that happens: a people can have force without `qiwam`, motion without uprightness, and ambition without a structure that can stand before Allah.

### 2. **Yubashshiru al-mu'minin alladhina ya'maluna al-salihat**: Real Glad Tidings Are Joined to Faith That Works

Then the ayah continues:

**wa yubashshiru al-mu'minin**

and it gives glad tidings to the believers.

But the believers are not left undefined:

**alladhina ya'maluna al-salihat**

those who do righteous deeds.

This matters. The Quran's `bisharah` is not flattery. It is not a vague atmosphere of religious positivity. It is glad tidings for those whose faith has entered action, whose inward assent is taking visible moral shape.

[PAUSE]

Then:

**anna lahum ajran kabira**

that they will have a great reward.

Not a small compensation. Not a barely surviving outcome. `Ajran kabira` carries amplitude. The work of living by what is `aqwam` can feel costly in the present. It may slow you down where your nafs wants speed, restrain you where anger wants release, and discipline you where appetite wants indulgence. So the ayah reassures the believer that none of this disciplined uprightness is lost. The reward is not thin. It is great.

Then, in immediate contrast, the verse after it names the other side:

**wa anna alladhina la yu'minuna bi-al-akhirah**

and those who do not believe in the Hereafter...

The contrast is striking. The ayah does not merely say those who commit certain outer acts. It names a deeper fracture: disbelief in the Akhirah.

When the next world disappears from a person's live horizon, the self becomes trapped inside urgency, appetite, impression, and immediate payoff. That is exactly the terrain in which haste becomes dangerous.

### 3. **Wa yad'u al-insanu bi-al-sharri du'a'ahu bi-al-khayr**: Human Haste Can Want Harm With the Same Intensity It Wants Benefit

Then comes one of the most exposing lines in the surah:

**wa yad'u al-insanu bi-al-sharri du'a'ahu bi-al-khayr**

The human being calls for evil as he calls for good.

[PAUSE]

This can include the one who, in anger, frustration, despair, or blindness, asks for something harmful with the same earnestness with which he would ask for mercy or provision. It can also include the broader pattern of pursuing destructive outcomes as though they were beneficial: running after relationships that corrupt, ambitions that hollow out the soul, victories that poison the heart, or escapes that later become cages.

The ayah is terrifying because it refuses our flattering story about ourselves. We assume that if we want something intensely, it must contain some good for us. But the Quran says the human being can seek `sharr` with the same emotional posture he uses in seeking `khayr`.

Then comes the diagnosis:

**wa kana al-insanu 'ajula**

and the human being has always been hasty.

`Ajul` is not just fast-moving. It is impatient with unfolding. It wants fruit before season, verdict before reflection, relief before wisdom, answer before ripening, and control before surrender.

Haste is spiritually dangerous because it makes the immediate appear decisive. Once the soul loses patience with process, it becomes easy to mistake poison for medicine simply because poison promised speed.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Quran Straightens More Than Belief; It Straightens the Entire Human Posture

Many people treat guidance as though it means having the right opinion.

The Quran certainly rectifies belief. But `yahdi lillati hiya aqwam` is much larger than that. It means the Quran teaches the straightest way to stand as a human being.

[PAUSE]

It straightens how we love, how we grieve, how we earn, how we speak, how we disagree, how we wait, how we use power, how we carry pain, how we endure delay, how we think about death, and how we interpret blessing.

That matters because our crises are often not caused by total ignorance. They are caused by warped proportion. We exaggerate what is small and neglect what is great. We rush the near and forget the far. We protect the ego and wound the soul. We preserve convenience and lose integrity. In all of that, the Quran keeps guiding to what is `aqwam`, what has truer balance and more durable alignment.

This is why revelation can feel at first like resistance. If a soul has been bent around appetite, the straight line feels severe. If a life has been organized around speed, the Quran's patient proportions can feel like interruption. But that interruption is mercy. A splint does not flatter the broken limb. It holds it in the form it needs in order to heal.

### Theme 2: One of Our Deepest Spiritual Dangers Is Wanting the Wrong Thing Too Quickly

The final ayah in this passage is psychologically brilliant.

It tells us that the human being may call for evil the way he calls for good because he is `ajul`.

[PAUSE]

That means one of the soul's great vulnerabilities is not simply wickedness in the dramatic sense. It is impatient misreading.

We want an outcome now, and because we want it now, we rename it as good.

We want revenge now, so we call it justice.

We want escape now, so we call it freedom.

We want attention now, so we call it influence.

We want relief now, so we call it healing.

We want possession now, so we call it love.

We want visible results now, so we call compromise wisdom.

The Quran steps into that blur and says: no. The intensity of your wanting is not proof of goodness. Your desire is not a trustworthy scale when it is rushed.

That is why belief in the Hereafter matters so much in this passage. The Akhirah stretches the horizon. It teaches the soul to live beyond the next hour, the next wound, the next appetite, the next applause, the next panic. Once the horizon stretches, haste loosens its grip. A person who genuinely lives toward the Hereafter no longer needs every answer immediately.

## Closing Synthesis

These verses place two truths beside each other with stunning clarity.

The Quran guides to what is most upright.

The human being is hasty.

[PAUSE]

That pairing explains much of spiritual life.

If we want to understand why divine guidance can be so clear and yet people still ruin themselves, this is one answer: the nafs is often in a hurry that truth is not willing to satisfy.

The Quran wants to build what can stand.

The hasty self wants what can be had now.

The Quran wants the most upright way.

The hasty self wants the nearest way.

The Quran wants your wholeness.

The hasty self often wants your immediate soothing, even if it comes at the cost of your future.

[PAUSE]

So this passage teaches us to ask a harder question than "What do I want?"

It teaches us to ask:

Is what I want actually `aqwam`?

Is it upright before Allah?

Is it sound enough to carry weight?

Or am I asking for `sharr` with the emotional confidence of someone asking for `khayr`?

The mercy of the Quran is that it does not leave us inside that confusion. It guides. It gives glad tidings. It warns. And in doing all three, it trains the soul to slow down long enough to recognize that the straightest path is often not the fastest one, but it is the one that finally holds.
