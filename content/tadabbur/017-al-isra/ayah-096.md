# Allah Is Enough as Witness

## Surah Al-Isra (17:96)

[ayah:17:96] قُلْ كَفَىٰ بِٱللَّهِ شَهِيدًۢا بَيْنِى وَبَيْنَكُمْ ۚ إِنَّهُۥ كَانَ بِعِبَادِهِۦ خَبِيرًۢا بَصِيرًا

*Qul kafa billahi shahidan bayni wa-baynakum, innahu kana bi-'ibadihi khabiran basira.*

**Say: Allah is sufficient as witness between me and you. Indeed, He is ever, concerning His servants, All-Aware, All-Seeing.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

The argument has reached a point where more argument would not heal it.

The Qur'an has been declared inimitable. Every kind of example has been diversified in it. The opponents have demanded signs according to their own terms. Their deeper objection has been named: they resisted the idea that Allah would send a human messenger. Allah answered that if angels walked securely on earth, He would send an angel messenger to them. Human beings walking on earth receive a human messenger.

Now the Prophet is told what to say after all of that:

Allah is enough as witness between me and you.

This is not withdrawal because the Prophet lacks proof. It is not impatience. It is not the end of compassion. It is the moment when a servant carrying truth stops trying to force recognition from those who keep moving the terms and places the matter before the One whose witness is sufficient.

There is a kind of peace in this ayah, but it is not soft peace. It is judicial peace. The dispute is not left unresolved in the air. It is handed to Allah as witness. He knows the Messenger's truthfulness. He knows the deniers' objections. He knows what was asked sincerely and what was asked as a condition. He knows what came from confusion, what came from pride, what came from social pressure, and what came from refusal after clarity.

Al-Tabari reads this as Allah commanding His Prophet to say to the idolaters: Allah is sufficient as a witness between me and you that I am His messenger to you, and He knows His servants. Ibn Kathir frames it similarly: Allah knows that the Prophet has conveyed what he was sent with, and He knows the condition of those addressed. Al-Muyassar states the heart of it: Allah is enough as a witness to my truthfulness and your denial; He is fully aware and seeing of His servants.

There is no widely established separate asbab al-nuzul report for this ayah as an isolated event. Its context is the argument itself. After the demanded signs, after the human messenger objection, after the refutation, Allah gives His Messenger the final witness formula.

When people refuse to recognize what Allah has made clear, the believer does not need to become frantic. Allah is enough as witness.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. `Qul`: The Prophet Is Given the Final Word to Say

The ayah begins with `qul`: say.

This word has appeared repeatedly in the surrounding passage. Say, if mankind and jinn gathered. Say, Glory be to my Lord. Say, if angels walked on earth. Say, Allah is enough as witness. The Prophet is not improvising under pressure. He is being given the speech that fits each pressure.

That matters because the opponents are trying to drag him into their frame. They demand signs. They object to his humanity. They ask for spectacle. But each time, Allah gives him the answer. Prophetic speech remains under divine instruction.

`Qul` also teaches the believer that not every argument is answered by emotional overflow. Sometimes the right answer is a revealed sentence that reorders the whole scene. The Prophet is not told to list every proof again here. He is told to place Allah's witness between himself and them.

Al-Tabari's tafsir keeps the command direct: say to them, Allah is sufficient as witness. Ibn Kathir also treats it as the closing response after their obstinacy. The word `qul` preserves the Messenger's servanthood in speech. Even his final answer is received from Allah.

This is a needed discipline. When we are accused, misunderstood, or pressured to prove ourselves endlessly, our speech can become desperate. We may over-explain, over-defend, or try to control the inner verdict of people. This ayah teaches a different steadiness: say what Allah gives you to say, then let Allah witness the truth of the matter.

[PAUSE]

The servant does not need to win every room when Allah has witnessed the truth.

### 2. `Kafa billahi`: The Sufficiency That Ends the Frenzy

The phrase `kafa billahi` carries the sense: Allah is enough, sufficient is Allah, Allah suffices.

`Kafa` is a word of adequacy. It says the matter does not need another ultimate witness beyond Him. People may demand more. They may say, "Bring this sign, then this sign, then this document, then this angel." The ayah cuts through the multiplying demands: Allah is enough.

The construction with `bi-Allah` is a known Arabic form of sufficiency, and it appears elsewhere in the Qur'an. In 4:79, Allah says He is sufficient as witness. In 4:166, Allah bears witness to what He sent down to the Prophet, and the angels bear witness, and Allah is enough as witness. In 48:28, Allah is enough as witness that He sent His Messenger with guidance and the religion of truth.

Those echoes matter. The Prophet's mission is not dependent on the opponents granting him legitimacy. Allah's witness is the foundation. Human recognition may come late, partially, or not at all. But the truth of the message is not suspended until the deniers agree.

This is deeply freeing, but only if held with sincerity. A liar can also say, "Allah is my witness." So the phrase is not a tool for evading accountability. It is a refuge for a truthful messenger after proof has been conveyed and refusal remains. Allah's witness comforts the truthful and terrifies the false claimant.

For the believer, `kafa billahi` should become a medicine against performative religion. If Allah is enough as witness, then hidden sincerity matters more than public applause. If Allah is enough as witness, then misunderstood obedience is not wasted. If Allah is enough as witness, then the da'i does not need to convert every rejection into personal failure.

### 3. `Shahidan bayni wa-baynakum`: The Witness Between Two Sides

Allah is described here as `shahidan`: witness.

A witness is not merely someone who knows privately. A witness establishes the matter. A witness stands over the dispute with knowledge that cannot be bribed, distorted, forgotten, or intimidated. Allah's witness is perfect because His knowledge is perfect, His seeing is perfect, and His judgment is perfect.

The phrase `bayni wa-baynakum` means between me and you. The dispute is placed in the space between Messenger and rejecters. The Prophet is not told to say only, "Allah witnesses me." He says Allah is witness between me and you. That means Allah witnesses both sides: the one conveying and the ones refusing, the sincerity of the Messenger and the resistance of the audience.

This is not the first time a prophet places a dispute before Allah. Nuh says in 10:71 that if his standing and reminding by Allah's signs is unbearable to his people, they should gather their plan, then he trusts Allah. Hud tells his people to plot against him all together and not delay him, because he relies upon Allah, his Lord and their Lord. Messengers repeatedly reach moments when they can no longer make people want guidance. They can convey, warn, clarify, and remain patient. Then Allah witnesses.

Al-Muyassar explicitly names both sides: Allah is enough as witness to the Prophet's truthfulness and their denial. That double witness should make us careful. When we invoke Allah as witness in any conflict, we are not inviting Him to see only what favors us. We are inviting His knowledge over the whole matter.

[PAUSE]

Allah's witness is never partisan ignorance. He sees the whole truth between us.

### 4. `Innahu kana bi-'ibadihi`: The Witness Knows Them as Servants

The second half begins: `innahu kana bi-'ibadihi` — indeed, He has ever been, concerning His servants...

The word `inna` gives emphasis. The witness is sufficient because of who Allah is and how He knows. The verb `kana` here does not imply that Allah's knowledge used to be and then stopped. In such divine-name contexts, it points to an enduring reality: He has ever been, and is, fully aware and seeing.

Then comes `bi-'ibadihi`: concerning His servants.

This is a tender and severe phrase at once. The rejecters are still His servants in the sense of being His created slaves under His ownership, even when they refuse. The Prophet is His servant in the sense of chosen servanthood and messengerhood. Allah knows all His servants: those who obey, those who resist, those who are confused, those who are arrogant, those who hide sincerity, and those who hide malice.

The word `ibad` also returns the argument to its true scale. The people have been trying to evaluate the Messenger. They have demanded signs from him. They have objected to his humanity. But all of them stand under Allah's ownership. The Messenger and the deniers are not equals negotiating reality in an empty universe. They are servants before the Lord.

This should humble anyone who carries truth. Allah is witness between me and you, but I am also among His servants. His knowledge includes me. His seeing includes me. His witness is not a weapon I own; it is a reality I stand beneath.

That is why this ayah can produce both comfort and taqwa. Comfort, because Allah knows the truth when people distort it. Taqwa, because Allah also knows the parts of me I might prefer the dispute to hide.

### 5. `Khabiran Basira`: Inner Knowledge and Full Seeing

The ayah closes with two names or attributes: `Khabir` and `Basir`.

`Khabir` carries the sense of being fully aware, intimately informed, knowing the inner reality of things. It is not surface awareness. It is knowledge of what lies beneath, what motivates, what is hidden, what is subtle.

`Basir` carries the sense of seeing. Allah sees what is outward, visible, embodied, and manifest. He sees actions, gestures, gatherings, movements, faces, and consequences.

Together, these two names complete the witness. Allah knows the inward and sees the outward. He knows why they asked for signs and what they would have done if signs came. He sees what they said publicly and knows what was concealed privately. He sees the Prophet's conveyance and knows his burden. He sees the believer's obedience and knows the sincerity or weakness inside it.

This pairing appears with deep wisdom. If only `Basir` were mentioned, a person might think the witness is limited to what appears. If only `Khabir` were mentioned, a person might forget that outward actions are also fully seen. Together they leave no hiding place and no lost sincerity.

Al-Tabari reads the closing as Allah being aware of His servants and seeing their deeds. Al-Muyassar says He is aware of their affairs and seeing of them, nothing hidden from Him. Ibn Kathir places it in the context of Allah knowing the Prophet's truth and the rejecters' state.

What people misread outwardly, Allah knows inwardly; what people hide inwardly, Allah sees completely.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Allah's Witness Frees the Truthful From Endless Self-Defense

The first principle is this: when truth has been conveyed with clarity, the believer does not need to live enslaved to people's recognition.

This is easy to say and hard to live. Human beings crave vindication. We want the people who misunderstood us to understand. We want the person who accused us to admit it. We want the room to recognize sincerity. We want the da'wah effort to be appreciated, the sacrifice to be seen, the patience to be acknowledged, the truth to be publicly undeniable.

But sometimes, after all sincere effort, people still refuse. They reinterpret your words. They demand more proof. They move the condition. They object to the very form in which guidance came. At that point, continuing to chase their verdict can deform the soul.

This ayah gives the Prophet a sentence of release: Allah is sufficient as witness between me and you.

Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir both root the ayah in the Prophet's dispute with those who denied his message. The Prophet had conveyed. The Qur'an had answered. The signs of truth were present. The final witness belongs to Allah. This is not passivity. It comes after conveyance. It is not laziness. It comes after patient argument. It is the end of frantic self-defense when self-defense would no longer serve guidance.

The Qur'an gives similar comfort elsewhere. In 13:43, when disbelievers say the Prophet is not sent, he is told to say: sufficient is Allah as witness between me and you, and whoever has knowledge of the Book. In 29:52, he is told to say: sufficient is Allah between me and you as witness; He knows what is in the heavens and the earth. The witness of Allah is a recurring refuge for messengers under denial.

For us, the application is not to stop explaining too early. We owe people clarity where we can give it. We owe apologies where we wronged them. We owe evidence where claims require evidence. But there comes a point when the need to be recognized becomes its own captivity. The heart keeps returning to the same imagined courtroom, replaying arguments, drafting speeches, seeking a verdict from people who may never judge fairly.

At that point, this ayah teaches release with dignity: Allah is enough as witness.

[PAUSE]

The truthful servant can keep serving without forcing every human witness to become fair.

### Theme 2: Allah's Witness Is Comfort Only When We Are Willing to Be Fully Known

The second principle is this: invoking Allah as witness comforts the sincere and exposes the self-deceived.

It is tempting to use "Allah knows" as a shield. Someone criticizes us, and we say, "Allah knows my heart." Someone asks for accountability, and we say, "Allah is my witness." Sometimes that is true and needed. But this ayah does not let us use Allah's witness cheaply because it closes with `Khabir` and `Basir`. Allah knows inwardly and sees outwardly.

That means Allah's witness includes the motive we present and the motive we hide. It includes the pain that shaped us and the pride we defend. It includes the truth we spoke and the exaggeration we slipped into it. It includes the good deed and the desire to be seen doing it. It includes the injustice done to us and the injustice we committed while responding.

So the phrase "Allah is enough as witness" is safe only for the one who wants Allah's whole knowledge, not only the part that vindicates him.

Al-Muyassar's reading is helpful because it makes both sides explicit: Allah witnesses the Prophet's truthfulness and the deniers' denial. In our conflicts, we often want Allah to witness one side only. But He is witness between me and you. Between means the whole space is exposed.

This should produce a beautiful kind of muhasabah. Before I seek Allah's witness against someone else, I should stand under Allah's witness myself. Did I convey truth truthfully? Did I argue for Allah or for my ego? Did I become harsh because I was defending revelation, or because I enjoyed winning? Did I hide behind sincerity while neglecting adab? Did I tell the whole truth or only the useful part?

The comfort remains, but it becomes purer. The sincere person is not the person with no flaws. The sincere person is the one willing to be fully known by Allah and corrected by Him.

There is also a warning for religious work. A person can speak for the truth and still need Allah to purify how he speaks. A person can defend the Prophet and still carry anger that is more personal than principled. A person can be right in the matter and wrong in the manner. If Allah is witness between us and others, then He witnesses not only whether our side had evidence, but whether our heart carried amanah while presenting it.

This does not paralyze the believer. It refines him. He can still say, "Allah is enough as witness," but now he says it with lowered shoulders, aware that the witness of Allah is mercy only because Allah also opens the door of repentance. The One who knows everything hidden is also the One to whom the hidden self can return before the Day when all hidden things are tested.

That return is part of the gift.

[PAUSE]

Allah's witness is not a spotlight we aim at others. It is a light under which we all stand.

### Theme 3: `Khabir` and `Basir` Heal the Pain of Being Misread

The third principle is this: Allah's complete knowledge heals the wound of partial human perception.

Human beings misread one another constantly. We judge from fragments. We see a facial expression and imagine a motive. We hear one sentence and build a story. We watch someone's outward ease and assume they are fine. We see someone's mistake and reduce them to it. We receive someone's advice and suspect arrogance. We see someone's silence and call it indifference.

This partial seeing can be painful when we are the ones misread. A person may be trying sincerely and still be accused. A parent may carry invisible sacrifices. A student may be fighting a private battle. A da'i may be grieving while still serving. A repentant person may still be remembered by an old sin. A quiet believer may have a heart full of du'a that no one notices.

Then the ayah says Allah is `Khabir` and `Basir`.

He knows what is inside and sees what is outside. He knows the effort beneath the small deed. He sees the tear no one saw. He knows the restraint behind the silence. He sees the harm you refused to return. He knows the intention that people mocked. He sees the struggle beneath the stumble.

This does not mean human accountability is meaningless. Outward actions matter. Harm must be repaired. Claims require care. But the believer's deepest self does not live at the mercy of human misreading. Allah's knowledge is whole.

The Qur'an gives this comfort elsewhere. In 67:13-14, Allah says whether you conceal your speech or make it public, He knows what is within the breasts; does He not know who created, and He is the Subtle, the Aware? In 40:19, He knows the treachery of the eyes and what the breasts conceal. These verses can frighten us, but they also comfort us. Nothing sincere is lost in the gap between what people saw and what was true.

So when you are misread, do not let the pain of partial witnesses make you forget the complete Witness. Correct what needs correcting. Clarify what should be clarified. But do not hand your soul entirely to those who see only a sliver.

[PAUSE]

People see moments. Allah knows the servant.

### Theme 4: The Final Witness Makes Da'wah Patient and Humble

The fourth principle is this: knowing Allah is witness allows the caller to truth to remain patient without becoming controlling.

Da'wah can become anxious when we forget this ayah. We may begin with sincere concern, but when people do not respond, concern can mutate. We may become harsh, manipulative, performative, or despairing. We may measure our worth by immediate acceptance. We may treat rejection as proof that we failed, or as permission to hate the people we were sent to invite.

But the Prophet is taught to say Allah is enough as witness. This means the caller conveys, clarifies, embodies, and invites, but does not own the final verdict. Allah sees the conveyance. Allah knows the hearts. Allah guides whom He wills with wisdom.

This ties directly to the next movement of the surah, where guidance and misguidance are returned to Allah. The Prophet's role is not to manufacture guidance inside people. His role is to convey the message truthfully. Allah witnesses whether that conveyance happened and whether the response was sincere.

This should make our da'wah more patient. We can keep speaking with mercy because we are not desperate to force outcomes. We can keep boundaries because we are not trying to buy acceptance. We can admit when we do not know because Allah's witness frees us from pretending. We can let people process because guidance is not our possession.

It should also make our da'wah more humble. If Allah is `Khabir` and `Basir`, He knows the person in front of me better than I do. He knows the wound beneath their question, the arrogance beneath their argument, the sincerity beneath their confusion, or the fragility beneath their anger. I should speak truth, but I should not pretend to possess Allah's full knowledge of them.

The result is a beautiful balance: firmness in truth, softness in posture, patience in effort, and release in outcome.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Sufficient Witness

The ayah begins with command: `qul`, say. The Prophet's speech is given by Allah, not driven by pressure.

Then comes sufficiency: `kafa billahi`, Allah is enough. After demanded signs and objections to human messengerhood, the dispute is not left to human terms.

Then comes the role: `shahidan bayni wa-baynakum`, a witness between me and you. Allah witnesses both the Messenger's conveyance and the rejecters' response.

Then comes the reason this witness is complete: He is, concerning His servants, `Khabir` and `Basir`. He knows inward realities and sees outward actions.

So the architecture moves from speech to sufficiency, from sufficiency to witness, from witness to the divine knowledge that makes the witness perfect.

### Questions to Carry

Where am I still trying to force recognition from people after doing what Allah asked me to do?

Clarification has its place, but obsession with human verdicts can become captivity. Allah's witness frees the sincere servant to continue without becoming frantic.

Do I invoke "Allah knows" while being willing for Him to know all of me?

This phrase should not be used as a shield against correction. Allah knows the wound and the ego, the sincerity and the self-deception.

Whose partial judgment have I allowed to define me more than Allah's complete knowledge?

People may misread us, praise us wrongly, or blame us unfairly. None of those partial readings equals the full truth Allah knows.

When I call others to truth, do I remember that Allah knows them better than I do?

This protects da'wah from arrogance. We speak clearly, but we do not claim total knowledge of another servant's inward state.

What hidden deed or hidden struggle can I entrust more peacefully to Allah's witness?

Not every sincere thing will be recognized here. The believer learns to do some of the most important work under the gaze of Allah alone.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**When human recognition fails or refuses, Allah is enough as witness, because He knows every inward reality and sees every outward deed.**

### The Closing Invitation

Say the truth you are commanded to say, then let Allah witness what people cannot or will not see. Do not become careless with people, and do not become captive to them. Clarify with adab, serve with sincerity, repair what you owe, and release what belongs to Allah's judgment. The One who is `Khabir` and `Basir` has not missed you.

### Du'a

O Allah, make us truthful under Your witness.

Free our hearts from needing unfair people to validate what You already know.

Protect us from using Your witness to hide from correction.

Make us sincere in private and upright in public.

You are enough as witness between us and all that is disputed, hidden, misunderstood, and awaiting judgment.

Amin.
