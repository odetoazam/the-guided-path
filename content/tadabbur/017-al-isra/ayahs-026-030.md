# Give the Right, Avoid Waste, Speak Gently in Inability, and Do Not Let Your Hand Become a Moral Metaphor

## Surah Al-Isra (17:26-30)

[ayah:17:26] وَءَاتِ ذَا ٱلْقُرْبَىٰ حَقَّهُۥ وَٱلْمِسْكِينَ وَٱبْنَ ٱلسَّبِيلِ وَلَا تُبَذِّرْ تَبْذِيرًا

[ayah:17:27] إِنَّ ٱلْمُبَذِّرِينَ كَانُوٓا۟ إِخْوَٰنَ ٱلشَّيَـٰطِينِ ۖ وَكَانَ ٱلشَّيْطَـٰنُ لِرَبِّهِۦ كَفُورًا

[ayah:17:28] وَإِمَّا تُعْرِضَنَّ عَنْهُمُ ٱبْتِغَآءَ رَحْمَةٍ مِّن رَّبِّكَ تَرْجُوهَا فَقُل لَّهُمْ قَوْلًا مَّيْسُورًا

[ayah:17:29] وَلَا تَجْعَلْ يَدَكَ مَغْلُولَةً إِلَىٰ عُنُقِكَ وَلَا تَبْسُطْهَا كُلَّ ٱلْبَسْطِ فَتَقْعُدَ مَلُومًا مَّحْسُورًا

[ayah:17:30] إِنَّ رَبَّكَ يَبْسُطُ ٱلرِّزْقَ لِمَن يَشَآءُ وَيَقْدِرُ ۚ إِنَّهُۥ كَانَ بِعِبَادِهِۦ خَبِيرًۢا بَصِيرًا

*Wa ati dha al-qurba haqqahu wa al-miskina wa ibna al-sabili wa la tubadhdhir tabdhira.*

*Inna al-mubadhdhirina kanu ikhwana al-shayatin, wa kana al-shaytanu li-rabbihi kafura.*

**And give the relative his due, and the poor, and the traveler, and do not squander wastefully. Surely the squanderers are brothers of the devils, and Shaytan has always been ever ungrateful to his Lord. And if you must turn away from them seeking a mercy from your Lord that you hope for, then speak to them a gentle, easy word. And do not keep your hand chained to your neck, nor extend it fully all the way, lest you sit blamed and exhausted. Surely your Lord expands provision for whom He wills and constricts it. Surely He has always been fully aware of and seeing His servants.**

## Introduction

Money reveals things we do not always admit about ourselves.

It reveals what we fear.

It reveals what we worship.

It reveals how much gentleness survives in us when we feel we cannot give.

[PAUSE]

These verses are not simply a spending policy. They are a spiritual anatomy of wealth. Give people their right. Do not scatter your resources in waste. If you cannot help right now, do not let your inability turn into harshness. Do not become so tight-fisted that your hand is chained to your neck, and do not become so unrestrained that you are left blameworthy and depleted. Then the passage closes by relocating the whole question under Allah's sovereignty: He is the One who expands and constricts provision, and He does so with full knowledge and sight of His servants.

The hook of the passage is this: **Al-Isra teaches that wealth is not morally measured only by how much is spent, but by whether one gives rights, avoids waste, preserves gentleness in limitation, and stays balanced under the knowledge that rizq belongs to Allah's wise distribution.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Ati ... haqqahu**: What You Give Is Not Always Charity; Sometimes It Is Simply Someone Else's Right

The passage begins:

**wa ati dha al-qurba haqqahu**

Give the relative his due.

[PAUSE]

That word `haqqahu` matters. The ayah does not frame this first as optional generosity. It frames it as a right. Something owed. Something fitting. Something not invented by your mood.

Then the circle widens:

**wa al-miskina wa ibna al-sabil**

and the poor and the traveler.

The ayah is teaching that wealth is never purely private in meaning. It carries claims upon it.

Then:

**wa la tubadhdhir tabdhira**

Do not squander wastefully.

The verbal noun intensifies the prohibition. This is not merely "avoid a little excess." It is a rejection of the spirit of scattering wealth without purpose, proportion, or gratitude.

### 2. **Ikhwana al-shayatin / qawlan maysura**: Waste and Gentleness Are Both Theological Matters

Then the warning becomes severe:

**inna al-mubadhdhirina kanu ikhwana al-shayatin**

Surely the squanderers are brothers of the devils.

[PAUSE]

That is one of the harshest moral metaphors in the passage. Waste is not treated as stylish carelessness. It is spiritually affiliated with shaytanic ingratitude.

Then the reason:

**wa kana al-shaytanu li-rabbihi kafura**

Shaytan has always been intensely ungrateful to his Lord.

So tabdhir is not only financial mismanagement. It is a failure of shukr. Wealth received without reverence becomes easy to spill in ways that imitate the ingratitude of Shaytan.

But then comes a striking balance:

**wa imma tu'ridanna 'anhumu ibtigha'a rahmatin min rabbika tarjuhā**

If you must turn away from them while seeking a mercy from your Lord that you hope for...

In other words, if you genuinely cannot give now and are waiting on provision from Allah, the Quran does not command theatrical generosity detached from reality.

But even then:

**fa-qul lahum qawlan maysura**

Speak to them an easy, gentle word.

You may lack money in that moment. You are not permitted to lack mercy.

### 3. **La taj'al yadaka maghlulatan ... wa la tabsutha kulla al-bast**: The Hand Becomes a Moral Portrait of the Soul

Then the ayah gives one of the Quran's most memorable financial metaphors:

**wa la taj'al yadaka maghlulatan ila 'unuqika**

Do not make your hand chained to your neck.

This is miserliness pictured bodily. A hand unable to extend.

Then:

**wa la tabsutha kulla al-bast**

Do not extend it all the way open.

This is reckless release without measure.

[PAUSE]

Then the result:

**fataq'uda maluman mahsura**

lest you sit blamed and exhausted.

Again the image of sitting returns, as in verse 22. A person comes to rest in a diminished state. `Malum`: blamed. `Mahsur`: worn out, emptied, left unable.

Then the final correction:

**inna rabbaka yabsutu al-rizqa liman yasha'u wa yaqdir**

Your Lord expands provision for whom He wills and constricts.

Balance in spending is easier when you remember that rizq is not self-generated territory. It is governed by Allah.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Wealth Has a Moral Shape Long Before It Has a Number

These verses teach us to stop thinking about money only quantitatively.

The Quran cares about amount, yes. But it also cares about shape.

[PAUSE]

Is what you have being read as `haqq`, as something that carries obligations?

Or are you behaving as if every surplus is automatically your toy?

Are you wasting in ways that signal ingratitude?

Are you becoming hard in moments of inability?

Are you protecting yourself so tightly that even rightful giving feels like injury?

Or are you releasing so recklessly that you end up unable to sustain responsibility?

The hand becomes a portrait of the heart. Chained or indiscriminate, both are distortions. The Quran wants a hand trained by shukr, balance, and trust.

### Theme 2: Even When You Cannot Give Materially, You Are Still Accountable for the Manner of Your Refusal

This may be the most psychologically beautiful part of the passage.

Sometimes you do not have enough.

Sometimes you genuinely cannot meet the need before you.

The Quran recognizes that.

[PAUSE]

But it still commands:

**fa-qul lahum qawlan maysura**

Speak an easy word.

That means poverty is not a license for cruelty. Constraint is not permission for humiliation. Inability does not authorize hardness.

There is something profoundly humane here. The Quran protects the dignity of the one asking, even when the one asked cannot provide.

And it protects the conscience of the one unable to give, by teaching that gentleness itself is still a form of obedience.

Then the final verse settles the heart. Allah expands and constricts with full knowledge. So if He has narrowed your hand for a time, you do not need to convert that narrowing into shame, performance, or panic. You need to remain truthful, gentle, and balanced under His sight.

## Closing Synthesis

This passage begins with `haqq` and ends with `rizq`.

Between them it teaches a whole ethic of material life.

[PAUSE]

Give people their due.

Do not treat waste as harmless.

Do not imitate Shaytan's ingratitude with your spending.

If you cannot help now, still speak gently.

Do not chain your hand to your neck.

Do not fling it open without wisdom.

Remember that Allah is the One who opens and narrows provision.

And remember that He is `khabir basir`.

He sees not only what left your hand, but what kind of soul that hand belonged to.

[PAUSE]

So these verses ask us a searching question:

What theology is my spending announcing?

Does it announce fear?

Vanity?

Ingratitude?

Carelessness?

Or does it announce that I know wealth is from Allah, that others have a claim upon it, that even limitation should not make me harsh, and that balance itself is an act of worship?

The Quran does not want merely a generous hand.

It wants a hand educated by tawhid.
