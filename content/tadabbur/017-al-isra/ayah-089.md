# Every Example, and Still the Refusal

## Surah Al-Isra (17:89)

[ayah:17:89] وَلَقَدْ صَرَّفْنَا لِلنَّاسِ فِى هَـٰذَا ٱلْقُرْءَانِ مِن كُلِّ مَثَلٍ فَأَبَىٰٓ أَكْثَرُ ٱلنَّاسِ إِلَّا كُفُورًا

*Wa-laqad sarrafna lil-nasi fi hadha al-qur'ani min kulli mathalin fa-aba aktharu al-nasi illa kufura.*

**And We have certainly diversified for mankind in this Qur'an from every example, but most of mankind refused except disbelief.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

The surah has just widened the room until no human audience can hide inside its own confidence.

If all mankind and jinn gathered together, supporting one another, pooling language, power, memory, intellect, imagination, and every hidden capacity creation possesses, they would not bring the like of this Qur'an. That was the previous ayah. It was not addressed only to one poet in Makkah, or one tribe, or one generation. It imagined the strongest possible coalition of created beings and still declared their inability.

Now comes the sorrowful turn.

The problem was never that the Qur'an gave one narrow kind of proof and left people without another way in. The problem was never that revelation spoke in only one register, addressed only one temperament, or entered only one corner of human life. Allah says He has certainly diversified for people in this Qur'an from every example.

There is warning and mercy. There is story and law. There is command and parable. There is direct address and remembered history. There is cosmic sign and domestic instruction. There is the grave and the marketplace, the heavens and the human heart, the rise of nations and the private moment when a person lies to himself. The Qur'an turns the truth so that it meets the human being from many directions.

Yet the ayah closes: most people refused except disbelief.

That is a frightening diagnosis because it removes one of our favorite excuses. We like to imagine that rejection is mostly an information problem. If only the evidence were clearer. If only the presentation were different. If only there were one more sign, one more miracle, one more argument suited exactly to the listener. But this ayah says the Qur'an has already diversified the examples. Allah has not spoken to humanity in a single flat mode. The refusal remains.

Classical tafsir reads this ayah as a statement of Allah's full clarification and the human majority's obstinate response. Al-Tabari explains `sarrafna` as Allah's repeated clarification and varied presentation of proofs, reminders, and lessons in the Qur'an for people. Ibn Kathir connects it to the many kinds of examples, exhortations, and evidences by which the truth is made clear. Al-Muyassar states the sense with stark simplicity: Allah has explained and varied examples for mankind in this Qur'an, but most people would accept only denial.

There does not appear to be a separate, widely documented asbab al-nuzul report for this ayah as an isolated occasion. Its immediate context is doing the work: after the Qur'an's inimitability is declared, and before the deniers demand a series of spectacular signs in verses 90-93, Allah exposes the deeper issue. The demand for more proof can sometimes be sincere. Here, the surah is showing us a demand that comes after every kind of example has already been given.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. `Wa-laqad`: Certainty Before the Diagnosis

The ayah opens with a heavy cluster: `wa-laqad`.

The `wa` connects this verse to what came before. The challenge of the previous ayah is not left floating as an isolated proof. It flows into this verdict: the Qur'an is inimitable, and its guidance has been diversified. Then the `lam` of emphasis and `qad` with the perfect verb place certainty over the statement. This is not a tentative claim about how much guidance people might have received. It is a confirmed declaration from Allah.

That matters because human beings often argue from the feeling of incompleteness. A person can say, "I still do not feel convinced," and quietly turn that feeling into a claim that Allah has not clarified enough. The ayah begins by taking the measure of clarification out of the unstable inner court of the human mood. Allah says, with emphasis, that He has done this.

The perfect verb also matters. `Sarrafna` is presented as something already accomplished. The Qur'an's variety is not a future promise waiting on human demands. It is already present in the revelation being recited. Before the opponents in the next verses ask for springs from the earth, gardens, falling sky, angels, houses of ornament, and ascents into heaven, this ayah has already declared: the real work of guidance has already been given in the Qur'an.

Al-Tabari's reading helps us here because he treats the ayah as Allah's testimony that He has varied the forms of explanation and reminder. Ibn Kathir likewise frames it as the many examples by which Allah clarifies truth and warns creation. The emphasis at the beginning means the servant should be careful before saying, "Allah did not give enough."

This does not mean every person has understood every proof. It does not mean da'wah never needs patience, translation, explanation, or tenderness. But it does mean the failure of every heart to submit cannot be blamed on a poverty in revelation.

The ayah begins with certainty because the human heart is very skilled at turning its resistance into an accusation against clarity.

### 2. `Sarrafna`: Truth Turned Many Ways

The central verb is `sarrafna`: We diversified, varied, turned over, presented in different forms.

The root behind it carries the field of turning, directing, diverting, disposing, or moving something from one state or direction to another. In Form II, the doubled middle letter gives the sense of repeated or intensive action: not merely one turn, but varied turning; not one presentation, but repeated distribution across forms.

This is different from simply saying, "We mentioned." Mention can happen once. `Tasrif` suggests range, rotation, and repeated presentation. The truth is turned so that different sides of it become visible. One person may be awakened by a story. Another by a command. Another by the image of resurrection. Another by the collapse of a tyrant. Another by the tenderness of divine mercy. Another by the exactness of a legal boundary. Another by the exposure of the nafs. The Qur'an does not flatten all human beings into one psychological doorway.

The same idea appears with striking closeness in Al-Kahf 18:54: Allah says He has diversified in this Qur'an for people from every example, yet the human being is, more than anything, argumentative. It also appears in Az-Zumar 39:27, where Allah says He has struck for people in this Qur'an from every example so that they may remember. In Ar-Rum 30:58, a similar phrase appears, and the response of rejecters again exposes the refusal to receive. The Qur'an itself repeats the claim that it repeats, varies, and turns examples. Even the statement about variety comes in varied locations.

That is part of the mercy. Allah knows the human being is not reached only through abstract syllogism. We are embodied. We remember scenes. We are startled by reversals. We are pierced by images. We are trained by laws. We are moved by promises. We are sobered by threats. We understand ourselves through stories before we can always name ourselves through concepts.

So `sarrafna` is not redundancy. It is mercy moving through different doors.

And it also becomes evidence against the excuse of the one who keeps asking for the truth to arrive in a form that will not disturb him. The Qur'an has already come in many forms. If every form is rejected, the issue may not be the form.

### 3. `Lil-nas` and `aktharu al-nas`: The Universal Address and the Majority Response

The ayah says Allah diversified examples `lil-nas`: for mankind, for people.

Then it says `aktharu al-nas`: most people refused.

The repetition is painful. The same humanity for whom the Qur'an diversified examples becomes the humanity whose majority refuses. The first `al-nas` receives mercy; the second `al-nas` exposes response. Allah's address is broad, but human reception is not equally sincere.

This is not the only place the Qur'an warns us about the majority. Yusuf 12:103 tells the Prophet that most people will not be believers, even if he is eager. Al-An'am 6:116 says that if you obey most of those on earth, they will mislead you from Allah's way because they follow conjecture. Saba' 34:13 says few of Allah's servants are grateful. The Qur'an repeatedly breaks the spell of numbers.

That matters because one of the pressures on faith is social arithmetic. We look around and ask, "If this is true, why do most people not live by it?" The ayah answers before the question finishes: Allah may diversify truth for all people, and most people may still refuse.

Al-Muyassar's concise reading keeps the moral edge: most people would accept only disbelief or denial. Ibn Kathir often notes in verses of this kind that the many proofs do not benefit those whose response is stubborn rejection. Al-Tabari's wording around refusal and denial keeps the blame where the ayah places it: not on the absence of examples, but on the posture of those who refuse.

This repetition also guards the believer from contempt. The ayah does not say examples were diversified only for the elite, the intelligent, the spiritual, or the already-guided. It says `for people`. The Qur'an still speaks outward. Da'wah still remains an invitation. The majority's refusal does not cancel the universality of the address.

So the believer learns two things at once: never let the majority define truth, and never let the majority's refusal make you stop carrying mercy toward people.

### 4. `Fi hadha al-Qur'an min kulli mathal`: Examples Inside This Recitation

The ayah does not say only that examples exist. It says they are diversified `fi hadha al-Qur'an`: in this Qur'an.

The demonstrative `hadha`, this, brings the listener near. It points to the recited revelation in front of them, the Qur'an being heard, challenged, resisted, and demanded beyond. The examples are not hidden in some inaccessible archive. They are in this Qur'an.

Then comes `min kulli mathal`: from every example. `Mathal` can carry the sense of example, likeness, parable, striking case, or illustrative pattern. It is a way of making truth visible through a form the human mind can hold. A mathal does not shrink truth into a story. It brings truth close enough for the heart to see itself.

This is why Qur'anic examples are not decorative. When Allah gives the example of a good word as a good tree in Ibrahim 14:24-25, the point is not botanical charm. It teaches rootedness, upwardness, fruitfulness, and permission from the Lord. When Allah compares false invocation to a spider's house in Al-Ankabut 29:41, the point is not literary flourish. It exposes fragility beneath apparent structure. When Allah says in Al-Hashr 59:21 that if this Qur'an were sent down upon a mountain, it would humble itself and split from fear of Allah, He says these examples are struck for people so that they may reflect.

So the mathal is not a lesser form of argument. It is often the form that gets past the defenses abstract argument cannot enter.

But the phrase `min kulli mathal` should be held carefully. It does not mean the Qur'an contains every possible image or every conceivable detail. It means Allah has given every kind of example needed for guidance, admonition, proof, and remembrance. The sufficiency is moral and revelatory, not encyclopedic in the modern sense.

This matters for how we read. We can approach the Qur'an asking it to answer every curiosity according to our preferred category, or we can ask: what example has Allah placed here to turn me, warn me, steady me, or heal me?

The ayah tells us the examples are already inside this Qur'an. The question is whether we will enter as servants or stand outside demanding a different door.

### 5. `Fa-aba`: Refusal After Clarification

The turn of the ayah is `fa-aba`: but refused.

The `fa` is sharp. It links the diversification of examples to the response that follows. After such variety, one might expect gratitude, remembrance, surrender, or at least humbled attention. Instead: refusal.

The verb `aba` is stronger than simply failing to know. It is to refuse, decline, reject, hold back from accepting. The Qur'an uses this verb with moral weight elsewhere. In the Adam story, Iblis `aba` and was arrogant. The refusal was not lack of data. He knew the command. His refusal came from a diseased evaluation of himself, Adam, and Allah's command.

Here, most people `aba`. They refuse after diversified examples. This means the ayah is diagnosing a will, not merely an intellect. A confused person can ask and learn. A limited person can misunderstand and be taught. A wounded person can move slowly toward truth. But refusal is something else. Refusal is when the heart meets clarity and still says no.

This distinction is pastorally important. Not every struggling person is refusing. Some people are genuinely confused. Some carry trauma, poor teaching, inherited distortions, or half-formed questions. The Qur'an makes room for teaching, patience, and different kinds of address precisely because Allah has diversified examples for people.

But the ayah also warns us not to romanticize every rejection as innocent confusion. There is such a thing as refusing truth because accepting it would require surrender.

The next verses make this concrete. The rejecters demand springs, gardens, falling sky, angels, ornate houses, ascent into heaven, and a written book brought down for them. Yet this ayah has already told us the Qur'an itself has diversified every example. The demand for more can become a strategy for never submitting to what is already enough.

### 6. `Illa kufura`: Nothing Comes Out Except Covering

The ayah ends with `illa kufura`: except disbelief, denial, ingratitude.

The structure is constricting. Most people refused except kufur. It is as if all the varied examples meet a narrowed response. Revelation comes with range; refusal collapses into one dark output.

The root behind `kufur` carries the sense of covering. A farmer who covers seed can be described with the same root in older Arabic usage. Spiritually, kufur covers truth, covers blessing, covers the signs one should have acknowledged. This is why kufur is not only intellectual denial. It is also ingratitude. The ungrateful person covers the gift by treating it as absent, owed, ordinary, or self-produced.

That meaning fits this ayah beautifully and painfully. Allah diversifies examples in the Qur'an for people. What would gratitude look like? It would receive the examples as mercy. It would say: my Lord has spoken to my condition in many ways. He did not leave me with one kind of reminder. He turned truth toward me again and again.

Kufur covers that mercy. It acts as if Allah has not clarified. It treats the Qur'an's varied guidance as insufficient, irrelevant, or unworthy. It hears one example and dismisses it. It hears another and says the style does not suit it. It hears a story and calls it old. It hears a warning and calls it harsh. It hears mercy and calls it naive. It hears law and calls it restrictive. Every door is treated as the wrong door because the heart does not want to enter.

This ending also echoes the surah's earlier diagnosis. In 17:67, when harm touches people at sea, those they call upon vanish except Allah; then when He saves them to land, they turn away, and the human being is ungrateful. In 17:83, when Allah blesses the human being, he turns away and distances himself, and when harm touches him, he despairs. Now, in 17:89, the Qur'an has been diversified, but most people refuse except kufur.

Al-Isra keeps returning to the same human wound: receiving mercy while acting as if mercy has not arrived.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Allah Varies Guidance Because Human Hearts Need More Than One Door

One principle of this ayah is that Allah's guidance is not monotonous. It comes with merciful variety because human beings are not reached in only one way.

Think of how different people wake up. One person hears a warning about death and finally stops lying to himself. Another person hears about Allah's mercy and returns after years of shame. Another person is moved by the precision of law because it gives shape to a life that was dissolving. Another is broken open by the story of Yusuf because it names betrayal, patience, temptation, and eventual unveiling. Another is steadied by the du'a of Musa because fear suddenly becomes speakable. Another is pierced by a parable because an image says what a lecture never could.

This variety is not accidental. `Sarrafna` tells us that Allah has turned and diversified examples in the Qur'an. Al-Tabari's tafsir emphasizes repeated clarification and varied exposition. Ibn Kathir points to the range of examples, warnings, and proofs by which Allah makes guidance clear. Al-Muyassar gives the same broad frame: the Qur'an contains varied examples so people may be reminded and guided.

This helps us understand Qur'anic repetition. A modern reader may ask, "Why does the Qur'an repeat stories? Why return to the same themes? Why mention resurrection so often? Why warn again, promise again, command again?" But repetition in the Qur'an is rarely mechanical repetition. It is variation. The story appears in a new surah, under a new pressure, with a different phrase, a different silence, a different angle of the same truth. Musa's story in Ta-Ha is not doing exactly what Musa's story in Al-Qasas does. Adam's story in Al-Baqarah is not doing exactly what Adam's story in Al-A'raf does. The same event can become many examples because the human heart needs to be reached from many directions.

Al-Kahf 18:54 says nearly the same thing, then adds that the human being is more than anything argumentative. That pairing is stunning. Allah diversifies examples; the human being diversifies objections. Az-Zumar 39:27 says examples are struck in the Qur'an so that people may remember. The goal is not entertainment or literary ornament. The goal is dhikr: for the heart to wake up to what it already should have known.

This theme has practical consequence for how we teach and receive the Qur'an. A parent, teacher, imam, or friend should not assume that one mode reaches every person. Some hearts need fear, some need hope, some need beauty, some need structure, some need story, some need patient reasoning, some need to be asked a question they cannot escape. The Qur'an models a da'wah that is truthful without being flat.

But the theme also challenges the receiver. If Allah has diversified examples, then we should be careful about dismissing the mode that does not immediately match our taste. A person may say, "I only like spiritual passages, not law." Another may say, "I only want rational proof, not story." Another may say, "I only respond to mercy, not warning." The ayah says the Qur'an has come with many examples because we need the whole medicine, not only the flavor we prefer.

The objection may arise: if guidance is so varied, why do most people still refuse? The ayah answers with grief and realism. Variety does not force surrender. It removes excuses, opens doors, and meets the servant in many ways, but the will can still close itself. Guidance is not magic that overrides moral agency. It is mercy that calls, clarifies, repeats, and turns toward us.

The Qur'an does not lack doors. The danger is that the heart keeps moving away from every entrance.

### Theme 2: The Majority Is Not the Measure of Truth

A second principle is that numbers do not decide guidance.

This is hard for us because social proof has become one of the strongest forces in modern life. We measure ideas by visibility, products by ratings, people by followers, credibility by consensus, and even moral confidence by how many people seem comfortable with a thing. When a path is crowded, it feels safe. When a path is lonely, it feels suspect.

Then the Qur'an says: Allah diversified examples for mankind, but most of mankind refused except disbelief.

That sentence breaks the illusion that widespread refusal is evidence against revelation. It is possible for Allah's speech to be clear, varied, merciful, and sufficient, while most people still refuse it. The majority response tells us something about human beings before it tells us anything about the truth.

The Qur'an repeats this lesson in many places. Yusuf 12:103 tells the Prophet that most people will not believe, even though he is deeply eager. That verse is tender because it acknowledges prophetic concern. The Prophet's longing for people to be guided does not transform the majority into believers. Al-An'am 6:116 warns that obeying most of those on earth would mislead a person from Allah's way because most follow conjecture. Saba' 34:13 says few of Allah's servants are grateful. These verses do not encourage arrogance toward people. They free the believer from worshipping numbers.

Al-Tabari's framing of 17:89 keeps the issue tied to refusal after clarification. Ibn Kathir's treatment of similar majority passages often shows that signs and examples benefit those who seek guidance, while those committed to denial only increase in distance. The majority's refusal is not a neutral poll. It is a moral response.

This matters when a believer feels socially outnumbered. A young person may look at classmates and think, "Everyone lives differently. Maybe I am the strange one." A worker may sit in an office where certain forms of dishonesty are normalized and feel foolish for refusing. A Muslim family may try to hold a boundary around consumption, speech, modesty, prayer, or money and feel surrounded by a world that treats the boundary as backward. The nafs whispers: so many people cannot all be wrong.

The Qur'an says: yes, they can. And sometimes they are.

But this must be held with humility. The ayah should not make us smug. It should not produce a personality that enjoys being against people. Remember the first half: Allah diversified examples `for people`. The believer who follows truth while most refuse still carries concern for people. The Prophet is told in other places not to destroy himself in grief over their refusal, which means his concern was real. The minority status of truth should create firmness, not contempt.

So the practical application is delicate. We should not take the crowd as our qiblah. We should not panic when obedience makes us socially strange. We should not assume that mass acceptance purifies a thing Allah has warned against. But we should also keep making the invitation beautiful, patient, and clear, because the Qur'an's examples are for people even when most people refuse.

### Theme 3: Refusal Often Hides Behind the Demand for More

A third principle is that the demand for more evidence can become a way to avoid surrender to the evidence already given.

This is not always the case. Some questions are sincere. Some people really need explanation. Some doubts are wounds asking for careful treatment. The Qur'an itself answers questions, gives arguments, invites reflection, and dignifies the use of mind. We should not use 17:89 to silence honest seekers.

But the placement of this ayah is exposing a specific posture. Verse 88 has declared the Qur'an inimitable. Verse 89 says Allah has diversified every kind of example in it. Then verses 90-93 record a list of demands: make a spring gush from the earth, possess gardens with rivers, make the sky fall in pieces, bring Allah and the angels before us, have a house of ornament, ascend into heaven, and even then we will not believe in your ascension until you bring down a book we can read.

The sequence matters. The demands come after the declaration that the Qur'an is already enough as revelation and that its examples have been diversified. So the surah is teaching us to recognize a spiritual tactic: endless conditions.

In ordinary life, this can look very respectable. "I would take prayer seriously if my life were less busy." "I would repent if I felt more certain." "I would obey this command if I understood every wisdom behind it." "I would believe more deeply if Allah showed me something unmistakable." "I would change if someone answered one more issue." Sometimes there is sincerity there. But sometimes the condition protects the self from the cost of obedience.

The Qur'an elsewhere names this pattern. Al-An'am 6:111 says that even if angels were sent down, the dead spoke to them, and everything were gathered before them, they would not believe unless Allah willed. Al-Isra itself already said in 17:59 that demanded signs were withheld not because Allah lacked power, but because earlier peoples denied them, and the she-camel was given to Thamud as a visible sign, yet they wronged her. More spectacle does not cure a heart committed to refusal.

Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir both help us keep the balance. The Qur'an's examples are proofs, reminders, and warnings. Their rejection is not due to Allah failing to clarify. Al-Muyassar's wording is blunt: most people accept only denial. The tafsir tradition does not treat the next demands as innocent curiosity floating in a vacuum. They are part of a larger pattern of obstinacy.

This should make us examine the difference between a question and a condition. A question says, "Help me understand so I can come closer." A condition says, "Meet my terms before I surrender." A question is open to being changed. A condition is often designed to keep the self in control. A question can coexist with humility. A condition often places Allah's revelation in the dock and appoints the ego as judge.

The lived application is simple and severe: when we ask for more clarity, we should also ask what we have done with the clarity already given. Have I acted on the ayah I already understand? Have I repented from the sin I already know is harming me? Have I fulfilled the duty whose proof is not unclear? Have I thanked Allah for the examples that already reached me?

Because sometimes the next proof we need is obedience to the previous proof.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of Every Example and the Refusal

The ayah has a tight architecture. It begins with emphatic certainty: Allah has certainly done something. It names that action with `sarrafna`: He has varied and diversified. It identifies the recipients: for people. It locates the mercy: in this Qur'an. It describes the scope: from every kind of example. Then it turns with `fa`: after all of that, most people refused. The final phrase narrows the response to `illa kufura`: nothing but denial and ingratitude.

So the movement is from divine abundance to human constriction. Allah gives variety; refusal produces a single dark answer. Allah addresses mankind; most of mankind turns away. Allah places examples inside this Qur'an; the rejecter keeps asking for another form outside the Qur'an. The ayah is not only saying that people disbelieved. It is showing the tragedy of varied mercy meeting a narrowed heart.

This is why 17:89 is the hinge between the inimitability challenge of 17:88 and the demanded spectacles of 17:90-93. Before the surah lists what the deniers want next, it tells us what they have already received: every kind of example in this Qur'an.

### Questions to Carry

Where have I mistaken my lack of surrender for a lack of clarity?

This question should be handled gently but honestly. Not every struggle is stubbornness, but some of our most polished explanations hide a simple refusal to obey what we already know.

Which kind of Qur'anic example do I keep avoiding because it does not match my preferred spiritual taste?

Some of us want mercy without warning. Some want law without tenderness. Some want stories without commands. The ayah invites us to receive the Qur'an's full range as mercy.

Do I let the majority's refusal make obedience feel less true?

The Qur'an prepares us for the fact that most people may refuse even after diversified examples. The believer needs a heart anchored in Allah's speech rather than social arithmetic.

When I ask for more proof, am I asking as a seeker or negotiating as someone trying to stay unchanged?

A sincere question moves toward guidance. A defensive condition protects the ego. The difference may be hidden from people, but it is not hidden from Allah.

How do I respond when the Qur'an turns the same truth toward me again?

Repetition can become irritation for a resisting heart, but mercy for a heart that knows it needs reminders. The next time an ayah returns, perhaps it is not because we already know it, but because Allah is still turning us toward it.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Allah has turned every kind of guiding example toward people in this Qur'an, but the heart that wants control can turn even abundant mercy into another occasion for refusal.**

### The Closing Invitation

Come back to the Qur'an as someone who has been given more doors than he deserves. Do not wait for the example that flatters your preferred self. Let the warning warn you, the mercy soften you, the story expose you, the law discipline you, the promise lift you, and the parable make you see. Allah has diversified the examples. Our task is not to keep demanding a different form until obedience becomes unnecessary. Our task is to enter through the door that is already open.

### Du'a

O Allah, do not make us among those who refuse after Your clarification.

Make the varied examples of the Qur'an reach the varied diseases of our hearts.

Protect us from turning questions into conditions and conditions into excuses.

Make us grateful for every ayah that warns us, heals us, humbles us, and calls us back to You.

O Allah, let this Qur'an be proof for us, not against us.

Amin.
