# Even If You Became Stone or Iron, the One Who Originated You Will Call You Back

## Surah Al-Isra (17:49-52)

[ayah:17:49] وَقَالُوٓا۟ أَءِذَا كُنَّا عِظَٰمًا وَرُفَٰتًا أَءِنَّا لَمَبْعُوثُونَ خَلْقًا جَدِيدًا

[ayah:17:50] قُلْ كُونُوا۟ حِجَارَةً أَوْ حَدِيدًا

[ayah:17:51] أَوْ خَلْقًا مِّمَّا يَكْبُرُ فِى صُدُورِكُمْ فَسَيَقُولُونَ مَن يُعِيدُنَا قُلِ ٱلَّذِى فَطَرَكُمْ أَوَّلَ مَرَّةٍ فَسَيُنْغِضُونَ إِلَيْكَ رُءُوسَهُمْ وَيَقُولُونَ مَتَىٰ هُوَ قُلْ عَسَىٰٓ أَن يَكُونَ قَرِيبًا

[ayah:17:52] يَوْمَ يَدْعُوكُمْ فَتَسْتَجِيبُونَ بِحَمْدِهِۦ وَتَظُنُّونَ إِن لَّبِثْتُمْ إِلَّا قَلِيلًا

*Wa qalu a-idha kunna 'izaman wa rufatan a-inna la-mab'uthuna khalqan jadida.*

*Qul kunu hijaratan aw hadida.*

*Aw khalqan mimma yakburu fi sudurikum fa-sayaquluna man yu'iduna, qul alladhi fatarakum awwala marratin, fa-sayunghiduna ilayka ru'usihim wa yaquluna mata huwa, qul 'asa an yakuna qariba.*

*Yawma yad'ukum fa-tastajibuna bi-hamdihi wa tazunnuna in labithtum illa qalila.*

**And they say: When we have become bones and scattered particles, will we really be raised as a new creation? Say: Be stone or iron, or some creation even greater in your breasts. Then they will say: Who will bring us back? Say: The One who originated you the first time. Then they will shake their heads toward you and say: When is it? Say: Perhaps it is near. On the Day He calls you, you will respond with His praise, and you will think you remained only a little.**

## Introduction

There is a kind of disbelief that feels bold only because it chooses the wrong horizon.

It stares at bones, dust, and decay and says: how could this ever return?

It mistakes disintegration for impossibility.

[PAUSE]

These verses answer that mistake with a force both rational and humiliating. The mockers imagine that bodily breakdown is the decisive problem. Allah tells them: fine, imagine something even harder. Become stone. Become iron. Become whatever seems greatest and least recoverable in your own chest. The issue is still not material difficulty. The answer remains the same: the One who brought you forth the first time.

Then the passage moves from argument into scene. The final resurrection is not presented as a laborious reconstruction process but as a call. He calls, and you answer. Then the whole remembered length of worldly stay contracts until it feels like `illa qalila`, only a little.

The hook of the passage is this: **these ayahs teach that resurrection is not difficult because difficulty is measured against created power, while the real problem is that the human being forgets first origination and overestimates the finality of disintegration.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **'Izaman wa rufatan / khalqan jadida**: The Mockery Begins From the Optics of Decay

They say:

`a-idha kunna 'izaman wa rufatan`

When we have become bones and `rufat`...

[PAUSE]

`'Izam` are bones, the recognizable remnants of a once-living structure. `Rufat` pushes further toward disintegration, crumbled scattered remains, broken particles. Their argument is visual. They are looking at what death does to the body and treating visible decomposition as proof against return.

Then:

`a-inna la-mab'uthuna khalqan jadida`

Are we really to be raised as a new creation?

The word `mab'uthun` carries the sense of being sent forth, raised up, dispatched again into life. What they are rejecting is not only reassembly but re-emergence before accountability.

So their problem is not merely biological imagination. It is moral resistance to a second standing after the first life.

### 2. **Kunu hijaratan aw hadida**: Allah Expands the Challenge Beyond Their Chosen Material

Allah replies:

`qul kunu hijaratan aw hadida`

Say: be stone or iron.

[PAUSE]

This answer is astonishing. It does not stay inside their chosen frame. It shatters it.

The mockers have chosen one particular image of impossibility: decomposed human remains. Allah says, very well, imagine yourselves as something even further in the direction of hardness, density, and resistance. Stone. Iron.

Then even beyond that:

`aw khalqan mimma yakburu fi sudurikum`

or some creation even greater in your breasts.

That is one of the most psychologically exact phrases in the passage. "Whatever seems greatest in your chests." In other words, pick the hardest case your imagination can produce. Choose the thing that feels most impossible to return.

The ayah relocates the obstacle. The real issue is not external matter. It is what has become `kabura fi sudurikum`, huge in your own inner estimation.

### 3. **Alladhi fatarakum awwala marratin / yad'ukum fa-tastajibun**: First Origination Makes Final Summons Intelligible

Then they ask:

`man yu'iduna`

Who will bring us back?

And the reply:

`alladhi fatarakum awwala marratin`

The One who originated you the first time.

[PAUSE]

`Fatarakum` is powerful. It is origination, bringing forth, initiating from non-prior human existence. The first bringing-into-being is the argument for the second. If origination from nothing already occurred, why should re-creation from prior material be the impossible step?

Then the ayah portrays their reaction:

`fa-sayunghiduna ilayka ru'usihim`

They will shake their heads toward you.

The gesture carries dismissal, mockery, impatient derision. It is the bodily punctuation of unbelief.

Then they ask:

`mata huwa`

When is it?

And the answer is brief:

`'asa an yakuna qariba`

Perhaps it is near.

Then the scene arrives:

`yawma yad'ukum fa-tastajibuna bi-hamdihi`

The Day He calls you and you answer with His praise.

This is extraordinary. The Resurrection is compressed into a summons and an immediate response. No long debate then. No swagger then. Just the call and the compelled answer.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: We Often Measure Divine Possibility by Creaturely Intuition

These verses expose a constant human mistake.

We take what looks impossible inside our limited experience and quietly promote that feeling into a verdict about reality.

[PAUSE]

Dead bodies decay. Therefore return feels impossible.

Time passes. Therefore the Day feels far.

Bones crumble. Therefore accountability feels abstract.

But all of this is creaturely inference pretending to be theological knowledge.

The ayah dismantles that by returning to first origination. You were not self-starting. Your existence itself is already the greater wonder. Once that is remembered, resurrection stops being the unbelievable sequel and becomes the logically subordinate act.

So the deeper disease is forgetfulness of beginning. A person who has truly remembered `fatarakum awwala marratin` cannot speak about the second creation with the same arrogance.

### Theme 2: The Day Will Shrink the Story We Thought Was So Long

The ending is quietly devastating:

`wa tazunnuna in labithtum illa qalila`

You will think you remained only a little.

[PAUSE]

All the decades we build our identities around, all the arguments we delay repentance with, all the pleasures we use to numb the thought of return, all the time we imagine is still ahead of us - suddenly the whole stretch contracts.

The dunya is long only from inside itself.

From the vantage point of resurrection, it becomes `qalila`.

That is one of the mercies of these verses. They reorder scale before the Day arrives. They invite us now to stop treating temporary extension as substantive permanence.

And notice that even the response to the divine call comes `bi-hamdihi`, with His praise. The One once denied is the One whose praise frames the final answer. Reality closes around His lordship whether the human being acknowledged it early or not.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs begin with bones and end with a call.

[PAUSE]

At the beginning, the disbeliever stares at decay and imagines impossibility.

At the end, Allah calls and the human being answers.

Between those two moments the whole illusion collapses.

Stone does not save you.

Iron does not save you.

Whatever seems largest in your chest does not save you.

The One who began you once does not become incapable because you have become difficult in your own imagination.

And when He calls, the long span we clung to becomes `illa qalila`.

### Questions to Carry

- What have you quietly treated as impossible only because you judged it by created power?
- How often do you remember your first origination when thinking about your final return?
- What in your chest has become `kabura` - larger in fear or denial than it truly is before Allah?
- If your whole stay will eventually feel like only a little, what deserves urgency now?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Resurrection becomes difficult only to the one who has forgotten first origination and mistaken the optics of decay for the limits of divine power.**

### Du'a

*Allahumma, do not let our hearts be deceived by what decays before our eyes. Make us remember that the One who began us can return us with ease. Shrink the false vastness of dunya in our sight before the Day shrinks it by force, and let us answer Your call now with praise before we answer it then in helpless certainty.*
