[ayah:17:70] وَلَقَدْ كَرَّمْنَا بَنِىٓ ءَادَمَ وَحَمَلْنَـٰهُمْ فِى ٱلْبَرِّ وَٱلْبَحْرِ وَرَزَقْنَـٰهُم مِّنَ ٱلطَّيِّبَـٰتِ وَفَضَّلْنَـٰهُمْ عَلَىٰ كَثِيرٍ مِّمَّنْ خَلَقْنَا تَفْضِيلًا

*wa-laqad karramnā banī ādama wa-ḥamalnāhum fī l-barri wa-l-baḥri wa-razaqnāhum min al-ṭayyibāti wa-faḍḍalnāhum 'alā kathīrin mimman khalaqnā tafḍīlā*

And We have certainly honored the children of Adam and carried them on land and sea and provided for them of good things and preferred them over much of what We have created, with definite preference.

---

## INTRODUCTION

Surah Al-Isra opens with a journey no human was supposed to take.

In a single night, Muhammad ﷺ is carried from Makkah to Jerusalem, and from Jerusalem through the seven heavens — past the angels, past the prophets, past every created boundary — until he stands in a proximity to Allah that no other being has ever occupied. He sees what the angels see. He passes through realms that human bodies were never designed to enter.

And then he comes back.

He comes back to a city that is trying to kill him. He comes back to people who will mock the story. He comes back to hunger, rejection, the death of his wife and uncle still fresh, and the daily work of building a community from almost nothing.

The surah that narrates this journey — Al-Isra — is obsessed with a question: **What is a human being?** What kind of creature gets carried through the heavens one night and struggles to wake up for Fajr the next morning? What kind of being is worth that journey?

Midway through the surah, Allah answers.

Not with a story. Not with a parable. With a *declaration* — introduced by the most emphatic grammatical construction Arabic possesses.

And the verb He chooses to describe what He did to the human species is not the verb you would expect. It is more than honoring. It is more than dignity. It is a verb that takes an already noble concept and *intensifies it until it becomes nature*.

Watch the verb.

## PART 1: THE LINGUISTIC JOURNEY

### I. *Karramna* — The Verb That Made Dignity Permanent

In Arabic, verbs come in forms — patterns that reshape the meaning of a root the way a lens reshapes light. The root stays the same. The form decides what kind of action it becomes.

Let me show you with a simple example before we touch the Quran.

The root ع-ل-م (*'alima*) means "to know."

In Form I — *'alima* — it means: he knew. Simple. One person, one act of knowing.

In Form II — *'allama* — the middle letter doubles, and the meaning *intensifies*. Now it means: he *taught*. Not just knew — he made that knowledge thoroughgoing, he transmitted it deeply, he worked it into someone else. Form II takes the basic action and makes it intensive, thorough, or causative.

Hold that distinction: Form I = the simple act. Form II = the intensive, thoroughgoing, permanent version.

Now listen to what Allah says:

**كَرَّمْنَا** — *karramnā*

The root is ك-ر-م (*karama*) — nobility, generosity, honor. In Form I, *karuma* means "was noble" — a description of a state. In Form IV, *akrama* means "honored" — a single act of showing honor. You *akrama* a guest when they visit. It's an event. It has a beginning and an end.

But Allah didn't say *akramnā*. He said **كَرَّمْنَا** — *karramnā*. Form II.

[PAUSE]

Form II of *karama* doesn't mean "We honored." It means **We ennobled thoroughly, permanently, and intensively.** The doubling of that middle letter — the *rā'* becoming *rr* — is not decoration. It is intensification. It is the difference between painting a wall and dyeing the fabric. One sits on the surface. The other goes all the way through.

When Allah used Form II, He was saying: dignity is not something I *did* to you. It is something I **worked into you**. It is not a coat you wear. It is the thread.

And notice the object: **بَنِىٓ ءَادَمَ** — *banī Ādam* — "the children of Adam." Not "the believers." Not "the righteous." Not "the obedient." The *species*. Every child born of Adam — every human being who has ever drawn breath — was *karrama'd* before they uttered their first word. Before they chose anything. Before they earned anything.

This is not a reward. It is a *starting condition*.

[PAUSE]

And then there is the emphasis that frames it. Before the verb even arrives, Arabic deploys its heaviest artillery:

**وَلَقَدْ** — *wa-laqad*

Three particles stacked: *wa* (and), *la* (emphatic lām — "certainly"), *qad* (particle of completion and certainty). This is the most emphatic way Arabic can introduce a past-tense declaration. It is the grammatical equivalent of a judge striking the bench. "We have **most certainly, without any doubt**, ennobled the children of Adam."

Why this level of emphasis? Because the declaration is about to challenge everything the listener assumes about where value comes from. In the world this was revealed into — and in every world since — human beings build hierarchies of worth. This tribe is better. That class is lower. This gender is weaker. That race is less. And Allah opens by slamming the gavel: *wa-laqad*. What I am about to say is not debatable. It is settled. It was settled before you arrived.

[PAUSE]

### II. Four Acts — and the Two That Are Intensified

Now step back and see what Allah does after the initial declaration. He lists four specific acts — four things He has done for the human species — and the *form* of each verb tells you something the words alone cannot.

**كَرَّمْنَا** — We ennobled (Form II — intensive)
**حَمَلْنَـٰهُمْ** — We carried them (Form I — simple)
**رَزَقْنَـٰهُم** — We provided for them (Form I — simple)
**فَضَّلْنَـٰهُمْ** — We preferred them (Form II — intensive)

Notice the pattern: **Intensive → Simple → Simple → Intensive.**

The first and last verbs — the ones that frame everything — are Form II. *Karramna* (ennobled thoroughly) and *faḍḍalnāhum* (preferred thoroughly). The two in the middle — *ḥamalnāhum* (carried) and *razaqnāhum* (provided) — are Form I. Simple, basic, tender.

Why does this matter?

Because the two Form I verbs describe what Allah *does for you*: He carries you across land and sea, He provides you with good things. These are acts of care. They are ongoing. They are nurturing. And Form I captures their gentleness — *ḥamala* is the same verb used for a mother carrying a child in her womb. There is no intensification needed. The simplicity *is* the tenderness.

But the two Form II verbs describe what Allah *made you*: ennobled and preferred. These are not acts of care — they are declarations of *status*. And status requires emphasis. It requires the intensive form. It requires the doubled letter that says: this is not a moment. This is a condition.

The architecture is deliberate: **dignity frames sustenance.** The ennoblement and the preference are the bookends. The carrying and the providing are inside. You are not dignified *because* you were carried and fed. You were carried and fed *because* you are dignified. The form of the verbs tells you which is the cause and which is the effect.

[PAUSE]

And there is something quietly remarkable about *ḥamalnāhum* — "We carried them." Form I. Not Form II (*ḥammalnāhum*), which would mean "We burdened them" or "We loaded them." Allah chose the form that means carrying someone *for their benefit*, not loading cargo onto a vehicle. The same root, two forms — and the difference is the difference between a father carrying his child on his shoulders and a laborer loading bricks onto a truck.

The land and sea — *al-barr wa-l-baḥr* — name the entire physical world. Every surface you have ever traveled on. And on all of it, you were carried. Not as freight. As a child.

[PAUSE]

### III. The Word That Isn't There — *Kathīr* and the Door Left Open

Now we arrive at the most theologically provocative word in the ayah — and it is provocative precisely because of what it *isn't*.

**وَفَضَّلْنَـٰهُمْ عَلَىٰ كَثِيرٍ مِّمَّنْ خَلَقْنَا تَفْضِيلًا**

*wa-faḍḍalnāhum 'alā kathīrin mimman khalaqnā tafḍīlā*

"And We preferred them over **much** of what We have created, with definite preference."

*Kathīr* — much. Many. A large amount.

Not *kull* — all. Not *jamī'* — the entirety. **Much.**

[PAUSE]

Allah — who could have said "over everything We created" — deliberately said "over much of what We created." And that single word has generated one of the longest-running conversations in Islamic scholarship.

What is excluded? Over whom are humans *not* preferred?

The classical scholars approached this like a composite sketch, each adding a layer:

Al-Tabari reports that some scholars read *kathīr* as meaning the preference is over the majority of creation — animals, plants, minerals, most beings — but that the angels are excluded from this preference. The human being, despite their dignity, was not declared superior to the angels.

Others, including readings attributed to Ibn 'Abbas, argued that humans *are* preferred even over the angels — and that *kathīr* is used here not to limit the scope but as a rhetorical understatement, the way you might say "he has done *much* good" when you mean "his goodness is extraordinary."

And a third reading: *kathīr* is precise and deliberate, and it means exactly what it says — humans are preferred over *much* of creation, and the question of what is excluded is left intentionally open. Allah closed the door on the *existence* of human dignity (that is settled by *wa-laqad*) but left the door open on its *scope*. You know you are honored. You do not know if you are the *most* honored. And that uncertainty is itself a message.

Here's what's remarkable: all three readings converge on the same practical conclusion. Whether *kathīr* excludes the angels, or is rhetorical, or is deliberately ambiguous — the effect is the same. **You were told enough to be grateful. You were not told enough to be arrogant.** The word *kathīr* is the guardrail that prevents dignity from becoming entitlement.

[PAUSE]

And then — as if to remove any remaining doubt that the *preference* is real — Allah adds the cognate accusative:

**تَفْضِيلًا** — *tafḍīlā*

This is the verbal noun of Form II *faḍḍala*, placed in the accusative as a *maf'ūl muṭlaq* — an absolute object. In Arabic grammar, when you repeat the root of the verb as a noun after it, you are doing something that has no clean equivalent in English. You are saying: "We preferred them... **with real, genuine, unmetaphorical preferring.**"

It is the grammatical equivalent of signing a document, then stamping it, then notarizing it. *Tafḍīlā* removes all ambiguity. It prevents any future reader from softening *faḍḍalnāhum* into something figurative. The preferring is absolute. The dignity is not a metaphor.

[PAUSE]

The construction holds two truths simultaneously: *kathīr* prevents arrogance. *Tafḍīlā* prevents self-contempt. You are not above everything. But your elevation is not imaginary. Both are real. And you need both.

## PART 2: THE THEMATIC DEPTHS

### Theme 1: The Dignity You Didn't Earn

There is a question that lives at the center of modern psychology, modern politics, and every late-night conversation about self-worth: **Where does your value come from?**

The modern West has two answers, and both are exhausting.

The first: your value comes from what you *achieve*. Your productivity, your output, your contribution. This is meritocracy — and its gift is motivation, but its curse is that it makes rest feel like failure. If your value is performance, then every unproductive day is an existential threat. The moment you stop performing, you stop mattering.

The second: your value is *inherent* and you should simply affirm it. Self-love. Self-acceptance. You are enough. This is the therapeutic corrective — and its gift is relief, but its weakness is that it has no *ground*. Telling yourself you have worth without being able to say *why* is like telling yourself the bridge is safe without checking the foundations. The affirmation works until 3 AM, when the voice asks: but why? Says who?

This ayah answers the 3 AM question.

**كَرَّمْنَا بَنِىٓ ءَادَمَ** — "We ennobled the children of Adam."

Your dignity is not earned. It is not a product of your grades, your salary, your Instagram following, your bench press, or your publication record. It was *karramnā* — worked into the species by God Himself, in the intensive form, before any individual human did anything at all.

But — and this is where the Quranic answer differs from both modern answers — your dignity is not *groundless* either. It is not a self-affirmation floating in air. It is grounded in the act of a specific Being: **نَا** — *nā* — "We." The *nā* suffix on every verb in this ayah is the royal "We" of the Creator. *Karramnā* — **We** ennobled. *Ḥamalnāhum* — **We** carried. *Razaqnāhum* — **We** provided. *Faḍḍalnāhum* — **We** preferred.

The ground of your dignity is not your own opinion of yourself. It is the opinion of the One who made you. And His opinion was delivered with *wa-laqad* — the most emphatic construction in the language. He was not tentative about this.

[PAUSE]

Now let this land where it actually lives.

When you look at someone sleeping on a park bench — matted hair, torn shoes, a smell that makes people cross the street — what does *karramnā banī Ādam* mean? It means *that person* was ennobled by the same Form II verb as the scholar, the king, and the prophet. Their dignity was not forfeited by their circumstances. You cannot un-*karrama* what God *karrama'd*. The intensive form doesn't wear off.

When you look at a baby born with a disability so severe that the world will call them "less" — *karramnā banī Ādam* applies to that child with the same grammatical weight as it applies to every other child of Adam. Because the verb's object is *banī Ādam* — the species — not *al-mu'minīn* (the believers) or *al-ṣāliḥīn* (the righteous) or *al-aṣiḥḥā'* (the healthy). The species. Without exception. Without footnote.

And when you look at *yourself* — on a day when you have failed spectacularly, when the sin you swore you'd never repeat is fresh, when you cannot believe how far you've fallen from what you could have been — *karramnā* is still active. Form II is permanent. It is *worked into you*. You can violate it. You can act beneath it. You can betray every expectation it creates. But you cannot erase it.

That is the difference between karrama and akrama. *Akrama* (Form IV) would mean God honored you — a single act, an event, revocable. *Karrama* (Form II) means God **made dignity your nature**. It is not an award. It is an attribute.

[PAUSE]

### Theme 2: The Weight of *Kathīr* — Dignity Is Not a License

But here is where the ayah does something that the modern self-worth conversation almost never does: **it sets a limit.**

*Kathīr* — "much," not "all."

In a world that tells you to believe you can do anything, be anything, that there are no limits to your potential and no boundaries to your worth — this ayah says: you are honored, and you are not the highest thing that exists.

This is not a deflation. It is a *calibration*.

Think of it this way. Imagine someone hands you a check for ten million dollars. If you believe the check is for ten million, you are grateful, responsible, and probably a little awed. But if they tell you the check is for a hundred billion — that you are the richest person who ever lived, that no one has ever been given more — something shifts. Gratitude is replaced by entitlement. Responsibility is replaced by recklessness. The larger the claim about your worth, the less carefully you tend it.

*Kathīr* is the difference between ten million and a hundred billion. Both are extraordinary. But one calibrates. The other corrupts.

[PAUSE]

And watch how this plays out in human history.

Every atrocity committed by human beings against the planet — every strip-mined mountain, every poisoned river, every species driven to extinction — rests on the assumption that humans are *above all of creation*. If you are the pinnacle, everything below you is a resource. If you are the highest, nothing else has a claim on your restraint.

But *kathīr* says: you are above *much*. Not all. Somewhere in creation, there exist beings — whether angels or other orders of existence — over whom you have not been given superiority. The door is left open. And that open door is the source of *adab* — the beautiful restraint that says: I have been given dignity, and I will carry it with humility, because I was not told I am the greatest thing God ever made.

*Tafḍīlā* gives you confidence. *Kathīr* gives you humility. And you need both in the same breath — which is exactly how this ayah delivers them.

[PAUSE]

Consider what happens when you lose one without the other.

Dignity without humility: the Pharaonic delusion. "I am your lord most high." Every tyrant in human history was someone who received *tafḍīlā* (the real elevation of being human) and deleted *kathīr* (the limit). They took the check and rewrote the amount.

Humility without dignity: the doormat condition. The person who has been told — by an abuser, a culture, a system — that they are worthless. They have internalized *kathīr* as "you are nothing" and lost *tafḍīlā* entirely. They accept mistreatment because they believe they deserve it.

This ayah is medicine for both conditions. For the tyrant: *kathīr* — there are things above you. For the abused: *tafḍīlā* — your elevation is absolute, signed, sealed, notarized by God, and no human being has the authority to revoke it.

[PAUSE]

## CLOSING SYNTHESIS

### The Architecture

Step back and see the four-act structure:

1. **كَرَّمْنَا بَنِىٓ ءَادَمَ** — We ennobled the children of Adam *(Form II — what you ARE)*
2. **حَمَلْنَـٰهُمْ فِى ٱلْبَرِّ وَٱلْبَحْرِ** — We carried them on land and sea *(Form I — how We CARE for you)*
3. **رَزَقْنَـٰهُم مِّنَ ٱلطَّيِّبَـٰتِ** — We provided them good things *(Form I — what We GIVE you)*
4. **فَضَّلْنَـٰهُمْ عَلَىٰ كَثِيرٍ مِّمَّنْ خَلَقْنَا تَفْضِيلًا** — We preferred them over much of creation *(Form II — where you STAND)*

The intensive verbs are the bookends. Dignity at the beginning, elevation at the end. And *inside* those bookends — inside the framework of who you are — sit the tender, simple, Form I verbs of daily care: carried and provided. The structure says: **your daily sustenance is cradled inside your cosmic dignity.** The food you eat, the road you travel — these are not separate from your ennoblement. They are *expressions* of it. You are not fed because you are hungry. You are fed because you are honored.

And notice the surah's context. This declaration sits inside Surah Al-Isra — the surah of the Night Journey. The Prophet ﷺ was *ḥamala'd* (carried) through the heavens in one night. And in this ayah, Allah says: that carrying is what I do for the entire species, every day, on land and sea. The Isra was not an exception. It was a dramatic demonstration of what God does quietly for every child of Adam: He carries them.

### Questions to Carry

- Where in your life are you deriving your sense of worth from *performance* — and what would shift if you accepted that your dignity was settled before you performed anything?

- When you encounter another human being — any human being — do you see *karramnā banī Ādam* on them? Or do you mentally edit the verse to say *karramnā* the people who look, think, and believe like me?

- Have you held both *kathīr* and *tafḍīlā* together? Or have you leaned into one at the expense of the other — either toward arrogance (deleting the limit) or toward self-contempt (forgetting the elevation)?

- What does the Form I verb *ḥamalnāhum* — "We carried them" — mean to you on a day when you feel like you are carrying everything alone?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Allah hammered dignity into the human species with an intensive verb before any human being had done a single thing worth honoring — then said "over much, not all" to ensure the honor would produce gratitude, not arrogance.**

### The Closing Invitation

Carry two things out of this ayah. First: *wa-laqad karramnā banī Ādam* — your dignity is not on the table. It is not negotiable. It was not given to you by any human institution, and it cannot be taken from you by any human institution. It was *karrama'd* — worked into you, by the One who made you.

Second: *kathīr*. Much. Not all. Your dignity comes with a calibration built in. You were honored enough to walk with confidence and humbled enough to walk with care. Both, in the same breath. Both, in the same ayah. Both, in the same life.

### Du'a

*Allāhumma, You ennobled us with a verb that means You made dignity our nature — not a reward for what we've done, but a gift woven into what we are. Help us live at the height of that karamah without forgetting the kathīr that keeps us honest. And on the days when we forget our worth, remind us: it was You who said wa-laqad. It was You who was certain. Even when we are not. Āmīn.*
