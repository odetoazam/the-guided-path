# Guided by Allah, Raised From Fragments

## Surah Al-Isra (17:97-99)

## Introduction

Allah has just been named enough as witness between the Messenger and the deniers.

Now the surah shows why His witness is not merely a comfort for the Prophet in this life. It is the beginning of the final sorting of reality. If Allah guides someone, that person is truly guided. If Allah lets someone go astray, no protecting allies can be found for them besides Him. The dispute is not a debate floating forever in history. It is moving toward resurrection.

And the resurrection described here is terrifyingly embodied.

Those who resisted guidance are gathered on their faces: blind, dumb, and deaf. The faculties they misused or refused in this life become a scene of humiliation in the next. Their refuge is Jahannam. Whenever it subsides, Allah increases them in blazing fire.

Then the Qur'an tells us why: they disbelieved in Allah's signs and mocked bodily resurrection. When we are bones and crumbled fragments, will we really be resurrected as a new creation?

The answer is not complicated. Do they not see that Allah, the One who created the heavens and the earth, is able to create the like of them?

The argument moves from the smallest fragments of the human body to the largest architecture of the visible cosmos. If Allah created the heavens and the earth, why would recreating human beings be impossible for Him? The objection shrinks resurrection by staring at bones and fragments while forgetting the Creator who brought skies and earth into being.

Classical tafsir reads the passage along these lines. Al-Tabari explains that true guidance belongs to whomever Allah guides, and the one Allah misguides has no helpers besides Him. He reads the gathering on faces as real humiliation and punishment, and the resurrection objection as denial after disbelief in Allah's signs. Ibn Kathir connects the passage to Allah's power over resurrection, noting that the One who created the heavens and earth is able to recreate human beings. Al-Muyassar states the whole movement clearly: whoever Allah guides is guided, whoever He misguides has no supporter, and those who denied resurrection will be gathered in disgrace and punished, while the Creator of the heavens and earth is certainly able to create their like.

There does not appear to be a distinct asbab al-nuzul report for these ayahs as an isolated incident. Their immediate context supplies the frame: after demanded signs, objection to human messengerhood, and Allah's witness, the surah turns to the final consequence of refusing guidance and denying resurrection.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. `Wa-man yahdi Allahu fa-huwa al-muhtad`: Guidance as Reality, Not Appearance

The passage begins with a conditional sentence: whoever Allah guides, he is the guided one.

The verb `yahdi` comes from the root of guidance, direction, and leading to the way. The noun `al-muhtad` is the one who has received guidance and is rightly guided. The structure is strong because it does not say merely, "Whoever Allah guides has guidance." It says: he is the guided one. The definite form gives a sense of true, actual guidance.

This matters after the previous arguments. The opponents thought they could judge the Messenger's truth by their conditions. They wanted to decide what proof should look like. But now Allah relocates guidance where it belongs. Guidance is not finally owned by the audience, the crowd, the demanders, or even the caller. Whoever Allah guides is guided.

Al-Tabari reads this as a statement that the one Allah enables and directs to faith is the one truly rightly guided. Al-Muyassar says whoever Allah guides to faith and following His Messenger is rightly guided. Ibn Kathir places similar statements under Allah's exclusive control over guidance and misguidance.

This does not cancel human responsibility. The same Qur'an calls, warns, commands, and blames people for refusal. But it teaches us that guidance is a divine gift, not a self-manufactured achievement. The guided person should be grateful before being confident. If Allah guided you, you did not rescue yourself from darkness by your own brilliance.

[PAUSE]

Guidance is not a trophy the ego owns. It is a gift the servant must guard with gratitude.

### 2. `Wa-man yudlil fa-lan tajida lahum awliya'a`: No Protectors Outside Allah

The second half mirrors the first: whoever He lets go astray, you will never find for them protectors besides Him.

`Yudlil` comes from the root of straying, losing the path, being misled or left in misguidance. The ayah then shifts address: `fa-lan tajida`, you will never find. The Prophet is addressed, but the lesson reaches every listener. Search wherever you want: you will not find for them `awliya'` besides Him.

`Awliya'` are protectors, patrons, allies, guardians, those relied upon for support. The plural matters. It imagines many possible supports: tribes, idols, leaders, arguments, wealth, social consensus, false gods, intellectual defenses, political power. None can protect a person whom Allah has left in misguidance.

This is deeply connected to the previous ayah, where Allah is enough as witness. If Allah witnesses a person's rejection and leaves him to the path he insisted on, no other witness or ally can overturn that reality. The person may be surrounded by supporters and still have no wali in the only sense that matters.

The Qur'an echoes this often. In 7:186, whoever Allah misguides has no guide, and He leaves them wandering in their transgression. In 39:36-37, Allah is not enough for His servant? Whoever Allah misguides has no guide, and whoever Allah guides has no one to mislead. These verses keep human confidence from attaching to false supports.

The lived application is stark. We should fear being admired while lost, defended while wrong, surrounded while abandoned by guidance. The crowd can protect reputation. It cannot protect the soul from Allah.

### 3. `Nahshuruhum ... 'ala wujuhihim`: The Gathering on Faces

The verse then turns from guidance in this life to gathering on the Day of Resurrection: We will gather them on their faces.

`Nahshuruhum` comes from the root of gathering, assembling, driving together for the final assembly. `Yawm al-qiyamah` is the Day of Standing, the Day when creation is raised and made to stand before Allah.

But here the gathering is described with an image of humiliation: `ala wujuhihim`, on their faces.

The face is the place of dignity, recognition, and direction. In this life, a person walks upright, sees forward, speaks, hears, chooses direction. To be gathered on the face is to have dignity overturned. The one who refused the path is now brought in a posture that itself announces reversal.

Classical tafsir affirms the reality of this gathering. Al-Tabari treats it as part of their punishment and humiliation. Ibn Kathir cites reports explaining that the One who made them walk on their feet is able to make them move on their faces. The point is not to make the image metaphorical because it is strange. The point is to let the strangeness show Allah's power and the severity of the reversal.

This also connects to the earlier blindness motif in Al-Isra. In 17:72, whoever is blind in this life will be blind in the Hereafter and more astray in path. Now the blindness becomes part of a cluster: blind, dumb, and deaf, gathered on faces.

The one who would not walk by guidance is gathered in a form that shows what refusal really was.

### 4. `Umyan wa-bukman wa-summa`: Faculties Refused, Faculties Stripped

The gathered are described as blind, dumb, and deaf.

These are not random conditions. Sight, speech, and hearing are the very faculties by which signs are encountered, truth is confessed, and guidance is received. The Qur'an was heard. Signs were seen. The tongue could have testified. But denial turned these faculties into instruments of refusal.

So the Hereafter displays the inner truth outwardly. The blindness was already present as refusal of guidance. The deafness was already present as refusal to listen. The muteness was already present as refusal to confess truth. On the Day of Resurrection, what was spiritual becomes visible.

This is a Qur'anic pattern. In 2:18, the hypocritical condition is described as deaf, dumb, and blind, so they do not return. In 22:46, the Qur'an says it is not the eyes that go blind, but the hearts in the breasts that go blind. In 20:124-126, the one who turns away from Allah's reminder is raised blind and asks why, and Allah answers that His signs came and were forgotten, so that person is forgotten that Day.

Al-Muyassar reads the description as humiliation and recompense. Al-Tabari ties it to their condition on the Day. The language shows that Hell is not arbitrary. It reveals what a person chose to become in relation to Allah's signs.

For us, the warning is immediate. Do not measure your faculties only by biological function. Ask whether your hearing receives truth, whether your sight recognizes signs, whether your tongue confesses what Allah loves. A person can have working eyes and a blind heart. Working ears and no listening. Fluent speech and no truthful testimony.

### 5. `Kullama khabat zidnahum sa'ira`: A Fire That Does Not Burn Out

The verse says their refuge is Jahannam. Whenever it subsides, We increase them in blazing fire.

`Ma'wahum` is their refuge, abode, place of return. The word can be used for a place one goes back to, but here it is terrifying: their place of return is Jahannam.

Then comes the ongoing punishment: `kullama khabat`, whenever it subsides, weakens, or dies down, `zidnahum sa'ira`, We increase them in blazing fire. `Sa'ir` carries the sense of kindled blaze, a fire stirred into intensity.

The image crushes the false hope that punishment will simply burn itself out. The fire's subsiding does not become relief. It becomes the occasion of increase. This is a severe answer to those who treated resurrection as ridiculous. The Day they denied is not only real; its consequence is sustained by Allah's will.

This should be spoken with gravity, not sensationalism. The Qur'an does not mention Hell so that believers become cruel. It mentions Hell so that hearts wake before the return is sealed. Warning is mercy before it becomes judgment.

Ibn Kathir and Al-Tabari both treat the increase as part of the punishment: whenever the flame lessens, it is intensified. Al-Muyassar similarly states that whenever the fire becomes calm, Allah increases its blaze.

The warnings of Hell are mercy now because Hell itself is not a metaphor later.

### 6. `Izaman wa-rufatan`: Bones and Crumbled Fragments

Verse 98 explains their recompense: they disbelieved in Allah's signs and said, when we are bones and `rufat`, will we really be resurrected as a new creation?

The word `izaman` means bones. It pictures the body after flesh has gone, after life has visibly departed. `Rufat` carries the sense of crumbled fragments, decayed remains, dust-like brokenness. The objection stares at decomposition and treats it as proof against resurrection.

The question is emotionally powerful because every human being knows the body decays. The denier uses the visible fact of decay to mock the unseen promise of re-creation. But the Qur'an does not deny decay. It quotes it. Yes, bones. Yes, fragments. The issue is not whether the body breaks down. The issue is whether breakdown limits the Creator.

This same objection appears repeatedly in the Qur'an: in 36:78, the person brings an example and forgets his own creation, saying who will give life to bones when they are decayed. The answer: the One who produced them the first time will give them life. In 75:3-4, man thinks Allah will not gather his bones, but Allah is able even to proportion his fingertips.

Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir both read this as denial of resurrection after becoming bones and dust. The Qur'an's response is consistent: first creation is proof of re-creation.

### 7. `Awalam yaraw ... khalaqa al-samawati wa-al-ard`: The Cosmos Answers the Grave

Verse 99 answers: do they not see that Allah, who created the heavens and the earth, is able to create the like of them?

The answer moves the gaze from bones to cosmos.

`Awalam yaraw` asks: have they not seen? The evidence is not hidden. The heavens and the earth stand over and beneath them. The One who created that vast reality is `qadir`, able, powerful, fully capable, to create their like.

This is one of the Qur'an's most repeated resurrection arguments. The greater creation points to the lesser. In 40:57, the creation of the heavens and the earth is greater than the creation of mankind, but most people do not know. In 46:33, the One who created the heavens and the earth and was not wearied by their creation is able to give life to the dead. The logic is simple and devastating: the One who originated the greater is not unable to repeat the smaller.

The phrase `mithlahum`, the like of them, can be read as creating them again or creating their likeness in resurrection. The point is divine ability. Their fragments do not escape Allah's knowledge or power.

This ayah also includes time: He appointed for them an `ajal`, a term, in which there is no doubt. The denier thinks decay is open-ended disappearance. Allah says there is an appointed term. Resurrection is not vague possibility. It is scheduled certainty.

Then the passage ends with refusal again: the wrongdoers refused except kufur. This echoes 17:89, where most people refused except kufur after the Qur'an diversified every example. The surah is deliberately showing the same disease at a later stage: they refused the examples, demanded signs, objected to human messengerhood, and now refuse resurrection.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Guidance Is a Gift, So Gratitude Must Guard It

The first principle is this: the guided person should be more grateful than self-assured.

The passage begins by saying whoever Allah guides is the guided one. This sentence should shake arrogance out of religious identity. If you pray, believe, recite, repent, or find your heart moved by an ayah, that is not merely a personality trait. It is guidance from Allah.

We often speak as if guidance is self-generated. "I found the truth." "I figured it out." "I am practicing because I am serious." There may be effort in all of that, and effort matters. But beneath the effort is Allah opening the way. Many people hear the same Qur'an and remain unmoved. Many see signs and look away. Many are intelligent and lost. Many are socially religious and spiritually asleep. If Allah guides you, then you are guided.

Al-Tabari and Al-Muyassar both keep the statement direct: guidance belongs to Allah's guiding. Ibn Kathir's broader tafsir on guidance and misguidance repeatedly reminds us that Allah guides whom He wills with justice and wisdom. This does not make us passive. It makes us dependent.

This dependence should produce two things.

First, gratitude. Every prayer is a gift. Every moment of tawbah is a gift. Every time an ayah hurts in the right way, that is a gift. Every time you recognize a sin before death, that is a gift. A guided person should say alhamdulillah with the urgency of someone rescued from a path he could have loved.

Second, fear of losing guidance. The ayah immediately says that whoever Allah lets go astray has no protectors besides Him. No social identity can protect you. No reputation for piety can protect you. No past sincerity can be treated as a permanent guarantee if the heart becomes arrogant and careless. The believer asks for firmness because guidance is held by Allah.

This is why Al-Fatihah keeps us begging: guide us to the straight path. The most repeated du'a in the Muslim life is not "thank You that we already own guidance." It is a daily request for guidance, because we are always in need of being led, kept, corrected, and returned.

The guided heart is not the heart that stopped needing Allah. It is the heart that knows it needs Him for every step.

### Theme 2: The Hereafter Reveals the Inner Shape of Choices

The second principle is this: the Hereafter does not merely punish; it reveals.

Verse 97 describes people gathered on their faces, blind, dumb, and deaf. This is terrifying, but it is not random. The people being described disbelieved in Allah's signs and denied resurrection. They refused to see, hear, and confess truth in this life. In the next life, their inward condition appears outwardly.

This should change how we think about sin. Sin is not only an isolated act placed on a scale. It shapes the self. Repeated refusal forms a posture. Arrogance trains the face away from the ground of surrender. Mockery trains the tongue away from truth. Selective listening trains the ear away from guidance. Looking without taking signs seriously trains sight away from recognition.

The Qur'an often shows this continuity. The one blind here is blind there. The one who forgot Allah's signs is forgotten in the sense of being left to consequence. The one who loved false supports finds no protectors besides Allah. The inner life does not vanish at death. It is disclosed.

Al-Tabari's tafsir on these verses treats the gathering as humiliation and recompense. Al-Muyassar names it as the consequence of denial. Ibn Kathir emphasizes Allah's ability to gather them on their faces, just as He made them walk on feet. The descriptions should not be softened into vague metaphor. They are part of the Qur'an's warning that the body of the Hereafter will tell the truth about the soul's choices.

This has practical force. If I keep refusing to listen to reminders, what kind of hearing am I training? If I keep using my tongue to mock believers, belittle guidance, or defend sin, what kind of speech am I training? If I keep looking at signs and turning them into entertainment or data without surrender, what kind of sight am I training?

The mercy is that we can ask these questions now. In dunya, faculties can still be turned back. The ear can listen. The tongue can repent. The face can go to sujood. The eyes can weep. The body can learn another posture before the Day when posture becomes consequence.

[PAUSE]

The Hereafter will make visible what the heart practiced becoming.

### Theme 3: Denial of Resurrection Often Begins With a Failure of Scale

The third principle is this: resurrection becomes unbelievable when we stare at fragments and forget the Creator.

The deniers say: when we are bones and crumbled fragments, will we really be resurrected as a new creation? Their question has emotional force because decay is visible. We have seen graves. We know bodies weaken. We know flesh returns to earth. We know bones dry and break. The Qur'an does not deny any of that.

But the denier makes a mistake of scale. He treats the difficulty of resurrection as if the fragments must reassemble themselves. He imagines bones and dust as independent realities outside Allah's power. The Qur'an answers by expanding the field of vision: look at the heavens and the earth.

If Allah created the heavens and earth, then human resurrection is not a strain on Him. The problem is not logic. The problem is that the heart has forgotten first creation. The one who was nothing, then became a living human being with hearing, sight, thought, longing, memory, and moral awareness, now looks at future dust and says, "Impossible."

This is why the Qur'an repeatedly says man forgets his own creation. In Ya-Sin, the denier brings decayed bones as an argument and forgets that he himself was created. In Al-Qiyamah, Allah says He can proportion even fingertips. In Al-Waqi'ah, Allah asks whether we created ourselves or He is the Creator. Resurrection arguments in the Qur'an are not abstract puzzles. They are reminders against forgetfulness.

Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir both identify verse 99 as the answer to the resurrection objection: the Creator of heavens and earth is able to create their like. Al-Muyassar states the same: is not the One who created the heavens and the earth able to resurrect them after death?

Modern people may not mock resurrection with the exact phrase "bones and fragments," but the failure of scale remains. We treat death as final because we see the body. We treat material processes as ultimate because we can measure them. We forget that the measurable world itself is created. The question is not whether dust can make itself alive. The question is whether Allah can raise what He created.

The grave looks final only when the heavens are no longer read as signs.

### Theme 4: The Appointed Term Turns Delay Into Accountability

The fourth principle is this: Allah's delay is not absence; it is an appointed term.

Verse 99 does not only say Allah is able to create the like of them. It also says He has appointed for them an `ajal` in which there is no doubt. That phrase changes the emotional meaning of time.

Human beings often misread delay. When punishment does not arrive immediately, the denier thinks the warning was empty. When resurrection does not happen within the horizon of his own lifetime, he treats it as distant myth. When Allah gives years after years, health after sin, provision after mockery, and another morning after another refusal, the heedless person mistakes mercy and reprieve for proof that no reckoning is coming.

The Qur'an says: appointed term.

An `ajal` is not vague postponement. It is a fixed endpoint known to Allah. A person's death has an ajal. Nations have ajal. The world itself moves toward an appointed Hour. In Al-A'raf 7:34, every nation has a term, and when their term comes, they cannot delay it by an hour or advance it. In Yunus 10:49, every nation has an appointed term, and when it arrives, there is no delay or advance. This means time is not empty space in which consequences disappear. Time is a measured corridor leading to a meeting.

Al-Tabari and Al-Muyassar both read this part of the ayah as Allah appointing for them a fixed time for death, resurrection, or recompense that contains no doubt. Ibn Kathir's resurrection framing keeps the same certainty: Allah's power is established, and the appointed return cannot be escaped.

This has direct spiritual application. We live inside delay. The person who wrongs others may not be exposed today. The one who mocks resurrection may continue laughing. The believer who obeys may not see vindication quickly. The body may enter the grave and seem lost to the earth. But none of this means the story is ungoverned. It means the appointed term has not arrived yet.

The phrase `la rayba fih`, no doubt in it, is important because doubt often grows in the space between warning and fulfillment. The longer something is delayed, the more the nafs whispers that it may never come. Allah closes that door. The term is appointed, and there is no doubt in it.

For the believer, this produces patience and urgency together. Patience, because Allah's timing is not random. Urgency, because appointed terms do arrive. There is no need to panic as if Allah forgot, and no room to procrastinate as if Allah's delay is permission to remain unchanged.

[PAUSE]

Every delayed consequence is still traveling toward its appointed term.

### Theme 5: Refusal Reappears Until It Is Repented From

A fifth principle is this: a spiritual disease untreated will keep reappearing in new arguments.

The passage ends: the wrongdoers refused except kufur. This wording deliberately echoes 17:89, where Allah said He had diversified in this Qur'an every kind of example, but most people refused except kufur. Between 17:89 and 17:99, several arguments have passed: demanded signs, objection to human messengerhood, Allah's witness, guidance and misguidance, resurrection, Hell, bones and fragments, cosmic creation, appointed term. Yet the same root disease returns: refusal.

This repetition is not accidental. The Qur'an is showing us that if the heart's posture is refusal, it can survive many topics. Refusal can object to the Qur'an's form, then object to the Messenger's humanity, then object to resurrection, then object to accountability. The surface issue changes. The inner posture remains.

This is painfully relevant. A person may think, "If this one question is answered, I will surrender." Sometimes that is sincere. But sometimes after one question is answered, another condition appears, then another, then another. The issue was never only the question. The issue was a heart protecting itself from the cost of truth.

Al-Muyassar's tafsir of 17:89 and 17:99 uses similar language of denial and rejection. Al-Tabari likewise frames both endings around refusing truth and denying Allah's proofs. The surah itself is teaching us to trace repeated objections back to repeated refusal.

This does not mean we should become suspicious of everyone with questions. The Qur'an dignifies sincere seeking. But it does mean we should examine patterns in ourselves. Do I keep changing the reason I am not obeying? First I say the evidence is unclear. Then I say the messenger is too human. Then I say resurrection is too strange. Then I say time is too long. Then I say most people do not live this way. At what point do I admit the issue may be refusal?

The mercy is that refusal can be repented from. The same will that says no can be humbled into saying yes. The same tongue that mocked resurrection can ask Allah for certainty. The same eyes that looked at bones and forgot the heavens can look at the heavens and remember the bones will rise.

So the closing phrase is not only a verdict on them. It is a mirror for us.

If the same refusal keeps wearing new clothes, repentance must go deeper than answering one question.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of Guidance, Reversal, and Re-Creation

The passage begins with guidance: whoever Allah guides is truly guided, and whoever He lets go astray has no protectors besides Him. That sets the spiritual law.

Then it moves to resurrection: those who refused are gathered on their faces, blind, dumb, and deaf, with Jahannam as their abode and the fire intensified whenever it subsides. That shows the embodied consequence.

Then it gives the reason: they disbelieved in Allah's signs and mocked resurrection after becoming bones and fragments. That exposes the argument beneath the punishment.

Finally, it answers: the Creator of the heavens and the earth is able to create their like, and He has appointed a term without doubt, but the wrongdoers refuse except disbelief.

So the architecture moves from Allah's guidance to human refusal, from refusal to bodily humiliation, from bodily decay to cosmic creation, and from cosmic proof back to the same disease of kufur.

### Questions to Carry

Do I treat guidance as a possession or as a gift I must keep asking Allah to preserve?

If Allah guides, we are guided. That should make us grateful, not proud; vigilant, not complacent.

Which faculty am I training: hearing that receives truth, or hearing that filters it away?

The Day will reveal what our faculties practiced. Listening to guidance now is mercy before hearing itself becomes a witness.

Where do I look at fragments in my life and forget the Creator of the heavens and earth?

Resurrection is the greatest example, but the pattern appears elsewhere. We see broken pieces and forget Allah's power to create, restore, and bring forth.

Do I secretly rely on protectors besides Allah to keep me safe while resisting guidance?

Reputation, people, money, cleverness, and identity cannot protect the soul if Allah leaves it to misguidance.

What would change if I remembered that the term appointed for me has no doubt in it?

Death and resurrection are not vague religious ideas. Allah has appointed an ajal, and the heart that remembers this lives differently.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The One who guides is the only true guide, the One who gathers deniers can expose every refused faculty, and the One who created the heavens and earth can raise bones and fragments as a new creation.**

### The Closing Invitation

Ask Allah for guidance like someone who knows he cannot guide himself. Use your eyes, ears, tongue, face, and body before they testify against you or are transformed into the shape of what you refused. When death makes the body look like fragments, lift your gaze to the heavens and earth and remember the Creator who began everything without strain and appointed a term without doubt.

### Du'a

O Allah, guide us and make us among the truly guided.

Do not leave us to misguidance or to protectors that cannot protect us from You.

Make our hearing, sight, speech, faces, and bodies servants of Your guidance.

Protect us from denying resurrection through forgetfulness of Your first creation.

Raise us on the Day of Resurrection with light, dignity, iman, and Your mercy.

Amin.
