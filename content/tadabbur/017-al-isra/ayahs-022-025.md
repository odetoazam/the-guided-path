# Do Not Sit Blamed and Forsaken: Tawhid, Parents, and the Humility of Mercy

## Surah Al-Isra (17:22-25)

[ayah:17:22] لَّا تَجْعَلْ مَعَ ٱللَّهِ إِلَـٰهًا ءَاخَرَ فَتَقْعُدَ مَذْمُومًا مَّخْذُولًا

[ayah:17:23] ۞ وَقَضَىٰ رَبُّكَ أَلَّا تَعْبُدُوٓا۟ إِلَّآ إِيَّاهُ وَبِٱلْوَٰلِدَيْنِ إِحْسَـٰنًا ۚ إِمَّا يَبْلُغَنَّ عِندَكَ ٱلْكِبَرَ أَحَدُهُمَآ أَوْ كِلَاهُمَا فَلَا تَقُل لَّهُمَآ أُفٍّ وَلَا تَنْهَرْهُمَا وَقُل لَّهُمَا قَوْلًا كَرِيمًا

[ayah:17:24] وَٱخْفِضْ لَهُمَا جَنَاحَ ٱلذُّلِّ مِنَ ٱلرَّحْمَةِ وَقُل رَّبِّ ٱرْحَمْهُمَا كَمَا رَبَّيَانِى صَغِيرًا

*La taj'al ma'a Allahi ilahan akhara fataq'uda madhmuman makhdhula.*

*Wa qada rabbuka alla ta'budu illa iyyahu wa bi-al-walidayni ihsana. Imma yablughanna 'indaka al-kibara ahaduhuma aw kilahuma fala taqul lahuma uffin wa la tanharhuma wa qul lahuma qawlan karima.*

**Do not set up with Allah another god, lest you sit blamed and forsaken. And your Lord has decreed that you worship none but Him, and that you show excellence to parents. If you are righteous, then surely He has always been forgiving toward those who repeatedly return.**

## Introduction

The surah has been teaching us how to read a life.

What do you seek?

What are you attached to?

What kind of book are you writing for yourself?

Then suddenly it gives one of the most concentrated moral summaries in the Quran.

[PAUSE]

But notice how it begins. It does not begin with family sentiment. It begins with tawhid.

Do not set another god beside Allah. If you do, you will sit `madhmum` and `makhdhul`, blamed and forsaken. Then immediately after that comes the command of exclusive worship and excellence to parents. That pairing is not accidental. The Quran is teaching us that how we stand before Allah determines how we stand before the two human beings through whom Allah brought us into the world.

And the passage is exquisitely concrete. Do not say `uff`. Do not snap. Speak nobly. Lower the wing. Pray for them. Then, just when the soul may panic under the weight of such tenderness, Allah says: your Lord knows what is in your inner selves, and He is forgiving toward the `awwabin`, the repeatedly returning.

The hook of the passage is this: **Al-Isra teaches that tawhid is the foundation of all right human relations, and that one of the first tests of whether worship has softened the heart is the way it speaks, bends, remembers, and prays in the presence of aging parents.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Fataq'uda madhmuman makhdhula**: Shirk Leaves the Human Being Morally Exposed and Spiritually Unprotected

The opening warning says:

**la taj'al ma'a Allahi ilahan akhara**

Do not place another god alongside Allah.

Then:

**fataq'uda madhmuman makhdhula**

so that you sit blamed and forsaken.

[PAUSE]

The image of `taq'uda`, sitting, is powerful. It suggests a final state, a reduced condition, one in which a person comes to rest in humiliation rather than strength. `Madhmum` means blameworthy, deserving censure. `Makhdhul` means left without support, abandoned, not helped.

This is what shirk does. It promises reinforcement and leaves the soul unsupported. It promises more allies and leaves the servant forsaken. It offers expanded belonging and ends in moral exposure.

That opening matters because everything after it must be read in that light. The commands that follow are not social niceties floating free. They are the fruit of exclusive worship.

### 2. **Wa bi-al-walidayni ihsana**: After Tawhid, the Quran Places Beauty of Conduct at the Door of the Parents

Then comes one of the Quran's most important pairings:

**wa qada rabbuka alla ta'budu illa iyyahu wa bi-al-walidayni ihsana**

Your Lord has decreed that you worship none but Him, and that you show ihsan to parents.

[PAUSE]

Not mere fairness. Not mere formal duty. `Ihsan`.

That word carries beauty, excellence, more than the minimum, care beyond transaction. The Quran does not frame parents here merely as people whose rights you grudgingly discharge. It frames them as recipients of `ihsan`.

Then the command narrows into the most difficult moment:

**imma yablughanna 'indaka al-kibara**

if one or both of them reach old age with you.

That phrase `indaka`, with you, matters deeply. Their weakness is no longer theoretical. It is in your space, your time, your fatigue, your routines, your impatience.

Then the verse goes all the way down to the smallest audible fracture:

**fala taqul lahuma uffin**

Do not say to them even `uff`.

The Quran starts where irritation first leaks into sound. Then:

**wa la tanharhuma**

do not scold them, do not drive them back harshly.

Then it lifts the standard:

**wa qul lahuma qawlan karima**

speak to them a noble word.

It is not enough to avoid abuse. Speech itself must become noble.

### 3. **Ikhfid lahuma janaha al-dhulli mina al-rahmah**: Mercy Must Lower Itself

Then the image becomes even more beautiful:

**wa ikhfid lahuma janaha al-dhulli mina al-rahmah**

Lower for them the wing of humility out of mercy.

[PAUSE]

This is not humiliation born of resentment. It is humility chosen out of `rahmah`. The metaphor of lowering the wing carries tenderness, nearness, protection, and voluntary gentleness. It is strength deliberately bending.

Then the du'a:

**rabbi irhamhuma kama rabbayani saghira**

My Lord, have mercy on them as they raised me when I was small.

The verse teaches memory as worship. Remember who bent over you when you were unable. Remember who carried your weakness before you learned to resent theirs.

Then comes the inner correction:

**rabbukum a'lamu bima fi nufusikum**

Your Lord knows best what is within yourselves.

And if you are righteous:

**fa-innahu kana li-al-awwabina ghafura**

He has always been forgiving toward the repeatedly returning.

The word `awwab` is full of hope. It is not the person who never strains, never slips, never feels the tightness of human limitation. It is the one who keeps coming back.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Ihsan to Parents Is One of the Earliest Social Proofs of a Heart Trained by Tawhid

It is easy to speak beautifully about worship in the abstract.

It is much harder to remain beautiful in the room where you are repeatedly inconvenienced, repeated stories are told, frailty slows everything down, and the people before you are no longer the strong figures you remember.

[PAUSE]

That is why this passage is so searching. It teaches that some of the truest evidence of tawhid appears not only in prayer rows and public piety, but in domestic tone, facial tension, word choice, patience, body language, and hidden du'a.

If Allah alone is your Lord, then you learn not to worship your own convenience.

If Allah alone is your Lord, then the people who raised you are not experienced merely as interruptions to your schedule.

If Allah alone is your Lord, then `uff` itself starts to feel spiritually revealing.

The ayah is not denying that caring for parents can be emotionally complex, especially where there has been pain, absence, or difficulty. But it is refusing the idea that irritation is morally neutral. It is telling us that in this arena, even the smallest leak of contempt matters.

### Theme 2: The Quran Balances Lofty Mercy With Honest Knowledge of the Inner Self

These verses are very demanding, but they are not naive.

Allah says:

**rabbukum a'lamu bima fi nufusikum**

Your Lord knows what is in your inner selves.

[PAUSE]

That means He knows the tenderness. He also knows the exhaustion.

He knows the child who wants to do right but struggles.

He knows the one carrying complicated history.

He knows the one who fails, repents, corrects himself, and comes back.

And that is why the door of `al-awwabin` is so important. The standard is not lowered, but mercy is kept open. The servant is not told, "If you ever feel strain, you are false." He is told, "Return. Return again. Become one who repeatedly comes back."

This is one of the mercies of the Quranic ethic. It commands beauty, then opens tawbah for the sincere struggler seeking that beauty.

## Closing Synthesis

This passage begins with the worst relational crime and moves to one of the most intimate relational duties.

Do not set another god beside Allah.

Then: worship Him alone, and show ihsan to your parents.

[PAUSE]

So the moral architecture is clear. Tawhid is not isolated transcendence. It is the source from which tenderness must flow.

And one of the first places that tenderness is tested is not in public acclaim but in private aging.

Can you restrain the `uff`?

Can you refuse the harsh rebuke?

Can you choose the noble word?

Can you lower the wing?

Can you remember the years when they lowered themselves toward your smallness?

Can you still pray for mercy?

[PAUSE]

And if you cannot do it perfectly every time, the verse leaves you with a path:

Become of the `awwabin`.

Keep returning.

Because the Lord who asked this of you knows what is in your nafs better than you do, and He has always been forgiving toward those who turn back to Him again and again.
