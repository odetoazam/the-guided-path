# Until You Meet Our Terms

## Surah Al-Isra (17:90-93)

[ayah:17:90] وَقَالُوا۟ لَن نُّؤْمِنَ لَكَ حَتَّىٰ تَفْجُرَ لَنَا مِنَ ٱلْأَرْضِ يَنۢبُوعًا

[ayah:17:91] أَوْ تَكُونَ لَكَ جَنَّةٌ مِّن نَّخِيلٍ وَعِنَبٍ فَتُفَجِّرَ ٱلْأَنْهَـٰرَ خِلَـٰلَهَا تَفْجِيرًا

[ayah:17:92] أَوْ تُسْقِطَ ٱلسَّمَآءَ كَمَا زَعَمْتَ عَلَيْنَا كِسَفًا أَوْ تَأْتِىَ بِٱللَّهِ وَٱلْمَلَـٰٓئِكَةِ قَبِيلًا

## Introduction

The previous ayah told us that Allah has diversified every kind of example for people in this Qur'an, yet most people refused except disbelief.

Now the refusal begins to speak.

It does not say, "We have listened to the Qur'an and found its guidance insufficient." It does not say, "We are ready to follow truth wherever it leads." It does not even ask one sincere question and wait for the answer. It builds a list. A spring from the earth. A private garden with date-palms and grapes. Rivers gushing through it. The sky falling in pieces. Allah and the angels brought face to face. A house of ornament. An ascent into the sky. Then, even after ascent, a book brought down that they can read.

This is not one request. It is a moving horizon.

Al-Isra has already prepared us for this. In 17:59, Allah explained that demanded signs were not withheld because He lacked power. Earlier peoples asked for signs, received them, denied them, and were destroyed. The she-camel of Thamud was a visible sign, and they wronged her. Signs, when demanded from a heart already committed to refusal, do not merely add information. They add accountability.

Classical tafsir records reports around these demands. Ibn Kathir cites narrations that leaders of Quraysh gathered and asked the Prophet for spectacular signs: to move mountains, make rivers flow, revive ancestors, bring angels, or bring Allah before them, and similar demands. Al-Tabari also reports the demand context and reads these verses as the speech of the idolaters who said they would not believe unless these conditions were met. Al-Muyassar captures the inner logic: when the Qur'an defeated them and they could not meet it, they began asking for miracles according to their desires.

That final phrase is important: according to their desires.

The issue is not that Allah cannot split earth, make rivers flow, send angels, raise a messenger, or send a written book. The issue is who gets to set the terms of guidance. The surah has just said the Qur'an itself is the inimitable sign. They answer by trying to replace revelation with spectacle under their control.

The heart that refuses the Qur'an often asks for a miracle it can manage.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. `Lan nu'mina laka`: The Door Is Locked Before the Sign Arrives

The first demand begins with `lan nu'mina laka`: we will never believe you, or we will not believe for you.

The word `lan` gives emphatic negation directed toward the future. This is not the gentle uncertainty of a person who says, "Help me understand." It is a forward-leaning refusal: we will not believe unless our stated condition is met.

Then comes `laka`, to you or for you. Faith is being framed almost like a concession they might grant to the Messenger if he performs according to their terms. They are not speaking as servants standing before Allah's truth. They are speaking as evaluators who imagine belief is something they can withhold until the Prophet satisfies their specifications.

This wording is spiritually revealing. The problem appears as a request for evidence, but the sentence begins with refusal. The sign has not yet been discussed, and already the future has been closed: we will not believe. The demand is built on a heart that has appointed itself judge.

Al-Tabari reads the line as the idolaters saying they would not affirm the Prophet until he caused a spring to gush from the land. Ibn Kathir's narration of the demand gathering makes the same posture visible: the signs requested are not humble pleas for guidance but conditions imposed after the Qur'an's message has been rejected.

This matters because there is a difference between a question and a condition. A question can be an act of humility. A condition can be an act of control. A question says, "Show me what I am missing." A condition says, "I will decide what Allah's messenger must do before I submit."

[PAUSE]

Sometimes unbelief does not begin with the absence of proof. It begins with the demand to control what proof is allowed to be.

### 2. `Hatta tafjura lana mina al-ardi yanbu'a`: Making the Earth Perform

The first requested sign is terrestrial: until you cause a spring to gush forth for us from the earth.

The verb `tafjura` comes from a root associated with splitting open and bursting forth. A `yanbu'` is a spring, a source where water wells up. The image is powerful: dry earth opens, water emerges, scarcity becomes flow.

In itself, this kind of sign is not impossible. Allah caused water to gush for Musa's people when the rock was struck. He brings water from the sky and revives dead land throughout the Qur'an. The issue is not divine power. The issue is demand.

Notice `lana`: for us. The spring is not requested as a sign that leads to obedience. It is requested as a benefit under their terms. The demand has the feel of local control: make our land produce a spring for us. The Messenger is being pressured to turn prophethood into public utility according to the people's wishlist.

Al-Muyassar says they demanded that he make a running spring gush from the land of Makkah. Al-Tabari explains `yanbu'` as a spring or flowing source of water. Ibn Kathir's cited reports include similar demands about removing mountains and making rivers flow. The pattern is consistent: transform the environment for us, and then we will consider belief.

This is a temptation that survives in subtler forms. A person may say, "If Allah fixes this exact worldly condition, then I will come back." "If Allah gives me the provision I named, then I will pray." "If Allah removes this dryness from my life in the way I imagine, then I will trust Him." The heart turns guidance into a transaction: make the earth gush for me, then I will believe for you.

But revelation is not a servant of our conditions. Allah gives signs as He wills. The spring may come, and the heart may still refuse. The dry place may remain, and the heart may still be guided.

### 3. `Jannah ... tufajjira al-anhara khilalaha tafjira`: The Sign Becomes Private Wealth

The second demand escalates: or you have a garden of date-palms and grapes, and you cause rivers to gush through it abundantly.

The demand moves from a public spring to private abundance. `Laka jannah`: a garden for you. Date-palms and grapes are signs of cultivated wealth, shade, sweetness, and settled prosperity. Then rivers are to be made to gush through the garden.

The verbal pattern intensifies. In verse 90, the demand was `tafjura`. Here it becomes `tufajjira`, Form II, and then `tafjira`, an emphatic verbal noun. It is as though they are not only asking for water; they want abundance staged, intensified, and undeniable. Make rivers burst through the garden in a way that cannot be missed.

There is a strange irony here. The Qur'an repeatedly describes gardens beneath which rivers flow as reward from Allah. The opponents take imagery associated with divine promise and turn it into a demand for worldly verification. They want a preview of power on their terms, not surrender to the Lord who grants gardens in the Hereafter.

This demand also exposes a common assumption: truth should be accompanied by visible worldly advantage. If you are really a messenger, where is your estate? Where is your garden? Where are the rivers? Where is the kind of wealth that makes people stop arguing?

But the Qur'an consistently refuses to make worldly luxury the proof of divine truth. Many prophets were opposed by elites precisely because those elites mistook wealth for worth. In Az-Zukhruf 43:31, they ask why this Qur'an was not sent down to a great man from one of the two towns. The measure is the same: greatness should look socially recognizable, economically impressive, and easy to rank.

So the demand for a garden is not innocent. It tries to make prophethood answer to the symbols of status.

[PAUSE]

The truth does not become truer because it owns a garden, and falsehood does not become safe because it sits among rivers.

### 4. `Tusqita al-sama'a ... kisafa`: Weaponizing the Warning

The third demand turns violent: or you make the sky fall upon us in pieces, as you have claimed.

Now the requested sign is not benefit but catastrophe. They are essentially saying: if you warn us of punishment, bring it down. Make the sky fall in fragments.

The phrase `kama za'amta`, as you have claimed, is cutting. `Za'ama` often carries the sense of assertion, claim, or allegation, and in contexts like this it has a dismissive edge. They do not receive the warning as revelation. They label it a claim.

This is a dangerous spiritual posture: turning warning into a dare.

The Qur'an records similar patterns elsewhere. People ask, "When is this promise, if you are truthful?" They seek to hasten punishment. In Al-Anfal 8:32, the rejecters say that if this is the truth from Allah, then rain stones upon us from the sky or bring painful punishment. The heart has become so inverted that it would rather dare destruction than humble itself before mercy.

Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir both connect these verses to demanded signs from the idolaters. In 17:59, the surah already explained why signs of this kind are not simply granted on demand: earlier peoples denied signs and were destroyed. If the sky falls after a dare, there may be no space left for repentance.

For us, the lived form may be less dramatic but still real. A person hears warning about the consequences of arrogance, betrayal, exploitative money, neglected prayer, or heedlessness and responds, inwardly, "Nothing has happened yet." That sentence is a quieter version of daring the sky. It mistakes Allah's delay for weakness, His mercy for absence, and His warning for empty speech.

The believer should learn to tremble before warnings while they are still warnings. If an ayah can wake us before consequence arrives, that is mercy.

### 5. `Ta'tiya billahi wa-al-mala'ikati qabila`: Demanding the Unseen on Their Terms

The next demand is even more transgressive: or you bring Allah and the angels before us face to face.

Here the demand crosses from earth and sky into the unseen. They want Allah and the angels brought into their court of inspection. The word `qabila` carries the sense of face-to-face, directly opposite, or in visible confrontation. It is an attempt to make the unseen submit to the terms of sensory verification.

The theological problem is enormous. Allah is not an object to be produced by a messenger for human inspection. Angels are not props in a demanded spectacle. Revelation comes from Allah; Allah is not summoned by the audience as supporting evidence.

This is why the answer at the end begins with `subhana rabbi`: Glory be to my Lord. The first correction is transcendence. Allah is exalted above the premise of their demand. The Prophet does not say, "I will try." He does not negotiate the terms. He declares Allah's glory above what they imply.

This demand also echoes a recurring human problem: the desire to collapse faith into control. The unseen is not respected as unseen. It must become visible, manageable, and forced into the categories of the one who refuses. But the Qur'an teaches that guidance is not the conquest of the unseen by the senses. It is surrender to Allah through the signs and revelation He chooses.

This does not mean Islam asks for blind irrationality. The Qur'an gives signs in creation, history, scripture, the self, and moral reality. But it does not let the creature drag the Creator into a courtroom designed by arrogance.

[PAUSE]

The Lord who sends signs is not Himself summoned as a sign under human command.

### 6. `Bayt min zukhruf ... tarqa fi al-sama'`: Luxury, Ascent, and the Moving Goalpost

Verse 93 continues the list: or you have a house of ornament, or you ascend into the sky.

`Zukhruf` refers to gold, ornament, embellishment, dazzling decoration. The demand returns to wealth: not merely a garden now, but an ornate house. Again, truth is being measured by visible possession. The Messenger is being asked to appear as the world expects power to appear.

Then comes ascent: `tarqa fi al-sama'`, you ascend into the sky. This demand seems more spiritual, but the next clause exposes it: we will never believe in your ascent until you bring down to us a book that we can read.

This is the moving goalpost inside the moving goalpost. First they demand ascent. Then they say even ascent will not be enough unless it produces a readable document delivered to them. The condition multiplies inside the condition.

The words `kitaban naqra'uhu` are striking in a passage about the Qur'an. The Qur'an is already being recited to them. The very name Qur'an is connected to recitation. Yet they demand another book, one they can read on their terms, as if the revealed Qur'an in front of them is not enough.

This returns us to 17:89. Allah has diversified every kind of example in this Qur'an. They answer by asking for a different book. The problem is not the absence of a readable revelation. It is refusal to read the revelation already given as revelation.

Al-Muyassar notes this clearly: they said they would not believe in the Prophet's ascension until he brought a written book from Allah that they could read, ordering them to believe. But the final answer does not indulge the premise. It restores the Prophet's station.

### 7. `Qul subhana rabbi`: The Answer Begins With Allah's Glory

After the long list, the Prophet is commanded to say: Glory be to my Lord. Am I anything but a human messenger?

The answer is short because the list does not deserve equal negotiation. It needs correction at the root.

`Subhana rabbi` declares Allah's transcendence. Allah is exalted above being manipulated, summoned, displayed, or made answerable to human terms. The word `rabbi`, my Lord, is intimate and corrective at once. The Prophet belongs to his Lord. He does not own the signs. He does not control the unseen. He does not convert messengerhood into performance.

Then comes the human station: `hal kuntu illa basharan rasula`: am I anything but a human messenger? The structure restricts the claim. He is not an angel, not a deity, not a controller of cosmic events, not a supplier of spectacles on demand. He is human, and he is messenger.

The pairing matters. If he were only human, they could dismiss him as ordinary. If they demanded more than messengerhood, they would distort his role. The truth is both: human messenger. His humanity is not a flaw in his message. His messengerhood is not a license to pretend he controls Allah's acts.

Al-Tabari reads the response as the Prophet being commanded to declare Allah's glory and to say he is only a servant messenger who cannot bring signs except by Allah's permission. Ibn Kathir frames the passage similarly: the Messenger is instructed to say that he is a human messenger, and the signs are with Allah.

[PAUSE]

The Messenger's greatness is not that he escapes servanthood. His greatness is that he fulfills it completely.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Conditional Faith Is Often Control Wearing the Clothing of Inquiry

The first principle is this: not every demand for evidence is a sincere search for truth. Sometimes it is the ego trying to control the conditions under which it will pretend to surrender.

We know this pattern in ordinary life. A person says, "I will apologize if they apologize first." "I will change when my circumstances change." "I will become serious when Allah gives me the exact opening I asked for." "I will obey when the command feels emotionally easy." Each sentence presents itself as reasonable. Each one keeps the self in charge.

The opponents in these verses do the same with belief. They do not say, "Guide us to what Allah wants." They say, "We will never believe you until..." Then they produce a list. The grammar of the list is the grammar of control: until you do this for us, or you have this, or you make that happen, or you bring this, or you ascend, and even then we will not believe until you bring down a book we can read.

Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir both preserve the demand-context of these verses. This was not simple intellectual uncertainty. It was a posture of imposed terms. Al-Muyassar's phrase that they sought miracles according to their desires is spiritually exact. Desire can become the hidden author of what we call proof.

The Qur'an elsewhere protects us from confusing conditions with sincerity. In 6:111, Allah says that even if angels were sent down to them, the dead spoke to them, and everything were gathered before them, they would not believe unless Allah willed. That verse is not dismissing signs. It is exposing a heart whose refusal would survive even overwhelming spectacle.

This theme should make us careful with ourselves. When we ask Allah for clarity, do we also ask Him for surrender? When we ask for a sign, are we prepared to obey if the sign comes in a form that humbles us rather than flatters us? When we say, "I need to understand," are we seeking enough understanding to follow, or total control before obedience?

There is a merciful way to ask. The prophets ask. Ibrahim asks how the dead are revived, not because he rejects, but so his heart may be reassured. Musa asks for help, expansion of his chest, and clarity of speech. Zakariyya asks for a sign and receives one. The difference is not that believers never ask. The difference is that believers ask from servanthood.

The demanders in Al-Isra ask from above. The Prophet is commanded to answer from below: Glory be to my Lord. I am only a human messenger.

So the practical invitation is to examine the sentence after "until." I will not pray until what? I will not repent until what? I will not forgive until what? I will not trust Allah until what? Sometimes the word after "until" names a real wound needing care. Sometimes it names the idol of control.

[PAUSE]

Faith begins when the servant stops making Allah's truth wait at the door of his own terms.

### Theme 2: Spectacle Cannot Heal a Heart That Refuses Guidance

The second principle is this: a sign can expose refusal, but it cannot force a heart to love guidance.

The requested signs in this passage are spectacular. Water erupting from earth. Gardens and rivers. Sky falling in fragments. Allah and angels brought face to face. Ornamented houses. Ascent into heaven. A readable book descending. If spectacle alone created faith, this list would be more than enough.

But the Qur'an has already shown us that spectacle can be witnessed and still denied. Thamud received the she-camel as a visible sign and wronged her. Fir'awn saw sign after sign and remained arrogant until the sea closed. The Children of Israel saw deliverance, provision, and divine favor, yet many still turned. The issue is not whether Allah can make signs visible. The issue is what the heart does with visibility.

Al-Isra 17:59 sits behind this passage like a warning lamp. Allah did not send the demanded signs because earlier peoples denied them. Signs of that kind bring final accountability. They are not toys for skeptics. They are mercy when Allah sends them as He wills; they are danger when demanded by those seeking to test the messenger rather than follow the Lord.

This should sober a modern assumption: "If only I saw something undeniable, I would believe." Perhaps. But the Qur'an asks a deeper question: what do you do with the signs already around you? What do you do with your own dependence, with death, with the moral law written into consequence, with the Qur'an's exposure of your inner life, with the alternation of night and day, with the fact that every blessing leaves you unable to create even one breath from nothing?

Spectacle can stun the senses while leaving the will untouched. A person can have an intense moment and then return unchanged. A crisis can make someone call upon Allah, then rescue can produce forgetfulness. Al-Isra itself said in 17:67 that when harm touches people at sea, those they call upon vanish except Allah, then when He saves them to land, they turn away.

This does not mean we belittle miracles. Miracles are real signs when Allah gives them. It means we stop treating spectacle as a substitute for a humbled heart. The greatest sign given to this ummah is the Qur'an, recited, remembered, lived, and inexhaustible. The previous ayahs have just declared that all humans and jinn together cannot produce its like, and that every kind of example has been diversified inside it.

So when the opponents ask for rivers, sky fragments, angels, gold houses, ascent, and another book, the surah is not impressed. It has already named the sign they are refusing.

For us, the application is immediate. Do not wait for your life to become cinematic before you obey. Do not make guidance dependent on a dramatic event. Do not despise the quiet sign because it came as an ayah you have heard before, a reminder from someone you know, a pang of conscience, a repeated pattern in your own sin, or a mercy that arrived without thunder.

[PAUSE]

The heart that will not bow before the Qur'an may turn even the splitting sky into another argument.

### Theme 3: The Messenger Is Human, and That Is Part of the Mercy

The third principle is this: the Messenger's humanity is not a weakness in revelation; it is part of how revelation reaches human beings.

The opponents demand what would make the Prophet appear more than human in the way they imagine power. They want control over earth, possession of extraordinary wealth, command over sky, direct production of angels, ascent into heaven, and delivery of a custom document. In response, he is told to say: Glory be to my Lord. Am I anything but a human messenger?

This answer protects tawhid and protects prophethood.

It protects tawhid because Allah alone controls signs. The Messenger does not summon Allah. The Messenger does not command angels. The Messenger does not make the sky fall by human will. The Messenger conveys what Allah reveals and does what Allah commands. When people demand that he act as owner of divine power, the answer is `subhana rabbi`: my Lord is exalted above this.

It protects prophethood because the Prophet's humanity is not an embarrassment to be overcome. The Qur'an repeatedly says that messengers eat food, walk in markets, speak human language, and come from their people. If guidance came in a form completely detached from human life, people would say, "This does not know our hunger, fear, family ties, fatigue, grief, temptation, and mortality." Allah sent human messengers so that obedience could be embodied.

The objection of rejecters often runs the other way: why would Allah send a human? Al-Isra will answer this directly in the next ayahs, saying that if angels were walking securely on earth, Allah would have sent an angel messenger to them. The messenger matches the addressed creation. Human beings are guided by a human messenger, not because Allah lacks power to send angels, but because the point is lived guidance.

Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir both frame the closing answer around the Messenger's limits before Allah's will. He is a servant messenger, not the owner of demanded signs. This is not a reduction of his rank. It is the perfection of his rank. The highest human being is still a servant of Allah.

This matters for Muslims too. We can distort love of the Prophet if we turn him into what the Qur'an tells him to deny. Real love honors him as Allah honored him: human messenger, servant, receiver of revelation, mercy to the worlds by Allah's sending, and the most complete model of surrender. We do not honor him by attributing to him independent control that belongs to Allah alone.

It also matters for how we think about religious leadership after him. People often want leaders who perform certainty, control outcomes, display power, and make faith feel victorious in worldly terms. But the prophetic answer teaches a different measure: servanthood, truthfulness, faithful conveyance, and dependence on Allah.

The believer should be relieved by this. The path to Allah does not require becoming superhuman. It requires following the human messenger sent by Allah. Prayer is human. Patience is human. Repentance is human. Hunger, tears, fear, courage, family, fatigue, and death are all included in the path because the Messenger walked the path as a human being.

[PAUSE]

Allah did not send guidance that floats above human life. He sent a human messenger so revelation could be lived.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of Demanded Signs and Prophetic Limits

The passage begins with a locked declaration: we will never believe you until. Then the demands move in widening circles. First earth must produce a spring. Then private land must become a garden with rivers. Then the sky must fall in pieces. Then Allah and the angels must be brought face to face. Then wealth must appear as a house of ornament. Then the Prophet must ascend into heaven. Finally, even ascent is declared insufficient without a readable book brought down to them.

The architecture is escalation and exposure. Each demand seems to ask for proof, but the list itself proves the problem. The condition keeps moving. Earth is not enough, wealth is not enough, cosmic catastrophe is not enough, angelic visibility is not enough, ascent is not enough, and even then another book is demanded.

Then the answer cuts through the whole structure: Glory be to my Lord. Am I anything but a human messenger?

So the passage moves from human conditions to divine transcendence, from demanded spectacle to messengerly servanthood, from the ego's list to the Prophet's limit. The cure for conditional faith is not a longer performance. It is the restoration of order: Allah is glorified above their demands, and the Messenger stands as human messenger.

### Questions to Carry

What have I placed after the word "until" in my own obedience?

Sometimes the condition is obvious: until life gets easier, until I feel ready, until someone else changes first. Naming the condition is the first step in seeing whether it is wisdom, wound, or control.

Do I ask Allah for guidance, or do I ask Him to meet the terms under which I have already decided guidance would be acceptable?

The difference is subtle. A seeker asks to be led. A negotiator asks to remain judge.

Where am I waiting for spectacle while neglecting the Qur'an already recited to me?

The demanded signs were dramatic, but the surah has just named the Qur'an as the inimitable sign filled with every kind of example. We should ask whether familiarity has made the greatest sign feel ordinary.

Do I honor the Prophet as Allah describes him: human messenger?

Real love does not need to exaggerate beyond revelation. His humanity is part of the mercy, and his messengerhood is the honor Allah gave him.

When a warning reaches me before consequence arrives, do I treat it as mercy or dare the sky to fall?

The warning that wakes us before punishment is a gift. A heart still alive does not need catastrophe before it listens.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The heart that refuses guidance keeps adding conditions, while revelation restores the truth: Allah is exalted above our demands, and the Messenger is a human servant sent to convey His word.**

### The Closing Invitation

Let the list end. Stop making obedience wait for the earth to open, the sky to fall, the unseen to appear, or your life to arrange itself into the exact sign you requested. The Qur'an is already here. The Messenger has already conveyed. Allah has already diversified the examples. Ask your questions with humility, seek clarity with sincerity, but do not let the ego keep rewriting the contract of surrender.
