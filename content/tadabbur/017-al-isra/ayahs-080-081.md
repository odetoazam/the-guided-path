# Enter With Truth, Exit With Truth, and Let Falsehood Vanish

## Surah Al-Isra (17:80-81)

[ayah:17:80] وَقُل رَّبِّ أَدْخِلْنِى مُدْخَلَ صِدْقٍ وَأَخْرِجْنِى مُخْرَجَ صِدْقٍ وَٱجْعَل لِّى مِن لَّدُنكَ سُلْطَـٰنًا نَّصِيرًا

[ayah:17:81] وَقُلْ جَآءَ ٱلْحَقُّ وَزَهَقَ ٱلْبَـٰطِلُ ۚ إِنَّ ٱلْبَـٰطِلَ كَانَ زَهُوقًا

*Wa-qul rabbi adkhilni mudkhala sidqin wa-akhrijni mukhraja sidqin wa-j'al li min ladunka sultanan nasira. Wa-qul ja'a al-haqqu wa-zahaqa al-batilu inna al-batila kana zahuqa.*

**And say: My Lord, cause me to enter with an entry of truth, and cause me to leave with an exit of truth, and appoint for me from Yourself a supporting authority. And say: Truth has come, and falsehood has vanished. Indeed, falsehood is ever vanishing.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

After pressure, prayer.

After prayer, du'a.

After du'a, proclamation.

That is the movement here. The Prophet has been warned about people trying to tempt him away from revelation, unsettle him from the land, and expel him. Then he is commanded to establish prayer and rise in the night. Now Allah teaches him what to ask for as he moves through the world: a truthful entry, a truthful exit, and supporting authority from Allah Himself.

This du'a is astonishing because it does not ask merely for safe entry and safe exit.

It asks for truthful entry and truthful exit.

That means the issue is not only whether the door opens. It is whether the servant enters in a way pleasing to Allah. It is not only whether the servant leaves a place. It is whether the departure is clean, sincere, guided, and true.

Then verse 81 gives the declaration that every believer wants to live under: truth has come, and falsehood has vanished. But notice the order. The proclamation comes after dependence. The servant asks Allah for truthful movement and support, then declares truth's arrival and falsehood's disappearance.

The passage teaches us that truth is not served by self-confident noise. It is served by servants who ask to enter truthfully, leave truthfully, be supported by Allah, and then speak truth as something that comes from Him.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. `Wa-qul rabbi`: The Command to Speak Begins With Need

Both verses begin with `wa-qul`: and say.

But the first saying is du'a. The second saying is proclamation. This pairing matters. Before the Prophet is told to say, "Truth has come," he is told to say, "My Lord."

`Rabbi` is intimate dependence: my Lord, my nurturer, my owner, the One who raises and sustains me. After verses about hostile pressure and possible expulsion, Allah does not command the Prophet to ground himself in strategy first. He grounds him in lordship.

That is the first medicine for any movement. Before entering, leaving, confronting, migrating, preaching, deciding, or beginning a new chapter, the servant says: my Lord.

Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir both place this du'a in the flow of Allah guiding and supporting the Prophet. Classical tafsir often connects the verse with the Prophet's movement from Makkah to Madinah, while also preserving the broader meaning of asking for righteous, truthful movement in all affairs. The power of the du'a is that it fits both the historical pressure and every later servant's need.

[PAUSE]

No transition is neutral. Every entry and exit needs a Lord.

### 2. `Adkhilni mudkhala sidq`: Do Not Just Let Me Enter; Make the Entry True

The first request is:

`adkhilni mudkhala sidq`

Cause me to enter with an entry of truth.

The verb `adkhilni` asks Allah to make the entry happen. The servant is not saying, "I will enter by my power." He asks to be entered by Allah. Then the noun `mudkhal` names the entry itself, the place or manner of entering.

Then comes the qualifying word:

`sidq`

Truthfulness, sincerity, soundness, correspondence between inward and outward reality.

An entry of sidq is not merely a successful entry. A person can enter a room with deception. Enter leadership with ego. Enter marriage with concealment. Enter knowledge with vanity. Enter activism with rage. Enter a city with fear. Enter worship with performance. Enter da'wah with a need to be admired.

The du'a asks for the opposite: let my entry be true.

This is why sidq is such a deep Qur'anic word. It is not only factual accuracy in speech. It is integrity of being. The truthful person is aligned. Their claim, intention, action, and direction are not at war with one another.

So the verse teaches us to ask not only, "Can I get in?" but "What enters with me?"

### 3. `Akhrijni mukhraja sidq`: Leaving Also Needs Truth

The second request mirrors the first:

`wa-akhrijni mukhraja sidq`

And cause me to leave with an exit of truth.

Many people think carefully about beginnings and poorly about endings. They enter with intention but leave with resentment. They begin with sincerity but exit with damage. They join something with adab but depart with betrayal. They start a role seeking Allah, then leave when the ego is bruised and call the exit principle.

The Qur'an teaches us to ask for truthful exits too.

Sometimes leaving is obedience. The Prophet's hijrah was not abandonment of mission; it was obedience under Allah's plan. Musa left Egypt in fear and need, but Allah turned that exit into preparation. Ibrahim left idol worship and people for his Lord. Leaving can be part of truth.

But leaving can also be escape from accountability, avoidance of repair, or refusal to be corrected. That is why the du'a matters. The servant does not trust the exit simply because it feels urgent. He asks Allah to make it sidq.

[PAUSE]

A truthful exit may be quiet. It may be painful. It may require apology before departure. It may require staying longer than the nafs wants, or leaving sooner than comfort prefers. The point is not movement itself. The point is truth in the movement.

### 4. `Min ladunka sultanan nasira`: Support Must Come From Near Allah

The third request is:

`wa-j'al li min ladunka sultanan nasira`

And appoint for me from Yourself a supporting authority.

`Sultan` can mean authority, proof, power, or decisive support. It is not mere dominance. In Qur'anic usage, sultan often refers to a real warrant, an authority that stands with truth. Here it is qualified by `nasira`: supporting, helping.

Even more important is `min ladunka`: from Yourself, from Your nearness, from Your own presence and grant.

The Prophet is not asking for borrowed authority from the people who might accept him if he compromises. He is not asking for the intimacy promised in 17:73. He is asking for a supporting authority from Allah.

That contrast is central to the passage. People can offer friendship, platforms, protection, and recognition. But if the cost is alteration of revelation or loss of sidq, the authority becomes poison. The du'a asks for authority that comes from Allah and supports truth rather than replacing it.

This is a crucial distinction for anyone carrying religious work. Power is not automatically corrupt, but power must be from Allah, for Allah, and in support of truth. Otherwise it becomes another form of pressure.

### 5. `Ja'a al-haqq`: Truth Comes; It Is Not Manufactured

Verse 81 begins the proclamation:

`ja'a al-haqq`

Truth has come.

The verb is simple: came. Truth arrives. It is not manufactured by the Prophet. It is not negotiated by society. It is not voted into existence. It comes from Allah.

This is one of the Qur'an's great ways of speaking about revelation. Truth comes; people receive or resist it. Falsehood may be loud before it comes, but the coming of truth changes the entire scene.

Classical tafsir connects this verse with the triumph of Islam and the collapse of idols, especially in the conquest of Makkah, while the wording remains universal. Wherever Allah brings truth, falsehood loses the basis on which it pretended to stand.

The cross-Qur'anic echo in 21:18 is powerful: Allah hurls truth against falsehood, and it crushes it so it vanishes. In 34:49, the Prophet is told to say: truth has come, and falsehood neither originates nor brings back. Al-Isra's wording belongs to that same Qur'anic confidence.

### 6. `Zahaqa al-batil`: Falsehood Does Not Merely Lose; It Vanishes

The next phrase is:

`wa-zahaqa al-batil`

And falsehood has vanished.

`Zahaqa` carries the sense of passing away, perishing, slipping out, losing its life-force. Falsehood is not described as simply defeated in a contest between equal powers. It is exposed as unable to endure when truth arrives.

This is important because falsehood often looks solid before truth confronts it. It has institutions, habits, money, slogans, decorations, inherited legitimacy, and emotional familiarity. People can live inside it long enough that it feels permanent.

But the verse says falsehood is vanishing by nature.

It may last for a while. It may harm people. It may build systems. It may appear dominant. But it lacks the durability of truth because it is not aligned with reality as Allah created and revealed it.

[PAUSE]

This gives courage without making us naive. Falsehood can wound before it vanishes. The believer still has to be patient, wise, prayerful, and truthful. But the heart does not bow before falsehood as though it were eternal.

### 7. `Inna al-batila kana zahuqa`: Falsehood Has Always Been Perishing

The verse closes:

`inna al-batila kana zahuqa`

Indeed, falsehood is ever vanishing.

The structure is emphatic. `Inna` gives force. `Kana` here does not mean falsehood only vanished once in the past. It points to an enduring quality: falsehood has always been of this nature. `Zahuq` is an intensive pattern: prone to vanish, bound to perish, unable to remain when exposed by truth.

This is the theological foundation beneath the proclamation. Truth does not merely win one event. Falsehood is ontologically unstable. It cannot be made permanent because it contradicts Allah's reality.

This is why the believer can make du'a for truthful entry and exit, ask for supporting authority, and then speak truth without despair. We do not know the timing. We do not control the battlefield. But we know the nature of falsehood.

It is zahuq.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Every Transition Needs Sidq

Human life is made of entries and exits.

Entering a new job. Leaving an old city. Entering marriage. Leaving a friendship. Entering parenthood. Leaving youth. Entering public service. Leaving a role. Entering a conversation. Leaving an argument. Entering worship. Leaving a sin.

Most of our anxiety around transitions is about outcome. Will this work? Will I be safe? Will people approve? Will I lose something? Will the door open?

This du'a teaches a deeper concern: will it be truthful?

A transition can succeed outwardly and fail spiritually. You can get the role but enter with arrogance. You can win the argument but leave with zulm. You can escape a painful situation but leave behind avoidable harm. You can move to a better place but carry the same dishonesty into it.

Sidq asks for alignment. It asks Allah to make the movement clean from the inside.

Al-Tabari's and Ibn Kathir's historically grounded readings around the Prophet's migration do not narrow the du'a; they intensify it. Hijrah itself needed sidq. The movement from Makkah to Madinah was not merely relocation. It was entry into a new phase of revelation and exit from a place of persecution under Allah's command.

The practical application is simple and demanding: before every major entry or exit, make this du'a. Then ask what sidq requires. Is there a right to return? A lie to correct? An intention to purify? A fear to surrender? A desire for status to name before Allah?

[PAUSE]

Do not only ask Allah to open doors. Ask Him to make you truthful when you walk through them.

### Theme 2: Authority Is a Mercy When It Supports Truth

Many spiritual people are rightly suspicious of power.

Power can corrupt. Authority can intoxicate. Platforms can bend intentions. Leadership can turn service into self-protection. So some people imagine the purest religious life is one with no authority at all.

But the du'a asks for sultan.

Not any sultan. `Sultanan nasira` from Allah's own presence. Supporting authority. Authority that helps truth, protects mission, strengthens obedience, and gives proof and backing where Allah wants the servant to stand.

This is an important balance. The Qur'an does not teach us to worship power or flee all responsibility. It teaches us to ask that authority be from Allah and for truth.

The Prophet had people offering him intimacy if revelation changed. That kind of support would have been ruin. Here Allah teaches him to ask for support from Himself. The source matters. The purpose matters. The moral direction matters.

For us, this applies to every form of influence. A parent has authority. A teacher has authority. A manager has authority. A writer, elder, imam, organizer, or older sibling may have authority. The question is not only whether we have influence, but whether our influence is nasir for truth.

Does it help people obey Allah, or does it make them dependent on us? Does it protect the vulnerable, or protect our image? Does it support sidq, or reward performance? Does it come with humility before Allah, or with the intoxication of being needed?

Ask Allah for supporting authority, and ask Him to protect you from authority that supports your ego.

### Theme 3: Truth Does Not Need to Become Loud to Be Real

Verse 81 is often heard as a triumphant declaration, and it is.

Truth has come. Falsehood has vanished.

But its triumph is not empty noise. It comes after du'a, prayer, firmness, and dependence. The Prophet is not told to shout slogans while detached from Allah. He is taught to ask for truthful movement and divine support, then declare the nature of truth and falsehood.

This matters because believers can misunderstand confidence. Qur'anic confidence is not bluster. It is not pretending falsehood has no temporary force. It is not using the truth as an excuse for arrogance. It is calm certainty rooted in Allah.

Falsehood is zahuq. That does not mean it never scares us. It means it has no ultimate permanence. It may occupy space, but it cannot become haqq. It may delay justice, but it cannot rewrite the nature of justice. It may decorate itself, but its life-force is borrowed and unstable.

This gives the believer a different emotional stance. We work without despair. We speak without panic. We resist without worshipping the enemy's size. We repent when falsehood has entered us, because if falsehood is vanishing by nature, then clinging to it is clinging to what is already dying.

[PAUSE]

The most immediate battlefield is often inside the self. Let truth come into the place where you have been defending a false story. Let truth come into the excuse, the grudge, the secret sin, the inflated self-image, the despair narrative. Falsehood vanishes when truth is allowed to arrive.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of Truthful Movement and Vanishing Falsehood

The passage moves from supplication to proclamation.

First, the Prophet is told to ask his Lord for a truthful entry. Movement into the next place, role, or phase must be made by Allah and marked by sidq.

Second, he asks for a truthful exit. Leaving also needs purification; not every departure is truthful simply because it is desired or necessary.

Third, he asks for supporting authority from Allah Himself. The servant needs sultan, but only a sultan that supports truth and comes from Allah.

Fourth, he is told to proclaim the arrival of truth and the vanishing of falsehood. The declaration rests on dependence, not ego.

The architecture is: enter truthfully, exit truthfully, be supported divinely, then speak truth confidently.

### Questions to Carry

What entry am I asking Allah to make truthful?

Think of the next door in your life. Do not only ask whether it opens. Ask what intention, adab, and honesty must enter with you.

What exit in my life needs sidq?

Leaving can be obedience or avoidance. Ask Allah to show you the difference and to clean the departure from harm, ego, and false narration.

What kind of authority am I asking for?

Do you want influence to support truth, or to support your self-image? The difference may not be visible to others, but it is visible to Allah.

Where am I trying to defeat falsehood without first depending on Allah?

Verse 81 follows verse 80. Proclamation comes after du'a. Let dependence come before declaration.

What falsehood inside me is already zahuq if I stop feeding it?

Some lies survive only because we keep protecting them. Let truth arrive there first.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The servant asks Allah for truthful entry, truthful exit, and supporting authority, then declares with calm certainty that truth has come and falsehood is, by its nature, already vanishing.**

### The Closing Invitation

Make this du'a before your next transition, even a small one. Ask Allah not only to move you, but to move you truthfully. Refuse authority that costs your sidq. Let truth enter one defended place in your heart today, and watch how much effort it takes to keep falsehood alive once Allah has shown you what it is.

### Du'a

Rabbi, cause us to enter every matter with sidq and to leave every matter with sidq. Grant us from Yourself a supporting authority that serves truth and never our egos. Let truth come into our hearts, homes, words, and work. Make falsehood vanish from us before it vanishes around us, and keep us under the banner of Your revelation with sincerity and courage.
