# What Remains After the Wind

## Al-Kahf 18:45-46

[ayah:18:45] وَٱضْرِبْ لَهُم مَّثَلَ ٱلْحَيَوٰةِ ٱلدُّنْيَا كَمَآءٍ أَنزَلْنَـٰهُ مِنَ ٱلسَّمَآءِ فَٱخْتَلَطَ بِهِۦ نَبَاتُ ٱلْأَرْضِ فَأَصْبَحَ هَشِيمًا تَذْرُوهُ ٱلرِّيَـٰحُ ۗ وَكَانَ ٱللَّهُ عَلَىٰ كُلِّ شَىْءٍ مُّقْتَدِرًا

[ayah:18:46] ٱلْمَالُ وَٱلْبَنُونَ زِينَةُ ٱلْحَيَوٰةِ ٱلدُّنْيَا ۖ وَٱلْبَـٰقِيَـٰتُ ٱلصَّـٰلِحَـٰتُ خَيْرٌ عِندَ رَبِّكَ ثَوَابًا وَخَيْرٌ أَمَلًا

*Wadrib lahum mathala al-hayati ad-dunya kama'in anzalnahu mina as-sama'i fakhtalata bihi nabatu al-ardi fa-asbaha hashiman tadhruhu ar-riyah. Wa kana Allahu 'ala kulli shay'in muqtadira. Al-malu wal-banuna zinatu al-hayati ad-dunya, wal-baqiyatu as-salihatu khayrun 'inda rabbika thawaban wa khayrun amala.*

**And strike for them the example of the life of this world: like water We sent down from the sky, then the vegetation of the earth mingled with it, then it became dry fragments scattered by the winds. And Allah is ever fully able over all things. Wealth and children are the adornment of the life of this world, but the enduring righteous deeds are better with your Lord in reward and better in hope.**

## Introduction

The garden has fallen.

The owner who once walked through abundance saying he did not think it would ever perish has been left turning his palms over what he spent. His fruit was encompassed. His trellises became part of the collapse. His followers could not help him. He could not rescue himself. And the Qur'an closed that scene by naming the reality that was true all along: there, walayah belongs to Allah, the True; He is best in reward and best in outcome.

Now the surah widens the lens.

The two gardens were not only the story of one rich man. They were a window into the life of this world. So the Qur'an turns from a particular garden to the entire dunya and says: strike for them the example of worldly life.

It is like water Allah sends down from the sky.

At first, that image feels merciful. Water descends. The earth responds. Vegetation grows. Life appears. Everything becomes green, mingled, alive, and full. This is not an ugly picture. The Qur'an does not make the world repulsive in order to teach detachment. It shows the world as genuinely beautiful, genuinely life-giving, genuinely productive, and then it shows what happens next.

The same vegetation becomes dry fragments. The winds scatter it.

Then the next ayah names the most powerful forms of worldly adornment: wealth and children. Not because they are evil, but because they are beautiful enough to deceive. They are adornment. They decorate life. They gladden the eye. They give comfort, continuity, honor, and a sense of rootedness. But they are not what remains. The enduring righteous deeds are better with your Lord in reward and better in hope.

Ibn Kathir reads these ayahs as Allah giving the similitude of worldly life: it flourishes like vegetation after rain, then dries, breaks, and is scattered by winds, while wealth and children are only adornment and lasting righteous deeds are better. Al-Tabari explains the example as worldly life being like rain sent from the sky, vegetation growing by it, then becoming dry scattered fragments; he explains wealth and children as beauty of the near life and the enduring righteous deeds as what remains for reward. Al-Muyassar gives the same central movement: worldly life resembles rain-fed vegetation that soon becomes broken dry matter scattered by winds, while wealth and children are adornment and lasting righteous deeds are better with Allah.

There is no separate asbab al-nuzul for these two ayahs beyond their placement after the two-gardens parable. Their immediate context is decisive: after one man mistakes abundance for permanence, Allah gives the permanent diagnostic for all of us.

The Qur'an does not ask us to hate the garden. It asks us to stop asking the garden to survive the wind.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. Strike the Example: The Parable Is Meant to Land

The ayah begins with an imperative: *wadrib lahum mathal* - strike for them an example.

The verb comes from a root field that includes striking, setting forth, travelling, and making an impact. When the Qur'an says to "strike" an example, it is not presenting a decorative illustration. It is placing an image before the listener with force. The example is meant to hit the imagination, to interrupt the false normalcy of the world, to make the unseen meaning visible.

This is especially important because the previous parable was already specific and dramatic. We might think the lesson is complete: a man had gardens, became arrogant, and lost them. But Allah now commands another parable, this time for worldly life itself. The movement is from case study to universal law. The two gardens were one version of the pattern. The whole dunya follows the same pattern.

The word *mathal* is not merely a comparison. In Qur'anic usage, a parable gathers moral, theological, and psychological reality into a concrete image. It does not only explain something unfamiliar through something familiar. It trains us to read the familiar correctly. Rain, plants, adornment, wealth, children, wind - these are ordinary realities. The Qur'an takes them and makes them witnesses.

Al-Tabari treats the ayah as Allah commanding the Prophet to present the likeness of worldly life for those addressed. Ibn Kathir places it among the Qur'an's recurring examples of the fleeting dunya. The command itself teaches that we need examples because abstraction alone does not cure attachment. A person can admit intellectually that life is temporary and still live as though permanence has been negotiated privately for them.

So the parable strikes. It does not flatter our self-image. It says: look at water, growth, dryness, wind. That is your world.

[PAUSE]

Some truths have to be pictured before the heart stops pretending it does not understand.

### 2. Water From the Sky: Worldly Beauty Begins as Mercy

The image begins with water sent down from the sky: *kama'in anzalnahu mina as-sama*.

This matters because worldly life does not begin in ugliness. It begins in gift. Water descends. The verb *anzalnahu* places the origin above human control. The "We" of divine action sends it down. No farmer manufactures rain. No garden owner commands the sky. Before there is produce, there is descent. Before there is cultivation, there is mercy.

Water in the Qur'an is one of the great signs of Allah's life-giving power. In 21:30, Allah says He made every living thing from water. In 16:10-11, He sends water from the sky, from which people drink and by which plants, olives, palms, grapes, and fruits grow. In 30:24, He sends rain and revives the earth after its death. So when 18:45 begins with water, it begins with a sign of mercy and power.

That is what keeps the reflection balanced. The world is not worthless in the sense of having no beauty, no benefit, no tenderness, no sign. It is worthless only when treated as ultimate. The same rain that can deceive the heedless is also a sign that should guide the believer. The problem is not that vegetation grows. The problem is that the heart sees growth and forgets descent.

The wording also echoes the two-gardens parable. The rich man's garden had a river running through it. His companion warned that the water could become sunken beyond reach. Now Allah gives the wider pattern: water descends, vegetation grows, and then it dries into fragments. The water that made the garden possible was never under the garden owner's lordship.

This changes how we look at provision. Your salary, health, intelligence, children, skills, friendships, and openings are not simply "there." They descend through channels Allah created. They may arrive by means - employers, parents, teachers, markets, bodies, laws, habits - but every means is downstream from Allah's sending. If you remember the descent, the gift becomes a sign. If you forget it, the gift becomes a veil.

[PAUSE]

Worldly beauty is safest when we can still see the sky in it.

### 3. The Mingling of Vegetation: Dunya Looks Richest Before It Dries

Then the ayah says the vegetation of the earth mingles with it: *fakhtalata bihi nabatu al-ard*.

The verb *ikhtalata* is Form VIII from the root field of mixing, blending, intermingling. The image is lush. Rain does not touch the ground and leave everything separate. It enters the earth's life systems. Roots drink. Shoots rise. Different kinds of plants intermingle. The earth becomes busy with growth.

This is a striking way to describe worldly life because dunya rarely deceives by appearing empty. It deceives by appearing full. Careers intermix with identity. Family intermixes with hope. Wealth intermixes with security. Beauty intermixes with self-worth. Reputation intermixes with meaning. The world becomes dense with relationships, colors, obligations, ambitions, pleasures, fears, and plans. It feels hard to separate yourself from it because everything has mingled.

The Qur'an does not deny this complexity. It names it. The vegetation mingles with the water. The world becomes alive and thick. The test is not in a sterile desert only; it is in the green season, when everything is growing at once.

This is why spiritual clarity is harder during abundance than during deprivation. In deprivation, the heart may be forced to ask for help. In abundance, the heart can mistake the mingling of means for independence. When every system is working, when relationships are warm, when money flows, when health supports plans, when your name is received well, it feels as though life has become self-explanatory.

Al-Muyassar captures the swiftness of this picture: rain comes, vegetation grows by it, then soon becomes dry matter. Ibn Kathir links this to the Qur'an's broader dunya examples, especially the way plant life delights before it fades. The lushness is part of the lesson, not a distraction from it. The world is most convincing before it becomes brittle.

That is why the believer needs remembrance in the green season. Not only when the plant has dried. The time to say "this is from Allah" is while things are intermingling beautifully. The time to detach the heart is while the heart still feels it has many reasons to cling.

[PAUSE]

The world does not have to be empty to be temporary.

### 4. Dry Fragments Scattered by Wind: The End of the Green Argument

Then comes the reversal: *fa-asbaha hashiman tadhruhu ar-riyah* - then it became dry fragments scattered by the winds.

Again we hear *asbaha*, the verb of becoming. A state changes. What was green becomes *hashim* - dry, broken, crushed plant matter. The word carries the feeling of brittleness. It is vegetation after life has gone out of it, something that can no longer stand, feed the eye, or root itself.

Then the winds scatter it. The verb *tadhruhu* comes from a root field of scattering, dispersing, blowing something away. The winds do not negotiate with the fragments. What once rose from the earth now has no grip on the earth. It is carried away.

This is the Qur'an's answer to the green argument of dunya. The world says: look how alive I am. The Qur'an says: wait until the wind.

The same image appears elsewhere in different forms. In 57:20, worldly life is like rain whose growth pleases cultivators, then it dries, turns yellow, and becomes debris. In 10:24, the earth takes its adornment until its people think they have power over it; then Allah's command comes and it becomes as though it did not flourish yesterday. In 39:21, Allah sends water, brings forth varied crops, then they dry, yellow, and become broken pieces. The repetition is mercy. Allah knows we forget the ending when we are standing in the middle.

The ending of 18:45 is not nihilism. The ayah does not say life is meaningless. It says worldly life, as worldly life, cannot be what we build ultimate hope upon. The plant had meaning as a sign, provision, beauty, and test. It did not have meaning as a god. The wind reveals the difference.

This is why the closing phrase matters: Allah is ever fully able over all things. The scattered fragments are not proof of chaos. They are proof of divine power. The One who sent the water can dry the plant. The One who made growth can end growth. The One who allowed the garden to stand can make it fall. Dunya is not fragile because Allah is absent. It is fragile because it is entirely under His power.

[PAUSE]

The wind does not defeat the garden. The wind obeys the One who gave the garden its season.

### 5. Wealth and Children: Adornment, Not Architecture

Ayah 46 begins with what the garden owner had boasted of in personal form: wealth and children.

The words are direct: wealth and children are the adornment of worldly life.

The word *zinah* means adornment, beauty, decoration, something that makes a thing attractive to the eye and desirable to the heart. This is not an insult. Adornment has beauty. Wealth can bring relief, generosity, responsibility, and ease. Children can bring love, continuity, tenderness, service, and joy. The Qur'an does not call them filth. It calls them adornment.

But an adornment is not the structure. It beautifies; it does not bear ultimate weight. If a person mistakes decoration for foundation, collapse begins inside the heart before it appears outside.

This is a precise correction to the garden owner. He said he had more wealth and stronger followers. The Qur'an now names wealth and children in a universal statement. They are not proof of permanent worth. They are not proof of Allah's final approval. They are not proof of better return. They are adornment of the near life.

The phrase *al-hayah ad-dunya* carries the meaning of the near life, the lower life, the life close at hand. It is immediate, visible, touchable. Wealth and children adorn that nearness. They make the near life feel compelling. But they do not automatically transfer into nearness to Allah.

Al-Tabari explains wealth and children as the beauty by which worldly life is adorned for people. Ibn Kathir says they are among the adornments of this life, but what remains and benefits is righteous deeds. Al-Muyassar makes the same contrast: wealth and children are beauty here, while enduring righteous deeds are better with Allah.

So the believer's task is not to despise wealth and children. It is to put them in their category. Receive adornment as adornment. Love children as trust. Use wealth as means. But do not ask either to become your final reward, your proof of worth, your guarantee of outcome, or your substitute for righteous deeds.

[PAUSE]

Adornments become dangerous when the heart starts building its house out of them.

### 6. The Enduring Righteous Deeds: What Stays With Your Lord

The contrast comes through the phrase *al-baqiyat as-salihat* - the enduring righteous deeds.

The word *baqiyat* comes from remaining, staying, enduring after other things pass. The word *salihat* comes from soundness, righteousness, repair, fitness. These are deeds that are right in themselves and remain in value with Allah.

The phrase has a range of tafsir discussion. Some early reports identify the enduring righteous deeds with words of remembrance such as glorification, praise, declaring Allah's oneness, and magnification. Others take it more broadly as all righteous actions that remain for the servant with Allah. Ibn Kathir mentions both the specific remembrances reported from the early Muslims and the broader meaning of righteous deeds. Al-Tabari also records interpretations and keeps the shared meaning: deeds of obedience that remain for the Hereafter. Al-Muyassar presents them broadly as righteous deeds whose reward remains.

That breadth is important. The Qur'an is not narrowing the believer's life; it is reordering it. Prayer remains. Charity remains. Truthfulness remains. Patience remains. Repentance remains. Service remains. Restraint remains. Qur'an recited and followed remains. A private tear remains. A temptation resisted remains. A child raised as an amanah rather than an ego-extension remains. Wealth spent for Allah remains. Forgiveness given for Allah remains.

Notice the grammar of comparison: they are better *with your Lord* in reward and better in hope. The location of value is shifted. Wealth and children are visible with people. Enduring deeds are valued with your Lord. That means some of the most important parts of your life may be invisible to the market, the family gathering, the public record, and even your own emotional need for recognition. They are with Allah.

The word *thawab* names reward, return, what comes back. The word *amal* here is hope, expectation, what the heart looks toward. The enduring righteous deeds are better in both directions: what they return and what they allow you to hope for.

This closes the parable's wound. The garden owner hoped in the garden. He expected better return because he read wealth as proof. Allah now says there is a better hope: the deeds that remain with your Lord.

[PAUSE]

What remains with Allah is more real than what impresses people.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### 1. The World Is a Sign Before It Is a Temptation

It is easy to read the water-and-vegetation parable as though the world is only a trap.

But the ayah begins with water from the sky. That beginning matters. The dunya is not a satanic invention. It is Allah's creation. Its beauty is not fake in the sense of being unreal. The rain is real, the green is real, the joy of provision is real, the love of children is real, the relief of wealth is real. The problem is that the world becomes a temptation when we stop reading it as a sign.

In daily life, this distinction changes everything. A meal can be either a veil or a sign. A salary can be either a veil or a sign. A child's laughter can be either a veil or a sign. A home can be either a veil or a sign. The object may be the same; the reading changes the heart.

The garden owner read his garden as self-proof. The believer reads the same kind of garden as divine gift. That is why Al-Kahf teaches us not to cure attachment by lying about beauty. The Qur'an does not say the plants were ugly. It says they grew by water Allah sent, then they dried. The believer's detachment is not built on denial. It is built on complete reading: origin, beauty, test, ending, return.

Ibn Kathir's framing of these ayahs as a similitude of worldly life helps us see that the dunya is being scaled, not erased. Al-Tabari's explanation of the rain and plant image keeps the concrete sequence intact. Al-Muyassar highlights how quickly the lush scene can become scattered matter. Together, the classical frame supports a balanced principle: worldly beauty is real, but it is not self-sustaining, not final, and not worthy of worship.

The modern resistance is understandable. Many people fear that religious seriousness will make them unable to enjoy life. They imagine that remembering death and impermanence will bleach color out of everything. But Qur'anic remembrance does the opposite when it is healthy. It gives color back to its Owner. It lets you enjoy without clinging, work without worshiping work, love without demanding that loved ones become eternal in this life, and grieve without believing the wind has the final word.

The practical discipline is to complete the reading of blessings. When something beautiful happens, ask four questions. Where did it descend from? What does it show me about Allah? How can I use it in obedience? What part of it will not remain? Those questions do not kill joy. They protect joy from becoming delusion.

[PAUSE]

A blessing becomes clearer, not smaller, when it points beyond itself.

### 2. The Green Season Is the Season of Greatest Spiritual Risk

Most of us assume hardship is the hardest test.

In many ways, it is. Pain can overwhelm. Loss can shake faith. Fear can narrow the heart. But the Qur'an repeatedly shows another danger: the test of the green season. When water descends, vegetation mingles, wealth grows, children flourish, reputation stabilizes, and the future looks manageable, the soul can quietly stop needing Allah.

No one usually says that out loud. The shift is subtler. Du'a becomes less urgent. Gratitude becomes more verbal than felt. Prayer becomes one item among many. Sins become easier to justify because life seems to be going well anyway. The person still believes in Allah, but the emotional center of reliance has migrated.

That is exactly what the two-gardens parable displayed. The owner did not begin in visible ruin. He began in success. His theological collapse happened while the garden stood. By the time the garden fell, the heart had already been exposed. So when Allah follows the story with the vegetation parable, He is teaching us to fear not only drought, but lushness.

This does not mean we ask Allah for hardship. The Prophet's way is to ask Allah for well-being, not trial. But when well-being comes, it must be governed by remembrance. The green season needs worship precisely because it feels safe. Wealth needs zakah and charity. Children need to be raised as trusts, not trophies. Health needs service. Influence needs humility. Knowledge needs fear of Allah. Beauty needs modesty and gratitude. Ease needs increased obedience.

The Qur'an gives similar warnings elsewhere. In 96:6-7, the human being transgresses when he sees himself as self-sufficient. In 89:15, when the human is honored and favored, he says his Lord has honored him; when provision is restricted, he says his Lord has humiliated him. Allah then corrects both readings. The problem is not honor or restriction in themselves, but the false theology we build around them.

The objection might be: can I ever feel happy, then? Yes. But happiness must remain porous to Allah. A sealed happiness becomes heedlessness. A grateful happiness becomes worship. The difference is whether the joy ends in the self or returns to the Giver.

So if you are in a green season, do not apologize for the rain. Just do not forget where it came from. Do not let mingled vegetation convince you that winter cannot come. Do not wait until the wind teaches you what dhikr could have taught gently.

[PAUSE]

The greenest moment is the time to anchor the heart before the wind begins.

### 3. Wealth and Children Are Adornment, So They Must Be Governed by What Remains

Wealth and children are among the strongest attachments in human life because they seem to answer two deep fears: the fear of vulnerability and the fear of disappearance.

Wealth says, "You will be safe." Children say, "You will continue." Wealth gives options, protection, mobility, relief from need. Children give love, legacy, name, memory, a sense that something of us will remain after us. These are powerful gifts. That is why the Qur'an names them directly.

But the ayah does not say wealth and children are the foundation of life. It says they are adornment. Beautiful, yes. Meaningful, yes. But not ultimate.

This is one of the most important corrections for religious life because many people bring worldly categories into worship. A person may assume Allah's favor is proven by financial ease. Another may assume children are proof of worth. Another may feel humiliated because they have neither. Another may turn children into a project of self-glory, measuring their own value through the child's achievements. Another may make wealth acquisition look religious by saying it is "for the family" while the heart is really serving status.

The Qur'an cuts through all of that. Wealth and children are adornment of the near life. They can become righteous means if governed by Allah. Wealth can be earned lawfully, purified, shared, and spent in mercy. Children can be loved, taught, protected, and entrusted back to Allah. But neither wealth nor children automatically become enduring righteous deeds. They become part of what remains only when they are folded into obedience.

This is why 18:46 is not anti-family and not anti-property. It is anti-misclassification. If wealth is adornment, do not worship it. If children are adornment, do not use them as idols of legacy. If righteous deeds remain, then make wealth and children servants of righteous deeds.

Al-Tabari's language around wealth and children as adornment helps keep the category clear. Ibn Kathir's discussion of enduring righteous deeds as what remains with Allah shows the path forward. The ayah is not asking us to choose between having wealth or having deeds in a simplistic sense. It is asking us which one governs the other.

In practice, this means asking: does my wealth increase my obedience or only my appetite? Does my love for my children make me more truthful, patient, generous, and prayerful, or more controlling and proud? Do I spend more energy decorating the near life than preparing what remains with my Lord? Do I grieve worldly lack more deeply than missed obedience?

[PAUSE]

The adornment is safe when it is worn by a heart that belongs to Allah.

### 4. Hope Has to Be Moved From What Scatters to What Remains

The final word of ayah 46 is *amala* - hope.

That is tender. Allah does not only say enduring righteous deeds are better in reward. He says they are better in hope. The Qur'an understands that human beings cannot live without expectation. We need something to look toward. We need a future in the heart. The question is not whether you will hope, but where hope will be housed.

The garden owner housed hope in the garden. He hoped it would not perish. He hoped the Hour would not come. Even if returned to his Lord, he hoped for better there because he had better here. His hope was built out of visible adornment. When the garden fell, his hope fell with it.

The Qur'an now relocates hope: the enduring righteous deeds are better in hope with your Lord.

This is a profound psychological mercy. If your hope is placed in what the winds scatter, your inner life will always be hostage to weather. If hope is placed in wealth, every financial tremor becomes existential. If hope is placed in children, every fear over them becomes unbearable. If hope is placed in reputation, every criticism becomes a threat to being. If hope is placed in career, every change in status becomes collapse. But when hope is placed in what remains with Allah, the heart can still grieve worldly changes without being spiritually annihilated by them.

This does not make the believer numb. The Qur'an never asks us to become stone. Ya'qub grieved Yusuf. Maryam suffered loneliness and fear. Musa felt pressure. The Prophet grieved rejection. But prophetic grief is not the same as hopelessness. Grief can live inside hope when hope is with Allah.

The practical application is to build a portfolio of deeds no wind can scatter. A prayer guarded when no one sees. Charity hidden from reputation. A reconciliation sought for Allah. A child taught to remember Allah without being used as your trophy. Wealth spent in a way that detaches the heart. Knowledge acted upon. Sins left quietly. Qur'an returned to again and again. These are not small because people cannot always see them. They are large because Allah keeps them.

And when the heart says, "But I need something to look forward to," the ayah answers: yes. Look forward to your Lord's reward. Look forward to what He preserves. Look forward to the outcome that does not yellow, break, and scatter.

Hope becomes stable only when it is placed where the wind cannot reach.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Water and the Remainder

These two ayahs move in four steps.

First, the Qur'an commands the example to be struck, making the image an impact rather than decoration. The listener is not merely informed that life is temporary; the listener is made to see life as rain-fed growth.

Second, the ayah traces the full worldly cycle: descent from the sky, mingled vegetation, dry fragments, scattered wind. The whole dunya is placed between divine sending and divine power. It is beautiful, but it is not self-sustaining.

Third, the next ayah names the specific adornments most likely to deceive: wealth and children. These are not condemned as evil; they are classified as adornment of the near life.

Fourth, the ayah reveals what belongs to the lasting category: enduring righteous deeds. They are better with your Lord in reward and better in hope. So the architecture moves from image to classification, from classification to reorientation, and from reorientation to hope.

### Questions to Carry

What green season in your life are you currently mistaking for permanence?

Do not answer only with fear. Answer with gratitude and honesty. Name what is growing, then name the fact that it grows by Allah and remains under Allah.

Where have wealth or children become more than adornment in your heart?

The sign may be panic, pride, control, comparison, or entitlement. When an adornment becomes architecture, the heart cannot bear even the thought of losing it. Return it to the category Allah gave it.

Which righteous deed are you neglecting because it does not decorate your near life?

Some deeds have no visible glamour. They do not increase status, income, or praise. But they remain with your Lord, and that is the only permanence that matters.

What hope are you placing in something the wind can scatter?

Be specific. A plan, a person, an achievement, a number, a timeline. Then ask what it would mean to move that hope toward Allah without abandoning responsibility.

How can you turn today's adornment into tomorrow's enduring deed?

Wealth can become charity. Children can become trusts raised with mercy. Time can become prayer. Influence can become service. The near life becomes safe when it is spent into what remains.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Worldly life is beautiful like rain-fed growth but fragile like dry fragments before the wind, so wealth and children must remain adornment while enduring righteous deeds become the hope we keep with Allah.**

### The Closing Invitation

Look at what is green in your life today and let it become dhikr before it becomes debris. Thank Allah for the water. Use the growth well. Love the adornments without building your soul out of them. Then send something ahead that will remain: a prayer, a truth, a gift, a repentance, a quiet obedience, a deed whose value is held with your Lord even if no one in the near life notices.

### Du'a

O Allah, do not let the beauty of the near life deceive us from You. Let our wealth and children be blessings that serve obedience, not adornments that own our hearts. Teach us to see every green season as Your gift and every wind as under Your command. Make us people of enduring righteous deeds, better in reward and better in hope with You. Ameen.
