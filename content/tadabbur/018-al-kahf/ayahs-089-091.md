# The People Without Cover

## Surah Al-Kahf (18:89-91)

[ayah:18:89] ثُمَّ أَتْبَعَ سَبَبًا

[ayah:18:90] حَتَّىٰٓ إِذَا بَلَغَ مَطْلِعَ ٱلشَّمْسِ وَجَدَهَا تَطْلُعُ عَلَىٰ قَوْمٍ لَّمْ نَجْعَل لَّهُم مِّن دُونِهَا سِتْرًا

[ayah:18:91] كَذَٰلِكَ وَقَدْ أَحَطْنَا بِمَا لَدَيْهِ خُبْرًا

*Thumma atba'a sababa. Hatta idha balagha matla'a al-shamsi wajadaha tatlu'u 'ala qawmin lam naj'al lahum min duniha sitra. Kadhalik, wa qad ahatna bima ladayhi khubra.*

**Then he followed a means, until, when he reached the rising-place of the sun, he found it rising upon a people for whom We had not made any cover from it. Thus. And We had encompassed what was with him in knowledge.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

The first horizon was sunset.

Dhul-Qarnayn reached the western edge, found the sun setting as it appeared from his place of vision, and found a people there. Allah gave him discretion. He answered with justice: the wrongdoer would face consequence and then return to the Lord, while the believer who acted righteously would receive the finest reward and easy dealing.

Now the journey turns.

He follows another means. The same man, the same pattern of movement, the same response to divine gift. But this time the horizon is the other side of light. Not the setting-place of the sun, but the rising-place. Not the sun descending into a dark spring from the traveler's vantage point, but the sun rising upon a people with no cover from it.

The scene is quieter than the western one. There is no direct divine choice stated here. No "either punish or take goodness." No speech from Dhul-Qarnayn. No policy announced. Instead, the Quran shows us exposure.

A people live beneath the sun without a *sitr* from it.

That word changes the atmosphere. The first journey tested what righteous power does when given discretion over a people. This journey tests how righteous power sees a people whose condition is defined by exposure, vulnerability, and lack of ordinary protection. The passage does not tell us to gawk at their strangeness. It tells us that Allah knew fully what Dhul-Qarnayn had with him, and by implication, what he met, what he carried, and what limits surrounded the encounter.

Classical tafsir often explains the people of this eastern horizon as having no buildings, trees, mountains, or coverings to shield them from the sun, though reports vary in their descriptive details. Ibn Kathir mentions that he reached the place of sunrise and found people with no shelter from it. Al-Tabari gathers reports about their lack of structures or cover, while al-Muyassar states the core meaning plainly: Allah had not made for them anything by which they could be covered from the sun. The Quran itself is restrained. It gives us the spiritually necessary line: they had no cover from it.

The restraint matters. The purpose is not ethnography. It is dhikr.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. "Then He Followed a Means" - Continuity Without Restlessness

The passage begins by repeating a sentence we have already heard: *thumma atba'a sababa* - then he followed a means.

The verb *atba'a* is Form IV from the root field of following, pursuing, and bringing one thing after another. It is active. It is not passive drifting. Dhul-Qarnayn does not merely find himself somewhere. He takes up a means and pursues it.

But notice the opening word: *thumma* - then. This is not the swift *fa* of immediate consequence from the earlier pair, "Allah gave him means, so he followed a means." Here the journey has sequence. One horizon is completed, then another means is followed. Righteous action is not a single burst of obedience. It is sustained responsiveness.

That is a hard lesson. Many people can respond once when a door opens. Fewer can keep following means after the first test has been completed. The soul enjoys beginnings. It likes the drama of the first horizon. But Dhul-Qarnayn's righteousness is shown through continuation. He does not treat one journey, one judgment, one successful use of authority as enough. He follows another means.

The repeated noun *sabab* keeps the theology anchored. These are still means, ropes, routes, access points. His reach remains derivative. He is not self-propelled in the absolute sense. He is using what Allah gave. The repetition guards the reader from being dazzled by travel. If the western journey made him look powerful, the eastern journey reminds us: the structure has not changed. Allah gave means. The servant follows means.

There is also an important difference between restless ambition and obedient continuation. Restless ambition moves because stillness feels unbearable. Obedient continuation moves because a trust remains. The Quran does not give us Dhul-Qarnayn's inner psychology in direct language, but the narrative placement teaches us to read his movement inside gratitude, not ego.

That is why the repeated sentence is not filler. It is a refrain of dependence.

### 2. "Until He Reached the Rising-Place of the Sun" - The Other Edge of Perception

The second journey mirrors the first. There we heard: until, when he reached the setting-place of the sun. Here we hear: until, when he reached the rising-place of the sun.

The word *matli'a* comes from the root field of rising, emerging, appearing, coming up into view. The sun that disappeared in the west now appears in the east. The two journeys form a wide arc across human perception: sunset and sunrise, departure and emergence, dimming and exposure.

But the Quran again speaks from the viewpoint of earthly encounter. Dhul-Qarnayn reaches the place where the sun rises from his horizon of travel. The point is not that the sun literally begins there in a cosmic sense. The point is that he reaches the eastern limit available to his journey, just as he reached the western limit before.

The two horizons also create a moral frame. At sunset, he met a people and received a choice about punishment or goodness. At sunrise, he meets a people marked by lack of covering. The first horizon emphasizes authority. The second emphasizes exposure.

This is how the Quran keeps power from becoming one-dimensional. A ruler does not only meet wrongdoers who need discipline. He also meets vulnerable people whose condition requires perception, restraint, and knowledge of limits. If all your authority knows how to do is judge, you will misread half the world. Some people before you are not primarily cases to be punished. They are people living in conditions you may not understand, beneath exposures you do not share.

The rising-place of the sun therefore becomes more than direction. It is a scene of vulnerability under brightness. The sun rises *upon* them. Light here is not only guidance imagery. It is heat, exposure, pressure, and the absence of shade.

The same created thing can be mercy in one context and trial in another. Sunlight grows crops, marks time, warms bodies, and guides travelers. But for a people without cover, it becomes a condition of exposure. The Quran teaches us not to romanticize creation in the abstract. We must notice how a mercy lands on different people in different circumstances.

### 3. "He Found It Rising Upon a People" - The Preposition of Exposure

The ayah says *wajadahaha tatlu'u 'ala qawm* - he found it rising upon a people.

Again, *wajada* gives the language of encounter and perception. Dhul-Qarnayn finds, sees, comes upon the reality of a place. But the verb attached to the sun is now *tatlu'u* - it rises, emerges, comes up.

The preposition *'ala* matters. The sun rises *upon* them. The word carries a sense of overness, covering from above, pressure from a higher direction. In many contexts, *'ala* can express honor, burden, authority, or simply spatial relation. Here, with the sun and a people without cover, it intensifies exposure. The sun is not merely rising in the distance. It is rising upon them.

That is the embodied force of the scene. These are not people watching a pretty sunrise from a shaded balcony. They are people under it.

The noun *qawm* appears again, just as it did at the western horizon. The Quran does not name them. It does not turn them into exotic spectacle. It does not satisfy the reader's curiosity with excessive description. The people are identified by their condition: Allah had not made for them a covering from the sun.

This is spiritually important because human beings often respond to unusual conditions with spectacle. We stare before we serve. We classify before we understand. We turn another people's hardship into a story about our own adventurousness. The Quran's restraint refuses that. It tells us enough to make us responsible, not enough to let us consume them.

Dhul-Qarnayn's gaze is also being purified. At the western horizon, the repetition of "he found" moved from sun to people. Here the same movement happens: he finds the sun rising, but the moral subject is the people beneath it. Geography becomes a test of seeing.

There is a lesson here for anyone who travels, studies, researches, photographs, reports, or leads. If you encounter a community under exposure, your first responsibility is not to make them material for your identity. It is to recognize that Allah already knows them fully.

### 4. "We Had Not Made for Them From Besides It a Cover" - Exposure Is Also Qadar

The phrase is striking: *lam naj'al lahum min duniha sitra* - We had not made for them, from besides it, a cover.

The verb *naj'al* comes from the root field of making, placing, appointing, causing to be in a certain state. It appears here after *lam*, making it jussive in form and negated in meaning: We did not make. This is not merely "they did not have." It is framed through Allah's making.

That does not mean the passage is asking us to be indifferent to their hardship. It means their condition is inside Allah's knowledge, decree, and wisdom, not outside His awareness. The people are not forgotten just because they are uncovered.

The word *sitr* comes from the field of covering, veiling, shielding, concealing, and protection. It can be physical covering, but the root also carries deep spiritual resonance. Allah is al-Sittir in the sense reported in the hadith tradition, the One who loves covering and modesty. In the Quran, covering can be mercy, protection, concealment from harm, or sometimes a veil that prevents perception depending on context.

Here, *sitr* is the physical absence of protection from the sun. The phrase *min duniha* means from besides it, apart from it, against it. There is nothing placed between them and the sun's force.

That image carries more than climate. It invites us to think about every form of exposure: people exposed to poverty without social cover, to violence without legal cover, to grief without family cover, to temptation without communal cover, to public shame without merciful cover, to systems they did not design and cannot easily escape.

But the ayah's wording keeps us from cheap pity. It says Allah had not made for them a cover. That means their condition is not a surprise to Allah, and it is not an invitation for the powerful to feel superior. It is an amanah for whoever encounters them with means.

Sometimes the people who lack cover are more known to Allah than the people who stand under structures and imagine themselves secure.

### 5. "Thus, and We Encompassed What Was With Him in Knowledge" - Allah Knows the Traveler Too

Then comes the closing: *kadhalik, wa qad ahatna bima ladayhi khubra*.

The word *kadhalik* - thus, like that, so it was - closes the scene with restraint. The Quran does not expand. It does not narrate a dramatic speech. It does not tell us exactly what Dhul-Qarnayn did with this people. It simply says: thus.

That restraint is part of the lesson. Not every encounter in a righteous life is narrated with outcome, dialogue, and policy. Sometimes Allah shows the condition, then reminds us that His knowledge encompasses the servant and what is with him.

The verb *ahatna* comes from the root field of surrounding, encompassing, encircling, comprehending from all sides. It is Form IV, and here it is Allah speaking in the majestic plural: We encompassed. Nothing about Dhul-Qarnayn's journey, equipment, army, resources, knowledge, condition, intention, or limits is outside Allah's knowledge.

The phrase *bima ladayhi* means what was with him, what he had at hand, what was in his possession or presence. This can include his means, resources, state, and circumstances. The word *khubra* is knowledge of reality, informed awareness, deep acquaintance with a matter. It is not shallow awareness. It is knowing from within and around.

So the passage closes by moving the reader's attention upward. We were looking at the sun over the people. Now we are told Allah's knowledge encompasses what is with Dhul-Qarnayn. The one who reaches horizons is himself surrounded by divine knowledge.

This is a needed protection. When a person has means and encounters exposed people, he may imagine himself as the one who knows. He has traveled. He has seen. He has capacity. But Allah says: We encompassed what was with him in knowledge. The traveler is known. The powerful one is known. The helper is known. The observer is known.

That means there are two exposures in the passage. The people are exposed to the sun. Dhul-Qarnayn is exposed to Allah's knowledge.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### 1. Repetition Is Where Sincerity Becomes Visible

The second journey begins the way the first active journey began: he followed a means.

At first, that can sound repetitive. But spiritual life is often tested in repetition. The first time you use a gift well, people may admire you. The second time, the drama is quieter. The third time, no one may notice. What remains then is the truth of your servanthood.

Dhul-Qarnayn follows another means. He does not let the moral clarity of the western judgment become a monument to himself. He does not stop at one successful act of rule. He continues.

This is one of the signs that his use of means is not simply conquest. Conquest often wants accumulation: more land, more glory, more proof of greatness. Obedient pursuit wants fulfillment of trust. The same outward movement can come from different inner engines. The Quran gives us the repeated *sabab* so we do not detach his movement from Allah's gift.

Ibn Kathir cites early explanations that Dhul-Qarnayn followed routes and paths through the earth. Mujahid and Qatadah are mentioned in that context. The physical meaning is clear enough: he had access to routes and used them. But the spiritual meaning sits inside the repeated structure: a servant keeps taking the available means without forgetting that they are means.

The Quran elsewhere teaches the value of this sustained obedience. In Surah Al-Ahqaf, those who say "our Lord is Allah" then remain upright receive angelic reassurance. In Surah Fussilat, the same idea appears: istiqamah after declaration. In Surah Al-Mulk, life and death are created to test who is best in deed, not who performs one impressive deed. The test is not only whether you can begin. It is whether you can continue without your beginning becoming vanity.

We need this because many of us have a spiritual pattern of bursts. We repent intensely, then fade. We serve with energy, then disappear. We begin projects with sincerity, then become bored when the work becomes ordinary. We use a means once and then think the story should now reward us with arrival.

Dhul-Qarnayn teaches another rhythm. Means are followed one after another. Horizons are reached one after another. Encounters are answered one after another. The servant keeps moving under Allah's knowledge.

The question for us is simple: after Allah helped you use one gift well, did you keep following the next means, or did you sit down inside the memory of your own usefulness?

### 2. Exposure Is Not Absence From Allah's Care

The people at the eastern horizon are described by what they lack: no cover from the sun.

That kind of description can frighten the heart. We often assume that visible lack means divine neglect. If a people have no cover, no protection, no apparent buffer from harsh conditions, the untrained soul asks: where is mercy?

But the ayah itself refuses that conclusion. It says, "We had not made for them from besides it a cover." Their lack is not outside Allah's making. Then the next ayah says Allah encompassed what was with Dhul-Qarnayn in knowledge. The whole scene sits inside divine knowledge.

This does not solve every emotional difficulty. The Quran is not asking us to speak glibly about hardship. Exposure is real. Heat is real. Vulnerability is real. Some people live without covers others take for granted. The point is not to deny their hardship. The point is to deny that hardship means Allah has forgotten them.

Al-Tabari's reports about the people emphasize their lack of ordinary shelter or structures from the sun. Al-Muyassar phrases it broadly and safely: Allah had not made for them anything by which they could be covered from the sun. The tafsir tradition notices the extremity of their condition, but the Quran's own theology keeps that condition under Allah's knowledge.

This is deeply connected to the Musa-Khidr passage before Dhul-Qarnayn. The ship looked damaged, but the damage was protection. The boy's death looked like pure loss, but hidden mercy related to the parents was disclosed. The wall looked like unpaid labor for an undeserving town, but it protected orphans' treasure until maturity. Al-Kahf is teaching us not to read the visible surface as the whole of reality.

Here, however, we are not given the hidden explanation in detail. We are given enough to know that Allah knows. Sometimes that is the form mercy takes in revelation: not an explanation of every exposure, but a firm refusal to let exposure be interpreted as abandonment.

In our world, people live under many suns without cover. Some are exposed economically. One medical bill, one job loss, one rent increase, and the heat hits directly. Some are exposed emotionally. No family system absorbs their grief. Some are exposed socially. A single mistake becomes public and there is no merciful covering. Some are exposed spiritually. They live in environments where every path of obedience is made harder.

The believer's response is not to stare and theorize. It is to remember that Allah knows them, and then to ask what means Allah has placed with us when we encounter them.

### 3. Do Not Turn Vulnerable People Into Spectacle

The eastern people could easily become spectacle.

The scene is unusual. A far horizon. The sun rising on people without cover. Reports in tafsir expand the imagination: people without conventional shelter, people living under extreme conditions, people who must adapt their lives to the sun's exposure. Human curiosity naturally wants more.

But the Quran does not indulge us.

It gives the condition, then closes: thus, and We encompassed what was with him in knowledge. No exotic catalog. No invitation to stare. No moral superiority for the traveler. No reduction of the people to strangeness.

This restraint is a profound ethic.

Many modern forms of attention turn vulnerable people into material. Poverty becomes photography. War becomes content. Refugees become symbols. Remote communities become documentaries consumed for emotional intensity. Even religious reminders can sometimes use the suffering of others as scenery for our own moral performance. We say, "Look at them," when the deeper command should be, "Remember Allah, and fulfill the trust that their exposure places upon you."

The Quranic style is different. It is exact enough to guide and restrained enough to protect. The people are not named because their identity is not the point. Their lack of cover is named because it matters to the moral scene. Then the narrative moves to Allah's encompassing knowledge.

This teaches an adab of looking. You may notice hardship. You may describe it truthfully. You may learn from it. But you may not consume it. The exposed are not props in the story of your insight.

This is especially important because Dhul-Qarnayn is powerful. Powerful travelers often return from vulnerable places with stories that enlarge themselves. "I saw such people. I reached such lands. I encountered such strange conditions." But the Quran does not make Dhul-Qarnayn the owner of their image. It places the scene under Allah's knowledge.

The same lesson applies to scholarship and da'wah. When we discuss communities, sinners, the poor, the culturally different, or people in unusual circumstances, we must ask whether our speech preserves their human dignity. Are we describing only what is needed for guidance, or are we feeding the appetite for spectacle?

Allah knows the people beneath the sun more completely than the traveler who sees them.

### 4. The Powerful Are Also Surrounded

The last line is the spiritual safety of the passage: We had encompassed what was with him in knowledge.

Dhul-Qarnayn is the one moving across horizons. He is the one with means. He is the one reaching places other people cannot reach. From the human angle, he appears to surround the earth. From the divine angle, he is surrounded by Allah's knowledge.

That reversal is essential.

Power creates the illusion of overview. The manager thinks they know the whole organization. The ruler thinks they know the country. The scholar thinks they know the tradition. The parent thinks they know the child's reality. The traveler thinks they understand a people because they have seen their condition. But the Quran breaks the illusion gently and firmly. Allah's knowledge encompasses what was with Dhul-Qarnayn.

The root behind *ahatna* gives a sense of surrounding from all sides. In the Quran, Allah's encompassing knowledge appears in several places. Allah encompasses what they do. Allah encompasses the disbelievers. Allah's knowledge encompasses all things. This encompassing is not only surveillance. It is perfect knowledge, power, and inability of anything to escape His grasp.

The word *khubra* deepens it. It is not shallow information. It is knowledge with full acquaintance. Allah knows the inner and outer, the apparent and hidden, the means and the motives, the resources and the limits, the actions and the consequences.

That means Dhul-Qarnayn cannot use the remoteness of the eastern horizon as cover from Allah. He may be far from ordinary observers, but he is not far from the One whose knowledge surrounds him. In fact, the more alone a person becomes with their power, the more this ayah should echo.

There is comfort here too. Allah knows what was with him. He knows the means you actually have, not the means people imagine you have. He knows your limits, constraints, pressures, and resources. He knows when you could have done more, and He knows when you truly could not. Human beings judge from fragments. Allah encompasses.

So this line both humbles and relieves. It humbles the powerful: you are known. It relieves the sincere: you are known accurately.

When you stand before exposed people with imperfect means, do not pretend to be savior. Do not run away from responsibility either. Act within the means Allah gave you, and remember that He encompasses what is with you in knowledge.

### 5. Covers Become Visible When You Meet Someone Without Them

One of the hidden mercies of this passage is that it teaches gratitude through contrast.

The people at the eastern horizon are described by their lack of *sitr*. They have no cover from the sun. The reader, if they are paying attention, is suddenly forced to notice something they may never have thanked Allah for: shade.

That is how many blessings work. They are quiet until they are absent. A roof is not dramatic until rain enters a home. Privacy is not dramatic until shame becomes public. Family support is not dramatic until grief has no one to receive it. A stable nervous system is not dramatic until anxiety makes the body feel unsafe. A community is not dramatic until someone has to practice faith alone. Covers are often invisible because they work by shielding us from what would otherwise become unbearable.

The Quran often teaches gratitude by making hidden protections visible. In An-Nahl, Allah mentions homes as places of rest, garments that protect from heat, and other garments that protect in battle. In Al-A'raf, clothing is described as something that covers nakedness and as adornment, while the garment of taqwa is better. In Quraysh, Allah reminds a people of food against hunger and security against fear. These are not abstract blessings. They are covers: against exposure, hunger, fear, shame, and harm.

This eastern scene joins that Quranic pedagogy. A people without cover from the sun make the covered person ask: what stands between me and the heat? What did Allah place between me and the pressures that would have broken me if they landed directly? Who has been shade for me? What laws, relationships, opportunities, health, wealth, or private mercies have functioned as *sitr* in my life?

The modern heart often notices what it lacks before it notices what covers it. We compare upward. We study people with more comfort, more wealth, more opportunity, more recognition. The eastern journey pulls the gaze another way. It says: look at people without a cover you possess. Not so you feel superior. Not so you reduce them to a lesson object. So your gratitude becomes truthful and your responsibility becomes sharper.

There is an objection here, and it is worth naming. Someone might say: "Isn't this using another people's hardship to make me grateful?" It can become that if the heart is careless. Gratitude becomes ugly when it says, "Alhamdulillah I am not them," and then walks away unchanged. Quranic gratitude is different. It says, "Alhamdulillah for the cover I did not earn, and now what does this cover require of me?" Blessing becomes responsibility. Shade becomes something to share. Privacy becomes a reason to cover others. Stability becomes a means to serve the exposed.

That is the difference between gratitude and relief at someone else's expense. Gratitude returns the blessing to Allah and then asks how to use it. Mere relief uses someone else's lack to feel better about itself.

Dhul-Qarnayn's presence in the scene matters because he is not only a witness. He has means. When he sees people without cover, the question is not only what he feels, but what his means can responsibly do. We are not told the outcome in detail, which protects the passage from becoming a simple policy manual. But the moral pressure remains: if Allah lets you notice a lack, perhaps He is asking what cover He placed with you.

So let the eastern sun expose your gratitude. Let it show you the covers you forgot. And let every cover become a trust, not a private luxury.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of The People Without Cover

The passage has a quiet three-part architecture.

First, repetition: Dhul-Qarnayn follows another means. The story returns to the theology of means so we remember that even repeated journeys are built on Allah's gifts.

Second, mirrored horizon: he reaches the rising-place of the sun, matching the earlier setting-place. The west tested justice under discretion. The east reveals exposure under the sun.

Third, divine enclosure: the people have no cover from the sun, but the scene closes with Allah encompassing what was with Dhul-Qarnayn in knowledge. Human beings may lack a physical covering, but neither they nor the powerful traveler are outside Allah's knowing.

The architecture moves from means, to horizon, to exposure, to encompassing knowledge. Dhul-Qarnayn reaches an edge, but Allah's knowledge surrounds the edge. The people are uncovered from the sun, but not uncovered from divine awareness. The traveler has means, but his means are known by the One who gave them.

### Questions to Carry

1. Do I continue using Allah's gifts after the first moment of inspiration passes?

Dhul-Qarnayn follows another means. Ask where you have stopped after one good use of a gift. Sustained obedience may be less dramatic than beginnings, but it is often where sincerity becomes visible.

2. How do I see people whose conditions are unlike mine?

The people without cover are not turned into spectacle by the Quran. When you encounter unfamiliar hardship, do you consume it, judge it, romanticize it, or let it become an amanah before Allah?

3. What covers has Allah given me that I rarely notice?

Shelter, family, privacy, legal protection, emotional support, shade, education, community, health. Many covers are invisible until we meet someone without them. Gratitude begins when ordinary protection becomes visible.

4. Where am I exposed and tempted to think Allah has forgotten me?

The absence of cover is not absence from Allah's knowledge. Let this ayah steady you without making your pain small. Allah knows the heat that reaches you.

5. Do I remember that my means are encompassed by Allah's knowledge?

You may have more capacity than some and less than others. Allah knows exactly what is with you. That should prevent arrogance when you can act and despair when your means are limited.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The eastern journey teaches that repeated obedience follows the means Allah gives, vulnerable people must not become spectacle, and even where human beings lack cover, both their exposure and our capacity are encompassed by Allah's perfect knowledge.**

### The Closing Invitation

Let this passage make you more grateful for covers you forgot were gifts, more careful with people whose exposure you do not share, and more honest about the means Allah has placed with you. Do not stare at vulnerability from a distance. Do not pretend to know more than Allah has shown. Follow the means you have, serve without spectacle, and remember that the One who sees the people under the sun also knows exactly what is with you.

### Du'a

O Allah, cover us with Your mercy, make us grateful for every protection we take for granted, and make us gentle with those living under exposures we do not understand. Do not let our means make us arrogant, and do not let our limits make us despair. Teach us to continue in obedience, to see vulnerable people with dignity, and to trust Your encompassing knowledge in every horizon You bring us to. Ameen.
