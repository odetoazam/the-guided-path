# When the Vulnerable Asked for a Wall

## Surah Al-Kahf (18:92-94)

[ayah:18:92] ثُمَّ أَتْبَعَ سَبَبًا

[ayah:18:93] حَتَّىٰٓ إِذَا بَلَغَ بَيْنَ ٱلسَّدَّيْنِ وَجَدَ مِن دُونِهِمَا قَوْمًا لَّا يَكَادُونَ يَفْقَهُونَ قَوْلًا

[ayah:18:94] قَالُوا۟ يَـٰذَا ٱلْقَرْنَيْنِ إِنَّ يَأْجُوجَ وَمَأْجُوجَ مُفْسِدُونَ فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ فَهَلْ نَجْعَلُ لَكَ خَرْجًا عَلَىٰٓ أَن تَجْعَلَ بَيْنَنَا وَبَيْنَهُمْ سَدًّا

*Thumma atba'a sababa. Hatta idha balagha bayna al-saddayn, wajada min dunihima qawman la yakaduna yafqahuna qawla. Qalu ya Dha al-Qarnayn, inna Ya'juja wa Ma'juja mufsiduna fi al-ard, fa-hal naj'alu laka kharjan 'ala an taj'ala baynana wa baynahum sadda.*

**Then he followed a means, until, when he reached between the two barriers, he found beyond them a people who could hardly understand speech. ## Introduction

The third journey begins with the same sentence.

Dhul-Qarnayn follows a means.

The repetition matters because the horizons have changed, but the theology has not. In the west, he found a people and answered with justice. In the east, he found a people under exposure and the Quran reminded us that Allah encompassed what was with him in knowledge. Now he follows another means and reaches a place between two barriers, where the crisis is neither sunset nor sunrise. It is vulnerability under threat.

The scene is different from the first two. At the western horizon, Allah speaks to Dhul-Qarnayn and gives him discretion. At the eastern horizon, the Quran speaks about a people without cover and then closes with Allah's encompassing knowledge. Here, the people themselves speak.

But even their speech is introduced through difficulty.

He finds a people who could hardly understand speech. Then those people manage to communicate one desperate request: Ya'juj and Ma'juj are spreading corruption in the land; will you take a payment from us if you make a barrier between us and them?

That is the human scene: a people at the edge of intelligibility, threatened by forces of corruption, standing before a man who has means they do not have. They do not ask for conquest. They do not ask for luxury. They ask for a wall.

Classical tafsir keeps this basic frame clear. Ibn Kathir describes the journey until Dhul-Qarnayn reaches between two barriers or mountains and encounters a people whose language was difficult to understand. Al-Tabari gathers reports around the barriers and explains the people's complaint that Ya'juj and Ma'juj were causing corruption in their land. Al-Muyassar summarizes the request plainly: these people ask Dhul-Qarnayn to take payment so he will make between them and Ya'juj and Ma'juj a barrier preventing their harm.

This passage is the plea before the construction. It is the moment before Dhul-Qarnayn refuses extraction and calls them into participation. If the next ayahs show righteous power building, these ayahs show why righteous power was needed.

They show a community whose first problem is not that they lack money. They offer money. Their problem is that they lack protection.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. "Then He Followed a Means" - The Third Refrain of Dependent Power

The passage opens: *thumma atba'a sababa*.

By now, the phrase has become the rhythm of Dhul-Qarnayn's story. Allah gave him means. He followed a means. Then he followed a means. Then again, he followed a means. The repetition is almost austere. The Quran does not decorate the transitions. It does not narrate the travel in romantic detail. It keeps bringing us back to the same theological fact: the servant moves by means Allah gave.

The verb *atba'a* is active pursuit. Dhul-Qarnayn is not carried passively. He takes the route, follows the access point, uses the rope. But the noun *sabab* keeps his action from becoming self-originating. It is a means, not independent power.

The word *thumma* also carries sequence. This is not one feverish rush. It is ordered continuation. He has moved from west to east, and now toward the place between barriers. Each journey is a new scene, but the refrain teaches that righteous authority does not retire into one good decision. It keeps responding to the next trust.

This is important because one of the dangers of service is becoming satisfied with yesterday's usefulness. You judged well once. You helped once. You gave once. You showed courage once. Then the soul begins to live off memory. But Dhul-Qarnayn's story does not let him become a monument to a past act. It keeps him moving by means.

The same pattern appears elsewhere in the Quran's moral teaching. Belief is paired again and again with righteous action, not because one act is enough, but because iman has to keep taking shape. Patience is praised not as a mood but as perseverance. Taqwa is not only a first avoidance; it is a continuing watchfulness. Dhul-Qarnayn's repeated following of means gives narrative form to that continuity.

The third refrain also prepares us for the third kind of test. The first was judgment. The second was exposure. The third is protection against corruption. Righteous power cannot specialize in only one virtue. It must know how to judge, how to see vulnerability, and how to build against harm.

### 2. "Between the Two Barriers" - The Geography of a Gap

The next phrase is *hatta idha balagha bayna al-saddayn* - until, when he reached between the two barriers.

Again, *balagha* means he reached, arrived, attained the point. But what he reaches is not a horizon this time. It is an in-between. The word *bayna* means between, the space separating two things and relating them at once. He arrives not at the west, not at the east, but at a gap.

The two *saddayn* are barriers, often understood in tafsir as two mountains or mountain walls. The root field behind *sadd* carries blocking, closing, obstructing. A *sadd* prevents passage. It is a barrier by nature.

But the scene is paradoxical. There are already two barriers, yet the people still need a barrier. They live between natural obstructions, but the dangerous opening remains. Geography has given them partial protection, not complete safety. The vulnerable space is not the mountain itself. It is the passage between.

This is a subtle and powerful image. Many communities are harmed not because there is no structure at all, but because there is a gap in the structure. There is law, but no enforcement. There is family, but no mercy. There is knowledge, but no wisdom. There is wealth, but no justice. There are walls on both sides, but the passage remains open to corruption.

Dhul-Qarnayn reaches the in-between because the in-between is where intervention is needed.

The Quran later uses another word for what he will make: not merely *sadd* but *radm*, a filled barrier. That difference belongs to the next passage, but it is prepared here. The people ask for a *sadd*, something to block. They know their problem in simple terms: make a barrier between us and them. Dhul-Qarnayn will answer with more thoroughness than they requested.

But before building anything, the Quran lets us feel the geography of need. A gap can be as dangerous as open land. The place between two strengths can become the place of greatest vulnerability.

### 3. "A People Who Could Hardly Understand Speech" - Vulnerability at the Level of Language

Dhul-Qarnayn finds *qawman la yakaduna yafqahuna qawla* - a people who could hardly understand speech.

The word *qawm* again gives us a people, a community. The Quran has used this word at each major encounter: a people at the western horizon, a people at the eastern horizon, and now a people between barriers. Dhul-Qarnayn's journeys are not about empty geography. Every place becomes morally serious because people are there.

The phrase that describes them is remarkable. *La yakaduna* means they hardly, scarcely, almost do not. The verb from *kada* often marks near-occurrence or near-nonoccurrence depending on the construction. Here, under negation, it intensifies difficulty: they could barely do the next act.

That next act is *yafqahuna qawla* - to understand speech. The root of *fiqh* is not merely hearing sounds. It is grasping meaning, understanding with penetration. *Qawl* is speech, a spoken statement, communicative utterance. The problem is not that there is no sound. The problem is comprehension.

Classical tafsir commonly explains this as a language barrier: Dhul-Qarnayn came upon people whose language was difficult for others to understand or who could hardly understand the speech addressed to them. The exact mechanics need not be overpressed. The Quran's wording places communication difficulty at the center of the encounter.

That matters because vulnerability is not only physical. It can be linguistic. A people who cannot easily understand speech are easy to exploit. Contracts can be imposed on them. Threats can confuse them. Help can fail because the helper does not understand the need. Power can misread them and then blame them for being unclear.

And yet these people speak in the next ayah.

That is deeply moving. The Quran first tells us how hard communication is, then preserves their request. Their speech crosses the barrier of near incomprehension because their need is urgent. Somehow, through whatever means Allah allowed, their plea reaches Dhul-Qarnayn.

The sequence teaches adab. When someone struggles to communicate, do not assume they have nothing to say. Do not confuse difficulty of expression with absence of truth. The oppressed, the poor, the foreigner, the child, the traumatized person, the person without polished language, the person outside your culture, all may struggle to make themselves understood. But their need may still be real, and their words may be carrying a trust you are accountable to hear.

### 4. "Ya'juj and Ma'juj Are Corrupters in the Land" - Naming Harm Without Exaggeration

The people say: *inna Ya'juja wa Ma'juja mufsiduna fi al-ard*.

They begin with emphasis: *inna*. Their claim is not casual. They are not gossiping. They are pleading from lived harm. Ya'juj and Ma'juj are named as the source of the danger, but the Quran does not pause here to satisfy curiosity about their identity, origin, number, or later eschatological role. In this passage, what matters is their moral action: they are *mufsidun fi al-ard*.

The word *mufsidun* is an active participle from the field of fasad: corruption, disorder, spoilage, ruin, decay of what should be sound. In Form IV, it carries the sense of causing corruption, spreading disorder, making things unsound. They are not merely sinners in private. They are corrupters in the land.

The phrase *fi al-ard* expands the scope. Their harm is not contained inside themselves. It enters the land, the social order, the people's safety. Fasad in the Quran often names damage that spreads: bloodshed, tyranny, social ruin, moral disorder, economic injustice, and the breaking of what Allah made sound.

This is why the people's request is not petty. They are not asking for a wall because they dislike neighbors. They are asking because corruption has become a recurring threat.

The Quran elsewhere treats fasad with great seriousness. In Al-Baqarah, when hypocrites are told not to spread corruption in the land, they claim they are only reformers. In Al-Qasas, Qarun is told to seek the Hereafter with what Allah gave him, not to seek corruption in the land, because Allah does not love corrupters. In Al-Ma'idah, severe public violence and terror are framed through making war against Allah and His Messenger and striving for corruption in the land. Fasad is not a decorative moral word. It is the unraveling of trust, safety, and order.

At the same time, the people's wording is precise. They name the corruption; they do not ask Dhul-Qarnayn to annihilate everyone beyond them. They ask for a barrier between us and them. That distinction matters. Fear can make people ask for excessive responses. Here, their request is protective: put something between the vulnerable and the corrupters.

The moral target is containment of harm.

### 5. "May We Assign for You a Payment" - The Transaction the Vulnerable Know

Then they ask: *fa-hal naj'alu laka kharjan* - so may we make for you a payment?

The question particle *hal* softens the request into an offer. They are not commanding him. They are asking whether they may assign something to him. The verb *naj'alu* means to make, place, assign, set up. They want to place for him *kharj* - tribute, payment, a levy, something drawn out as compensation.

The root field of *kharj* relates to coming out. A payment comes out from one's possession. A tax or tribute is something extracted or assigned. The word is economically charged. The vulnerable people assume protection must be purchased from power.

That assumption is painfully human.

When people have lived under threat, they often learn the transaction of survival. Pay the stronger group so they do not harm you. Pay the ruler so he protects you. Pay the gatekeeper so he opens the way. Pay the one with means because your need gives them leverage. The weak become accustomed to buying safety.

Their offer is not necessarily blameworthy. It may be the only category they know. They have a problem, Dhul-Qarnayn has capacity, and they propose compensation. But the next ayah will reveal the beauty of righteous power precisely because he refuses to extract from their desperation. He will say that what his Lord has established him in is better, then ask them to help with strength.

So this ayah sets the stage. It shows what the vulnerable expect from the powerful before it shows how the righteous powerful person breaks that expectation.

There is also a contrast with the man of the two gardens earlier in Al-Kahf. That man measured himself through abundance, numbers, and imagined permanence. Here, a vulnerable people offer wealth because they need safety. Wealth can be arrogance in one scene and desperation in another. The Quran trains us not to treat money with one flat reading. Sometimes money is a temptation to pride. Sometimes it is a plea from fear.

The question is what power does when money is offered from vulnerability.

### 6. "Between Us and Them a Barrier" - The Moral Need for Boundaries

The request closes: *'ala an taj'ala baynana wa baynahum sadda* - on condition that you make between us and them a barrier.

The repetition of *bayna* is the heart of the request: between us and between them. The people know that their problem is proximity without protection. They do not ask to become like Ya'juj and Ma'juj. They do not ask to dominate them. They ask for a boundary.

The word *sadd* returns from the earlier "two barriers." They are between natural barriers, but they ask for a made barrier in the remaining opening. A boundary is needed where harm crosses.

In spiritual life, boundaries are often misunderstood. Some people treat boundaries as lack of mercy. Others treat boundaries as hostility. But the Quran shows here that a barrier can be mercy for the vulnerable. When fasad spreads across an opening, love may require a wall.

This has many forms. A family may need a boundary against an abusive person. A community may need a boundary against predatory behavior. A believer may need a boundary against environments that repeatedly pull them into sin. A society may need boundaries against exploitation, misinformation, violence, or corruption. A heart may need a boundary around what it watches, hears, and repeatedly allows inside.

The Quran is not simplistic about walls. Some walls protect arrogance, like the imagined security of people who think fortresses will save them from Allah. Some walls imprison. Some walls divide unjustly. But some walls protect the weak from corrupters. The moral question is not "wall or no wall" in the abstract. The moral question is: what is being protected, from what harm, by whose authority, with what justice, and under what remembrance of Allah?

The people between the barriers know enough to ask for one necessary thing: make between us and them a barrier.

And that request becomes the doorway into Dhul-Qarnayn's most famous act.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### 1. Some People Need You to Hear Before You Can Help

The people in this passage can hardly understand speech, yet the Quran preserves their speech.

That is a mercy and a rebuke.

It is mercy because Allah does not erase people whose communication is difficult. Their plea is not lost. Their words enter revelation. It is a rebuke because human beings often dismiss those who cannot speak in the dominant language, the polished vocabulary, the accepted emotional tone, or the format power prefers.

Think about how many vulnerable people are unheard before they are helped. A refugee struggles to explain danger in a new language. A child cannot name abuse in adult terms. A worker does not know the legal vocabulary for exploitation. A spouse in distress speaks through tears and fragments. A community harmed by corruption may describe the problem in language outsiders find unsophisticated. The powerful listener hears difficulty and mistakes it for unreliability.

This ayah teaches us to slow down.

Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir mention the difficulty of speech or language in this encounter. The Quran itself makes the communication barrier part of the scene before giving us the request. That ordering matters. It tells the reader: do not treat clear communication as the price of compassion. Sometimes the need is real even when the words are hard to receive.

The Quran repeatedly dignifies people whom others might ignore. In An-Nisa, believers are asked what is wrong with them that they do not fight in the cause of Allah and for the oppressed men, women, and children who cry out for rescue from a wrongdoing town. In Al-Balad, the path of ascent includes freeing a neck and feeding in a day of hunger an orphan near of kin or a poor person in dust. In Al-Hujurat, human difference in peoples and tribes is framed as a path to knowing one another, not despising one another.

The first act of help is often not building. It is listening across difference.

That listening is not sentimental. It takes work. It may require translation, patience, humility, repetition, and the willingness to be corrected. It may require admitting that the categories you came with are not enough. Dhul-Qarnayn has reached people who barely understand speech, but he does not move on because communication is difficult. He remains long enough for their need to be known.

There is a personal test here for every person with capacity. Who do you find hard to understand? The poor? The elderly? Young people? Converts? Immigrants? People with trauma? People whose anger is rough because their wounds were ignored? People outside your class or education? The question is not whether all speech is equally wise. The question is whether difficulty makes you abandon the obligation to hear.

The Quran lets the barely understood speak.

### 2. Fasad Is Harm That Spreads Beyond the Sinner

The people's complaint is not merely "Ya'juj and Ma'juj are bad." It is: they are *mufsidun fi al-ard* - corrupters in the land.

That language matters because the Quran distinguishes private sin from spreading ruin. Every sin harms the soul, but fasad has a social quality. It spoils what others depend on. It makes the land unsafe, trust unstable, relationships fearful, and ordinary life difficult.

In Al-Kahf, this matters because the whole surah has been training us to see beyond surfaces. The wealthy garden owner did not only hold wealth; he developed a corrupt reading of reality. Iblis earlier in the surah is rejected as an ally because his enmity and misguidance are not neutral. The criminals on the Day of the Book fear a record that leaves nothing out. Now Ya'juj and Ma'juj are introduced not through speculation, but through the effect of their conduct: fasad.

Classical tafsir on this passage often describes Ya'juj and Ma'juj as harming the people, spreading destruction, or corrupting their land. Al-Muyassar frames them plainly as causing corruption in the earth. This grounding is important. The Quran does not invite us first into fantasy about them. It invites us into moral clarity about corruption.

Modern readers can become fascinated by Ya'juj and Ma'juj as apocalyptic mystery. There is a place for studying the broader eschatological material carefully, but this passage itself begins somewhere more immediate: people are being harmed. A righteous servant with means is asked to help. The first duty is not speculation. It is protection from fasad.

This principle applies widely. When corruption spreads, the question is not only whether individual sinners can be excused, understood, or analyzed. The question is also who is being harmed by the spread. Addiction industries, predatory lending, abusive leadership, public dishonesty, trafficking, violent disorder, spiritual manipulation, and normalized obscenity all have a fasad quality because they do not remain inside one person. They reshape the environment others must live in.

The Quranic response to fasad is not panic. It is moral seriousness. Name the harm. Protect the vulnerable. Refuse to become unjust in the process. Use means without forgetting Allah.

The people between the barriers teach us that sometimes the most faithful sentence a vulnerable community can utter is simply: corruption is reaching us, and we need a barrier.

### 3. The Vulnerable Often Expect Extraction Because That Is What Power Taught Them

The people offer *kharj*.

They do not say, "Please help us for Allah's sake." They say, in effect, may we make for you a payment so you will build a barrier? Their offer is practical, and perhaps sincere. But it also reveals the world they know: protection has a price.

This is one of the saddest patterns in human life. The vulnerable learn to negotiate with power because they have rarely seen power serve without taking. They expect extraction because extraction has been normal. The ruler taxes. The strongman demands tribute. The gatekeeper asks for a fee. The helper wants control. The expert sells access. The one who can solve the problem becomes expensive precisely because others are desperate.

The next ayah will show Dhul-Qarnayn breaking that pattern. But these ayahs let us sit with the expectation before the refusal arrives.

That expectation exists today in many forms. A family in crisis expects that every professional will drain them. A poor community expects that politicians will appear only when votes are needed. A student expects that mentorship always comes with strings. A victim expects that help will require public exposure of their pain. A masjid volunteer expects that leaders will use their labor but not dignify them. Over time, people stop believing in service without extraction.

The Quran gives us a model of righteous power precisely because this pattern is so common. Dhul-Qarnayn has every worldly reason to accept the payment. The people offered it. The task is difficult. The threat is real. He could justify the arrangement. Yet he will answer by remembering what his Lord has already established him in.

The contrast should make us examine ourselves. When people need what we have, do we become more generous or more expensive? When their desperation gives us leverage, do we feel the fear of Allah? Do we call our extraction "market rate," "what I deserve," "how the world works," while forgetting that Allah may have given us means precisely so we could relieve someone without turning their fear into revenue?

This does not mean all payment is forbidden. Workers deserve wages. Expertise can be compensated. Institutions need resources. The Quran is not abolishing economic exchange. The issue is moral posture: do we see vulnerable need as a chance to serve or a chance to profit?

Dhul-Qarnayn's refusal in the next ayah will only be beautiful if we first feel how normal their offer was.

### 4. Boundaries Can Be Mercy

The people ask for a *sadd* between themselves and Ya'juj and Ma'juj.

In some spiritual circles, boundaries are spoken of with suspicion, as though mercy always means access. But the Quran does not teach that. Mercy sometimes opens a door. Mercy sometimes closes one. Mercy sometimes feeds, forgives, listens, and embraces. Mercy sometimes builds a barrier so the vulnerable can breathe.

The question is not whether a boundary exists. The question is whether the boundary serves justice, protects life, and remains under Allah's guidance.

The Quran is full of meaningful separation. The sea is split for Musa and Bani Israel, becoming rescue for one people and judgment for Pharaoh's army. The believers are told not to take certain enemies as intimate allies when those enemies actively wish them harm. The Prophet is told to turn away beautifully from certain forms of rejection. In Al-Kahf itself, the youths withdraw into the cave to protect their faith. Separation can be obedience.

But separation can also be sin when it is built from arrogance, racism, cruelty, or refusal of obligation. That is why this passage is so important. It gives us a boundary request with a moral cause: Ya'juj and Ma'juj are corrupters in the land. The people are vulnerable. The barrier is requested not to preserve superiority, but to block harm.

This distinction is desperately needed in personal life. A person may need a boundary with someone who repeatedly abuses, manipulates, or drags them into disobedience. That boundary is not lack of forgiveness. It may be the condition that allows the heart to remain safe enough to forgive without being destroyed. A community may need a boundary against a harmful teacher, a predatory donor, or a pattern that keeps wounding people. That is not fitnah-making. It may be the end of fitnah.

At the same time, boundaries should not become idols. The people ask Dhul-Qarnayn to make a barrier; they do not worship the barrier. The next passage will show him returning the whole project to Allah. The wall protects, but Allah remains the Lord of protection.

So the believer learns to ask for and build boundaries without making them ultimate. Use the means. Close the gap. Block the corruption. But keep the heart attached to the One who owns every means and every outcome.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of When the Vulnerable Asked for a Wall

The passage moves in four stages.

First, continuation: Dhul-Qarnayn follows a means again. The repeated refrain keeps his power dependent and his service sustained.

Second, location: he reaches between the two barriers. Unlike the west and east, this scene is not a horizon but a gap. The danger lives in the opening between existing protections.

Third, communication: he finds a people who can hardly understand speech. The Quran makes their difficulty known before preserving their plea, teaching us that difficult communication does not erase real need.

Fourth, request: they name Ya'juj and Ma'juj as corrupters in the land and offer payment for a barrier between themselves and the harm. Their sentence holds the whole crisis: fasad is spreading, vulnerability is exposed, power has arrived, and the vulnerable expect protection to require payment.

The architecture prepares the next passage. Before Dhul-Qarnayn refuses tribute and builds with them, the Quran makes us hear the people whose fear made the offer. Righteous power is not abstract. It is tested when barely understood people ask for protection from corruption crossing the gap.

### Questions to Carry

1. Whose speech do I dismiss because it is hard for me to understand?

The people could hardly understand speech, yet their plea is preserved in revelation. Ask whether your impatience with someone's language, emotion, class, age, or culture has made you deaf to a real need.

2. Where is fasad crossing an open gap in my life or community?

The people are not vague. They name corruption and ask for a barrier. What harm keeps entering because a boundary has not been built?

3. Do vulnerable people expect extraction from me?

When others need your skill, money, position, or access, do they brace themselves to be used? Let this ayah prepare you for Dhul-Qarnayn's refusal: what Allah gave you may be better than anything desperation can offer.

4. Do I know the difference between a merciful boundary and an arrogant wall?

Some walls protect the weak. Some walls protect the ego. Examine what your boundaries are guarding and whether they are built under Allah's guidance.

5. Can I help people participate in their own protection?

The next ayah will show Dhul-Qarnayn refusing payment but requesting strength. Before you solve a problem for someone, ask whether there is a way to build with them that restores dignity rather than creating dependence.

**The third journey teaches that righteous power must hear vulnerable people across communication barriers, name spreading corruption without exaggeration, and prepare to build merciful boundaries without exploiting those who ask for protection.**

### The Closing Invitation

Carry this passage into every encounter where someone struggles to make their need understood. Listen longer than is convenient. Do not turn communication difficulty into dismissal. Name corruption where it spreads, but do not let fear make you unjust. And when people ask you for help from a place of vulnerability, let the next ayah already begin working in your heart: what your Lord has given you is better than what desperation can pay.

### Du'a

O Allah, make us listeners to those who are hard to understand, protectors of those harmed by corruption, and builders of boundaries that serve justice and mercy. Do not let us exploit need, ignore vulnerable speech, or confuse arrogance with protection. Give us the wisdom to close harmful gaps, the humility to use only the means You gave, and the sincerity to serve without extracting from those who are afraid. Ameen.
