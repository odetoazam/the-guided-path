# Eyes Covered From Remembrance

## Surah Al-Kahf (18:101)

[ayah:18:101] ٱلَّذِينَ كَانَتْ أَعْيُنُهُمْ فِى غِطَآءٍ عَن ذِكْرِى وَكَانُوا۟ لَا يَسْتَطِيعُونَ سَمْعًا

*Alladhina kanat a'yunuhum fi ghita'in 'an dhikri wa kanu la yastati'una sam'a.*

**Those whose eyes had been under a covering from My remembrance, and they were unable to hear.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

Hell has just been presented.

The previous ayah did not argue about Jahannam. It showed it. The denied reality was brought into view before those who had covered over truth. The surah had moved from the collapse of the barrier, to the surging crowd, to the Horn, to the total gathering, to the display of Hell itself. Every stage stripped away another layer of distance.

Now, before moving to the next rebuke, the Qur'an pauses over the people who face that display.

It tells us what was wrong with them before Jahannam became visible. The problem was not that the signs were absent. The problem was not that remembrance never came. The problem was not that revelation lacked clarity. The problem was that their eyes were in a covering from My remembrance, and they were unable to hear.

That is frightening because it moves the discussion from the outside world into the inner faculties. Sight and hearing are not minor details in the Qur'an. They are doors of guidance. Through sight, a person beholds signs in creation, history, scripture, consequence, mercy, decay, beauty, death, and the self. Through hearing, a person receives revelation, reminder, counsel, warning, correction, and the cry of another human being asking for justice. When these doors become blocked, a person can stand inside a universe full of signs and still live as if Allah is absent.

Classical tafsir reads the ayah with this moral seriousness. Ibn Kathir says these were people who neglected Allah's reminder, turning a blind eye and a deaf ear to it, refusing guidance and truth. Al-Tabari explains that they did not look into Allah's signs in a way that would make them reflect, remember, turn back, affirm Allah's oneness, and submit to His command and prohibition. He also transmits from Mujahid that "they could not hear" means they did not understand or know. Al-Muyassar states plainly that their eyes in worldly life were covered from Allah's remembrance, so they did not see His signs, and they could not bear hearing the proofs that lead to faith in Allah and His Messenger.

There is no separate asbab al-nuzul reported for this ayah. It belongs to the surah's closing eschatological movement, where the hidden state of the heart is finally connected to the visible outcome of the Hereafter. Al-Kahf has been teaching us all along that things are not always what they seem. Here it teaches the most severe version of that lesson: a person may appear to have eyes and ears, while the faculties that should have received guidance have become covered and disabled.

[PAUSE]

The tragedy is not only that they did not see. It is that they had trained themselves not to receive sight.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. "Those Who" - The Description Behind the Display

The ayah opens with *alladhina* - "those who." Grammatically, it reaches back to the disbelievers mentioned in the previous ayah. Jahannam was presented to the disbelievers; now the Qur'an describes them. It does not leave kufr as a label floating in the air. It gives the inward anatomy of it.

That movement matters. A label can become too easy. "Disbelievers" can sound like a category outside the listener. But "those whose eyes had been under a covering from My remembrance and who were unable to hear" makes the category morally visible. The Qur'an is not merely naming their final group. It is showing the disease by which they arrived there.

The relative construction also makes the description feel almost inseparable from the identity. These are not people who accidentally missed a message once. They are those characterized by covered sight and disabled hearing. Their condition has become stable enough that it can identify them.

This is one of the Qur'an's recurring methods. It does not only say "the righteous" and "the wicked"; it describes how righteousness and wickedness work in the faculties. The muttaqin are those who believe in the unseen, establish prayer, and spend from what Allah provides. The heedless are those who have hearts by which they do not understand, eyes by which they do not see, and ears by which they do not hear. The hypocrites have a disease in their hearts. The arrogant turn away as if they did not hear. Human identities are revealed through repeated responses.

So the ayah invites us to fear the formation of a self. You are not only what you claim. You are what your eyes repeatedly refuse to notice. You are what your ears repeatedly refuse to receive. You are what happens to your faculties after years of obedience or years of avoidance.

[PAUSE]

A person becomes, slowly, the kind of listener and seer they practice being.

### 2. "Their Eyes Had Been" - Sight as a Trust, Not a Neutral Instrument

The phrase *kanat a'yunuhum* means "their eyes had been." The verb *kanat* gives the condition a past continuity. It was not merely that their eyes are covered at the moment of judgment. Their eyes had been in that state. The Day reveals what had already become true.

The word *a'yun* is the plural of eye. It comes from a semantic field that includes the physical eye, a spring or source, and the precious center of attention. The eye is not merely a biological organ in Qur'anic language. It is tied to perception, recognition, tears, longing, vigilance, betrayal, comfort, and witness. Eyes can overflow with tears when they recognize truth. Eyes can be lowered out of modesty. Eyes can be dazzled by terror. Eyes can betray what the chest conceals.

Here the eyes are not absent. They exist. The people are not physically eyeless. The problem is that the eyes are under a covering with respect to remembrance. This distinction is spiritually important. The Qur'an often speaks to people who can physically see while failing to perceive. They can look at the sky and not receive it as sign. They can see the alternation of night and day and not become humble. They can watch nations rise and fall and not learn. They can witness mercy in their own lives and call it luck. They can see death take others and still live as if death forgot their address.

Al-Tabari's explanation highlights this: they were not looking into Allah's signs in a way that would make them reflect and take lesson. That is the difference between visual contact and guided sight. The eye may meet the sign, but the heart refuses to let the sign travel inward.

There is also a subtle mercy in the ayah's wording. Because sight is a trust, it can be healed before the final display. A person can ask Allah to restore perception. A person can choose to look again at what they have been avoiding. The Qur'an came while eyes were still open in this life. The tragedy of 18:101 is not that sight was never offered. It is that the offered sight was covered from remembrance until Jahannam itself had to become visible.

[PAUSE]

If the eye does not learn to see Allah's signs now, the Hereafter will teach it by exposure.

### 3. "In a Covering" - When a Veil Becomes an Environment

The phrase *fi ghita'in* means "in a covering." The word carries the sense of something that covers, veils, wraps, conceals, or prevents access. But the preposition *fi* is especially striking. The ayah does not simply say there was a covering over their eyes. It says their eyes were in a covering.

That gives the image a more enveloping force. A covering over the eyes can sound like a cloth placed externally. Eyes in a covering suggests an environment of concealment. They live inside it. Their way of seeing is surrounded by it.

This is how spiritual blindness often works. It rarely begins as a total darkness. It begins as a filter. A person sees, but through appetite. A person sees, but through pride. A person sees, but through resentment. A person sees, but through political loyalty, inherited prejudice, market desire, public image, or fear of losing comfort. After a while, the filter becomes normal. It no longer feels like a veil. It feels like "how things are."

That is one reason this ayah is so relevant to modern life. We live inside curated fields of perception. Algorithms learn what we want to see and return more of it. Social groups reward certain interpretations and punish others. Careers train us to value what can be measured. Entertainment trains us to flee silence. Consumer life trains us to see the world as material for appetite. None of these needs to announce itself as disbelief. It simply teaches the eye to avoid remembrance.

The Qur'an gives related images elsewhere. Some hearts have coverings so they do not understand revelation. Some ears have heaviness. A barrier can be placed between a person and the Qur'an when they refuse it. In Surah Al-Jathiyah, Allah describes one who takes desire as a god, whose hearing and heart are sealed and over whose sight is a covering. These are not random metaphors. They describe the cumulative effect of refusing guidance.

The wording distinction matters. If the ayah had only said they "did not see," we might imagine a lack of information. But "their eyes were in a covering" tells us that something stood between perception and remembrance. The issue is moral occlusion, not mere data shortage.

[PAUSE]

Sometimes the most dangerous darkness is the one that has become familiar enough to call normal.

### 4. "From My Remembrance" - The Object of Avoided Sight

The covering is *'an dhikri* - from My remembrance. Not from information. Not from general spirituality. Not from moral culture. From My remembrance.

The word *dhikr* carries remembrance, mention, reminder, recollection, and the revealed reminder itself. It can refer to remembering Allah with the tongue and heart. It can refer to revelation that reminds the human being of what they were created to know. It can refer to the Qur'an as the Reminder that returns the soul to reality.

The attached pronoun "My" gives the phrase its intimacy and severity. The problem was not that they disliked religious discourse in the abstract. They were covered from Allah's own remembrance. The One who created the eye sent reminders through signs and revelation, and the eye was covered from the Creator's call.

This connects deeply to the structure of Al-Kahf. The surah opened by praising Allah for sending down the Book to His servant and making it straight, so that it might warn and give good news. It then told the Prophet not to obey the one whose heart Allah had made heedless of His remembrance and who followed his desire. It described those who turn away after being reminded of the signs of their Lord and forget what their hands have sent forth. Now, near the end, the surah names the final condition: eyes in a covering from My remembrance.

The internal echo is powerful. Al-Kahf is not merely warning against disbelief as an idea. It is warning against a life organized around avoiding dhikr. The person may be intelligent, productive, admired, wealthy, powerful, or culturally sophisticated. But if the faculties are covered from remembrance, the whole life is moving toward a terrible unveiling.

Ibn Kathir brings another Qur'anic echo to this point: whoever turns away blindly from the remembrance of the Most Merciful is assigned a shaytan as companion. That link clarifies the danger. Turning away from dhikr does not leave the heart neutral. If remembrance does not accompany you, another companionship forms. The heart does not remain empty. Something else teaches it how to see.

The lived question is simple and hard: what does your eye habitually connect things to? When you see provision, does it connect to the Provider? When you see beauty, does it connect to the Maker? When you see your sin, does it connect to repentance? When you see another person's pain, does it connect to mercy and responsibility? Or has the eye learned to stop before remembrance?

[PAUSE]

The eye was created to be a road from the visible sign to the remembered Lord.

### 5. "They Were Unable to Hear" - When Refusal Becomes Incapacity

The final phrase is *wa kanu la yastati'una sam'a* - "and they were unable to hear." Again the verb of being gives continuity: they had been this way. The phrase does not simply say "they did not hear." It says they were unable.

The verb *yastati'una* comes from the field of ability, capacity, being able to do something. It is a form that often carries acquired power or practical capacity. Hearing here is *sam'a*, a verbal noun or indefinite object: a hearing, the act of hearing, receptive listening.

This is severe because it shows how moral refusal can harden into incapacity. At first, a person can hear but does not want to. Later, hearing itself becomes unbearable. Guidance feels irritating. Reminder feels intrusive. Correction feels like attack. Qur'an feels heavy. The adhan feels like interruption. A sincere adviser feels judgmental. The heart has trained itself away from reception until the very faculty of hearing guidance is weakened.

Al-Tabari says they did not have the capacity to hear Allah's reminder and His clarification in the Book in a way that would make them take lesson and distinguish guidance from misguidance. He transmits Mujahid's explanation that they did not understand or know. Al-Muyassar says they could not bear hearing the proofs that lead to faith. These explanations converge: the issue is not sound waves touching the ear. It is moral and spiritual reception.

The Qur'an often pairs sight and hearing because revelation addresses the whole person. Some truths must be seen in the world. Some must be received by listening. A person can resist both. Sight is blocked by covering; hearing is blocked by incapacity.

There is a warning here for how we treat uncomfortable truth. Every time we refuse to listen because listening would require change, we are training incapacity. Every time we mock correction before understanding it, we are training incapacity. Every time we use noise to drown the inner call to repent, we are training incapacity. The heart does not become deaf in one day. It becomes deaf by rehearsed avoidance.

But the reverse is also true. Listening can be trained back into the soul. Sit with the Qur'an even when the nafs wants escape. Let one ayah interrupt you. Let a trusted person correct you without instantly building a defense. Let silence return. Let the adhan arrive as a summons, not background sound. Ask Allah for ears that can bear guidance before the Day when the rejected guidance testifies.

[PAUSE]

The ear that cannot bear reminder in this life may have to bear the sound of truth when escape is gone.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### 1. The Heart Can Train Its Senses Away From Allah

Most of us think of sight and hearing as passive. If something is in front of us, we see it. If a sound reaches us, we hear it. But human life is more complicated than that. We learn what to notice. We learn what to ignore. A parent can hear one child's cough through a house full of noise. A trader can see a small shift in a market that others miss. A resentful person can detect insult where none was intended. A grateful person can notice mercy in a detail another person would walk past.

The senses are trained by love, fear, habit, desire, and worship.

This ayah shows the darkest form of that training. Their eyes had been in a covering from Allah's remembrance, and they were unable to hear. They had senses, but the senses no longer delivered them to guidance. The world was still full of signs. Revelation was still recited. Warnings still came. But the inner pathways had been damaged.

The principle is this: repeated avoidance does not leave the heart unchanged; it reshapes the faculties by which guidance is received.

Ibn Kathir's language of turning a blind eye and a deaf ear is important because it preserves responsibility. These people are not portrayed as innocent victims of missing evidence. Al-Tabari's explanation adds that they failed to look into signs in a way that would lead to reflection, lesson-taking, repentance, tawhid, and submission. That chain matters. Seeing is supposed to lead somewhere. Hearing is supposed to lead somewhere. When seeing and hearing stop before remembrance, the whole moral sequence is broken.

The modern tension is that we often call this "being busy," "being overwhelmed," or "not being in the right headspace." Sometimes those phrases describe real fatigue. A person can be wounded, anxious, depressed, or exhausted, and revelation does not ask us to treat human fragility cruelly. But there is another state that uses fragility as cover for avoidance. We do not want to think. We do not want to repent. We do not want to let the Qur'an name our condition. So we build a life in which remembrance cannot reach us deeply.

We fill every silence. We check the phone at the first hint of discomfort. We keep entertainment near enough that no moral thought has to finish itself. We consume news until the heart is furious but not repentant. We surround ourselves with people who confirm our self-image. We avoid gatherings where sincere correction might happen. Slowly, the eyes see everything except signs, and the ears hear everything except guidance.

This is why daily acts of dhikr are not ornamental. They are sensory rehabilitation. Prayer reorients the body. Qur'an retrains the ear. Looking at creation with praise retrains the eye. Visiting the sick retrains the soul to see mortality. Giving charity retrains the hand and eye to see provision as trust. Lowering the gaze retrains sight away from appetite. Listening humbly to counsel retrains hearing away from ego.

In Surah Al-A'raf, Allah describes people who have hearts, eyes, and ears but do not use them for understanding, seeing, and hearing. In Surah Al-Isra, the Qur'an speaks of coverings over hearts and heaviness in ears when the Qur'an is recited to those who reject. These ayahs are not merely condemning others. They are diagnosing the human capacity to misuse faculties until they cease to guide.

So the application is practical: audit what your senses are becoming. What do your eyes rush toward? What do they avoid? What kinds of speech do your ears enjoy? What kinds of truth do they resist? What environments make remembrance easier to receive, and what environments wrap your sight in covering?

[PAUSE]

The senses are not neutral windows. They become servants of whatever the heart repeatedly chooses.

### 2. Avoiding Remembrance Does Not Remove Accountability

There is a subtle lie people tell themselves: if I do not look at the truth, I will not be responsible for it. If I do not learn, I will not be obligated. If I do not listen to the reminder, I can keep living as I was. If I avoid the person who will advise me, I can preserve innocence. If I keep the Qur'an at a distance, I can avoid the discomfort of being addressed.

This ayah destroys that lie.

The people described are precisely those whose eyes were covered from Allah's remembrance and who could not hear. And where are they in the sequence? They are the disbelievers before whom Jahannam has just been presented. Avoidance did not protect them from accountability. It became part of the evidence against them.

The principle is clear: refusing the channels of guidance does not excuse the soul; it exposes the soul's refusal.

Al-Muyassar says their eyes in the worldly life did not see Allah's signs and they could not bear hearing the proofs leading to faith. Al-Tabari says they did not reflect on Allah's signs or listen to His clarification in the Book so as to know guidance from misguidance and faith from kufr. That means the inability is not innocence. It is the result of being overtaken by rejection, misery, and obedience to shaytan, in Al-Tabari's framing.

This is difficult because we live in a culture that often treats attention as a private preference. You choose what to consume. You choose what to mute. You choose what to block. You choose what to learn about. On one level, that is simply practical. No one can attend to everything. But spiritually, what we refuse to attend to can become a moral act. If Allah's reminder is asking for entry and we repeatedly close the door, we are not merely managing attention. We are choosing a world in which Allah's claim on us remains unheard.

Al-Kahf has already warned us about this. In 18:57, the surah asks who is more unjust than one who is reminded of the signs of his Lord, then turns away from them and forgets what his hands have sent forth. That verse speaks of coverings on hearts and heaviness in ears. Now 18:101 returns to the same tragedy at the edge of Jahannam's display. The one who turned from reminder in life meets exposure in the Hereafter.

This should change how we respond to discomfort. When a verse unsettles you, do not rush to escape. When an Islamic obligation feels inconvenient, do not bury it under complexity before you have listened. When a sin becomes visible to you, do not cover it with humor. When someone you trust says, "I think you need to look at this," do not make your first act self-defense.

The believer learns to say: perhaps this discomfort is mercy trying to enter before exposure comes by force.

There is a balance here. We should seek knowledge with steadiness, not panic. We should not overwhelm ourselves with every topic at once. We should not confuse intrusive anxiety with sacred responsibility. But we must not sanctify avoidance. The heart knows, more often than we admit, the difference between pacing and fleeing.

In Surah Az-Zumar, those who listen to speech and follow the best of it are praised as people Allah has guided. In Surah Al-Anfal, believers are those whose hearts tremble when Allah is mentioned and whose faith increases when His ayahs are recited to them. That is the opposite of 18:101. The guided heart may be shaken by remembrance, but it remains available to it.

The practical application is to build small acts of voluntary exposure. Read the ayah you are avoiding. Ask the question you fear. Let the reminder finish before you distract yourself. Sit for two minutes after prayer and ask, "What truth am I refusing to hear?" Write down one command of Allah you already know but keep postponing. Then take one step toward it.

[PAUSE]

It is better to let remembrance uncover you gently now than to meet reality when covering no longer works.

### 3. Dhikr Is Not Escape From Reality; It Is the Recovery of Reality

Some people hear "remembrance" and imagine something private, soft, or removed from the serious business of life. Dhikr becomes a spiritual mood, a phrase after prayer, a retreat from practical concerns. But in this ayah, being covered from dhikr leads to the most severe misreading of reality possible. They were covered from My remembrance, and then Jahannam was presented before them.

That means dhikr is not escape from reality. Dhikr is what allows a person to perceive reality correctly before the final display.

When Allah is remembered, creation becomes sign rather than accident. Wealth becomes trust rather than self-proof. Suffering becomes test rather than meaningless cruelty. Death becomes return rather than annihilation. Warning becomes mercy rather than threat. Time becomes amanah rather than empty duration. The self becomes servant rather than sovereign.

When Allah is forgotten, the same world is misread. Beauty becomes possession. Power becomes entitlement. Delay becomes denial. Sin becomes lifestyle. Warning becomes offense. Death becomes interruption. The Hereafter becomes mythology or abstraction. The eye still sees, but it no longer reads.

This is why Al-Kahf has been a surah of corrected perception from the beginning. The cave looked like disappearance, but it was preservation. The garden looked like success, but it exposed arrogance. Musa saw damage, killing, and unpaid labor, but beneath them were protection, mercy, and preservation by Allah's command. Dhul-Qarnayn's barrier looked like a permanent triumph, but it was mercy until the promise came. The surah trains us to stop trusting first appearances. Dhikr is the light by which appearances become signs.

Ibn Kathir's cross-reference to turning away blindly from the remembrance of the Most Merciful in 43:36 deepens this. When a person turns from dhikr, companionship shifts. A shaytan becomes attached. That is a terrifying way to understand heedlessness. Forgetting Allah does not simply remove sacred language from life; it allows another interpretive companion to teach the soul how to read the world.

The modern objection is that constant remembrance feels unrealistic. People have bills, children, trauma, deadlines, bodies, grief, and responsibilities. How can every sight and sound become dhikr? But the Qur'an is not asking us to become disembodied. It is asking us to refuse a world in which Allah is absent from interpretation. Dhikr can enter ordinary life as orientation: begin with His name, pause before disobedience, say alhamdulillah with attention, connect provision to Him, connect fear to du'a, connect failure to tawbah, connect success to gratitude, connect death to return.

This is not less realistic. It is more realistic. A life without dhikr is the unrealistic life because it edits out the One upon whom everything depends.

The practical implication is to create routes from perception to remembrance. When you see your child, remember trust. When you see your paycheck, remember provision. When you see your aging face, remember return. When you hear the Qur'an, stop multitasking if you can. When someone speaks truth, listen for Allah's mercy in the correction. When you feel the urge to avoid, name it before Allah: "My Lord, my heart wants to run from Your reminder. Do not leave me to myself."

Surah Al-Kahf is recited weekly by many believers as protection and reminder. But the protection is not magical detachment from its meanings. The surah protects by retraining perception. It teaches us what to see and how to hear before the trials of wealth, knowledge, power, time, and the end of days distort the senses.

Dhikr is the soul remembering how to read the world truthfully.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of Eyes Covered From Remembrance

The ayah has a two-faculty architecture built on the previous ayah's display.

First, Jahannam is presented to the disbelievers. Then the Qur'an identifies them as those whose sight had been covered from Allah's remembrance. The eye, which should have moved from sign to Lord, was trapped inside a covering.

Second, the ayah moves from sight to hearing. They were not only visually blocked; they were unable to receive reminder by listening. Revelation, proof, warning, counsel, and clarification reached the world, but their hearing could not carry it into guidance.

So the progression is: final display, covered sight, disabled hearing. The Day shows what life had hidden. They did not let remembrance make the unseen real in the world, so the Hereafter becomes visible in a way they can no longer refuse.

The architecture also mirrors Al-Kahf's whole discipline. The surah has trained sight through hidden meanings and trained hearing through recited remembrance. At the end, it shows the disaster of refusing both.

### Questions to Carry

Where have your eyes become trained away from Allah's signs? Look at the ordinary things you see every day: your body, your home, your work, your family, your losses, your habits. Which of these still leads you to remembrance, and which has become spiritually blank?

What truths do you avoid hearing because hearing would require change? It may be a verse, an obligation, an apology, a correction, or a warning you already know is true. The question is not whether the sound reached you, but whether you allowed it to become guidance.

What coverings feel normal because you have lived inside them too long? A covering can be entertainment, ideology, resentment, desire, fear, ambition, or the need to be approved of. Ask Allah to show you the veil that no longer feels like a veil.

How can you retrain one faculty this week? Choose one practice: listen to Qur'an without multitasking, lower your gaze from what darkens the heart, look at one blessing and name the Giver, or receive one piece of sincere advice without immediate defense.

Do you treat dhikr as a phrase, or as the recovery of reality? The test is what happens after remembrance. Does it change how you see provision, sin, people, time, and death?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The people before whom Hell is displayed were not ruined by lack of signs, but by eyes trained away from Allah's remembrance and ears that could no longer bear guidance.**

### The Closing Invitation

Let this ayah make you gentle but uncompromising with your own faculties. Do not wait for a final display to become honest about what you have refused to see. Ask Allah for eyes that turn signs into remembrance and ears that can receive truth before truth arrives as judgment. Begin small, but begin today: one sight returned to Allah, one reminder heard without escape, one covering named and lifted by tawbah.

### Du'a

O Allah, do not place our eyes in a covering from Your remembrance. Make what we see a path back to You, and make what we hear a mercy that enters the heart. Protect us from the practiced avoidance that becomes incapacity. Let the Qur'an retrain our sight, soften our hearing, and return us to You before the Day when every hidden reality is displayed. Ameen.
