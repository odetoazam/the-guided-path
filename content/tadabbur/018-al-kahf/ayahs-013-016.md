# When Young Hearts Stand

## Al-Kahf 18:13-16

[ayah:18:13] نَّحْنُ نَقُصُّ عَلَيْكَ نَبَأَهُم بِٱلْحَقِّ ۚ إِنَّهُمْ فِتْيَةٌ ءَامَنُوا۟ بِرَبِّهِمْ وَزِدْنَـٰهُمْ هُدًى

## Introduction

The story now slows down.

The previous passage gave us the frame: the companions of the cave and the inscription are wondrous, but they are not the greatest of Allah's signs. The youths took refuge. They prayed for mercy and right guidance. Allah struck over their ears for numbered years. Then He raised them so that the hidden duration became manifest.

Now Allah tells us who they were.

That order matters. The Qur'an does not begin by satisfying every curiosity about names, dates, city, king, or geography. It begins with the truth that saves the story from becoming folklore: they were young men who believed in their Lord, and Allah increased them in guidance.

Al-Tabari says Allah is narrating their news to the Prophet with truth, not with the uncertain guesses and competing claims of people. Ibn Kathir highlights their youth and says young people are often more receptive to truth than elders whose habits have hardened. Al-Qurtubi preserves both the spiritual honor of their youth and the legal-theological clarity of their stand: their withdrawal was because tawhid had become impossible to preserve inside their public environment.

The passage is built around a movement: Allah tells the story with truth; they believe; Allah increases them; Allah binds their hearts; they stand; they speak; they refuse false gods; they demand proof; they name invention against Allah as the greatest injustice; then they withdraw.

This is not a story of young people running away because they are fragile.

It is a story of young hearts made firm enough to stand, and wise enough to leave.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. A Story Told With Truth

The first words are emphatic: *nahnu naqussu alayka naba'ahum bil-haqq* — We narrate to you their account with truth.

The verb *naqussu* comes from the family Q-S-S, to follow a trace, narrate in sequence, recount carefully. A *qissah* is not merely a story in the entertainment sense. It is a traced account, an ordered following of what matters. The Qur'an does not scatter details for curiosity. It traces what guides.

Then the object is *naba'ahum*, their news, their weighty report. The family N-B-A carries significant news, not trivial updates. A *naba'* is information with consequence. This is not gossip about ancient youths. It is news that changes how believers understand faith under pressure.

The phrase *bil-haqq* guards the entire narration. With truth. In truth. By truth. Al-Tabari reads this as Allah saying He is narrating their account truthfully, without the false additions and uncertain claims circulating among people. Ibn Ashur notes that this opening also answers the polemical setting: when people ask about the sleepers as a test, Allah gives the account according to truth, not according to their controlling terms.

This matters for how we read Qur'anic stories. Revelation does not tell stories the way human curiosity tells stories. We often want names, maps, numbers, and decorative detail. Allah gives what guides. If a detail is absent, its absence is part of the teaching. If a detail is present, it is load-bearing.

The first mercy of this passage, then, is not the cave. It is truthful narration. When Allah tells a story, the believer is no longer trapped between rumor, legend, ideology, and speculation. The story becomes guidance because it has been taken out of the hands of competing human claims and placed under divine truth.

### 2. Young Men Who Believed, Then Were Increased

The Qur'an says: *innahum fityatun amanu bi-rabbihim wa zidnahum huda* — they were young men who believed in their Lord, and We increased them in guidance.

The word *fityah* carries youth, fresh strength, young manhood. The Qur'an could have called them servants, believers, a group, or people. It highlights youth. That is not incidental. Youth is often the age when identity is most socially exposed. To believe as a young person can mean losing the world before you have even fully entered it.

Ibn Kathir's observation is spiritually sharp: young people often respond to truth before the elders because they are less locked into inherited status and habit. This does not romanticize youth, but it does honor its openness. A young heart can be vulnerable to desire, yes. But it can also be quick to surrender when Allah opens it.

Their faith is described with *amanu bi-rabbihim*: they believed in their Lord. The name Rabb matters. They are not first described as believing in an abstract proposition. They attach their trust to the One who creates, owns, sustains, nurtures, and brings servants stage by stage to completion. When society threatens them, they know they are not socially orphaned. They have a Rabb.

Then Allah says: We increased them in guidance.

The sequence is one of the most hopeful in the Qur'an. They believed, and Allah increased. They took the first step of iman, and Allah opened further huda. Guidance is not static. It grows under Allah's care. This echoes the Qur'anic principle in Surah Muhammad: those who are guided, Allah increases them in guidance and grants them their taqwa (47:17).

This is important because many people wait to become fully strong before obeying. The ayah teaches the reverse. Start with the faith you have been given. Allah increases those who respond. The youths did not enter the story with every answer. They entered with iman in their Lord, and Allah gave more.

There is also a mercy in the pronoun: *zidnahum* — We increased them. Guidance did not emerge merely from their psychological resilience. It was a divine increase. The story honors their courage without making courage self-made.

### 3. Hearts Bound Firm When They Stood

Ayah 14 says: *wa rabatna ala qulubihim idh qamu* — and We bound firm their hearts when they stood.

The verb *rabatna* comes from R-B-T, to tie, bind, fasten, strengthen, secure. It is used for tying something so it does not scatter or flee. The object is not their tongues first, not their bodies first, but their hearts. Allah tied down the place that trembles before the body acts.

This is a profound psychology of courage. People often think courage begins in the limbs: standing, speaking, moving. The Qur'an says Allah bound their hearts when they stood. The visible stand rested on an invisible fastening.

The timing matters: *idh qamu*, when they stood. Allah's strengthening meets them in the act. They are not strengthened in abstraction while sitting safely. They stand, and Allah binds. This is a pattern in revelation. In Surah al-Anfal, Allah tells believers to be firm and remember Allah much when meeting a force, and elsewhere He speaks of sending tranquility and strengthening hearts. The heart's firmness is a gift, but it often arrives on the road of obedience.

The word *qalb* itself is the organ of turning. It comes from a family associated with turning over, changing, fluctuation. The heart is called heart because it turns. So when Allah binds hearts, He steadies what by nature can flip under pressure.

This should humble every courageous person. If you ever stood in a moment when you expected to collapse, do not narrate it only as personality. Allah may have tied your heart. If you ever spoke truth with a voice steadier than your inner fear, do not forget the unseen fastening.

Their first statement after this fastening is not about themselves. It is theology: Our Lord is the Lord of the heavens and the earth.

### 4. The First Sentence of Courage Is Tawhid

They say: *Rabbuna rabbu al-samawati wa al-ard* — Our Lord is the Lord of the heavens and the earth.

The repetition of Rabb is beautiful. They do not only say, "Allah is Lord of the heavens and earth." They begin with relationship: Our Lord. Then they expand the scale: Lord of the heavens and the earth. The One who owns us is not a tribal deity, not a private preference, not one option inside our city's religion. He is Lord of the entire created order.

This is why the next sentence becomes possible: We will never call upon any god besides Him. The negation *lan* points forward with emphatic refusal. It is not only "we do not." It is "we will not." They draw a line into the future.

The verb *nad'uwa* is from D-A-W, to call, invoke, supplicate. Worship begins with calling. To call on another as divine is to relocate dependence. The youths refuse to let social pressure redirect the voice of need.

Then they say: if we did, we would have said *shatata*. The word *shatat* comes from S-H-T-T, excess, going far beyond bounds, outrageous deviation. It is speech that has traveled far from truth. They understand shirk not merely as a wrong option but as a sentence too outrageous for the mouth.

Notice the echo with the opening of Al-Kahf. Ayah 5 said the claim that Allah took a child was a grave word coming from mouths. Here the youths say that calling upon a god besides Him would be *shatat*. The surah is teaching adab of speech about Allah. Some sentences are not merely incorrect. They are violations of reality.

Al-Qurtubi and Ibn Kathir both present this as their declaration of tawhid before a people of shirk. Al-Tabari emphasizes that they refused to worship any deity besides Allah. But the Qur'an shows us the inner architecture: relationship with Rabb, cosmic scale, refusal of false invocation, and moral horror at the speech that would follow.

### 5. Where Is the Clear Authority?

Ayah 15 turns from declaration to argument: these are our people who have taken gods besides Him. Why do they not bring for them a clear authority?

The verb *ittakhadhu* from A-KH-DH in Form VIII gives the sense of taking up, adopting, choosing for oneself. Their people have taken gods. False religion is often described this way in the Qur'an: not as something proven, but as something adopted. The act reveals desire, inheritance, politics, fear, or habit pretending to be worship.

Then comes the demand: bring a *sultan bayyin*. *Sultan* in Qur'anic usage can mean authority, proof, warrant, decisive power. *Bayyin* means clear, manifest, clarifying. The youths are not asking for emotional sincerity. They are asking for divine authorization. Where is the clear warrant for these gods?

This argument appears throughout the Qur'an. False worshippers are repeatedly asked whether Allah sent down any authority for what they worship or claim. Tawhid is not anti-reason. It demands proof. Shirk often survives by inheritance and atmosphere, not by evidence.

Then the ayah closes with a severe moral principle: who is more unjust than one who invents a lie against Allah?

The verb *iftara* from F-R-Y in Form VIII means to fabricate, invent, cut out a false claim. The object is *kadhiba*, a lie. The wrong is not only that people are mistaken. They are attributing to Allah what He did not authorize. That is why it is *zulm*, injustice: it puts speech in the wrong place, worship in the wrong place, fear in the wrong place, and divine right onto created things.

This connects directly to the surah's opening warning against those who say Allah has taken a child. The cave youths are living the surah's opening theology under pressure. They refuse to let people speak about Allah without knowledge. They ask for proof. They name invention against Allah as the greatest injustice.

The Qur'an is showing young believers with theological clarity. Their courage is not vague defiance. It is disciplined by evidence.

### 6. Withdrawal With Mercy Spread Open

Ayah 16 begins: *wa idhi i'tazaltumuhum wa ma ya'buduna illa Allah* — and when you have withdrawn from them and what they worship besides Allah.

The verb *i'tazala* means to withdraw, separate oneself, keep apart. It is not merely physical distance. It is principled separation. They withdraw from the people and from the worship-system, not from concern, not from humanity, not from truth. They separate because worship has become corrupted.

Then comes the command: *fa'wu ila al-kahf* — take refuge in the cave. The same family A-W-Y from the earlier refuge returns. What began as their movement is now framed as guidance: go to the cave.

And then the promise is astonishing: your Lord will spread for you from His mercy. The verb *yanshur* comes from N-SH-R, to spread, unfold, disperse, open out. Mercy is pictured not as a tiny ration in a tight place, but as something spread open inside constriction. The cave is narrow, but Allah's mercy expands.

This is one of the deepest inversions in the passage. Public life looked spacious but was spiritually constricting. The cave looked constricting but became spacious with mercy. The measurement of space changes when Allah is present.

Then: He will prepare for you from your affair *mirfaqa*. The word comes from R-F-Q, gentleness, facility, something useful, a means of ease or support. Their earlier du'a asked Allah to prepare for them right guidance in their affair. Now the promise echoes it: He will prepare for you, from your affair, a facility. The prayer is being answered in the language of the prayer.

This is how Qur'anic architecture teaches trust. They asked for mercy and prepared guidance. Allah promises mercy spread and prepared facility. The cave is not only a hiding place. It is the answer to du'a in stone form.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Youth Is Not an Excuse to Delay Serious Faith

It is easy to treat youth as a waiting room.

Later, when life settles. Later, when desires cool. Later, when marriage happens. Later, when the career stabilizes. Later, when the social cost is lower. Later, when the heart feels older and therefore supposedly readier for Allah.

Al-Kahf disrupts that story. The first detailed description of the sleepers is not their number, lineage, or political setting. It is their age-stage and their faith: young men who believed in their Lord.

The principle is this: youth is not spiritually empty time before real servanthood begins; youth can be the very place where Allah opens extraordinary guidance.

Ibn Kathir's note that young people often respond more readily to truth than hardened elders is not a sentimental slogan. It is a warning and a hope. The warning is for elders who think age automatically brings wisdom while habit may actually harden the soul. The hope is for the young believer who feels outnumbered and imagines that serious faith belongs to a later season.

The Qur'an gives other young figures this honor. Ibrahim challenges his people's idols while still described in youthful terms by them. Yusuf resists temptation in the prime of beauty and vulnerability. Musa is given strength and maturity, then tested before his public mission. Maryam is young and yet bears a trust no one else bears. The Qur'an does not wait for people to become socially established before Allah can use them.

The modern tension is that youth is aggressively claimed by the age. It is marketed as experimentation without consequence, identity without accountability, freedom without surrender. A young believer is told, subtly or loudly, that seriousness about Allah is a form of self-deprivation. Enjoy now, repent later. Fit in now, become principled later.

But the sleepers teach that some doors of guidance open precisely when a young person refuses to sell the heart early. They believed, and Allah increased them. That increase matters. You do not need to possess the entire map to begin. You need to respond truthfully to the guidance already in front of you.

Concrete application is simple but difficult: do not despise small acts of early obedience. A young person who guards prayer while peers mock it, refuses a gathering that would poison the heart, asks serious questions, seeks righteous friends, or makes du'a for firmness may be standing at the beginning of an increase from Allah.

And for those no longer young, the lesson remains: do not patronize young faith. Protect it. Make room for it. Do not demand that young believers become cynical before you respect them. Allah honored the sleepers by naming their youth inside revelation.

### Theme 2: Courage Is a Gift Before It Is a Personality Trait

We often admire courage as temperament.

Some people are bold, we say. Some are natural leaders. Some are fearless. But the Qur'an lets us see behind the visible stand: We bound firm their hearts when they stood.

The principle is this: the believer's public firmness depends on Allah's private fastening of the heart.

This reframes courage. The youths stood and spoke, but the hidden actor was Allah. He tied their hearts so they would not scatter under pressure. The heart, by its nature, turns. It imagines consequences, remembers attachments, fears humiliation, longs for safety, and trembles under authority. If it remains firm in a moment of obedience, that firmness is not self-generated.

This connects to the Qur'an's prayer in 3:8: Our Lord, do not let our hearts deviate after You have guided us, and grant us mercy from Yourself. It also echoes moments where Allah sends tranquility or strengthens believers under pressure. The Qur'an consistently teaches that the unseen stability of the heart is one of Allah's greatest gifts.

Al-Tabari describes Allah strengthening their hearts and inspiring them with patience. Al-Muyassar says Allah made their hearts firm when they stood before the disbelieving ruler or people and declared truth. Classical tafsir does not reduce the stand to youthful rebellion. It is divine strengthening joined to iman.

The objection arises from people who feel afraid: if I tremble, does that mean I have no courage? Not necessarily. Courage in the Qur'an is not the absence of fear. It is the heart being held enough to obey. The youths may have felt fear. The point is that Allah bound their hearts so fear did not become lord.

This is liberating. You can ask Allah for courage. You can ask Him to tie your heart before a hard conversation, a public truth, a private temptation, a family confrontation, a medical appointment, a grief that might scatter you. You do not have to pretend to be naturally brave.

The practical sign of this theme is gratitude after firmness. When Allah lets you stand, say alhamdulillah. Do not rewrite the moment as proof of your superior personality. The young sleepers stood because Allah bound their hearts. So do we, whenever we stand well.

### Theme 3: Tawhid Gives the Believer Both Love and Refusal

The youths' declaration begins with belonging: Our Lord.

Then it expands into universality: Lord of the heavens and the earth.

Then it becomes refusal: we will never call upon any god besides Him.

This is the shape of tawhid in a pressured world. It is not mere negation. It is love and allegiance first, then refusal. The believer does not say no to false gods because the heart is empty. The believer says no because the heart already has a Lord.

The principle is this: the strongest refusal grows from the clearest belonging.

If tawhid is taught only as argument, it may become dry. If it is taught only as feeling, it may become vague. The youths show both. They know their Lord, and they can reason against false worship. They can say "Our Lord is the Lord of the heavens and earth," and they can demand clear authority from those who took gods besides Him.

This balance matters now. Many Muslims are pressured to make faith either purely private emotion or purely public polemic. The Qur'an refuses both reductions. Tawhid is intimate enough to say "our Lord" and rigorous enough to ask "where is your clear authority?"

Al-Qurtubi's discussion of their statement makes clear that they are rejecting shirk on the basis of Allah's exclusive lordship. Ibn Kathir frames their people as idolaters and the youths as those Allah guided to truth. Al-Tabari emphasizes their refusal to invoke any deity besides Allah. The shared tafsir frame is not merely moral rebellion. It is tawhid.

The tension is that refusal can be costly. Saying "we will never call upon another god" may mean losing approval, career paths, family ease, social access, or the image of being moderate in the eyes of people who define moderation as compromise with falsehood. The youths are not cruel, but they are clear.

Concrete application begins with identifying the gods people are pressured to call upon now. Not only statues. Approval. Nation. Desire. Market. Self-expression. Race. Party. Romance. Career. Religious personalities. Public image. Anything that receives the dependence, fear, obedience, or sacrifice due only to Allah becomes a rival in practice, even if our theology remains verbally correct.

The youths teach us to let "our Lord" become enough belonging that refusal becomes possible. You cannot say no to every false lord by willpower alone. You say no because you have already said yes to the Lord of the heavens and the earth.

### Theme 4: Withdrawal Can Be an Act of Clarity, Not Defeat

The command to enter the cave comes after the stand.

That order matters. They do not withdraw because they have no convictions. They withdraw because their convictions have become clear. They separate from the people and what they worship besides Allah, then the cave becomes the next obedient step.

The principle is this: when an environment demands worship-compromise, withdrawal for Allah can be victory in the form of disappearance.

This is hard for an age addicted to visibility. We often assume that if truth is not publicly dominant, it is losing. If believers step back, they have failed. If a person leaves a corrupt environment, they are weak. But Al-Kahf teaches a more subtle map. Sometimes the most faithful act is to remove yourself from a system that is training you to bow.

The cave is not glamorous. It does not look like triumph. It looks like being pushed to the margin. But Allah promises to spread mercy there and prepare facility. That promise overturns worldly spatial logic. A palace without tawhid is narrow. A cave with mercy is expansive.

This theme echoes Ibrahim's separation from his people and what they worship besides Allah, after which Allah grants him Ishaq and Ya'qub and makes prophethood continue through his line (19:48-49). It also echoes the Qur'anic command to be with those who call upon their Lord morning and evening in this same surah (18:28). Separation from false worship must be joined to nearness to Allah and righteous companionship, not isolation into ego.

The modern objection is practical: should believers just withdraw from society? No. The Qur'an does not make the cave a universal permanent rule. The Prophet remains among his people calling them for years. Yusuf serves in Egypt. Musa confronts Pharaoh. Dhul-Qarnayn travels with power and justice. The lesson is not simplistic retreat. It is discernment under tawhid.

Ask: is this environment merely uncomfortable, or is it requiring disobedience? Is my presence a form of witness, or has it become slow surrender? Am I leaving because Allah is more beloved, or because people annoy me? Am I seeking a cave of mercy, or a cave of resentment?

When withdrawal is needed, the ayah gives hope. Allah can spread mercy in the place that looks small. He can prepare *mirfaq*, facility, gentleness, support, a usable way forward. The believer does not need the world to call a place spacious for Allah to make it spacious.

### Theme 5: Faith Does Not Fear Proof

The youths do not merely say, "This is what we feel."

They do not merely say, "This is our identity."

They do not merely say, "Our group has its truth and your group has its truth."

They ask their people for clear authority. Bring a *sultan bayyin*. Bring warrant. Bring evidence. Bring something that justifies calling upon these gods besides Allah.

The principle is this: true faith does not fear proof, because truth and clear authority belong together.

That is a needed correction in two directions. Some people imagine faith as a leap away from evidence, a private emotional preference that should never be asked to account for itself. Others imagine proof as a weapon against faith, as though sincerity must disappear when reasons are requested. The Qur'an gives us something more integrated. The youths believe in their Lord, Allah increases them in guidance, Allah binds their hearts, and then their speech includes a demand for clear authority.

Their courage is not anti-intellectual. Their spirituality is not vague. Their refusal is not tribal stubbornness. They understand that worship requires authorization. If a person will bow, call, sacrifice, obey, fear, love, and organize life around a supposed deity, then the demand for proof is not arrogance. It is basic moral sanity.

This theme echoes across the Qur'an. Again and again, those who worship besides Allah are challenged: did Allah send down authority for this? Do they have knowledge? Do they have a book? Do they have a clear proof? The Qur'an does not let inherited religion hide behind age. It does not let popular religion hide behind numbers. It does not let emotional religion hide behind sincerity. In matters of worship, the question remains: who authorized this?

Al-Tabari explains the youths' challenge as a demand that their people bring clear proof for the gods they had taken besides Allah. Al-Muyassar makes the same point directly: why do they not bring clear evidence for worshipping them? Ibn Kathir frames the statement as part of their denunciation of their people's idolatry. The tafsir voices converge here: the youths are not merely separating socially; they are exposing the absence of proof beneath the public religion of their people.

The modern objection is that asking for proof can sound harsh or dismissive. We live in a time where many beliefs are protected by personal feeling. "This is meaningful to me" is often treated as enough. But the Qur'an is tender with people and uncompromising with false claims about Allah. A belief can be meaningful to someone and still be false. A practice can be inherited and still be unauthorized. A spiritual language can feel beautiful and still be a lie against Allah.

This does not give believers permission to become arrogant debaters. The youths' proof-demand comes from hearts Allah bound, not egos looking for victory. There is a difference between asking for clear authority because Allah deserves truth and asking for evidence because we enjoy humiliating people. The first is worship. The second may be another disease of the self.

In practice, this theme asks us to examine our own religious speech too. Do we have clear authority for what we attribute to Allah? For what we declare halal or haram? For the despair we put in Allah's name? For the harshness we call "defending truth"? For the permissions we grant ourselves because they feel compassionate? The ayah's proof-demand is not only aimed outward at idolaters. It disciplines every mouth that speaks about Allah.

Faith does not fear proof. It fears lying about the One it loves.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Standing Youths

This passage unfolds like a full anatomy of faithful courage.

First, Allah takes control of the narration: We narrate their account with truth. Then He identifies the essence of the youths: they believed in their Lord. Then He names the divine increase: We increased them in guidance. Then He reveals the hidden support beneath the visible action: We bound firm their hearts. Then comes the public stand: they rose and declared their Lord as Lord of the heavens and earth. Then comes refusal: they will never call upon another god. Then comes proof: their people have no clear authority for false gods. Then comes moral judgment: inventing a lie against Allah is the greatest injustice. Finally comes separation: withdraw, take refuge in the cave, and your Lord will spread mercy and prepare facility.

The architecture moves from divine truth to inner faith, from inner faith to increased guidance, from increased guidance to firm hearts, from firm hearts to public speech, from public speech to principled separation, and from separation to mercy.

### Questions to Carry

Do I treat youth, or any life stage, as an excuse to postpone serious obedience? The sleepers were young, but Allah made their youth the setting of iman and increased guidance. Ask what act of obedience you are delaying by pretending a later version of yourself will be more ready.

Where do I need Allah to bind my heart? Name the place where your heart flips: approval, fear, desire, conflict, family pressure, private temptation. The youths stood because Allah fastened what would otherwise tremble.

Does my refusal of falsehood grow from love of Allah or only from dislike of people? Tawhid begins with "our Lord." Refusal becomes clean when belonging to Allah comes first.

What claims around me are accepted without clear authority? The youths ask for *sultan bayyin*. Train yourself not to be impressed by inherited confidence, social pressure, or spiritual-sounding speech without proof.

Do I ask for proof with humility or with ego? The youths' demand comes after Allah binds their hearts, not after their pride is inflamed. Let evidence-seeking remain an act of loyalty to Allah, not a way to enjoy superiority over people.

Is there a cave I need to enter, or a cave I am using to avoid responsibility? The ayah requires discernment. Withdrawal is blessed when it preserves worship and opens into Allah's mercy, not when it hides resentment or fear of service.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Al-Kahf 18:13-16 teaches that young believers stand only by Allah's truthful narration, increased guidance, and bound hearts; then tawhid gives them the clarity to refuse false gods, demand proof, and withdraw into a cave where mercy becomes spacious.**

### The Closing Invitation

Ask Allah for the faith of the sleepers before asking for the cave of the sleepers. Let Him increase the guidance you already have. Let Him bind your heart where it trembles. Speak truth with the scale of the heavens and earth behind you, but do not confuse courage with constant visibility. If Allah calls you to stand, stand. If Allah calls you to withdraw for His sake, enter the cave trusting that your Lord can spread mercy in places the world thinks are narrow.

### Du'a

O Allah, narrate our lives under Your truth and do not leave us to the false stories people tell about us or that we tell about ourselves. Make us among those who believe in their Lord and are increased in guidance. Bind our hearts when we must stand, purify our tongues with tawhid, protect us from inventing speech about You without proof, and when we must withdraw for Your sake, spread for us from Your mercy and prepare for us from our affairs facility, gentleness, and right guidance. Ameen.
