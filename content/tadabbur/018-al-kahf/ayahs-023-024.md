# Do Not Promise Tomorrow Without Allah

## Al-Kahf 18:23-24

[ayah:18:23] وَلَا تَقُولَنَّ لِشَا۟ىْءٍ إِنِّى فَاعِلٌ ذَٰلِكَ غَدًا

[ayah:18:24] إِلَّآ أَن يَشَآءَ ٱللَّهُ ۚ وَٱذْكُر رَّبَّكَ إِذَا نَسِيتَ وَقُلْ عَسَىٰٓ أَن يَهْدِيَنِ رَبِّى لِأَقْرَبَ مِنْ هَـٰذَا رَشَدًا

*Wa la taqulanna li-shay'in inni fa'ilun dhalika ghada, illa an yasha'a Allah. Wadhkur rabbaka idha nasita, wa qul asa an yahdiyani rabbi li-aqraba min hadha rashada.*

**And never say of anything, Indeed, I will do that tomorrow, except if Allah wills. And remember your Lord when you forget, and say, Perhaps my Lord will guide me to something nearer than this in right guidance.**

---

## Introduction

Tomorrow feels close enough to own.

That is why we speak about it so easily. I will do it tomorrow. I will fix it tomorrow. I will call them tomorrow. I will repent tomorrow. I will start praying properly tomorrow. I will write, travel, apologize, study, change, return, build, finish, begin - tomorrow.

The word leaves the mouth as if tomorrow were already in the hand.

Then Al-Kahf stops us.

After the cave story has taught us about hidden time, resurrection, divine promise, public dispute, and the limits of speculative knowledge, the surah turns to the Prophet and, through him, to every believer: do not say about anything, "I will do that tomorrow," except if Allah wills.

This is not a small etiquette phrase. It is not a verbal decoration pasted onto future plans. It is a spiritual discipline that breaks the illusion of ownership over time.

The asbab thread reported by the mufassirun gives the ayah an even sharper tenderness. Ibn Kathir, al-Tabari, and others mention reports that when Quraysh asked the Prophet, peace be upon him, about matters connected to the People of the Book, including the companions of the cave, he said he would answer them tomorrow, but did not say "if Allah wills." Revelation was delayed. Then these ayahs came, teaching him not to attach future action to himself independently of Allah's will.

We must be careful here. This is not a story that diminishes the Messenger. It is the Qur'an teaching the Ummah through the most beloved servant of Allah. The Prophet's life becomes the place where Allah trains us in the most refined adab. If the Messenger is taught to say "except if Allah wills," then what are we, whose knowledge is thinner, whose resolve is weaker, whose distractions are louder, and whose tomorrows are never guaranteed?

Al-Tabari explains the command as a prohibition from saying one will do something in the future without connecting it to Allah's will. Ibn Kathir connects it to the delayed revelation and explains that if one forgets, one should remember Allah afterward. Al-Muyassar states the meaning simply: do not say you will do something tomorrow unless you say, if Allah wills; and if you forget, remember your Lord and seek guidance to what is nearer to correctness.

The passage has three movements.

First, it removes independent ownership from future intention.

Second, it opens a door after forgetfulness: remember your Lord when you forget.

Third, it turns the believer's heart toward a better path: perhaps my Lord will guide me to something nearer than this in rushd.

The ayah does not tell us to stop planning. It tells us to stop planning like people who own tomorrow.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. La Taqulanna: A Strong Prohibition on Confident Speech

The ayah begins with *wa la taqulanna* - and do not ever say.

The verb *taqul* is speech. It is not yet action. The correction begins at the tongue before the hand has done anything. That matters because the Qur'an knows that human arrogance often appears first in language. We speak as if the future has already submitted to us. We narrate ourselves as doers before Allah has granted the doing.

The ending with the heavy emphatic nun intensifies the prohibition. This is not a light recommendation. It has force: do not say, do not let this become your manner of speech, do not let certainty about your own future action leave your mouth unqualified.

Why such force around a sentence?

Because speech trains the heart. If a person repeatedly says "I will do" without remembrance of Allah, the tongue slowly teaches the nafs that capacity belongs to the self. The plan becomes detached from the Planner. The intention becomes a little kingdom. Tomorrow becomes imagined property.

This does not mean every grammatical future tense is sinful. The Qur'an itself uses future language. The issue is the posture of independent certainty. The believer may plan, promise, schedule, and prepare, but the heart must know that no future act escapes Allah's will.

There is a contrast with the previous ayah. In 18:22, the Prophet is told not to enter speculative argument about what Allah did not make central. Here he is told not to speak about what has not yet arrived as if it were secured. The surah disciplines both directions: do not throw guesses into the unseen past, and do not claim mastery over the unseen future.

This is why 17:36 is such a close echo: do not pursue what you have no knowledge of. Hearing, sight, and heart will be questioned. Future action is also a domain where knowledge is limited. I do not know whether I will live until tomorrow. I do not know whether my strength will remain. I do not know whether the door I expect will open. I do not know whether Allah will place a better path in front of me.

[PAUSE]

The first act of humility is often not changing the plan. It is changing the sentence.

### 2. Li-Shay'in: The Command Covers Every Thing

The phrase continues: *li-shay'in* - of anything.

The word *shay'* is one of the broadest words in Arabic: a thing, any matter, anything that can be spoken of or intended. Its indefiniteness expands the command. Do not say of anything. Not only major religious missions. Not only revelation-related promises. Not only public claims. Anything.

This breadth is spiritually merciful because we often reserve Allah's will for big uncertainties while acting as if small things belong to us. We say "if Allah wills" about travel, surgery, marriage, or death, but not about ordinary work, messages, meetings, errands, or repentance. Yet the ayah says *li-shay'in*. The small future is as dependent on Allah as the large future.

This is not meant to make life tense. It is meant to make life truthful.

The believer lives in means. We set alarms. We write tasks. We make promises responsibly. We honor appointments. But beneath all means is the deeper reality that the smallest motion requires permission from Allah. A finger moves because He allows it. A thought completes because He sustains it. A person reaches tomorrow because He extends the thread of life.

In 81:29, Allah says that you do not will unless Allah, Lord of the worlds, wills. In 76:30, He says you do not will unless Allah wills. These ayahs do not cancel human will; they place it inside divine will. Human willing is real, accountable, and morally weighty, but it is never independent.

So the breadth of *li-shay'in* is a training in total dependence. The phrase asks us: which part of your future have you quietly exempted from needing Allah? Your work? Your health? Your child's safety? Your next prayer? Your own repentance? Your ability to finish what you started?

Al-Kahf answers: no thing lives outside His will.

### 3. Inni Fa'ilun Dhalika Ghada: The Illusion of the Self as Doer

The forbidden sentence is: *inni fa'ilun dhalika ghada* - indeed, I am doing that tomorrow.

The word *inni* brings emphasis: indeed I. Then *fa'il* is an active participle: a doer. It presents the self as the one who will carry out the action. *Dhalika* points to the specific intended matter. *Ghada* names tomorrow.

The whole phrase is a miniature architecture of human confidence: I, doer, that, tomorrow.

Each part can be true in a limited way. There is an "I" that intends. There is action we are responsible for. There is a concrete task. There may be a tomorrow. But when the sentence is closed without Allah, the architecture becomes false. It makes the self sound independent, the action guaranteed, the object secured, and tomorrow accessible.

The Qur'an does not remove the human being from the sentence. It does not say you are not a doer in any sense. It teaches the human doer to speak as a servant. The servant says: I intend, but Allah enables. I plan, but Allah disposes. I promise with honesty, but not with lordship over time.

There is also a subtle danger in *ghada*. Tomorrow often becomes the place where we store obedience we do not want to perform today. "I will repent tomorrow" can be spiritually different from "I will write tomorrow." One is a plan; the other may be evasion. The ayah's discipline applies to both, but it especially pierces the moral tomorrow we use to calm the conscience.

In 59:18, believers are told to fear Allah and let every soul look to what it has sent ahead for tomorrow. That tomorrow is the Hereafter. In 31:34, no soul knows what it will earn tomorrow. The Qur'an lets tomorrow remain both urgent and unknown. We prepare for it, but we do not possess it.

[PAUSE]

The believer does not cancel tomorrow. The believer removes the throne he had placed there for himself.

### 4. Illa An Yasha'a Allah: The Exception That Restores Reality

Then comes the exception: *illa an yasha'a Allah* - except if Allah wills.

This phrase restores the world to its true order.

The verb *yasha'a* speaks of divine willing. The subject is Allah. The future action is not denied, but it is placed under His will. The servant's intention is no longer floating alone. It is tethered to the One whose will governs existence.

This is the meaning carried in the phrase we commonly say: in sha Allah. But the ayah wants more than a formula. A person can say the words while the heart remains arrogant, just as a person can forget the words but later return with humility. The phrase is meant to reshape consciousness.

When the believer says "if Allah wills," he is not using Allah's will as an excuse for laziness. He is not saying, I might not do it because I am unreliable, so I will hide behind a pious phrase. That is a misuse. The Qur'anic phrase joins sincerity of intention with humility before divine enabling. We should still mean what we say. We should still keep promises. We should still prepare. But we prepare as servants, not owners.

The Qur'an gives us beautiful examples of this adab. In 37:102, Isma'il tells his father Ibrahim, "You will find me, if Allah wills, among the patient." He is not weak in resolve; he is placing patience under Allah's enabling. In 28:27, the righteous man of Madyan says to Musa, "You will find me, if Allah wills, among the righteous." Again, the phrase does not reduce integrity. It purifies it. In 12:99, Yusuf says, "Enter Egypt, if Allah wills, secure." Even after Allah has raised him to authority, he attaches safety to Allah's will.

So *illa an yasha'a Allah* teaches the believer how to hold resolve without illusion. It is possible to intend firmly and still know that your firmness is poor without Allah. It is possible to be responsible without being sovereign.

### 5. Wadhkur Rabbaka Idha Nasita: A Door After Forgetfulness

The next command is tender: *wadhkur rabbaka idha nasita* - remember your Lord when you forget.

The ayah could have ended with prohibition and exception. But Allah knows us. We forget. Even after being taught, we forget. Even after knowing, we speak too quickly. Even after intending humility, the tongue runs ahead of the heart.

So the ayah opens a path back.

The command is *udhkur* - remember. The object is not merely a phrase, but *rabbaka* - your Lord. This is intimate. When you forget, return not only to wording, but to Lordship. Remember the One who nurtures, owns, sustains, teaches, corrects, and guides you.

This matters because forgetfulness can produce shame that leads to distance. A person forgets to say "if Allah wills," forgets Allah in a plan, forgets dependence, forgets adab, and then thinks the moment is lost. The ayah says: remember when you forget. The return itself is worship.

Classical tafsir discusses whether this means saying the exception after remembering, even if delayed, or more broadly remembering Allah after forgetfulness. Ibn Kathir mentions the instruction to say "if Allah wills" when one remembers. Al-Tabari records the command to remember Allah after forgetting the exception. Al-Qurtubi discusses the juristic and linguistic implications. For tadabbur, the spiritual opening is clear: Allah did not close the door after forgetfulness.

This is consistent with the Qur'an's wider mercy. In 7:201, when a passing impulse from Shaytan touches the people of taqwa, they remember, and then they see clearly. In 3:135, the righteous are those who, when they commit an indecency or wrong themselves, remember Allah and seek forgiveness. Dhikr after lapse is a mark of life.

You are not only judged by whether you forgot. You are also shaped by whom you remember after forgetting.

### 6. Asa An Yahdiyani Rabbi Li-Aqraba Min Hadha Rashada: Asking for Nearer Guidance

The final command is: *wa qul* - and say.

What should the believer say after learning this adab of future speech? *Asa an yahdiyani rabbi li-aqraba min hadha rashada* - perhaps my Lord will guide me to something nearer than this in right guidance.

The word *asa* carries hope: perhaps, it may be. On the tongue of a servant, it is humble hope. The servant does not demand. He hopes from his Lord.

Then comes *yahdiyani rabbi* - my Lord will guide me. The pronoun is intimate: my Lord. The same Lord remembered after forgetfulness is asked for guidance beyond the present state.

The phrase *li-aqraba min hadha* means to something nearer than this. Nearer to what? Nearer to rushd. Nearer to the right path, nearer to sound judgment, nearer to what is more upright, nearer to what Allah loves. The believer is not merely asking to recover from a mistake. He is asking to be guided to something closer than the current level.

This is stunning. Forgetfulness becomes a doorway to greater guidance.

The story began with the youths asking: grant us from Yourself mercy and prepare for us from our affair *rashada* - right guidance. Now the Prophet is taught to ask that his Lord guide him to something nearer than this in *rashada*. The same word-field frames the cave story. The youths needed rushd when fleeing fitnah. The Prophet and Ummah need rushd when speaking, planning, forgetting, and returning.

This is a key Al-Kahf theme. Guidance is not static. Allah increased the youths in guidance in 18:13. The sleepers asked for rushd in 18:10. The Prophet is taught to ask for nearer rushd in 18:24. The surah is teaching that even the guided ask for guidance. Even the corrected ask for nearer correctness. Even the Messenger is taught words that keep the Ummah moving toward more precise servanthood.

In 47:17, Allah says those who are guided are increased in guidance and given their taqwa. In 3:8, the believers ask Allah not to let their hearts deviate after He has guided them. Guidance is not a certificate placed on the wall. It is a living dependence.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Planning Is Worship When It Knows It Is Dependent

Many people live between two extremes.

One extreme is control. It plans obsessively, speaks confidently, and feels humiliated by interruption. It treats every delay as an insult and every failed plan as proof that life is against it. The person in this state does not merely use calendars and commitments; he leans on them for existential safety. Tomorrow must obey because he has written it down.

The other extreme is passivity. It hears "if Allah wills" and uses it as a way to avoid effort. It does not prepare. It does not honor promises. It says "in sha Allah" when it really means "probably not." It mistakes vagueness for tawakkul.

These ayahs cut through both extremes.

The principle is this: the believer plans with seriousness but speaks with dependence.

The Prophet is not told never to intend future action. The companions of the cave were not condemned for sending someone to the city. The Qur'an is full of commands that require planning: migration, charity, contracts, battle preparation, family responsibilities, seeking knowledge. Islam does not produce disorganized souls. But it does not allow organization to become a false god.

Ibn Kathir's connection to the delayed revelation shows that even a noble intention to answer a question must be attached to Allah's will. Al-Tabari's explanation makes the adab general: do not say you will do a future thing unless Allah wills. Al-Muyassar makes the same point in plain language. The tafsir scaffolding is unified: future action must not be claimed independently.

This connects with 8:60, where believers are commanded to prepare whatever strength they can. Preparation is real. It also connects with 3:159, where after consultation and resolve, the Prophet is told to rely upon Allah. Resolve and reliance belong together. The believer does not abandon means; he refuses to worship them.

The modern objection is practical: If I add "if Allah wills" to everything, will that make me less accountable? It can, if the phrase is abused. But the ayah is not teaching evasive speech. It is teaching truthful speech. If you intend to do something, intend it honestly. If you promise, keep your promise. If you schedule, show up. But let your heart know that fulfillment is a gift.

This changes how we respond when plans fail. Instead of collapsing into rage or shame, we can say: I intended, I tried, I planned, but Allah did not will that path in that form. What is the nearer rushd now? What is the next guided step?

[PAUSE]

Tawakkul is not the death of planning. It is planning with the soul's forehead on the ground.

### Theme 2: "In Sha Allah" Is Not a Phrase to Escape Responsibility

One of the saddest things that can happen to a sacred phrase is that it becomes a joke about unreliability.

Someone says, "I'll come, in sha Allah," and everyone hears, "Maybe, probably not." Someone says, "I'll send it, in sha Allah," and the phrase becomes a soft exit. A word meant to humble the servant before Allah's will becomes a social cushion for weak commitment.

These ayahs do not allow that.

The forbidden phrase is not "I intend to do this." It is "I am definitely the doer of that tomorrow" without Allah. The correction is not to make intention less sincere. It is to make intention more truthful. The phrase "if Allah wills" should deepen honesty, not weaken it.

Look at the Qur'anic models. Isma'il says to Ibrahim in 37:102, you will find me, if Allah wills, among the patient. That is not a weak promise. It is one of the most courageous responses in the Qur'an. The righteous man in Madyan says to Musa in 28:27, you will find me, if Allah wills, among the righteous. Yusuf says in 12:99, enter Egypt, if Allah wills, secure. In each case, the phrase does not dilute responsibility. It places responsibility under Allah.

So the principle is this: "if Allah wills" should make us more faithful to our word because it reminds us that our word is witnessed by Allah.

Classical tafsir keeps the phrase tied to adab with Allah, not social slipperiness. Ibn Kathir speaks of attaching the exception to future intention. Al-Tabari treats it as connecting the planned act to divine will. No mufassir turns it into permission to be careless.

The tension is that we really do not control the future. Sometimes we sincerely intend and cannot fulfill. Illness comes. Emergencies come. Strength leaves. The phrase protects us from claiming what we cannot guarantee. But that protection must not become a cover for avoidable negligence.

The practical application is simple: when you say "in sha Allah," mean both halves. Mean "I truly intend this" and "I only reach it by Allah's permission." If you do not intend it, do not hide behind Allah's Name. If you intend it, prepare. If Allah prevents it, communicate honestly. Sacred language deserves sacred character.

[PAUSE]

Do not use the Lord of tomorrow to excuse laziness today.

### Theme 3: Forgetfulness Is Not the End if It Leads to Dhikr

There are people who spiral after forgetting.

They forget a prayer and feel filthy. They forget Allah in a plan and feel fake. They forget an adab they know and feel disqualified. They forget to say the right thing, do the right thing, respond the right way, and then Shaytan whispers that the whole path is hypocrisy.

The ayah gives a different training: remember your Lord when you forget.

This is a mercy because human beings are named by forgetfulness. We are not angels. We are not consistent in our awareness. We can be sincere and still forget. We can love Allah and still speak too quickly. We can know the lesson and still need the lesson again.

The principle is this: a living heart is not a heart that never forgets; it is a heart that returns to its Lord when it remembers that it forgot.

Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir both connect the command to remembering Allah after forgetting the exception. That concrete meaning is important. If you forgot to attach your plan to Allah's will, remember Him when you realize. But the wording also forms a broader spiritual habit: do not let forgetfulness harden into heedlessness. Let it become a trigger for return.

The Qur'an teaches this pattern elsewhere. In 7:201, when those with taqwa are touched by a passing impulse from Shaytan, they remember, and then they see clearly. In 3:135, when the righteous wrong themselves, they remember Allah and seek forgiveness. In 18:63, later in the same surah, the servant of Musa says that none made him forget the fish except Shaytan, and that forgetfulness becomes part of the path toward the meeting with Khidr.

This matters for religious growth. Many people think the path is a straight line of never slipping. Then when they forget, they despair. But the Qur'an gives us a more realistic and merciful map: forget, remember; slip, return; speak too quickly, correct the tongue; plan without adab, restore adab; lose the thread, ask for nearer guidance.

The application is to build return rituals. When you realize you forgot Allah in a plan, say "if Allah wills" sincerely and renew dependence. When you forgot a duty, seek forgiveness and repair it. When you forgot who owns the outcome, pause and remember your Lord by Name. Do not let the interval between forgetfulness and remembrance become long.

[PAUSE]

The danger is not that you forgot once. The danger is becoming comfortable in a forgetful state.

### Theme 4: Every Correction Can Become a Step Toward Nearer Rushd

The final du'a in the passage is not merely, "My Lord, forgive me for forgetting."

It is larger: perhaps my Lord will guide me to something nearer than this in right guidance.

That means correction is not only about returning to the previous level. Sometimes Allah uses a correction to raise the servant beyond where he was before the mistake. A person forgets, then remembers more deeply. A person speaks carelessly, then learns adab that reshapes years of future speech. A person is delayed, then discovers a better path. A person is humbled, then becomes more guided than he would have been if he had never noticed the flaw.

This is one of the most hopeful principles in the Qur'an: repentance and correction can become increase.

The companions of the cave asked Allah in 18:10 to prepare for them from their affair *rashada*. Allah increased them in guidance in 18:13. Here the Prophet is taught to ask for what is nearer to *rashada*. The surah is saturated with the idea that guidance is not only initial rescue. It is ongoing refinement.

Ibn Kathir explains the phrase as asking Allah to guide to what is closer to guidance and success. Al-Muyassar reads it as seeking what is nearer to correctness. The wording does not leave the believer stuck at "I forgot." It moves him toward "My Lord can guide me nearer."

This connects to 47:17: those who are guided, Allah increases them in guidance and gives them their taqwa. It connects to 1:6, the daily prayer for guidance to the straight path, recited even by those already Muslim. It connects to 2:186, where Allah is near and answers the supplicant when he calls.

The modern tension is perfectionism. Some people cannot receive correction without collapsing. If they are wrong, they feel worthless. If they forget, they feel fraudulent. If their plan fails, they feel Allah has closed the door. But the ayah teaches a different interior sentence: perhaps my Lord will guide me to something nearer than this.

Imagine living with that sentence. A failed plan becomes: perhaps there is nearer rushd. A forgotten adab becomes: perhaps Allah is teaching me deeper remembrance. A delayed answer becomes: perhaps Allah is refining my dependence. A public correction becomes: perhaps Allah is rescuing me from a subtler arrogance.

This is not naive optimism. It is hope grounded in Lordship. The One asked is *rabbi* - my Lord, the One who nurtures through command, delay, correction, and opening.

### Theme 5: Tomorrow Belongs to Allah, So Today Must Become Honest

The passage mentions tomorrow, but its demand is today.

Do not say, "I will do that tomorrow," except if Allah wills. That sentence must be spoken now. Remember your Lord when you forget. That remembering happens when awareness returns. Say, perhaps my Lord will guide me nearer. That hope must be placed on the tongue today.

The Qur'an often uses the future to awaken present truth. Death is future, so repent now. The Hour is coming, so prepare now. Tomorrow is unknown, so speak honestly now.

The principle is this: because tomorrow belongs to Allah, today's speech must become truthful, humble, and alive with remembrance.

This is especially important with deferred obedience. "Tomorrow" can be a mercy when it is used for responsible planning. It can also be a narcotic when it delays what Allah is asking now. I will make tawbah tomorrow. I will repair the relationship tomorrow. I will stop the haram tomorrow. I will learn my religion tomorrow. I will become serious tomorrow.

But no soul knows what it will earn tomorrow, as 31:34 says. And no person knows whether tomorrow will arrive with the same strength, softness, opportunity, or life. The believer does not panic over this. He becomes honest.

The asbab thread adds weight. Revelation was delayed so the Ummah would learn that even the Prophet does not control the arrival of tomorrow's answer. The Messenger waits for Allah's revelation. If revelation itself descends by Allah's will, then how can we imagine our ordinary projects are independent?

This should make us gentler with ourselves and more serious with Allah. Gentler, because many delays are not proof of worthlessness. More serious, because we cannot keep storing obedience in a future we do not own.

The practical application is to turn future language into worship. Before a plan, say "if Allah wills" with presence. Before a major task, ask for help. After forgetting, remember. After being corrected, ask for nearer rushd. And when the thing you keep pushing to tomorrow is actually an obligation of today, stop using tomorrow as a hiding place.

Tomorrow belongs to Allah. The honesty of this moment has been entrusted to you.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayahs gather much of Al-Kahf into a single discipline of the tongue.

The surah has shown us time hidden inside a cave, sleepers raised after years, people disputing about numbers, and revelation refusing to let the unseen become a playground. Now it brings that training into our ordinary speech about tomorrow.

You do not know the hidden past except by Allah's teaching. You do not own the future except by Allah's willing. You do not escape forgetfulness except by Allah's reminder. You do not reach nearer guidance except by Allah's guidance.

### The Architecture of Do Not Promise Tomorrow Without Allah

The passage moves through correction, exception, recovery, and aspiration.

First comes correction: do not say of anything that you will do it tomorrow with independent certainty. Then comes the exception that restores reality: except if Allah wills. Then comes recovery after human weakness: remember your Lord when you forget. Finally comes aspiration: ask your Lord to guide you to what is nearer than this in rushd.

The structure is mercifully complete. It prevents arrogance before the plan, restores dependence inside the plan, opens repentance after forgetfulness, and turns the whole experience into a prayer for greater guidance.

### Questions to Carry

Where do I speak about tomorrow as if I own it? Notice the areas where your language becomes most confident: work, family, worship, money, health, repentance. Those are often the places where dependence needs to be restored.

Do I use "in sha Allah" as worship or as evasion? The phrase should join sincere intention with humility before Allah's will. If it has become a way to avoid responsibility, the tongue needs to be retrained.

What do I do immediately after I forget? The ayah does not leave the forgetful person outside. It teaches return: remember your Lord when you forget, and do not let a lapse become a home.

Am I asking only to recover, or am I asking to be guided nearer than before? Allah can turn correction into increase. The believer should not merely ask to get back to normal; he should ask for nearer rushd.

What obedience have I hidden inside the word tomorrow? Some plans belong tomorrow. Some duties are being delayed by the nafs. Ask Allah to show you the difference with mercy and clarity.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The believer may plan for tomorrow, but he must speak as a servant: intending sincerely, attaching every future to Allah's will, remembering after forgetfulness, and asking to be guided nearer to rushd.**

### The Closing Invitation

Let these ayahs change one ordinary sentence today. Before you promise, pause. Before you plan, remember. Before you say "tomorrow," place tomorrow back in Allah's hand. And if you already forgot, do not stand outside the door in shame. Remember your Lord now and ask Him to guide you to something nearer, cleaner, truer, and more rightly guided than the path you were about to take alone.

### Du'a

O Allah, Lord of our tomorrows and our forgotten moments, do not leave us to the arrogance of our own plans. Teach our tongues to say "if You will" with living hearts. When we forget, return us to Your remembrance quickly. When we are corrected, make the correction a door to greater guidance. Guide us, our Lord, to what is nearer than this in rushd, and make our intentions truthful, our promises honest, and our futures surrendered to You. Ameen.
