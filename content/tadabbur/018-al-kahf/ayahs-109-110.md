# The Ink That Ran Out Before the Words Did

## Al-Kahf 18:109-110

[ayah:18:109] قُل لَّوْ كَانَ ٱلْبَحْرُ مِدَادًا لِّكَلِمَـٰتِ رَبِّى لَنَفِدَ ٱلْبَحْرُ قَبْلَ أَن تَنفَدَ كَلِمَـٰتُ رَبِّى وَلَوْ جِئْنَا بِمِثْلِهِۦ مَدَدًا

[ayah:18:110] قُلْ إِنَّمَا أَنَا۠ بَشَرٌ مِّثْلُكُمْ يُوحَىٰٓ إِلَىَّ أَنَّمَآ إِلَـٰهُكُمْ إِلَـٰهٌ وَٰحِدٌ ۖ فَمَن كَانَ يَرْجُوا۟ لِقَآءَ رَبِّهِۦ فَلْيَعْمَلْ عَمَلًا صَـٰلِحًا وَلَا يُشْرِكْ بِعِبَادَةِ رَبِّهِۦ أَحَدًۢا

*Say: If the sea were ink for the words of my Lord, the sea would be exhausted before the words of my Lord were exhausted, even if We brought the like of it as supplement. Say: I am only a man like you, to whom it has been revealed that your god is one God. So whoever hopes for the meeting with his Lord, let him do righteous work and not associate in the worship of his Lord anyone.*

---

## Introduction

You've been inside this surah for a long time now.

Al-Kahf. The Cave. And if you've been paying attention, you've noticed that every story in it is about the same thing: **something hidden.**

The sleepers in the cave — hidden from time. Their bodies asleep for centuries while the world moved on without them. Hidden behind a wall of divine protection that looked, from the outside, like ordinary stone.

Musa and al-Khidr — hidden wisdom. A boat damaged, a boy killed, a wall rebuilt — every action looked like injustice on the surface, and every action concealed a mercy beneath it. The knowledge was always there. You just couldn't see it from where you stood.

Dhul-Qarnayn — hidden walls at the edges of the earth. A barrier holding back Yajuj and Majuj that would stand until Allah's appointed time. Something massive, built to contain chaos, invisible to most of humanity.

Story after story. Hidden, hidden, hidden.

And now the surah arrives at its closing argument. Two ayahs. Two commands to speak — both beginning with *qul* — **Say.** And what the Prophet ﷺ is commanded to say will reframe everything.

Because the real hidden knowledge in this surah was never in the cave. It was never behind al-Khidr's strange actions. It was never behind Dhul-Qarnayn's wall.

**The real hidden knowledge is in the speech of Allah Himself** — and that speech is so vast, so limitless, so beyond containment, that the entire ocean could be converted into ink and it would run dry before the words ran out.

And then — without a breath, without a transition — the one who delivers this message about infinity turns to you and says: *I am only a man like you.*

That is where we're going.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### I. The Ink That Extended and the Sea That Could Not

There is a word game in this ayah that no translation can carry. It happens in two words, separated by an entire verse, and the listener who catches it will never forget it.

The first word:

مِدَادًا — *midaadan* — **ink.**

In Arabic, every word wears its root on its face. The root of *midaad* is *mim-dal-dal* — م-د-د — and this root means **to extend, to stretch, to supply.** Ink is not just a liquid in Arabic. It is *that which extends* — the instrument through which thought stretches from the mind onto the page. The word itself tells you what ink does: it extends what was internal into something visible, something that can be read, something that can travel.

Now Allah says: imagine the entire sea — ٱلْبَحْرُ — converted into this substance. Every ocean, every body of water on earth. Turned into the instrument of extension. Imagine dipping a pen the size of a mountain into the Pacific and writing the words of your Lord across a page the size of the sky.

لَنَفِدَ ٱلْبَحْرُ — *the sea would be exhausted.*

[PAUSE]

The verb: *nafida.* نَفِدَ. Form I — the simplest, most direct form. To be exhausted. To run out. To be depleted. No external agent draining it. The sea would exhaust *itself.* The mere attempt to record divine words would drain it completely — not because something drained it, but because **the task is larger than the container.**

And notice the tense. *La-nafida* — لَنَفِدَ — this is the **perfect tense.** In Arabic, the perfect tense signals completion. Finality. Done. Over. The sea's exhaustion is described as a *completed event* — even though it's hypothetical. Allah uses the tense of certainty for the finite thing. The sea IS done. It IS over. It IS exhausted. The perfective form closes the door.

Now the second half:

قَبْلَ أَن تَنفَدَ كَلِمَـٰتُ رَبِّى — *before the words of my Lord were exhausted.*

The same verb — *nafida* — but now in the **imperfect subjunctive.** تَنفَدَ. And the imperfect tense in Arabic signals something incomplete. Ongoing. Open-ended. Not yet finished. *Not finishing.*

Stay with this. The grammar is doing the theology.

**The ocean gets the perfect tense — the tense of completion. It's done.**
**The words get the imperfect tense — the tense of incompletion. They are never done.**

The finite thing is described with the finite tense. The infinite thing is described with the infinite tense. **The Arabic grammar itself encodes the difference between what runs out and what does not.**

No translation can carry this. In English, both sentences use "would be exhausted." But in Arabic, one is *nafida* — completed, over — and the other is *tanfada* — not completed, never over. The tense tells you which one is infinite before your mind does.

[PAUSE]

Now — the word game.

The ayah opened with *midaadan* — مِدَادًا — ink. Root: م-د-د. That which extends.

The ayah closes with: وَلَوْ جِئْنَا بِمِثْلِهِۦ مَدَدًا — *even if We brought the like of it as supplement.*

*Madadan.* مَدَدًا. Root: **م-د-د.** The same root. Extension. Supplement. Reinforcement.

The ayah opens with the ocean-as-ink (*midaad* — that which extends thought onto page) and closes with the ocean-supplement (*madad* — that which extends supply). The root that means "to extend" **bookends the entire verse.** It wraps around the ayah like a frame. Extension at the beginning, extension at the end — and everything inside that frame is about the impossibility of extension reaching far enough.

The root of extension itself confesses: **I cannot extend far enough.**

Even if another ocean were brought — a whole second sea, the like of the first — as *madad*, as supplement, as reinforcement — even then, the words would not be exhausted.

Double the ocean. It doesn't matter. The task is not difficult. **The task is impossible.** The infinite is not a very large finite. It is a different category entirely. And the Arabic, by using the same root for both the attempt (ink) and the reinforcement (supplement), makes the futility echo.

[PAUSE]

Here's the analogy that makes this land.

Think about trying to record the life of one human being. Not their biography — their actual life. Every neuron firing. Every heartbeat. Every breath. Every involuntary thought. Every sensation of texture against skin. For one hour.

You couldn't. The data would overwhelm any storage system ever built.

Now scale that to every human who ever lived. Every moment. Every atom's behaviour in every moment. Every quantum event beneath every atom.

And that is still a finite thing. That is still just creation. The words of your Lord include not just the record of creation but every creative command that brought it into being — every *kun* — every promise made to every soul — every conversation with every prophet — every decree — and things no creation has ever heard, words spoken in pre-eternity, words reserved for realities that have not yet been made.

The ocean is a metaphor that is designed to *fail.* Its failure is the point. The listener is meant to imagine the ocean and then watch it collapse — and in the gap between the ocean's exhaustion and the words' continuation, grasp something about the nature of Allah's speech that no image can hold.

[PAUSE]

And notice who is speaking in the middle of this ayah. It begins with *qul* — Say — the Prophet speaking. رَبِّى — **my** Lord. First person. Intimate. Personal.

But then: وَلَوْ جِئْنَا — *even if **We** brought.* First person **plural majestic.** This is Allah's voice breaking through the Prophet's speech. The Prophet was commanded to say "my Lord's words" — and in the middle of that speech, his Lord's own voice enters: "even if *We* brought the like of it."

The infinite Speaker interrupts the finite speaker *within the same sentence* — as if to demonstrate the very point being made: the words cannot be contained, not even within the grammatical frame that's trying to describe them.

### II. The Pivot: From Ocean to Ordinary Skin

Now watch what comes next. No transition. No buffer. No moment to absorb the image of an ocean-as-ink failing to contain the words of God.

قُلْ إِنَّمَا أَنَا۠ بَشَرٌ مِّثْلُكُمْ — *Say: I am only a man like you.*

[PAUSE]

From infinity to ordinariness in four words.

The one who just told you that the ocean would run dry before Allah's words did — that same person now turns to you and says: **I am only skin like yours.**

The word: *bashar.* بَشَرٌ. From the root *ba-shin-ra* — ب-ش-ر. This root means **skin.** The outer surface. The visible, touchable, mortal layer. *Bashar* is the word for human that emphasizes our materiality — not our intellect (*insan*), not our mortality (*adam*), not our social nature (*nas*). Our **skin.** Our body. Our ordinary, vulnerable, bleeding, aging, perishable outer layer.

And it's **indefinite.** Not *al-bashar* — THE human. Just *basharun* — **a** human. Any human. One of many. Unspecified. Interchangeable. The Prophet who carries infinite speech is described with the most generic, most unspecified, most ordinary word for a human body.

*Mithlukum.* مِّثْلُكُمْ. **Like you.** The same. Equivalent. Not "better than you" or "elevated above you" or "chosen from among you." *Like you.* Same skin. Same hunger. Same fatigue. Same mortality.

[PAUSE]

Now the question this juxtaposition creates:

How can **the same person** deliver a message that all the oceans of the world cannot contain, and be made of the same skin as the person receiving it?

This is not a contradiction. This is the design.

Think of it like this. If an angel delivered the message, you could dismiss it. "Of course an angel says that — he's an angel. He doesn't know what it's like to be hungry, to be tired, to bury a child, to be tempted. His obedience is irrelevant to my condition."

But a *bashar*? Someone who gets hungry the way you get hungry? Someone who bleeds the way you bleed? Someone whose feet swelled from standing in prayer, whose heart broke when his son died, who was rejected and mocked and pelted with stones?

**If he can carry the message, you have no excuse not to carry the response.**

The ordinariness of the vessel is what makes the content inescapable. The *bashar* is not a bug. It is the argument.

And then one word unlocks how this is possible:

يُوحَىٰٓ إِلَىَّ — *it is revealed to me.*

*Yuha.* يُوحَىٰ. This is Form IV — **passive voice.** Not "I receive revelation" (active). Not "Allah reveals to me" (active with agent). **It is revealed.** The revelation is the grammatical subject. The Prophet is the grammatical recipient. The divine voice is not even named as agent — because the focus is not on who sends but on *what arrives* and *where it lands.*

The passive form is the theological statement: **the Prophet does not produce revelation. It happens to him.** He is the surface onto which the infinite is inscribed — the way a page receives ink. He doesn't create the words. The words land on him. And the fact that the surface is ordinary — *bashar*, skin, human — makes the landing more astonishing, not less.

The root of *yuha*: *waw-ha-ya* — و-ح-ي. This root means **to communicate swiftly and subtly** — a whispered instruction, a gestured signal, an inspiration that arrives without the noise of ordinary speech. The very sound of the word — يُوحَى — is soft, whispered, arriving quietly. You almost have to lean in to hear it. The phonetics of the word perform its meaning.

[PAUSE]

Now notice the pronoun shift. In 18:109, the Prophet said: كَلِمَـٰتِ **رَبِّى** — the words of **my** Lord. First person singular. Intimate. Personal. The relationship between the Prophet and his Lord is one of closeness — *Rabbi*, the possessive of tenderness.

In 18:110, after announcing his humanity, the address widens: فَمَن كَانَ يَرْجُوا۟ لِقَآءَ **رَبِّهِ** — whoever hopes for the meeting with **his** Lord. Third person. Universal. The intimacy of *Rabbi* (my Lord) gives way to *Rabbihi* (his Lord) — the relationship is no longer private. It's available. Whoever hopes. Anyone.

**The infinite words belong to "my Lord." The meeting is offered to everyone through "his Lord."** The pronoun shift maps the movement from private revelation to universal invitation.

### III. The Last Word

Now watch how the surah ends.

وَلَا يُشْرِكْ بِعِبَادَةِ رَبِّهِۦ أَحَدًۢا

*And let him not associate in the worship of his Lord — anyone.*

The verb: *yushrik.* يُشْرِكْ. Form IV — **causative.** Not Form I *sharika* (to be a partner). Form IV: to **cause** partnership. To **introduce** an associate. The human is the active agent who creates the violation. Shirk is not something that happens to you. It is something **you do.** You are the one who brings the partner in.

And then — the last word of the surah:

**أَحَدًا.** *Ahadan.* **Anyone.**

Not "don't associate idols." Not "don't associate other gods." Not "don't associate the things the Quraysh worship."

Anyone.

Indefinite. Sweeping. Absolute. The scope of the prohibition is as wide as the scope of the ocean image was vast. *Ahadan* includes every possible partner — stone, angel, saint, ego, reputation, audience. Al-Razi noted that this indefinite form is what includes *riya'* — showing off in worship — because the "anyone" you associate could be the audience watching you pray.

[PAUSE]

Now step back and look at the architecture of this surah's closure.

The surah that began with ٱلْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ — **All praise belongs to Allah** — ends with أَحَدًا — **anyone** [that you must not associate]. The first word was an affirmation: all praise. The last word was a prohibition: no one. The surah opens by giving everything to Allah and closes by warning you not to give a single thing to anyone else.

And the progression within these two ayahs mirrors the entire surah:

The cave stories gave you complexity. Sleepers, hidden timelines, Khidr's mysterious wisdom, Dhul-Qarnayn's cosmic walls. Layer upon layer of depth. You might think the message of the Quran is complex — that you need to master all these stories, decode all these symbols, to understand what Allah is asking of you.

And then the last ayah strips all of that away. **It all comes down to one thing.**

Don't worship anything else.

The complexity resolves into simplicity. The ocean of words condenses into a single prohibition. The infinite is asking for something astonishingly finite: *sincerity.*

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### I. The Boredom of the Saturated

Here's a condition that the first listeners of this surah did not have: **information saturation.**

You and I live in the age of "I've heard everything." Podcasts. Books. Lectures. Articles. Social media threads that promise to "blow your mind" and deliver a moderate dopamine bump followed by forgetting. We consume more information in a single week than a medieval scholar encountered in a year.

And the result — the strange, counterintuitive result — is that we feel like we've *already heard* whatever you're about to say.

You mention the Quran and someone's inner voice says: "I know. I know about the Quran. I've heard about it. I've listened to lectures. I've read translations. I've watched YouTube videos. I get the gist."

This ayah demolishes that posture completely.

If the entire ocean — every body of water on planet earth — were converted into ink, and you started writing the words of your Lord, **the ocean would run out.** Not eventually. Not after a long time. It would be *nafida* — exhausted, done — before the words even slowed down. Bring another ocean. It runs out too. Bring another. Gone.

What you have "heard" — the Quran, the hadith, the lectures — is a fraction of a fraction of a fraction of what Allah has said. It is a cup of water from a sea that cannot be contained by all the seas that exist.

The boredom you feel with knowledge is not a sign that you've gone deep enough. It is a sign that you have never gone deep at all. You have been skimming the surface of an ocean and calling it familiar. The surface is all you've seen. Beneath it is a depth that would take every ocean of ink to record and still leave the words flowing.

[PAUSE]

Here's the practical edge of this.

Every time you open the Quran and think "I know this ayah" — every time a surah begins and you feel the pull of familiarity, the yawn of repetition — this ayah is speaking directly to you.

You don't know the ayah. You've *seen* the ayah. You've *read* the ayah. You may have even memorized it. But the *kalimat* — the words of your Lord — are deeper than any ocean's depth. And the proof is that every generation of scholars who spent their lives in a single surah died saying: "I did not exhaust it." Because the words do not exhaust. The readers do.

**You are not bored because the Quran is shallow. You are bored because you haven't gone deep enough to feel how deep it actually is.**

The first few metres of the ocean look the same everywhere. It's only when you descend — past the sunlight, past the comfortable zone, past the point where your lungs complain — that the ocean starts showing you things no surface swimmer has ever seen.

### II. The Terrifying Mercy of a Human Messenger

Now the second theme — and this one is harder. Because it asks you to sit with something uncomfortable.

**Why does it matter that the Prophet was a man like you?**

In our time, we have an instinct to elevate our spiritual leaders beyond humanity. We want them to be different. Special. Set apart. Not because we want to worship them — but because if they're *too much* like us, their message becomes *too binding.* If the Prophet were an angel, or a divine manifestation, or something fundamentally other — we could admire him from a distance. We could say: "That's beautiful, but he's him. I'm me. Different category."

بَشَرٌ مِّثْلُكُمْ — *a man like you* — destroys that escape route.

He was skin. *Bashar.* The same material you're made of. He got cold. He got hungry. He mourned. He bled at Ta'if. He cried when his son Ibrahim died. He struggled. He was human in every way that you are human — and then the infinite words of God landed on him.

This means two things simultaneously, and both of them should keep you up at night.

**First: the content is not limited by the container.** The words that all the oceans cannot hold were delivered through a human throat. The infinite was carried by the finite. This is not despite his humanity — it is *through* it. يُوحَىٰ — the passive voice — means the revelation happened TO him, THROUGH him, not FROM him. He was the page. The ink — the *midaad*, the instrument of extension — came from elsewhere.

**Second: if a man like you can carry it, you can respond to it.** The response the surah asks for is not superhuman. It is precisely human. *Fa-man kana yarju liqa'a Rabbihi* — whoever **hopes** to meet his Lord. Not whoever has achieved perfection. Not whoever has mastered all knowledge. Whoever *hopes.* Whoever carries in their chest the ache of wanting to meet the One who spoke these words.

And the response is two things: فَلْيَعْمَلْ عَمَلًا صَـٰلِحًا — let him do righteous work. وَلَا يُشْرِكْ — and let him not associate anyone.

**Good work. Sincerity.**

That's it. That's what the ocean image — the infinite speech of God, the exhaustion of all the seas — reduces to. The infinite asks for something almost heartbreakingly simple: do good, and mean it.

[PAUSE]

But that word — يُشْرِكْ — *yushrik* — Form IV, causative. Don't **cause** partnership. Don't **introduce** an associate.

Notice: you are the agent. Shirk doesn't sneak in on its own. You open the door. You bring the partner in. Maybe the partner is an idol — but maybe the partner is the audience. The people watching you pray. The person you want to impress with your charity. The reputation you're building with your knowledge.

This is what al-Razi saw in the indefinite أَحَدًا — "anyone." It's not just stone idols. It's *riya'* — the performance of worship for an audience other than Allah. The subtlest shirk is not bowing to a statue. It is bowing to Allah while checking whether anyone noticed.

And this comes right after the ocean image. Right after the infinite. As if Allah is saying: the content I have given you is limitless. Please don't cheapen it by performing it for someone else's approval.

The ocean cannot contain My words. **Can your sincerity?**

[PAUSE]

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture

Two commands to speak. Two *qul*'s. And between them, the arc of the entire surah.

**18:109** — *Qul.* Say: the ocean would run dry as ink before the words of your Lord ran out. **Infinity upward** — the vastness of divine speech.

**18:110** — *Qul.* Say: I am only a man like you. **Descent downward** — the ordinariness of the human vessel.

Infinity → Ordinariness.

And the movement mirrors the entire surah's journey: the cave stories escalated in scope — from a local cave, to a transcendent journey with al-Khidr, to the edges of the earth with Dhul-Qarnayn. Wider and wider and wider. And then, at the summit, the surah does not keep ascending. **It lands.** It comes home. To you. To your skin. To the simple question: what will you do about it?

The root م-د-د wraps 18:109 — مِدَادًا at the opening, مَدَدًا at the close — the root of extension confessing that it cannot extend far enough. And then the root of extension gives way to the root of ordinariness: بَشَرٌ. Skin. Material. Here.

The pronouns trace the intimacy gradient: رَبِّى (my Lord) → رَبِّهِ (his Lord). What begins as the Prophet's private relationship with the Infinite becomes — through his humanity, through his shared skin — an invitation to everyone. The meeting is available because the messenger is *bashar.* If he were more, you could excuse yourself. Because he is like you, the door is open and the excuse is gone.

And the last word: أَحَدًا. **Anyone.** The surah that began with all praise belonging to Allah (ٱلْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ) ends with the prohibition against giving any of it to anyone else. The first word gives everything to Him. The last word protects that gift.

### Questions to Carry

When you open the Quran and feel the pull of familiarity — the sense that you "already know" — can you hear the ocean running dry? And do you realise that the words are still flowing, and you've only ever stood on the shore?

If the Prophet who carried infinite speech was made of the same skin as you — what is the excuse you've been keeping in your back pocket for why the message doesn't quite apply to your situation?

The ayah asks you to do good work and not associate anyone. When you pray, who else is in the room besides Allah? When you give, whose approval are you seeking? Can you name the *ahad* — the "anyone" — that you've been letting through the door?

And the question beneath all the questions: **you are the same material as the one who carried the infinite. What are you carrying?**

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The sea runs dry as ink before the words run out — and the one who tells you this is made of the same skin as you, which means you have no excuse and no limit.**

### The Closing Invitation

The next time you read a single ayah of the Quran — any ayah, even one you've read a thousand times — remember what the ocean-image is really saying.

You are not reading a book. You are dipping a teaspoon into an ocean that would exhaust all other oceans if they tried to contain it. The words on the page in front of you are not the whole message. They are the portion you've been entrusted with — carried to you through the skin of a man like you, delivered in a whisper — يُوحَى — so quiet you have to lean in.

Lean in.

### Du'a

اللَّهُمَّ لَا تَجْعَلْنَا مِمَّنْ يَقْرَأُ كَلِمَاتِكَ وَقَدْ مَلَّهَا قَلْبُهُ — O Allah, do not make us among those who read Your words while their hearts have grown tired of them. وَارْزُقْنَا عُمْقًا فِي تِلَاوَتِنَا يَكْشِفُ لَنَا مَا لَمْ نَرَهُ مِن قَبْلُ — Grant us depth in our recitation that reveals what we have not seen before. وَأَخْلِصْ عِبَادَتَنَا مِنْ كُلِّ أَحَدٍ سِوَاكَ — And purify our worship of every "anyone" besides You.
