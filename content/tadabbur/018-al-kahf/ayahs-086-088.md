# Justice at the Western Horizon

## Surah Al-Kahf (18:86-88)

[ayah:18:86] حَتَّىٰٓ إِذَا بَلَغَ مَغْرِبَ ٱلشَّمْسِ وَجَدَهَا تَغْرُبُ فِى عَيْنٍ حَمِئَةٍ وَوَجَدَ عِندَهَا قَوْمًا ۗ قُلْنَا يَـٰذَا ٱلْقَرْنَيْنِ إِمَّآ أَن تُعَذِّبَ وَإِمَّآ أَن تَتَّخِذَ فِيهِمْ حُسْنًا

[ayah:18:87] قَالَ أَمَّا مَن ظَلَمَ فَسَوْفَ نُعَذِّبُهُۥ ثُمَّ يُرَدُّ إِلَىٰ رَبِّهِۦ فَيُعَذِّبُهُۥ عَذَابًا نُّكْرًا

[ayah:18:88] وَأَمَّا مَنْ ءَامَنَ وَعَمِلَ صَـٰلِحًا فَلَهُۥ جَزَآءً ٱلْحُسْنَىٰ ۖ وَسَنَقُولُ لَهُۥ مِنْ أَمْرِنَا يُسْرًا

*Hatta idha balagha maghriba al-shamsi wajadaha taghrubu fi 'aynin hami'ah, wa wajada 'indaha qawma. Qulna ya Dha al-Qarnayn, imma an tu'adhdhiba wa imma an tattakhidha fihim husna. Qala amma man zalama fa-sawfa nu'adhdhibuhu, thumma yuraddu ila rabbihi fa-yu'adhdhibuhu 'adhaban nukra. Wa amma man amana wa 'amila salihan fa-lahu jaza'an al-husna, wa sa-naqulu lahu min amrina yusra.*

**Until, when he reached the setting-place of the sun, he found it setting in a dark spring, and he found near it a people. We said: O Dhul-Qarnayn, either punish them or take among them a way of goodness. He said: As for whoever wrongs, we shall punish him; then he will be returned to his Lord, and He will punish him with a terrible punishment. But as for whoever believes and acts righteously, he will have the finest reward, and we shall speak to him from our command with ease.**

## Introduction

Dhul-Qarnayn has been given means.

The previous ayahs told us the order very carefully. Allah established him in the earth. Allah gave him of everything a means. Then he followed a means. The story does not begin with a conqueror's ambition. It begins with divine gift and human response. The ropes are from Allah. The climbing is from the servant.

Now the first rope brings him to a horizon.

The Quran could have filled the scene with the language of empire. It could have described armies, banners, roads, commands, spoils, and fear. Instead, it shows us a man reaching the place where the sun appears to set, finding a people there, and receiving a choice from Allah. That is the first test of power in this story: not how far he can travel, but what he does when real people stand within his reach.

The westward journey is visually immense. The sun sinks. The horizon closes. The traveler reaches an edge of human perception. But the moral center of the passage is not the sunset. It is the people near it.

This is one of the Quran's quiet corrections of our imagination. We are drawn to the spectacular part of the scene: the sun, the dark spring, the far horizon. The Quran moves our attention to the ethical part: a people, a ruler with capacity, and the question of punishment or goodness.

Classical tafsir keeps both pieces in view. Ibn Kathir explains that Dhul-Qarnayn reached the farthest western point accessible to him and saw the sun as though it were setting into a dark, muddy body of water from the perspective of someone standing at that horizon. Al-Tabari discusses the wording around the spring and the reports connected to it, while the moral flow of the ayah turns quickly to the people found there and the choice given to Dhul-Qarnayn. Al-Muyassar summarizes the scene with the same practical movement: he reached the western limit, found a people, and was given the choice either to punish or to deal with them in goodness.

That movement is the key. The Quran is not teaching astronomy. It is teaching how power is tested at the edge of reach.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. "Until, When He Reached" - Power Arrives at a Limit

The passage opens with *hatta idha balagha* - "until, when he reached."

The word *hatta* carries the sense of progression up to a point. Dhul-Qarnayn has followed a means, and the narrative now follows that pursuit until it reaches a boundary. The verb *balagha* means to arrive, to reach, to attain a point. It is not just movement. It is movement arriving at its intended extent.

This matters because the story of power begins with limit.

Dhul-Qarnayn is powerful, but the first journey is narrated as reaching a boundary, not as possessing infinity. He goes as far as the means take him. He reaches the setting-place of the sun from the perspective of earthbound travel. The phrase *maghriba al-shams* names the westward horizon, the place of sunset, the direction where light appears to disappear.

The root behind *maghrib* carries the field of setting, departing, becoming distant, and going away. The same root gives us *gharb*, the west, and *ghurba*, estrangement. There is a feeling in the word of something moving away from the visible world. The sun that filled the sky is now departing from sight.

But the Quran does not let this image become mystical escape. It ties the horizon to accountability. The man who reaches the edge does not become beyond responsibility. He becomes responsible at the edge.

That is one of the first lessons of the phrase. Means can take you far, but wherever they take you, you are still under Allah. The farthest horizon does not become a place outside command. The end of your map is still inside the dominion of your Lord.

There is also a contrast with the Musa-Khidr journey before this. Musa reached a meeting-place of two seas and entered a journey where his knowledge met its limit. Dhul-Qarnayn reaches the western horizon and enters a journey where his power meets its moral test. Al-Kahf places both men at edges. At one edge, knowledge is humbled. At another, authority is examined.

### 2. "He Found It Setting" - The Quran Speaks From Human Sight Without Becoming Captive to It

The ayah says *wajadahaha taghrubu* - "he found it setting."

The verb *wajada* is important. It means he found, he encountered, he perceived it in that way. The Quran does not say the sun literally entered the spring as an astronomical claim. It says he found it setting in that scene. From his vantage point, from the horizon available to him, this is what appeared before his eyes.

This wording is precise and merciful. Human beings live by perception. We say, "the sun rose," and "the sun set," because that is how the world appears to us from the ground. The Quran speaks in the language of lived human experience without needing to flatten that language into a technical manual.

The phrase continues: *fi 'aynin hami'ah* - in a dark, muddy spring or body of water. The word *'ayn* can mean an eye, a spring, a source of water, and by extension a visible point or source. Its semantic field is rich because an eye and a spring both gather and issue forth. One sees. One flows. Here, the horizon image is watery, dark, and low.

The adjective *hami'ah* is linked to dark mud or blackened sediment. Classical discussion also notes related recitational and interpretive material around heat and mud in the description. The safest way to sit with the ayah is not to force the image into an argument the ayah is not making. Dhul-Qarnayn reached a western limit where, from his perspective, the sun appeared to sink into a dark body of water.

Why does that image matter spiritually?

Because the Quran places the first test of righteous power at sunset, not sunrise. The scene is not bright expansion. It is descent, dimming, and edge. The visible world is becoming less clear. The ruler reaches a place where light is going down. And precisely there, where sight is dramatic but limited, he finds people and must judge.

Power often loves clear daylight. It loves certainty, display, the feeling that everything is visible and controllable. But many of the hardest decisions are made in muddy light, at horizons, among people whose full story is not immediately clear. The Quran shows Dhul-Qarnayn not as someone intoxicated by the spectacle, but as someone who can move from perception to responsibility.

He found the sun setting. Then he found a people.

The second finding is more important than the first.

### 3. "And He Found Near It a People" - Geography Becomes Moral Encounter

The ayah repeats the verb *wajada*: he found it setting, and he found near it a people.

The repetition is deliberate. First he finds a scene. Then he finds human beings. The eye moves from horizon to community. In the Quranic imagination, travel is not complete when you reach a place. It becomes morally serious when you encounter the people of that place.

The word *qawm* refers to a people, a community, a group bound by some shared life. It is indefinite here: a people. Their name is not given. Their ethnicity is not named. Their language is not recorded. The Quran does not ask us to classify them before it asks us to notice how they are treated.

That restraint matters. Powerful people often reduce communities to labels. A region becomes "the west," "the border," "the problem population," "the locals," "the hostile group," "the poor," "the backward," "the useful," "the dangerous." Once a people become a label, it becomes easier to treat them as a mass rather than as morally differentiated human beings.

The next two ayahs will undo that flattening. Dhul-Qarnayn will not respond to the qawm as one undifferentiated mass. He will distinguish the wrongdoer from the believer who acts righteously. This is already hidden in the way the Quran first names them broadly and then lets justice make distinctions.

The phrase *'indaha* - near it, at it, by it - ties the people to the horizon scene. They are located near the place where he sees the sun setting. But morally, they are not scenery. They are the point.

This is one of the greatest tests of travel, leadership, scholarship, and even religious work: do people become scenery for your story, or do they remain servants of Allah with claims upon your justice?

Dhul-Qarnayn is not praised because he reached a distant place. He is shown because when he reached it, the people he found there did not disappear into his ambition.

### 4. "Either Punish, or Take Among Them Goodness" - The Trust of Discretion

Then comes the divine address: *qulna ya Dha al-Qarnayn* - "We said: O Dhul-Qarnayn."

The story that began with means now receives direct divine speech. The address dignifies him and tests him at once. He is not merely traveling by worldly capacity. He is being dealt with under divine command and accountability.

The choice is framed with *imma ... wa imma* - either this, or either that. The two options are: *an tu'adhdhiba* - that you punish, and *an tattakhidha fihim husna* - that you take among them goodness, or adopt toward them a good course.

The grammar after *an* places the verbs in the subjunctive. They are possible courses of action placed before him. He has discretion. That is terrifying. It would be easier, in one sense, if the command were mechanically specific: do exactly this, no judgment required. But power often tests a person by giving them room to decide within moral bounds.

The first verb, *tu'adhdhiba*, comes from the field of punishment and chastisement. It is not random violence. In the Quran, punishment has moral context. The second verb, *tattakhidha*, is from the field of taking, adopting, making something one's course or choice. He may take among them *husna* - goodness, beauty, excellence, a good dealing.

Notice the asymmetry. The first option is an act done to them: punish. The second is a posture taken among them: take in them goodness. The wording invites us to imagine not only non-punishment, but an active policy of good treatment, reform, gentleness, or beneficent rule.

Classical tafsir generally reads this as Allah giving Dhul-Qarnayn authority over the people: he may punish those who persist in wrongdoing and treat well those who respond rightly. But the ayah presents the choice before his response so we can see his character. Power has been placed in his hand. What kind of ruler will he be?

This is where many people fail. They assume having the right to punish means punishment should become the first language of authority. They assume that the presence of wrongdoing removes the need for ihsan. But Dhul-Qarnayn's answer will show a more disciplined soul. He does not abolish punishment. He does not romanticize wrongdoing. He also does not punish indiscriminately. He divides by moral reality.

The test of discretion is whether power can stay obedient when it has options.

### 5. "As for Whoever Wrongs... As for Whoever Believes" - Justice Differentiates

Dhul-Qarnayn answers with structure.

First: *amma man zalama* - as for whoever wrongs. Then: *wa amma man amana wa 'amila salihan* - and as for whoever believes and acts righteously.

The repeated *amma* creates a clear division. He does not answer, "As for this people, we will punish them," nor, "As for this people, we will be gentle with all of them." He refuses collective flattening. The category is not ethnicity, location, weakness, foreignness, or usefulness. The category is moral response: wrongdoing on one side, belief and righteous action on the other.

The word *zalama* comes from the field of wrongdoing, injustice, misplacing something from its proper place, violating a right. It is broad enough to include disbelief and oppression in context, and tafsir often reads it here as those who persist in kufr or wrongdoing after the call. Dhul-Qarnayn says such a person will face worldly punishment from his authority. Then comes *thumma* - then, after that, he will be returned to his Lord. The passive *yuraddu* removes all illusion of escape. Even if a ruler punishes, the human being is still returned to the Lord, and Allah's punishment is described as *nukra* - terrible, severe, unfamiliar in its dread.

Then the second category appears: whoever believes and acts righteously. Iman is joined to righteous action, as it is throughout the Quran. Inner assent and outward repair belong together. For such a person, there is *jaza'an al-husna* - the finest reward, or a reward of the best. Then Dhul-Qarnayn adds that he will speak to him from his command with *yusra* - ease.

That last phrase is beautiful. Righteous governance is not only about distributing reward. It is also about the tone of command. The believer who acts righteously is not crushed under authority. He is spoken to with ease. The ruler's words become light, manageable, gentle, and facilitative.

This is justice with both edges intact. The wrongdoer is not excused. The righteous are not burdened harshly. Worldly authority remembers the greater court, and worldly command is softened for those who walk in iman and righteous action.

That is the first portrait of Dhul-Qarnayn's rule.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### 1. The Edge of Your Reach Is Still Inside Allah's Test

Many people imagine that accountability is strongest at the center.

When people are watching, when the rules are clear, when the community knows your name, when your role is supervised, you feel the weight of responsibility. But when you reach the edge of your reach, something changes. The place is far. The people are unfamiliar. The normal checks are absent. You have capacity, distance, and perhaps no one nearby who can stop you.

That is where power reveals itself.

Dhul-Qarnayn reaches the western horizon. In narrative terms, he is far from the center. He has followed a means until he arrives at a limit. Yet the first thing we learn is not that he is free from Allah, but that Allah speaks to him there. The far edge of the map is not a zone outside divine address.

This is a principle: the farther your means carry you, the more carefully you must remember the One who gave the means.

Al-Kahf has been teaching this from the beginning. The adornment on the earth is a test to see who is best in deed. The man with two gardens failed because abundance made him imagine permanence and self-sufficiency. Musa's journey taught that even prophetic knowledge must remain humble before what Allah has not yet disclosed. Now Dhul-Qarnayn's journey teaches that reach must remain accountable at the frontier.

The Quran makes the same point elsewhere through Dawud. In Sad, Allah addresses Dawud as a khalifah in the earth and immediately commands him to judge between people with truth and not follow desire. Authority is not presented as self-expression. It is presented as judgment under Allah. Sulayman, when given an extraordinary sign in the movement of the throne, interprets it as a test from his Lord to see whether he will be grateful or ungrateful. The righteous do not read power as proof that they are beyond trial. They read power as the trial.

The modern version is not only political office. It is any place your capacity exceeds someone else's vulnerability. A manager with an employee. A parent with a child. A teacher with a student. A spouse who controls finances. A community leader with people who trust them. A person with social media influence over those who cannot answer back. A donor whose money shapes decisions. Every one of these is a western horizon in miniature: a place where you have arrived with means and found people near your reach.

The question is what happens next.

Do the people become scenery for your self-image, or do they become amanah? Do you use distance from accountability to become harsher, or does distance from human supervision make you more conscious that Allah still sees? Do you treat the edge as permission, or as test?

Dhul-Qarnayn's first journey tells us that the edge of reach is not the edge of Lordship. Allah is there before you arrive.

### 2. Justice Begins by Refusing to Flatten People

One of the easiest forms of injustice is collective simplification.

We meet a group, hear a label, inherit a story, or suffer harm from some members of a community, and then the soul begins to flatten. "They are all like this." "Those people only understand force." "This community is corrupt." "This family is impossible." "These employees are lazy." "These children are ungrateful." The label becomes a shortcut, and the shortcut becomes a moral blindfold.

Dhul-Qarnayn finds a qawm, a people. But when he answers, he does not treat them as a single moral block. He says: as for whoever wrongs, and as for whoever believes and acts righteously.

That distinction is justice.

Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir read his answer as a policy that distinguishes the one who remains upon wrongdoing from the one who responds with faith and righteous action. Al-Muyassar presents the same division in plain terms: the wrongdoer is punished in this world and then returned to Allah, while the believer who acts righteously receives good reward and gentle command. The moral categories are individual and responsive.

The Quran repeats this principle constantly. No bearer of burden bears the burden of another. Allah commands justice and ihsan. Allah commands that trusts be rendered to their people and that judgment between people be with justice. Even hostility is not allowed to push believers away from justice, as Al-Ma'idah teaches: do not let hatred of a people cause you to be unjust; be just, that is nearer to taqwa.

This is not softness toward evil. It is precision against evil.

Collective flattening often feels strong, but it is actually lazy. It spares the powerful from the harder work of discernment. It is easier to punish a category than to judge a case. It is easier to speak harshly about a group than to distinguish the wrongdoer, the repentant, the sincere, the fearful, the ignorant, the oppressed, and the righteous within it.

Dhul-Qarnayn's answer gives us a discipline for every place we hold authority: do not let the existence of wrongdoing make you unjust to those who are not wrongdoers. Do not let compassion for the righteous become denial of oppression. Hold both.

There is also a personal version of this. Sometimes we flatten ourselves. We commit a wrong and say, "I am corrupt." We see a good deed and say, "I am fine." The Quranic way is more precise. Name the wrongdoing as wrongdoing. Name iman and righteous action as mercy. Do not turn one act into a false total identity, but do not refuse the moral category of the act either.

Justice begins when things are placed where they belong.

### 3. Worldly Punishment Is Never the Final Court

Dhul-Qarnayn says of the wrongdoer: we shall punish him; then he will be returned to his Lord, and He will punish him with a terrible punishment.

The structure is sobering. There is a worldly consequence, then a return to the Lord. The ruler's punishment is not erased, but it is not ultimate. It is followed by the divine court.

This does two things at once.

First, it gives worldly authority seriousness. Dhul-Qarnayn does not say, "Allah will judge them later, so I have no responsibility now." That would be spiritualized negligence. If Allah gives authority to restrain wrongdoing, then refusing to restrain it can itself become injustice. Communities are harmed when leaders use pious language to avoid difficult accountability.

Second, it humbles worldly authority. Dhul-Qarnayn does not say, "Our punishment settles everything." He says the wrongdoer will then be returned to his Lord. The greater judgment belongs to Allah. No ruler, court, parent, teacher, or community can become the final owner of another soul's destiny.

This balance is hard. Some people want worldly authority to do nothing because they fear harshness. Others want worldly authority to become absolute because they fear disorder. The Quran gives a middle discipline: act justly within your entrusted scope, but remember that every person returns to the Lord.

The passive wording *yuraddu* is powerful. He will be returned. The wrongdoer may imagine escape. He may survive worldly punishment. He may even manipulate human courts. But return is not optional. The same surah has already shown that the Book will be placed, the criminals will fear what is in it, and they will find what they did present. Nothing small or great is left out. The return to the Lord is the horizon behind every horizon.

This also protects the heart of the one harmed. Human justice may be necessary, but it is often incomplete. Sometimes the wrongdoer is beyond your reach. Sometimes the process fails. Sometimes consequences arrive but do not feel proportionate to the wound. This ayah does not tell you to stop caring about justice. It tells you not to imagine that human consequence is the last word. The Lord remains.

And because the Lord remains, worldly punishment must remain humble. It cannot become revenge disguised as justice. It cannot pretend to know the unseen. It cannot enjoy humiliation. It can restrain, discipline, and protect, but it must do so knowing that the final accounting belongs to the One whose knowledge is perfect.

Dhul-Qarnayn's sentence about the wrongdoer is therefore not bloodthirsty. It is ordered. Worldly consequence, then return to Lordship. Human authority, then divine judgment. Action now, accountability forever.

### 4. Ease Is Part of Righteous Governance

The final line is easy to pass over because the punishment language before it is so severe.

As for the one who believes and acts righteously, he will have the finest reward, and we shall speak to him from our command with ease.

Not only reward. Ease in command.

This is a profound picture of righteous authority. Dhul-Qarnayn does not merely say, "Good people will be rewarded." He says his own dealing with them will be made easy, gentle, manageable. The word *yusr* carries ease, facility, lack of harshness, and smoothness. It is the opposite of making obedience unnecessarily crushing.

Many people in authority know how to punish. Fewer know how to make obedience easy for the obedient.

Think of a parent who only becomes attentive when a child makes a mistake, but never makes goodness feel light and loved. Think of a manager who disciplines failure, but makes honest work exhausting through unclear expectations and sharp speech. Think of a religious teacher who warns against sin, but speaks to sincere believers as if they are always one mistake away from being discarded. That is not the way of this ayah.

Dhul-Qarnayn's policy has punishment for wrongdoing, reward for faith and righteous action, and ease in speech for the righteous. His command does not become a burden for those already walking rightly. That is ihsan inside authority.

The Quran repeatedly ties righteousness to ease. Allah wants ease for you and does not want hardship for you in the context of fasting concessions. In Al-Layl, the one who gives, has taqwa, and affirms the best will be eased toward ease. In Taha, Musa and Harun are commanded to speak gently even to Pharaoh, in the hope that he might remember or fear. If gentleness has a place even in addressing a tyrant, then how much more should ease have a place in dealing with believers who act righteously?

This is not indulgence. Ease is not the absence of standards. Dhul-Qarnayn has just affirmed punishment for wrongdoing. But because he has standards, he also knows how to honor sincere obedience. Harshness toward everyone is not strength. It is often the sign of a ruler who cannot distinguish states.

There is a personal lesson too. When you are trying to reform your own life, do not make the path of obedience harder than Allah made it. Some people punish themselves even when they are trying to obey. They speak to themselves with constant contempt. They treat every good deed as insufficient and every mistake as identity. This ayah teaches another inner governance: punish the wrongdoing by repenting and restraining it, but speak to the believing, striving part of yourself with ease. Help it continue.

Allah's religion is serious, but seriousness is not the same as heaviness in every command. Righteous power knows how to lighten the road for those walking toward Allah.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of Justice at the Western Horizon

The passage moves through three layers.

First, there is arrival: Dhul-Qarnayn follows the means until he reaches the western horizon. The scene is vast, dim, and visually dramatic. He finds the sun setting as it appears from his vantage point, then he finds a people near that horizon.

Second, there is entrusted discretion: Allah addresses him directly and places before him two possible courses - punishment or taking among them goodness. Power is tested by the presence of options.

Third, there is differentiated justice: Dhul-Qarnayn answers by dividing not according to tribe, location, or usefulness, but according to moral reality. The wrongdoer receives worldly consequence and then returns to the Lord. The believer who acts righteously receives the finest reward and is spoken to with ease.

So the architecture itself teaches the theology of authority. Means lead to horizons. Horizons lead to people. People create moral responsibility. Responsibility requires distinction. Distinction must remember both the worldly duty to restrain harm and the ultimate return to Allah.

This is righteous power in its first displayed act: not conquest, but judgment with scale.

### Questions to Carry

1. What happens to me at the edge of my reach?

Notice who you become when you are far from supervision, when others have less power, or when your decision will shape someone else's life. The horizon is not outside Allah's sight. It is often where the real test begins.

2. Do I flatten people into groups when I am hurt, afraid, or in charge?

Dhul-Qarnayn finds a people, but answers by distinguishing the wrongdoer from the believer who acts righteously. Ask where you have let a label replace justice. Ask whom your generalizations have harmed.

3. Do I confuse consequence with revenge?

The wrongdoer is punished, then returned to his Lord. Human consequence has a place, but it is not the final court. Let this humble your anger and strengthen your trust in Allah's complete judgment.

4. Do I make obedience easy for those who are trying?

The righteous person receives not only reward, but ease in command. If you lead, teach, parent, advise, or manage, ask whether your speech helps sincere people continue or makes righteousness feel heavier than Allah made it.

5. Can I hold justice and ihsan together?

Some hearts lean toward punishment without mercy. Others lean toward mercy without moral clarity. Dhul-Qarnayn's answer holds both: wrong is restrained, faith and righteous action are honored, and the command to the good is gentle.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**When Dhul-Qarnayn reaches the western horizon, the Quran shows that righteous power is not measured by distance traveled, but by justice that distinguishes wrong from faith, remembers the final return to Allah, and makes obedience easy for those who do good.**

### The Closing Invitation

Carry this passage into every place where Allah has given you reach. Your authority may be small, but it is still a trust. Find the people, not just the horizon. Refuse the laziness of flattening. Restrain wrong without becoming intoxicated by punishment. Honor iman and righteous action. And when someone is trying to obey Allah under your care, speak from your command with ease.

### Du'a

O Allah, make us just when You give us reach, humble when You give us means, and gentle with those who are trying to obey You. Protect us from flattening people, from enjoying punishment, and from forgetting that every soul returns to You. Teach us to restrain wrong without transgressing, to honor faith and righteous action, and to make Your path easier for those walking toward You. Ameen.
