# The Refusal That Defined a Sovereign

## Introduction

[Arabic text]
**قَالَ مَا مَكَّنِّي فِيهِ رَبِّي خَيْرٌ فَأَعِينُونِي بِقُوَّةٍ أَجْعَلْ بَيْنَكُمْ وَبَيْنَهُمْ رَدْمًا ﴿٩٥﴾ آتُونِي زُبَرَ الْحَدِيدِ ۖ حَتَّىٰ إِذَا سَاوَىٰ بَيْنَ الصَّدَفَيْنِ قَالَ انفُخُوا ۖ حَتَّىٰ إِذَا جَعَلَهُ نَارًا قَالَ آتُونِي أُفْرِغْ عَلَيْهِ قِطْرًا ﴿٩٦﴾ فَمَا اسْطَاعُوا أَن يَظْهَرُوهُ وَمَا اسْتَطَاعُوا لَهُ نَقْبًا ﴿٩٧﴾**

[Transliteration]
*Qāla mā makkannī fīhi Rabbī khayrun fa-a'īnūnī bi-quwwatin aj'al baynakum wa-baynahum radmā. Ātūnī zubara al-ḥadīdi ḥattā idhā sāwā bayna al-ṣadafayni qāla unfukhū ḥattā idhā ja'alahu nāran qāla ātūnī ufrigh 'alayhi qiṭrā. Fa-mā isṭā'ū an yaẓharūhu wa-mā istaṭā'ū lahu naqbā.*

[Translation]
"He said: That in which my Lord has established me is better — but assist me with strength, and I will make between you and them a barrier. Bring me blocks of iron. Until, when he had leveled them between the two mountain sides, he said: Blow. Until, when he had made it fire, he said: Bring me, that I may pour over it molten copper. So they could neither scale it nor could they penetrate it."

---

A desperate people offer tribute to a powerful sovereign, begging for protection from forces they cannot stop. It is the oldest transaction in human history — the weak paying the strong for safety. And Dhul-Qarnayn refuses the payment.

This refusal — and the building project that follows — is one of the most detailed and theologically dense passages in the Quran. Three ayahs. A speech, a construction, and a result. Together they answer the question: what does righteous power look like when it actually builds something?

---

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Refusal: مَا مَكَّنِّي فِيهِ رَبِّي خَيْرٌ (*Mā makkannī fīhi Rabbī khayr*)

When the people between the two mountains offer Dhul-Qarnayn payment — *kharj*, tribute — he does not simply decline. He explains **why**.

*Mā makkannī fīhi Rabbī khayrun.* "That in which my Lord has established me is better."

Notice the word *makkannī* — the same root م-ك-ن that appeared in 18:84 when Allah said "We established him in the earth." There, Allah was the subject: *makkannā*. Here, Dhul-Qarnayn echoes the same word back: *makkannī* — "He established me." He is naming the source. What I have did not come from me. It came from my Lord. And what my Lord gave me is *khayr* — better — than anything you can offer.

This is not false modesty. It is not a diplomatic refusal. It is a theological statement. The person who knows where his provision comes from does not need provision from below. He is already supplied from above. The tribute of a desperate people cannot improve on what Allah has already given.

[PAUSE]

But then comes the second half, and it changes everything: *fa-a'īnūnī bi-quwwatin* — "but assist me with strength." He refuses their money. He does not refuse their participation. He enlists them. The barrier will be built through their labor, directed by his vision. This is not charity. It is partnership. Protection is not something he delivers to them. It is something they build together.

### 2. The Word *Radm* — What Kind of Barrier?

The people originally asked for a *sudd* — a dam, an obstruction. Dhul-Qarnayn promises them a *radm* — a completely different word.

A *sudd* blocks. A *radm* fills. The root ر-د-م means to fill a gap entirely, to pack something so completely that nothing can pass through. While a *sudd* leaves a barrier between two sides, a *radm* eliminates the space itself. Dhul-Qarnayn does not merely block the passage. He fills it. The gap between the two mountains ceases to exist.

The word choice reveals his method: thoroughness. He does not build half-measures. When he commits to protecting a people, he does not leave cracks.

### 3. The Twin Denial in 18:97: اسْطَاعُوا vs. اسْتَطَاعُوا

The most linguistically stunning moment in this passage is the final ayah:

*Fa-mā isṭā'ū an yaẓharūhu wa-mā istaṭā'ū lahu naqbā.*

"So they could neither scale it nor could they penetrate it."

Two statements of inability — but the Quran uses two different words for "could." The first is *isṭā'ū* — a shortened, compressed form. The second is *istaṭā'ū* — the full, uncompressed form. Both come from the same root ط-و-ع, but the first drops the *ta*, making it phonetically lighter and faster.

Why the difference? Classical scholars noted that scaling a wall (*yaẓharūhu*) is a lighter, quicker action — you attempt to go over quickly. So the Quran uses the lighter, quicker word: *isṭā'ū*. Penetrating a wall (*naqbā*) is harder, more laborious work — drilling, tunneling, sustained effort. So the Quran uses the heavier, fuller word: *istaṭā'ū*.

[PAUSE]

The sound matches the meaning. The phonetic weight of each word mirrors the physical weight of the action it describes. Even in describing failure — even in a sentence about what they *could not* do — the Quran calibrates its language to the exact texture of the impossibility.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Power That Refuses to Extract

Every empire in human history has been built on extraction. The strong take from the weak — labor, tribute, resources, loyalty — in exchange for protection. This is the bargain of civilization. And it is precisely the bargain that Dhul-Qarnayn rejects.

He has the power. They have the need. The exchange is obvious. And he says: what my Lord gave me is better than your tribute. Then he builds for them anyway.

This inverts the fundamental logic of sovereignty. Power, in the Quranic framework, is not a license to extract. It is a trust to deploy. The sovereign who has been given *tamkeen* — divine establishment — uses that establishment for others, not over them. He is not enriched by the people he protects. He is enriched by the Lord who established him.

What does this look like in practice? It looks like someone who builds the barrier, enlists the people's labor, and walks away without a contract, without a tribute arrangement, without a monument bearing his name. The project serves its purpose. The builder serves his Lord. Neither requires compensation from the beneficiaries.

### Theme 2: Building Together — *A'īnūnī bi-Quwwa*

The request — "assist me with strength" — is easy to overlook. But it carries a profound insight about the nature of protection.

Dhul-Qarnayn does not build the barrier alone. He does not descend as the all-powerful savior who solves the problem while the people watch. He enlists them. Bring me iron blocks. Blow on the fire. Bring me copper. Each command draws the people deeper into the construction of their own defense.

This is dignity in action. The people who live behind this barrier will know that their hands built it. They were not rescued. They participated in their rescue. Dhul-Qarnayn's leadership does not diminish the people he serves — it activates them. He transforms passive recipients into active builders.

The practical wisdom here is profound. A barrier built *for* a people is external. A barrier built *with* a people is owned. When the wind blows and the wall is tested, the people who built it stand behind it differently than the people who merely received it. They defend what their own hands made.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture

These three ayahs follow a precise structure:

**18:95** — Speech: the theological refusal + the invitation to partnership
**18:96** — Action: the building process in technical detail
**18:97** — Result: complete impregnability, expressed in sound-matched language

The movement is from word to deed to proof. Dhul-Qarnayn says what he believes, does what he says, and the result vindicates both. There is no gap between his theology and his engineering. What he refuses to take and what he commits to build are expressions of the same principle: power exists to serve, not to extract.

### Questions to Carry

- When you have been given the means to help someone — do you extract payment, or do you build?
- Where in your life do you protect people in a way that diminishes them — doing for them what they could do with you?
- What would change in your work if you genuinely believed that divine provision is better than human tribute?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The righteous sovereign refuses payment not from modesty but from theology — what Allah has given is better than what any people can offer — and builds not for them but with them.**

### Du'a

*Allāhumma, make us among those who use what You have given in service of those who need it, without extracting from the people we serve. Teach us to build with others, not over them. And let our work be a radm — complete, thorough, and done for Your sake alone.*
