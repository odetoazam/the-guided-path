# The Discipline Before the Journey

## Al-Kahf 18:66-70

[ayah:18:66] قَالَ لَهُۥ مُوسَىٰ هَلْ أَتَّبِعُكَ عَلَىٰٓ أَن تُعَلِّمَنِ مِمَّا عُلِّمْتَ رُشْدًا

[ayah:18:67] قَالَ إِنَّكَ لَن تَسْتَطِيعَ مَعِىَ صَبْرًا

[ayah:18:68] وَكَيْفَ تَصْبِرُ عَلَىٰ مَا لَمْ تُحِطْ بِهِۦ خُبْرًا

[ayah:18:69] قَالَ سَتَجِدُنِىٓ إِن شَآءَ ٱللَّهُ صَابِرًا وَلَآ أَعْصِى لَكَ أَمْرًا

[ayah:18:70] قَالَ فَإِنِ ٱتَّبَعْتَنِى فَلَا تَسْـَٔلْنِى عَن شَىْءٍ حَتَّىٰٓ أُحْدِثَ لَكَ مِنْهُ ذِكْرًا

## Introduction

Musa has reached the meeting place.

The fish has escaped into the sea. The young companion has remembered what happened at the rock. Musa has recognized the sign he had been seeking. They have returned by their own tracks and found a servant from among Allah's servants, one to whom Allah had given mercy from Himself and taught knowledge from Himself.

Now the great prophet speaks.

And the first thing we hear from him is not a claim of rank, not a demand for explanation, not a reminder of his prophethood, not a question about the strange sign of the fish. He asks permission to follow.

This is one of the most beautiful educational scenes in the Qur'an. Musa, the prophet who spoke to Allah, the one sent to Fir'awn, the one through whom the sea split, the one who received revelation and law, comes to a servant whom Allah has taught something he has not been taught in that form. And Musa asks: may I follow you so that you teach me from what you have been taught of rushd?

But the answer is not easy. The servant says: you will never be able to have patience with me. Then he explains why: how can you have patience over what you have not encompassed in knowledge?

Before the journey begins, the Qur'an gives us the real subject of the journey. It is not curiosity. It is patience under partial knowledge. It is the discipline of walking with events whose wisdom has not yet been disclosed. It is the adab of learning from someone who has been given knowledge you do not possess. It is the humility of saying if Allah wills even about your own patience. It is the restraint of not asking until the appointed time of explanation.

Ibn Kathir identifies this servant as Khidr, following the well-known tafsir tradition, and reads Musa's request as a request to learn what Allah had taught him. Al-Tabari emphasizes Musa's humility in seeking to follow and be taught, and the servant's warning that Musa would not be able to remain patient over what he had not encompassed in knowledge. Al-Muyassar frames the same scene as Musa asking to accompany him for guidance, Khidr warning him of inability to be patient, Musa promising patience by Allah's will, and Khidr setting the condition that he not ask until Khidr initiates explanation.

There is no separate asbab al-nuzul for this passage that changes its local reading. Its placement inside Al-Kahf is central: after the surah has warned against argumentative refusal, it now shows the opposite posture. Musa does not argue with the existence of knowledge beyond him. He seeks it with humility. But even sincere seeking requires discipline.

The journey begins before the ship, the boy, or the wall. It begins with whether knowledge can make a great soul humble enough to follow.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. "May I Follow You?" - The Humility of a Prophet

Musa's first verb is *attabi'uka* - may I follow you?

The root field of following carries the sense of going behind, tracing, accompanying in the path of another. This is the same prophet who led Bani Isra'il out of bondage, confronted Fir'awn, and stood as a messenger of Allah. Yet here, in relation to a specific knowledge Allah has given another servant, he asks to follow.

The question form matters: *hal attabi'uka* - may I? Musa does not impose himself. He does not say, "I am coming with you." He asks permission. Knowledge has adab at the doorway. Even when the seeker is noble, the act of seeking requires lowering oneself to the discipline of the path.

Al-Tabari reads Musa's wording as seeking companionship in order to learn. Ibn Kathir highlights the humility of Musa asking this servant to teach him. The scene teaches that rank in one domain does not erase the need to learn in another. Prophethood does not make Musa arrogant before knowledge Allah has placed elsewhere.

This is a needed correction for us. People who know something often find it hard to become beginners again. A scholar in one field struggles to be a student in another. A parent struggles to learn from a child. A leader struggles to receive correction from someone with less status. A religious person struggles to admit ignorance in front of someone they expected to teach.

Musa's question breaks that ego. He shows that the pursuit of rushd begins with the ability to say: may I follow?

[PAUSE]

The one who cannot follow cannot be taught the parts of guidance that only appear on the road.

### 2. "So That You Teach Me" - Knowledge as Gift, Not Possession

Musa continues: *'ala an tu'allimani mimma 'ullimta* - so that you teach me from what you have been taught.

The verb *tu'allimani* comes from teaching, causing another to know. But Musa immediately qualifies the source: from what you have been taught. The servant does not own the knowledge as self-originated possession. He has been taught. The passive form points beyond the teacher to Allah.

This wording protects both teacher and student. It protects the teacher from arrogance: whatever you teach is from what you were taught. It protects the student from misplaced dependence: the teacher is a means, not the source. Allah is the One who grants knowledge.

The phrase *mimma* - from what - is also humble. Musa does not demand all of it. He asks for a portion from what the servant has been given. The seeker who wants beneficial knowledge does not begin by trying to seize everything. He asks for what Allah opens through the teacher.

This resonates with the earlier description in 18:65: the servant had been taught knowledge from Allah. The current ayah keeps that source visible. Musa's request is not merely for information. It is for taught guidance from a servant whose knowledge is itself a received mercy.

There is a subtle warning here for every religious learning environment. The more sacred the knowledge, the more dangerous it becomes to treat it as personal property. The teacher who forgets he was taught becomes harsh, possessive, or self-impressed. The student who forgets Allah is the source becomes attached to personalities, brands, and gatekeepers. Musa's wording keeps the chain pure: teach me from what you were taught.

[PAUSE]

Sacred knowledge becomes safest when both teacher and student remember that neither of them is its owner.

### 3. Rushd - Guidance That Ripens Judgment

What does Musa seek? *Rushda*.

Rushd is not merely information. It carries the sense of sound guidance, right direction, mature judgment, uprightness in choosing the correct path. Earlier in Al-Kahf, the youths of the cave asked Allah to prepare for them from their affair *rashad*. The same field returns here. Al-Kahf is a surah of rushd: guidance that knows what to do under pressure, in uncertainty, inside fitnah.

Musa does not ask, "Teach me something strange." He does not ask for spectacle, secret trivia, or hidden power. He asks for rushd. That is the proper intention of seeking knowledge: not to feel superior, not to collect unusual insights, not to win arguments, but to be guided into soundness.

This matters because the Musa-Khidr journey is often approached as a story of mysteries. But the Qur'an frames the request as rushd. The purpose of the journey is not to make us intoxicated with hidden knowledge. It is to teach us how limited our sight is and how patient we must be before Allah's wisdom.

Al-Muyassar renders the request as guidance and right direction. Al-Tabari similarly understands it as knowledge by which Musa may be guided. The classical framing keeps the spiritual aim clear.

In our age, people often seek knowledge for stimulation. We want the new angle, the hidden take, the dazzling interpretation. But Musa asks for rushd. The question for every lesson is: does this make me more rightly guided? More patient? More humble? More obedient? More able to stand where Allah placed me?

[PAUSE]

Knowledge that does not increase rushd may brighten the mind while leaving the path dark.

### 4. "You Will Not Be Able" - The Teacher Names the Real Test

The servant answers: *innaka lan tastati'a ma'iya sabra* - indeed, you will never be able to have patience with me.

The phrase begins with emphasis: *innaka*. Then comes *lan*, a strong negation directed toward the future. The verb *tastati'a* comes from ability, capacity, being able. The issue is not Musa's sincerity. It is capacity under a specific kind of trial.

The word *ma'iya* - with me - matters. The servant does not say, "You are not patient." Musa is a prophet of immense patience. He endured Fir'awn, exile, fear, leadership, and the burdens of Bani Isra'il. The warning is specific: you will not be able to have patience *with me*.

That distinction is merciful. A person may be patient in one arena and impatient in another. Patient with hardship, impatient with ambiguity. Patient with poverty, impatient with delayed explanation. Patient with enemies, impatient with teachers. Patient with pain, impatient with not knowing why.

Then comes *sabr*. In this journey, sabr is not passive endurance of suffering. It is disciplined restraint before incomplete knowledge. It is the ability not to react before the meaning of an event has been disclosed. It is patience over the gap between what you see and what Allah knows.

Ibn Kathir and al-Tabari both emphasize that the servant knew Musa would object because the outward form of the coming events would seem blameworthy before their inner wisdom was explained. This is the educational test: can Musa wait when the visible surface contradicts what he expects of righteousness?

[PAUSE]

Sometimes the hardest sabr is not enduring pain. It is enduring the delay of explanation.

### 5. "What You Have Not Encompassed" - The Limit of Human Knowledge

The servant continues: *wa kayfa tasbiru 'ala ma lam tuhit bihi khubra* - and how can you have patience over what you have not encompassed in knowledge?

This is the key sentence of the whole journey.

The verb *tuhit* comes from surrounding, encompassing, encircling a thing. To encompass something in knowledge is to have the whole relevant reality within your grasp. Musa will see parts of events, but not their full enclosure. He will see a damaged ship, a killed boy, a repaired wall. He will not yet see the poor owners, the seizing king, the parents' future grief, the hidden treasure, the righteous father, the timing of mercy.

The word *khubr* refers to knowledge by awareness, experience, informed acquaintance with reality. It is not surface sight. It is deep knowledge of what is actually going on. The servant says: you cannot be patient over what you have not encompassed in khubr.

This sentence explains so much of human distress. We suffer not only because events hurt, but because we do not encompass them. We see one frame and assume the whole film. We see loss and do not see protection. We see denial and do not see mercy. We see delay and do not see preparation. We see damage and do not see rescue.

This does not mean every painful event should be explained away casually. The Qur'an does not ask us to pretend evil is good. Musa's moral discomfort is real, and the coming events are meant to shock him. But the passage teaches that our sight is partial, and partial sight should produce humility before Allah's knowledge.

This connects with 2:216, where you may dislike a thing while it is good for you and love a thing while it is bad for you, and Allah knows while you do not know. The Musa-Khidr journey becomes the narrative embodiment of that principle.

[PAUSE]

The eye sees an event. Allah knows the web in which the event is held.

### 6. "If Allah Wills" - Musa's Promise Under Divine Will

Musa answers: *satajiduni in sha'a Allahu sabiran* - you will find me, if Allah wills, patient.

This is a beautiful answer because Musa promises and limits the promise at the same time. He does not say, "I am patient" as though patience were self-owned. He says, "You will find me, if Allah wills, patient."

The future verb *satajiduni* - you will find me - expresses resolve. Musa is sincere. He intends to be patient. But the phrase *in sha'a Allah* places that resolve under Allah's will. Al-Kahf has already taught the discipline of future speech in 18:23-24: do not say you will do something tomorrow except that Allah wills. Now Musa embodies that adab inside the story.

This is not verbal decoration. It is theology entering intention. A believer still resolves, plans, promises, and strives, but never imagines that future obedience is guaranteed by the self. Even patience needs Allah's permission and aid.

The word *sabiran* returns the central test. Musa accepts that patience is the condition. He does not argue against the teacher's diagnosis. He responds with hope, resolve, and dependence.

There is a lesson here for spiritual ambition. We often make promises after being moved: I will never fall again, I will always pray like this, I will be patient from now on, I will never complain, I will never ask why. Sincerity is good, but self-confidence is dangerous. The prophetic adab is to bind intention to Allah's will.

[PAUSE]

The phrase if Allah wills is not a way to weaken resolve. It is how resolve admits where its strength comes from.

### 7. "I Will Not Disobey You in Any Command"

Musa adds: *wa la a'si laka amra* - and I will not disobey you in any command.

The verb *a'si* comes from disobedience, refusal, rebellion against command. The word *amr* is command, matter, instruction. Musa is entering the journey under a teacher's condition. He is not surrendering prophethood or moral responsibility; he is agreeing to the discipline of this specific companionship.

This line is striking because Musa is a messenger with his own command from Allah. Yet he recognizes that in this learning context, obedience to the teacher's instruction is part of the path. The seeker cannot demand to learn while refusing the structure of learning.

But the next ayah will define the command clearly: do not ask about anything until I bring you a reminder from it. The obedience required here is not blind worship of a teacher. It is the agreed restraint necessary for the lesson to unfold.

This distinction is important. The Qur'an is not licensing spiritual abuse or unaccountable authority. It is showing that some forms of learning require disciplined trust within boundaries set by Allah. Musa will object when he sees what appears morally unbearable. The story does not erase moral conscience. It trains it under delayed explanation.

Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir both frame Musa's statement as a promise to comply with the servant's instruction. The servant's condition then sets the terms.

For us, the principle is that knowledge has costs. If we want rushd, we may have to obey disciplines we did not invent: listening before speaking, practicing before judging, waiting before concluding, asking at the right time, and accepting that not every lesson can be understood at the first moment it wounds our expectations.

[PAUSE]

The seeker who refuses discipline may want insight, but he does not yet want to be taught.

### 8. "Do Not Ask Until I Bring You Reminder"

The servant sets the condition: *fa-in ittaba'tani fa-la tas'alni 'an shay'in hatta uhditha laka minhu dhikra* - if you follow me, do not ask me about anything until I bring about for you from it a reminder.

The condition begins with following: if you follow me. Following is now tied to restraint. The prohibition is specific: do not ask me about anything. The word *shay'* makes it broad. Nothing that occurs on the road is to be questioned before its time.

The endpoint is not permanent silence. It is *hatta* - until. The teacher will bring explanation. The word *uhditha* comes from bringing about, initiating, producing something newly. The explanation will be given when the teacher brings it forth. And what will he bring? *Dhikra* - a reminder.

This is beautiful. The journey begins with a request for rushd and a warning about patience, and the condition ends with dhikr. The explanation is not merely information; it is reminder. It will reconnect the event to the meaning Allah placed inside it.

The adab here is about timing. Some questions are valid but premature. A question asked too early can become resistance rather than learning. The same question, asked after the lesson unfolds, can become understanding.

This is one of the hardest disciplines in spiritual life. We want explanations before obedience, before patience, before trust, before the road. Allah sometimes gives explanation after the event, sometimes much later, and sometimes not in this life. The servant's condition trains Musa inside a small version of that larger human condition.

[PAUSE]

Not every unanswered question is rejected. Some are being held until the lesson has enough road beneath it.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### 1. The Adab of Seeking What You Do Not Yet Know

The scene opens with Musa asking permission to follow. This is not incidental politeness. It is the foundation of the journey. Before knowledge enters, ego must lower.

Many of us seek knowledge while trying to remain in control of how we are seen. We want to learn without appearing ignorant, ask without being corrected, receive without being reshaped. We want teachers who confirm our instincts and lessons that flatter our existing map. But Musa's request begins elsewhere: may I follow you?

This is spiritually immense because Musa is not a small figure. The Qur'an has already shown his rank across many surahs. Yet he does not treat rank as a barrier to learning. He does not confuse what Allah gave him with what Allah gave another servant. The humility of the seeker is not humiliation. It is precision. Musa knows why he has come: to learn from what this servant has been taught.

Ibn Kathir and al-Tabari both preserve the dignity and humility of this request. Musa's greatness is not diminished by asking. It is revealed. Only a truly great soul can be lowered by Allah into studenthood without breaking.

The modern tension is credentialed ego. A person with degrees, experience, followers, age, religious practice, or leadership can become almost unteachable. Every correction feels like disrespect. Every unfamiliar insight feels like threat. Every teacher must pass through the filter of the self's rank. But the Qur'an shows Musa asking to follow.

The practical lesson is to re-enter studenthood regularly. Ask questions whose answers may change you. Learn from people who do not need your approval. Let the Qur'an correct your most polished assumptions. Let your spouse, child, student, or critic reveal something true without your ego demanding a status adjustment first.

[PAUSE]

The door to rushd is often lower than the ego expected to bow.

### 2. Patience Is Hardest Where Knowledge Is Partial

The servant's warning is not that Musa lacks courage, sincerity, or faith. The warning is about patience where knowledge is incomplete. How can you be patient over what you have not encompassed in knowledge?

This is the human condition in one sentence.

We live inside partial knowledge. We see moments, not totalities. We see harm, not always protection. We see delay, not always preparation. We see loss, not always what loss prevented. We see a closed door, not always the cliff behind it. We see a painful opening, not always the mercy traveling through it.

That partiality is where sabr becomes hard. If everything were explained before it happened, patience would be easier. If every loss arrived with a visible label naming its wisdom, trust would feel almost automatic. But Allah often asks us to live in the space between event and explanation.

Al-Kahf will now narrate three events that appear troubling on the surface. The educational contract tells us how to read them before they happen. The issue will be knowledge not yet encompassed. Musa's objections will not come from a corrupt heart. They will come from a prophetic moral instinct encountering events whose hidden dimensions have not been disclosed.

This saves us from two errors. First, it saves us from arrogance: we do not encompass reality. Second, it saves us from cruelty: we should not throw hidden wisdom at people's wounds as though grief were ignorance. The Qur'an lets Musa struggle. It lets the journey be difficult. It teaches humility without mocking pain.

The practical response is to say, in hardship: I do not encompass this. That sentence is not resignation to meaninglessness. It is faith in Allah's knowledge. It leaves room for grief, action, du'a, and patience without pretending to be Lord over the whole map.

[PAUSE]

Sabr begins when the heart admits: I see truly, but I do not see totally.

### 3. The Danger of Promising Patience Without Dependence

Musa says: you will find me, if Allah wills, patient.

That phrase should train our speech. Patience is not self-generated heroism. It is a gift Allah must keep renewing. A person can be patient yesterday and overwhelmed today. Patient in public and broken in private. Patient with one trial and unable to bear another. Musa knows to place his promised patience under Allah's will.

Al-Kahf has already taught this adab directly: do not say of anything that you will do it tomorrow except that Allah wills. Now the surah embodies the principle through Musa. The earlier instruction is not abstract etiquette. It becomes necessary in the most spiritually intense learning journey of the surah.

We often make future promises from the emotion of the present. After a lecture, we promise permanent transformation. After a crisis, we promise never to return to heedlessness. After a spiritual high, we promise discipline. The intention may be sincere, but sincerity does not equal capacity. Capacity comes from Allah.

This does not mean we stop resolving. Musa does resolve. "You will find me patient" is a real commitment. But it is protected by "if Allah wills." The believer's future speech carries both effort and dependence.

The tension is that some people use "if Allah wills" as a way to avoid responsibility. That is not Musa's use. He is not being vague. He is making a promise while confessing that its fulfillment is in Allah's hand. True reliance does not weaken action; it purifies it from self-certainty.

Practically, attach every serious intention to du'a. I will be patient, if Allah wills, so Allah help me be patient. I will leave this sin, if Allah wills, so Allah protect me from myself. I will repair this relationship, if Allah wills, so Allah soften my tongue. Future obedience should never be imagined without future neediness.

[PAUSE]

The servant of Allah plans with resolve, then places the plan back in the hand of Allah.

### 4. Some Questions Need the Road Before the Answer

The condition is clear: do not ask me about anything until I bring you from it a reminder.

This does not mean questions are bad. Musa's whole journey begins with a question. The Qur'an is full of questions. The issue is timing and posture. Some questions, asked too early, interrupt the very experience meant to teach their answer.

Imagine trying to understand a medicine before knowing the illness, a legal ruling before knowing the facts, a map before knowing the destination, a teacher's method before entering the lesson. Sometimes explanation must wait until enough of the reality has unfolded. The servant is not forbidding understanding. He is sequencing it.

This is especially hard for intelligent people. The mind wants to close loops quickly. It wants to classify, judge, approve, reject. But the Musa-Khidr journey requires suspended judgment under a trusted divine arrangement. Musa is not being asked to abandon reason. He is being asked to let reason wait for the data Allah has not yet disclosed.

Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir both explain the condition as restraint from questioning until the servant himself explains. The explanation will come, but at the appointed time. This is the adab of the journey.

The modern world trains us against this. We demand instant explanation, instant transparency, instant closure. When Allah's decree unfolds slowly, impatience can turn into accusation. When a teacher, parent, doctor, judge, or elder withholds explanation for a wise time, we may assume disrespect. Certainly, people can abuse opacity, and Islam does not sanctify manipulation. But there remains a real category of wisdom that cannot be understood before its time.

The practical invitation is to distinguish between the question that must be asked now and the question that can be held in trust. In worship, in hardship, in learning, in relationships, some answers ripen only after obedience has walked a while.

[PAUSE]

A premature question can be a refusal to let the lesson finish speaking.

### 5. The Journey from Rushd to Dhikr

Musa asks for rushd. The servant says he will bring dhikr. Guidance and reminder frame the whole contract.

This is significant. Musa is not seeking spectacle, and the servant is not promising entertainment. The journey will contain extraordinary events, but the Qur'an frames them as guidance and reminder. Their purpose is to bring the heart back to a truer relationship with Allah's knowledge.

Rushd is sound direction. Dhikr is awakened remembrance. Together they show what beneficial knowledge does: it guides the path and wakes the heart. It does not merely satisfy curiosity. It does not merely reveal hidden mechanisms. It changes how a person stands inside reality.

This protects us from misreading the entire story. People can become fascinated with Khidr as a figure of secret knowledge while missing the Qur'an's training in humility, patience, and submission to Allah's wisdom. The story is not an invitation to chase the unseen. It is an invitation to know the limits of what we see.

Al-Muyassar's reading of Musa's request as guidance, and of the servant's condition as delaying explanation until he initiates reminder, keeps the pedagogical structure intact. The hidden meanings will be disclosed not to make Musa feel entertained, but to remind him.

In our own learning, we should ask: does this knowledge produce rushd and dhikr? Does it make me more rightly guided and more remembering of Allah? Or does it only make me more unusual, more argumentative, more fascinated with what others do not know?

The Qur'an's deepest knowledge brings us back to servanthood. It does not inflate us above ordinary obedience. It makes us more careful before Allah, more patient with what we do not encompass, and more humble in the presence of what He teaches whom He wills.

The secret of the journey is not secret knowledge. It is remembered servanthood before the knowledge of Allah.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Discipline Before the Journey

This passage is built like a covenant of learning.

First comes the seeker's humility: Musa asks permission to follow and be taught from what the servant was taught of rushd. This establishes the aim, the posture, and the source of knowledge.

Second comes the teacher's diagnosis: you will not be able to have patience with me, because patience is difficult over what one has not encompassed in knowledge. This names the central test before any event occurs.

Third comes the seeker's promise: you will find me, if Allah wills, patient, and I will not disobey you in any command. Musa combines resolve with dependence and willingness to accept discipline.

Fourth comes the teacher's condition: if you follow me, do not ask about anything until I bring you from it a reminder. The journey will have explanation, but explanation will come at its appointed time.

The architecture moves from following to teaching, from rushd to sabr, from partial knowledge to promised patience, from command to restrained questioning, and finally to dhikr. Before the events test Musa, the Qur'an teaches us how to stand before every event whose wisdom we have not yet encompassed.

### Questions to Carry

1. Where am I too proud to say, "May I follow and learn?"

Look for the places where rank, age, experience, or previous knowledge makes you resistant to becoming a student again. Musa's greatness did not prevent his humility.

2. What knowledge am I seeking: information, amazement, or rushd?

Beneficial knowledge should make the path sounder. If learning makes you more argumentative but less guided, something in the intention needs repair.

3. What current trial am I judging as though I encompass it fully?

You may see something real without seeing everything. Let partial knowledge make you careful before turning pain into accusation against Allah.

4. Do my future promises include dependence on Allah?

Resolve is good, but self-certainty is fragile. Place your intended patience, repentance, worship, and reform under Allah's will and help.

5. Which question may need patience before it receives its answer?

Some questions should be asked immediately. Others become clear only after the road unfolds. Ask Allah for the wisdom to know the difference.

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Al-Kahf 18:66-70 teaches that the path to deeper guidance begins with humble following, seeks rushd rather than spectacle, requires patience before what we have not encompassed in knowledge, and accepts that some explanations come only after the appointed reminder.**

### The Closing Invitation

Enter the next unclear part of your life with the adab Musa shows here. Ask for guidance, admit what you do not encompass, promise patience only with Allah's help, and resist the urge to force every answer before its time. Let the journey teach you before you judge the journey.

### Du'a

Make us humble enough to follow where You place guidance, patient enough to endure what we have not encompassed, and honest enough to say if You will before every promise. Do not let our questions become rebellion, and do not let our ignorance become accusation. Open for us the reminder at the time You know is best. Ameen.
