# Their Words Do Not Own Honor, and Their Conjecture Does Not Define Reality

## Surah Yunus (10:65-67)

[ayah:10:65] وَلَا يَحْزُنكَ قَوْلُهُمْ ۘ إِنَّ ٱلْعِزَّةَ لِلَّهِ جَمِيعًا ۚ هُوَ ٱلسَّمِيعُ ٱلْعَلِيمُ

[ayah:10:66] أَلَآ إِنَّ لِلَّهِ مَن فِى ٱلسَّمَـٰوَٰتِ وَمَن فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ ۗ وَمَا يَتَّبِعُ ٱلَّذِينَ يَدْعُونَ مِن دُونِ ٱللَّهِ شُرَكَآءَ ۚ إِن يَتَّبِعُونَ إِلَّا ٱلظَّنَّ وَإِنْ هُمْ إِلَّا يَخْرُصُونَ

## Introduction

One of the hardest things to carry is not only opposition itself, but the emotional fog opposition creates.

Sometimes the words of other people do not merely disagree with us. They press on the heart. They insinuate weakness. They try to make falsehood feel heavy and truth feel lonely. A person can begin to think that repeated voices somehow create reality, or that mockery itself is a kind of power.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs rescue the heart from that illusion. The Prophet is told not to let their speech grieve him because all honor belongs to Allah entirely. The surah then exposes shirk as empty following of conjecture, not knowledge. And then, as if to restore the soul's sense of solid ground, Allah points us back to the night and the day: rest, sight, rhythm, order, signs.

The hook of the passage is this: **Yunus teaches that hostile speech has no authority over truth because all honor belongs to Allah, false worship rests on conjecture rather than knowledge, and the created world itself keeps testifying to divine order for hearts willing to truly listen.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Wa la yahzunka qawluhum**: Speech Can Wound, But It Does Not Rule

The ayah begins with tenderness:

**wa la yahzunka qawluhum**.

Do not let their words grieve you.

[PAUSE]

The grief named here matters. The Qur'an does not pretend that denial is emotionally weightless. Words affect the heart. False accusation, ridicule, dismissal, and pressure can all create sorrow. But the ayah interrupts that sorrow before it hardens into a false reading of reality.

Their **qawl** is still only their speech. It is not final judgment. It is not moral sovereignty. It is not the measure of what is true.

### 2. **Inna al-'izzata lillahi jami'an**: Honor Is Not Distributed Away From Him

Then comes the great correction:

**inna al-'izzata lillahi jami'an**.

Surely all honor belongs to Allah entirely.

[PAUSE]

The word **'izzah** carries honor, might, inviolable dignity, unassailable strength. And the ayah does not give Allah some of it. It gives Him all of it, **jami'an**. Entirely. Collectively. Without remainder.

That changes how we read the pressure of other people. If honor belongs to Allah, then the loudness of falsehood cannot grant it nobility, and the loneliness of truth cannot strip it of dignity. Much of our pain comes from unconsciously treating public approval as though it were the source of 'izzah. The ayah cuts that root.

### 3. **In yattabi'una illa al-zann**: False Worship Imitates Following, But It Has No Knowledge Beneath It

Ayah 66 then widens the frame:

**wa ma yattabi'u alladhina yad'una min duni Allahi shuraka'**.

Those who call upon partners besides Allah are not actually following anything solid.

[PAUSE]

Then the surah names what is really underneath:

**in yattabi'una illa al-zann**.

They follow nothing but conjecture.

This is devastating. The outer form may look like religion, conviction, inheritance, or devotion. But beneath it is **zann** when cut off from revelation: suspicion, assumption, ungrounded mental construction. Something can be socially inherited and still epistemically empty.

### 4. **Wa in hum illa yakhrusun**: Conjecture Eventually Turns Into Fabricated Precision

Then:

**wa in hum illa yakhrusun**.

They do nothing but guess.

[PAUSE]

The movement is subtle and important. Conjecture inside the mind often becomes confident speech outside the mind. People guess, then present the guess as certainty. This is one of the recurring moral diseases in Yunus: speaking about Allah, judgment, partners, and truth without actual knowledge.

### 5. **Ja'ala lakumu al-layla li-taskunu fihi wa al-nahara mubsiran**: The Cosmos Re-Steadies the Heart

Then the surah turns to the created world:

**huwa alladhi ja'ala lakumu al-layla li-taskunu fihi wa al-nahara mubsiran**.

He made the night for you so you may find rest in it, and the day as giving sight.

[PAUSE]

This is not an abrupt topic change. It is therapy for the destabilized heart. After words, noise, conjecture, and fabricated claims, Allah points us back to the deep grammar of reality: night that receives the exhausted self, day that opens the field of sight, ordered mercy, lived dependence.

The word **li-taskunu** is especially beautiful. The night is not merely dark. It is made as a place of **sukun** - stilling, settling, repose. The ayah is giving us a world in which tawhid is not only argued. It is inhabited.

### 6. **Li-qawmin yasma'un**: Hearing Means Receiving the Sign, Not Merely Being Near It

The ending matters:

**inna fi dhalika la-ayatin li-qawmin yasma'un**.

Surely in that are signs for a people who listen.

[PAUSE]

The surah could have ended with seeing, since the day is named **mubsiran**. But it ends with listening. As though the real failure of shirk is not shortage of visible signs, but refusal of inward reception.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: We Often Give Other People's Voices More Weight Than Allah Has Given Them

There is a subtle form of weakness that enters the heart when repeated human voices start functioning like revelation. Not because we consciously worship people, but because their approval, mockery, or narratives begin to define what feels honorable or shameful.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs break that spell. If all honor belongs to Allah, then we do not need falsehood to applaud us in order to stand upright. And we do not need truth to become socially dominant before we trust it. A believer is repeatedly being taught to relocate emotional gravity back to Allah.

### Theme 2: The Signs of Allah Are Often Missed Not Because They Are Rare, But Because They Are Regular

Night and day happen so continuously that we stop reading them. Rest and sight become ordinary. Rhythms become background. But the Qur'an keeps returning to these cycles because repetition should deepen recognition, not erase it.

[PAUSE]

The human being often chases dramatic proof while living inside daily mercy. We say we want signs, yet we sleep every night in a world Allah softened for us and wake into a day He opened for sight. The problem is often not lack of evidence. It is dulled listening.

## Closing Synthesis

The passage begins by protecting the Prophet's heart from sorrow under hostile speech, then relocates all honor to Allah alone. It strips shirk down to conjecture and fabricated guessing, and finally reintroduces the believer to the steady signs of night and day as a lived grammar of divine mercy and order.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with these ayahs is to ask: whose words have I allowed to define my sense of worth more than Allah's? Where am I mistaking social volume for truth? And how often do I ask for signs while rushing past the nightly and daily mercies through which Allah is already teaching me who He is?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the passage lands here: **do not let human speech overrule your heart, because honor remains Allah's alone, falsehood remains conjectural no matter how loudly it speaks, and the world around you is still full of signs for any soul willing to listen.**
