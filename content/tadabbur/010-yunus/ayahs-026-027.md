# Allah Measures Evil by Its Like and Good With More

## Surah Yunus (10:26-27)

[ayah:10:26] ۞ لِّلَّذِينَ أَحْسَنُوا۟ ٱلْحُسْنَىٰ وَزِيَادَةٌ ۖ وَلَا يَرْهَقُ وُجُوهَهُمْ قَتَرٌ وَلَا ذِلَّةٌ ۚ أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ أَصْحَـٰبُ ٱلْجَنَّةِ ۖ هُمْ فِيهَا خَـٰلِدُونَ

[ayah:10:27] وَٱلَّذِينَ كَسَبُوا۟ ٱلسَّيِّـَٔاتِ جَزَآءُ سَيِّئَةٍۭ بِمِثْلِهَا وَتَرْهَقُهُمْ ذِلَّةٌ ۖ مَّا لَهُم مِّنَ ٱللَّهِ مِنْ عَاصِمٍ ۖ كَأَنَّمَآ أُغْشِيَتْ وُجُوهُهُمْ قِطَعًا مِّنَ ٱلَّيْلِ مُظْلِمًا ۚ أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ أَصْحَـٰبُ ٱلنَّارِ ۖ هُمْ فِيهَا خَـٰلِدُونَ

*Lil-ladhina ahsanu al-husna wa ziyadah. Wa la yarhaqu wujuhahum qatarun wa la dhillah. Ula'ika ashabu al-jannah. Hum fiha khalidun.*

*Wa alladhina kasabu al-sayyi'at jaza'u sayyi'atin bi-mithliha wa tarhaquhum dhillah. Ma lahum min Allahi min 'asim. Ka-annama ughshiyat wujuhuhum qita'an mina al-layli muzlima. Ula'ika ashabu al-nar. Hum fiha khalidun.*

**For those who do ihsan is the best and an increase. No dark covering will overtake their faces, nor humiliation. Those are the companions of the Garden; they will abide therein forever. And those who earned evil deeds: the recompense of an evil deed is only its like, and humiliation will overtake them. They will have from Allah no protector. It is as though their faces were covered with pieces of the dark night. Those are the companions of the Fire; they will abide therein forever.**

## Introduction

These ayahs arrive after dunya has just been compared to a beautifully adorned field cut down overnight. Once that illusion is shattered, the surah does not leave us in abstraction. It shows where lives actually go.

But notice how the passage does not begin with geography. It does not begin with gardens and fire first. It begins with what settles upon the human face.

[PAUSE]

Some faces are not overtaken by gloom or humiliation. Other faces seem covered with torn darkness from the night itself. Before the ayah names the final homes, it names the visible condition of the soul when truth is no longer hideable.

That matters because we spend much of life trying to manage appearance. We work on presentation, polish, control, reputation, tone, image. We try to decide what reaches other people and what remains hidden. But these ayahs speak from the Day when the face is no longer a mask. It becomes disclosure.

The hook of the passage is this: **Yunus teaches that Allah's justice is exact with evil and overflowing with good, and that the face eventually carries the spiritual atmosphere of what the person pursued all along.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Lil-ladhina ahsanu al-husna wa ziyadah**: The People of Ihsan Receive More Than a Matched Return

The first half of the contrast opens with:

**lil-ladhina ahsanu al-husna wa ziyadah**.

For those who do ihsan is the best and an increase.

[PAUSE]

The ayah begins with the verb **ahsanu** from the root of beauty and excellence, then gives them **al-husna**, the best, the beautiful good, and then adds something more: **wa ziyadah**. And an increase.

That extra phrase matters. When the evil side of the contrast is described, it will be measured tightly, one deed by its like. But the side of ihsan is not described with mere equivalence. It opens into surplus. The reward is not boxed into bare arithmetic.

One way to sit with this is that Allah is teaching us His generosity before teaching us His punishment. The people of beauty toward Him are not met with cold transaction. They are met with goodness, then something beyond what the sentence strictly needed to say.

### 2. **Wa la yarhaqu wujuhahum qatarun wa la dhillah**: Nothing Dark Will Overrun Their Faces

Then the ayah says:

**wa la yarhaqu wujuhahum qatarun wa la dhillah**.

No dark covering will overtake their faces, nor humiliation.

[PAUSE]

The verb **yarhaqu** carries the sense of something pressing onto, covering over, descending with burden. The image is not simply that they feel fine inwardly. It is that nothing oppressive overruns the face.

Then come the two things denied: **qatar** and **dhillah**. A dark grim covering, and humiliation. Outer atmosphere and inner abasement. The ayah removes both. No blackening mood. No disgrace. No collapse of dignity before Allah.

The face is chosen because the face is where we read people most instinctively. Even in dunya we say: it was all over his face. Relief shows there. Shame shows there. Fear shows there. The ayah speaks in language we already understand and then brings it to the scale of akhirah.

### 3. **Wa alladhina kasabu al-sayyi'at**: Evil Is Described as Something Earned

The opposite side begins:

**wa alladhina kasabu al-sayyi'at**.

Those who earned evil deeds.

[PAUSE]

The word **kasabu** is important. The ayah does not speak of evil as something that merely happened around them. It was earned, acquired, brought into one's account. This is moral possession.

That makes the contrast sharper. The people of ihsan are named through a mode of acting beautifully. The people of evil are named through accumulation. One side is moving toward Allah through excellence. The other is building a ledger against itself.

### 4. **Jaza'u sayyi'atin bi-mithliha**: Evil Is Paid Back Exactly, Not Inflated

Then comes one of the most precise formulations in the passage:

**jaza'u sayyi'atin bi-mithliha**.

The recompense of an evil deed is by its like.

[PAUSE]

This is severe, but it is also just. Evil is not rewarded with multiplied injustice. It is answered with its like. The punishment is not arbitrary excess. It is measure.

That makes the earlier phrase even more luminous. Good was met with **al-husna wa ziyadah**. Evil is met with **bi-mithliha**. The asymmetry is the theology. Allah is not more eager to punish than to reward. His justice is exact. His generosity exceeds.

### 5. **Wa tarhaquhum dhillah**: The Same Verb Returns, but Now the Burden Lands

Then the same root appears again:

**wa tarhaquhum dhillah**.

Humiliation will overtake them.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the most powerful turns in the two ayahs. The same verb used in the first verse as a denied burden now returns as an imposed reality. What never reaches one group settles fully upon the other.

That repetition creates a moral mirror. The difference is not whether there is unveiling. The difference is what arrives when unveiling comes.

### 6. **Ma lahum min Allahi min 'asim** and **ughshiyat wujuhuhum**: No Shield, Only Exposure

Then:

**ma lahum min Allahi min 'asim**.

They have from Allah no protector.

And then:

**ka-annama ughshiyat wujuhuhum qita'an mina al-layli muzlima**.

It is as though their faces were covered with pieces of dark night.

[PAUSE]

First the ayah removes defense, then it deepens the image of exposure. No one can shield them from the consequence, and the face itself appears wrapped in broken sections of darkness. Not one smooth blanket, but pieces, fragments, torn portions of night.

It is a devastating image because night usually hides things. Here night itself becomes the thing displayed on the face. What once concealed now testifies.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Allah Is Not Symmetrical in Generosity and Punishment

Many of us carry, somewhere deep down, a distorted idea of Allah. We imagine Him as difficult to please, eager to catch failure, and restrained in reward. We may not say that out loud, but we often live as though it were true.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs dismantle that imagination with grammar before they do so with doctrine. Good is not answered with bare sameness. It is met with **al-husna wa ziyadah**. Evil is not met with wild inflation. It is met **bi-mithliha**. By its like.

That means the divine economy is not balanced in the flat way our fear imagines. Allah's punishment is just, but His generosity spills beyond strict equivalence. This should not make us casual about sin. It should make us ashamed of how suspicious we can become of our Lord's mercy.

There is a practical spiritual effect here too. If a person thinks Allah only deals in bare transaction, they will either become performative or despairing. But if they know He rewards beauty with more than it strictly earned, then ihsan becomes conceivable. One can strive not only from fear of loss, but from confidence in divine largeness.

### Theme 2: The Face Eventually Tells the Truth

We live in an age of controlled surfaces. People learn how to post, pose, phrase, and curate. Whole emotional worlds are hidden behind disciplined presentation. A person can appear composed while disintegrating, generous while corrupt, calm while burning, righteous while hungry for applause.

[PAUSE]

The Qur'an does not deny that surfaces can mislead in dunya. But it keeps preparing us for a Day when the surface is no longer detachable from reality. The face becomes a readout.

That is why these ayahs are frightening and merciful at once. They tell us that humiliation is not finally an issue of public embarrassment here. It is a condition before Allah. And honor is not finally crowd approval here. It is the absence of dark burden on the face before Him.

So the question quietly shifts. We spend energy asking: how do I look to people? The ayah teaches us to ask: what is gathering on my face before Allah, even if nobody sees it yet? What moral atmosphere am I carrying toward that meeting?

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayahs place two groups side by side. Those who do ihsan receive the best and an increase; neither dark grimness nor humiliation overruns their faces, and they are the companions of the Garden. Those who earn evil deeds are repaid by the like of what they earned; humiliation overtakes them, no protector stands between them and Allah, and their faces appear covered in torn darkness from the night before they are named companions of the Fire.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask: do I secretly expect Allah to be narrow in reward and severe beyond measure in punishment, even though these ayahs teach the opposite? What kind of face am I preparing through the life I am living now? And where have I been more committed to managing appearance before people than to seeking the kind of inward truth that will one day appear openly before Allah?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the passage lands here: **Allah's justice answers evil with exactness, but His generosity meets ihsan with more than equivalence, and in the end the face reveals whether the soul was moving toward honor or toward humiliation all along.**
