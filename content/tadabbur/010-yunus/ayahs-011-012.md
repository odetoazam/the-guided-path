# Humans Want Speed for Themselves and Forget Allah's Mercy in Delay

## Surah Yunus (10:11-12)

[ayah:10:11] ۞ وَلَوْ يُعَجِّلُ ٱللَّهُ لِلنَّاسِ ٱلشَّرَّ ٱسْتِعْجَالَهُم بِٱلْخَيْرِ لَقُضِىَ إِلَيْهِمْ أَجَلُهُمْ ۖ فَنَذَرُ ٱلَّذِينَ لَا يَرْجُونَ لِقَآءَنَا فِى طُغْيَـٰنِهِمْ يَعْمَهُونَ

[ayah:10:12] وَإِذَا مَسَّ ٱلْإِنسَـٰنَ ٱلضُّرُّ دَعَانَا لِجَنۢبِهِۦٓ أَوْ قَاعِدًا أَوْ قَآئِمًا فَلَمَّا كَشَفْنَا عَنْهُ ضُرَّهُۥ مَرَّ كَأَن لَّمْ يَدْعُنَآ إِلَىٰ ضُرٍّ مَّسَّهُۥ ۚ كَذَٰلِكَ زُيِّنَ لِلْمُسْرِفِينَ مَا كَانُوا۟ يَعْمَلُونَ

*Wa law yu'ajjilu Allahu li-al-nasi al-sharra isti'jalahum bi-al-khayri la-qudiya ilayhim ajaluhum. Fa-nadharu alladhina la yarjuna liqa'ana fi tughyanihim ya'mahun.*

*Wa idha massa al-insana al-durru da'ana li-janbihi aw qa'idan aw qa'iman. Fa-lamma kashafna 'anhu durrahu marra ka-an lam yad'una ila durrin massahu. Ka-dhalika zuyyina li-al-musrifina ma kanu ya'malun.*

**If Allah were to hasten for people the evil as they hasten the good, their term would have been finished for them. So We leave those who do not hope for meeting Us wandering blindly in their transgression. And when harm touches the human being, he calls upon Us on his side, sitting, or standing. But when We remove his harm from him, he goes on as if he had never called upon Us concerning a harm that touched him. Thus is made fair-seeming to the excessive what they used to do.**

## Introduction

One of Allah's mercies is how slowly He gives us what we would deserve if our worst impulses were answered immediately.

That is the startling opening of these verses. Human beings often demand speed when they want relief, vindication, pleasure, or reversal. But if Allah applied that same speed to the destructive things we deserve, or to the reckless things we invoke against ourselves, our story would collapse very quickly. Delay is not always neglect. Very often it is mercy.

[PAUSE]

Then the passage turns to another familiar pattern: in distress, the human being remembers Allah in every posture. On his side, sitting, standing. Need makes him total. But once relief comes, he moves on as though the du'a never happened. The crisis makes him sincere for a moment. Ease tests whether he can remain sincere after being saved.

The hook of the passage is this: **Yunus teaches that Allah's slowness with us is often a mercy we mistake for absence, and that one of the deepest signs of spiritual excess is to call on Him passionately in harm but return to forgetfulness as soon as relief arrives.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Yu'ajjilu ... isti'jalahum**: The Root of Haste Is Repeated Back to Us

Verse 11 says:

**wa law yu'ajjilu Allahu li-al-nasi al-sharra isti'jalahum bi-al-khayr**.

[PAUSE]

The same root of haste is repeated. Allah is showing us our own appetite for quickness by echoing it linguistically. Human beings want good fast. Relief fast. Vindication fast. Openings fast. The verse then asks us to imagine what would happen if divine dealing mirrored human impatience without mercy.

### 2. **La-qudiya ilayhim ajaluhum**: Immediate Justice Would End the Story Too Quickly

Then:

**la-qudiya ilayhim ajaluhum**.

Their term would have been finished for them.

[PAUSE]

This is terrifying and tender at once. Terrifying, because we begin to sense how much destruction we are protected from. Tender, because the verse reveals that many delays in our lives are not signs that Allah is inattentive. They are signs that He is giving us time we would not give ourselves.

### 3. **Fi tughyanihim ya'mahun**: Wandering Blindly Inside Transgression

Then:

**fi tughyanihim ya'mahun**.

They wander blindly in their transgression.

[PAUSE]

This is not ordinary movement. It is blind movement inside overstepping. A person can be very active and still be spiritually disoriented. In fact, some people speed up precisely because they do not want to stop long enough to notice where they are.

### 4. **Li-janbihi aw qa'idan aw qa'iman**: Distress Produces Total Invocation

Verse 12 then portrays the human being in difficulty:

**li-janbihi aw qa'idan aw qa'iman**.

On his side, sitting, or standing.

[PAUSE]

The verse practically runs through the body's available positions. Harm has reached him so thoroughly that every posture has become du'a posture. In distress, the illusion of independence collapses quickly.

### 5. **Marra ka-an lam yad'una**: Relief Can Produce Spiritual Amnesia

Then:

**marra ka-an lam yad'una**.

He goes on as if he had never called upon Us.

[PAUSE]

That "as if" is devastating. The crisis was real. The tears may have been real. The helplessness was real. But ease returns and the self reassembles its old myth: I am fine on my own again.

The verse is not mocking du'a in distress. It is exposing forgetfulness after relief.

### 6. **Zuyyina li-al-musrifina**: Excess Makes Distortion Look Beautiful

Finally:

**ka-dhalika zuyyina li-al-musrifina ma kanu ya'malun**.

Thus is made fair-seeming to the excessive what they used to do.

[PAUSE]

That explains the whole pattern. Spiritual excess is not only doing too much in quantity. It is overstepping the truth about the self. It is living beyond your creaturely limits until forgetfulness starts looking normal and return to heedlessness starts feeling justified.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Divine Delay Is Often Protective Mercy

We spend a lot of our lives interpreting delayed answers as absence or rejection. This verse forces a more trembling possibility: perhaps the delay itself is a shelter.

[PAUSE]

If Allah answered every reckless impulse, every self-destructive wish, every curse spoken in frustration, every consequence we immediately earned, we would not survive long. Time itself is one of His mercies.

### Theme 2: The Real Test Is Not Whether You Remember Allah in Crisis, but Whether You Stay Remembering After Relief

Many people become suddenly lucid under pain. Illness, heartbreak, fear, loss, public collapse, anxiety, all of it can produce very real du'a. But the verse shows that the deeper exam comes after the door opens and the pressure lifts.

[PAUSE]

Can gratitude outlive desperation? Can remembrance survive healing? That is often where sincerity is either stabilized or exposed.

## Closing Synthesis

These verses teach that if Allah hastened destructive consequence for people with the same speed they seek good for themselves, their term would already be over. Then they portray the human being calling on Allah intensely in every posture when harmed, only to move on as if that calling had never happened once relief arrives. This pattern is tied to spiritual excess and blind wandering in transgression.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask: where in my life have I mistaken Allah's delay for absence when it may actually have been protection? And after previous hardships lifted, what happened to the intensity of my du'a, gratitude, and dependence? Did relief deepen remembrance, or did it quietly restore my illusion of independence?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the passage lands here: **Allah's delay is often a mercy we are too impatient to recognize, and the heart proves its truth not merely by crying out in pain, but by staying turned toward Him after the pain has passed.**
