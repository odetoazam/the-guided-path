# Faith Can Begin Small, Shaking, and Afraid - Then Be Taught Tawakkul

## Surah Yunus (10:83-84)

[ayah:10:83] فَمَآ ءَامَنَ لِمُوسَىٰٓ إِلَّا ذُرِّيَّةٌ مِّن قَوْمِهِۦ عَلَىٰ خَوْفٍ مِّن فِرْعَوْنَ وَمَلَإِي۟هِمْ أَن يَفْتِنَهُمْ ۚ وَإِنَّ فِرْعَوْنَ لَعَالٍ فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ وَإِنَّهُۥ لَمِنَ ٱلْمُسْرِفِينَ

[ayah:10:84] وَقَالَ مُوسَىٰ يَـٰقَوْمِ إِن كُنتُمْ ءَامَنتُم بِٱللَّهِ فَعَلَيْهِ تَوَكَّلُوٓا۟ إِن كُنتُم مُّسْلِمِينَ

*Fa-ma amana li-Musa illa dhurriyyatun min qawmihi 'ala khawfin min Fir'awna wa mala'ihim an yaftinahum. Wa inna Fir'awna la-'alin fi al-ard wa innahu la-mina al-musrifin.*

*Wa qala Musa ya qawmi in kuntum amantum bi-Allahi fa-'alayhi tawakkalu in kuntum muslimin.*

**But none believed in Musa except a young offspring group from his people, upon fear of Fir'awn and their chiefs lest he put them through trial. Indeed Fir'awn was surely exalted in the land, and indeed he was surely among the excessive ones. And Musa said: O my people, if you have believed in Allah, then upon Him place your trust, if you are truly submitting.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

Sometimes we imagine that once truth has been publicly vindicated, faith should spread easily.

But the Qur'an is more realistic than our triumphal fantasies. Truth may be clear, falsehood may be exposed, and still the social cost of believing can remain terrifying. People do not live only inside arguments. They live inside regimes, families, punishments, vulnerabilities, and fear.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs honor that reality without surrendering to it. The believing group around Musa is small and afraid. Fir'awn's elevation in the land is real. His excess is real. So Musa does not shame the believers for their vulnerability. He teaches them the next necessary station: if you have believed in Allah, then rely upon Him.

The hook of the passage is this: **Yunus teaches that early faith may begin under fear and fragility, but true iman must be educated into tawakkul, because submission to Allah is not complete while trust still remains chained to the tyrant's power.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Fa-ma amana li-Musa illa dhurriyyatun**: Faith Begins as a Small, Vulnerable Cluster

The ayah says:

**fa-ma amana li-Musa illa dhurriyyatun min qawmihi**.

None believed in Musa except a small offspring group from his people.

[PAUSE]

The word **dhurriyyah** gives the scene a tenderness and vulnerability. It suggests a smaller, younger, less socially armored group. The Qur'an is teaching us not to despise small beginnings. Sometimes the earliest carriers of truth are not the established and protected, but the exposed and impressionable.

### 2. **'Ala khawfin**: Faith and Fear Can Coexist at the Beginning

Then:

**'ala khawfin min Fir'awna wa mala'ihim an yaftinahum**.

Upon fear of Fir'awn and their chiefs lest they put them through trial.

[PAUSE]

This is psychologically merciful. The ayah does not wait for fearlessness before recognizing faith. They believe **'ala khawf** - under fear, with fear pressing on them. Their fear is not denied. It is named.

That matters because some people assume that if fear still exists, faith must be defective in its entirety. But the Qur'an often deals more gently with beginnings. Fear may remain while the heart is still turning.

### 3. **An yaftinahum**: Power Threatens Not Only Bodies but Faith

The concern is:

**an yaftinahum**.

Lest he put them through fitnah.

[PAUSE]

This is deeper than generic harm. **Fitnah** is testing, burning, coercive trial, pressure that exposes what is inside. Tyrannical power does not only want outward compliance. It wants to destabilize loyalty itself.

### 4. **La-'alin fi al-ard**: Fir'awn Is Not Merely Strong, But Exalted Against Right

Then the ayah diagnoses Fir'awn:

**wa inna Fir'awna la-'alin fi al-ard**.

Fir'awn was surely exalted in the land.

[PAUSE]

This is not praise. It is a description of dominating elevation, self-exalting rule, a height that becomes oppressive. The believers' fear is not irrational. They live under someone socially towering over them.

### 5. **Wa innahu la-mina al-musrifin**: Tyranny Is Excess That Has Lost All Bounds

Then:

**wa innahu la-mina al-musrifin**.

He was surely among the excessive ones.

[PAUSE]

**Israf** is overstepping measure, going beyond bounds, spending and acting without restraint. Fir'awn's political evil is therefore tied to a deeper spiritual condition: excess without limit. Tyranny is not only domination. It is diseased transgression at scale.

### 6. **Fa-'alayhi tawakkalu**: Belief Must Ripen Into Reliance

Then Musa says:

**in kuntum amantum bi-Allahi fa-'alayhi tawakkalu**.

If you have believed in Allah, then upon Him rely.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the Qur'an's great تربيه movements. Musa does not say: if you believe, you will feel no fear. He says: if you believe, direct your trust properly. The next step after frightened faith is trained tawakkul.

### 7. **In kuntum muslimin**: Submission Is Tested at the Level of Trust

He closes:

**in kuntum muslimin**.

If you are truly submitting.

[PAUSE]

This links Islam and tawakkul intimately. Submission is not only verbal assent or outward belonging. It is the practical surrender of one's safety calculus to Allah.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Qur'an Respects Fragile Beginnings Without Letting Them Stay Fragile Forever

This passage is so beautiful because it neither mocks fear nor canonizes it. The believing group is small and under threat. The Qur'an names that honestly. But Musa does not leave them there. He takes their real, trembling iman and teaches it the next discipline.

[PAUSE]

This is how spiritual growth often works. Allah does not always ask us to begin with perfected states. But He does ask that what is real at the beginning be educated, strengthened, and redirected.

### Theme 2: Tyrants Want to Own Not Only the Public Square but the Believer's Imagination of Safety

Fir'awn's real power is not only external punishment. It is the way his regime colonizes the imagination. People start believing that survival, provision, dignity, and future all remain locked inside his approval or anger.

[PAUSE]

Musa breaks that spell with one sentence: if you have believed in Allah, rely upon Him. Tawakkul becomes liberation from the tyrant's monopoly over what feels safe.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs show that only a small, vulnerable group believed in Musa, and they did so under real fear of Fir'awn and his elite lest they be driven into fitnah. Fir'awn's elevation and excess are named plainly, and then Musa moves his people from frightened belief toward rightly directed trust: if you have believed in Allah, then rely upon Him, if you are truly submitting.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask: where is my own faith still real but frightened? What regime, person, institution, or imagined consequence still seems to own my safety more than Allah does? And if I say I believe and submit, how much of my practical trust has actually been transferred to Him?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the passage lands here: **Allah can begin a community of faith in small and fearful hearts, but He does not leave them there; He teaches them tawakkul so that submission becomes stronger than the fear of tyrants.**
