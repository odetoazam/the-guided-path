# Allah Is Calling to the Home of Peace

## Surah Yunus (10:25)

[ayah:10:25] وَٱللَّهُ يَدْعُوٓا۟ إِلَىٰ دَارِ ٱلسَّلَـٰمِ وَيَهْدِى مَن يَشَآءُ إِلَىٰ صِرَٰطٍ مُّسْتَقِيمٍ

*Wa Allahu yad'u ila dari al-salam wa yahdi man yasha'u ila siratin mustaqim.*

**And Allah invites to the Home of Peace and guides whom He wills to a straight path.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

Sometimes the most tender thing in an ayah is where it appears.

This verse comes right after Yunus has shown us worldly life as a field that becomes most decorated just before it is cut down. The earth takes its adornment, its people think they have control over it, then Allah's command comes by night or day and it is left like a mown field, as though it had not flourished yesterday.

[PAUSE]

At that point, the surah could have continued only with warning. It could have left us staring at the collapse of dunya and the fragility of everything we build. Instead, Allah opens a door. `Wa Allahu yad'u.` And Allah is calling.

This is not a small transition. It means divine warning is not given to trap us in dread. It is given so that, while the illusion is breaking, we still hear the invitation.

That is the hook of the ayah: **after exposing the instability of worldly life, Allah does not merely say "be careful." He personally calls toward a home that will not collapse, a peace that will not be interrupted, and a path that actually leads there.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. `Wa Allahu yad'u`: The Caller Himself Is Allah

The verse begins with striking simplicity:

`wa Allahu yad'u`

And Allah calls.

[PAUSE]

The subject is named first. Allah Himself. The ayah does not say merely that there is a call, or that a messenger has a message, or that guidance exists in the abstract. It places the whole emotional weight on the identity of the Caller.

Then the verb comes in the present tense: `yad'u`. He calls. Not only called. Not only will call. There is an ongoingness here. An open invitation still being extended.

This matters spiritually because many of us imagine our relationship to Allah through duty before we imagine it through invitation. We know we are commanded. We know we are accountable. We know we are warned. All of that is true. But this ayah reminds us that before the servant even speaks of his own movement, Allah has already described His own: He is calling.

There is mercy in that order. The first movement toward salvation did not begin with us. It began with Him.

### 2. `Ila dar al-salam`: Not a Moment of Peace, but a Home of It

Then Allah says:

`ila dar al-salam`

To the Home of Peace.

[PAUSE]

The ayah does not say merely `ila al-salam`, to peace. It says `dar al-salam`, the abode, the dwelling, the settled home of peace.

`Dar` gives a sense of lasting habitation, of arrival, of somewhere you are not merely visiting. This is already a correction to how we often seek peace in dunya. We look for episodes of peace, moods of peace, weekends of peace, a little silence after noise, a little relief after pressure. But dunya, by its nature, does not remain a `dar al-salam`. Even its sweetest moments are temporary. Even its calm carries the possibility of interruption.

So the ayah lifts the horizon. Allah is not calling us merely to more manageable circumstances inside the same unstable world. He is calling to a home where peace is native, not borrowed.

[PAUSE]

Classical tafsir identifies `dar al-salam` as Jannah, the abode safe from every rupture, fear, death, sickness, humiliation, and separation. Some also note the resonance of `al-Salam` as one of Allah's Names, so the phrase carries not only safety as a condition, but peace as something related to the One from whom all true peace proceeds.

Whether we emphasize the safety of the abode or its relation to the Lord of peace, the effect is the same: Allah is redirecting our hunger. We were made to want peace, but we keep trying to secure it in a world designed to refuse permanence.

### 3. `Wa yahdi man yasha'u ila siratin mustaqim`: The Home and the Path Are Joined

Then the ayah completes the picture:

`wa yahdi man yasha'u ila siratin mustaqim`

And He guides whom He wills to a straight path.

[PAUSE]

This is beautiful because the verse gives both destination and road. `Dar al-salam` is the home. `Sirat mustaqim` is the way to it.

Without the second phrase, someone might imagine peace as a wish, a dream, or an emotion. But Allah joins the home to a path. Real arrival has a way of living that leads there.

And yet the ayah also protects us from arrogance. It does not say: Allah calls, so whoever wants can simply master the road by themselves. It says: `wa yahdi man yasha'`. He guides whom He wills.

This is not meant to paralyze the servant. It is meant to humble him. The call is open, but successful walking is still a gift. We do not stride to Allah's home as self-powered achievers. We walk there as people being guided.

There is a deep consolation in that too. If guidance were left entirely to the strength of our own inner consistency, most of us would lose hope quickly. But the One who calls is also the One who guides. The One who invites is also the One who takes by the hand.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: We Keep Seeking Peace in Places That Cannot Hold It

One of the quiet tragedies of the human being is that we sincerely want peace, but we keep looking for it in unstable containers.

We look for peace in income levels, in other people's approval, in a cleaner schedule, in being more admired, in getting the right home, in finally resolving one relationship, in one big win, in one season where nothing hard happens. Some of those things may bring relief. Some are real blessings. But none of them are `dar al-salam`.

[PAUSE]

The dunya, as the previous verse just taught us, becomes most adorned precisely before it can be cut down. So when we demand from dunya the kind of peace only Allah's final home can provide, we are asking a field not to be a field. We are asking a passing arrangement to behave like an eternal abode.

This ayah is merciful because it does not shame the longing for peace. It honors it by correcting its direction. Yes, you were made to want rest, safety, settledness, and an end to inward fracture. But do not expect the world of interruption to satisfy the appetite meant for `dar al-salam`.

### Theme 2: The Call Is Broad, but Walking the Path Requires Humble Need

There is another delicate balance in the ayah.

Allah calls, but Allah also guides whom He wills.

[PAUSE]

That means the servant lives between hope and humility. Hope, because there is a call and it is from Allah Himself. Humility, because even after hearing the call, we are still in need of hidayah at every step.

This protects us from two opposite diseases.

One is despair: the feeling that Allah only wants to expose, judge, or push away. This ayah destroys that lie. He calls.

The other is self-confidence in religious form: the feeling that once we know where the path is, arrival is now basically in our control. This ayah destroys that lie too. He guides whom He wills.

So the right posture is neither panic nor entitlement. It is pleading movement. We hear the invitation, and then we keep asking the Caller to make us among those actually guided to walk it.

## Closing Synthesis

Placed between the collapse-parable of worldly life and the unveiled akhirah contrast of the next verses, this ayah functions like a merciful opening in the wall. Allah has just shown that dunya cannot be trusted to remain. Before the surah moves to the radiant and darkened faces of final outcome, it tells us something essential: the Lord who warned us is also calling us.

[PAUSE]

He is calling to `dar al-salam`, not to a passing pause of relief, but to an abiding home of peace. And He is guiding, by His will, to a `sirat mustaqim`, because the home and the road belong together.

One way to carry this ayah is to ask: where have I been demanding `dar al-salam` from a world that can only offer moments? When I say I want peace, do I mean nearness to Allah and arrival at His home, or merely softer dunya on my own terms? And have I learned to hear guidance not as proof of my spiritual competence, but as one more gift from the One who called me first?

### Questions to Carry

- What kind of peace are you actually chasing right now: temporary easing, or the road to `dar al-salam`?
- Where has dunya convinced you it could become a permanent home if only you managed it well enough?
- Do you hear Allah more readily as Commander and Judge than as the One who is calling you?
- How often do you ask not only to know the path, but to be guided upon it?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**After exposing dunya as a field cut down overnight, Allah opens the real horizon: He Himself is calling to the only home where peace is native, and He Himself must guide the servant onto the straight path that leads there.**

### Du'a

*Allahumma, do not let our hearts settle for scraps of peace in a world that cannot keep them. Make us hear Your call with hope, walk Your straight path with need, and arrive by Your mercy at `dar al-salam`, where no fear, rupture, or humiliation remains.*
