# When You Declare Things Haram and Halal, You Are Speaking About Allah

## Surah Yunus (10:59-60)

[ayah:10:59] قُلْ أَرَءَيْتُم مَّآ أَنزَلَ ٱللَّهُ لَكُم مِّن رِّزْقٍ فَجَعَلْتُم مِّنْهُ حَرَامًا وَحَلَـٰلًا قُلْ ءَآللَّهُ أَذِنَ لَكُمْ ۖ أَمْ عَلَى ٱللَّهِ تَفْتَرُونَ

[ayah:10:60] وَمَا ظَنُّ ٱلَّذِينَ يَفْتَرُونَ عَلَى ٱللَّهِ ٱلْكَذِبَ يَوْمَ ٱلْقِيَـٰمَةِ ۗ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ لَذُو فَضْلٍ عَلَى ٱلنَّاسِ وَلَـٰكِنَّ أَكْثَرَهُمْ لَا يَشْكُرُونَ

*Qul ara'aytum ma anzala Allahu lakum min rizqin fa-ja'altum minhu haraman wa halalan. Qul a-Allahu adhina lakum am 'ala Allahi taftarun.*

*Wa ma zannu alladhina yaftaruna 'ala Allahi al-kadhiba yawma al-qiyamah. Inna Allaha la-dhu fadlin 'ala al-nasi wa lakinna aktharahum la yashkurun.*

**Say: have you considered what Allah has sent down for you of provision, and then you have made some of it unlawful and lawful? Say: has Allah given you permission, or is it about Allah that you fabricate? And what do those who fabricate lies about Allah think about the Day of Resurrection? Indeed Allah is surely full of favor toward mankind, but most of them are not grateful.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

There is a form of religious arrogance that hides inside pious language.

It does not always look like open rebellion. Sometimes it looks like confidently assigning sacred labels, speaking with certainty about what Allah has forbidden or allowed, building boundaries, permissions, and prohibitions around provision, all without trembling before whether Allah actually said any of it.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs expose that crime with remarkable directness. Allah sends down provision. Human beings receive it. Then they start classifying it on their own authority: this is haram, this is halal. The Qur'an interrupts the whole process with one decisive question: did Allah authorize this, or are you fabricating against Him?

Then the passage makes the moral weight even heavier by turning to the Day of Resurrection. Speaking loosely about halal and haram is not a small side issue. It is speech about Allah that will stand before Him.

The hook of the passage is this: **Yunus teaches that to declare things halal and haram without divine permission is to speak in Allah's name without authority, and that this ingratitude toward His provision is one of the gravest forms of fabrication against Him.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Ma anzala Allahu lakum min rizq**: Provision Arrives as Something Sent Down

The argument begins:

**ma anzala Allahu lakum min rizq**.

What Allah has sent down for you of provision.

[PAUSE]

This is beautiful and humbling. Rizq is described as something **anzala** - sent down. Even provision from the earth is framed through divine descent, reminding the heart that sustenance is not self-generated ownership but received gift.

That matters because the next crime becomes uglier in light of that gift. What came down from Allah as provision is then taken by humans and subjected to unauthorized sacred labeling.

### 2. **Fa-ja'altum minhu haraman wa halalan**: They Reclassify What They Did Not Author

Then:

**fa-ja'altum minhu haraman wa halalan**.

Then you made some of it unlawful and lawful.

[PAUSE]

The verb **ja'altum** is striking. You made. You assigned. You rendered. The issue is not simply that they stumbled into confusion. They actively imposed categories.

And the contrast **haraman wa halalan** shows the scale of the claim. This is not only about taste or custom. This is legislation in the name of the sacred.

### 3. **A-Allahu adhina lakum am 'ala Allahi taftarun**: The Field Is Reduced to Permission or Fabrication

Then the Qur'an narrows the matter:

**a-Allahu adhina lakum am 'ala Allahi taftarun**.

Has Allah given you permission, or is it about Allah that you fabricate?

[PAUSE]

This is one of the most important legal and spiritual questions in the surah. The field is reduced to two options only. Either there is divine permission, or there is fabrication.

Notice there is no middle category for confident religious speech without proof. The ayah does not say: perhaps you mean well. Perhaps it is inherited. Perhaps it is culturally useful. It asks one thing: permission or fabrication?

### 4. **Wa ma zannu... yawma al-qiyamah**: False Sacred Speech Must Imagine Its End

Then:

**wa ma zannu alladhina yaftaruna 'ala Allahi al-kadhiba yawma al-qiyamah**.

What do those who fabricate lies about Allah think about the Day of Resurrection?

[PAUSE]

The passage forces future reckoning into present speech. What exactly do these people imagine will happen when they stand before Allah after attributing to Him what He never said?

This is terrifying because it means the sin is not only in private error. It is in presuming upon the divine voice.

### 5. **Inna Allaha la-dhu fadlin... wa lakinna aktharahum la yashkurun**: The Underlying Sin Is Ingratitude

Then the ayah closes:

**inna Allaha la-dhu fadlin 'ala al-nas**.

Indeed Allah is surely full of favor toward mankind.

And:

**wa lakinna aktharahum la yashkurun**.

But most of them are not grateful.

[PAUSE]

This is the moral key. The problem is not only legal distortion. It is failure of shukr. Allah gave provision in generosity, and instead of receiving it with gratitude under revelation, they turned His favor into a stage for unauthorized control.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Speaking for Allah Without Proof Is a Spiritual Crime, Not Just a Technical Error

Many people think careless religious speech is a minor issue compared to obvious sins. These ayahs teach otherwise.

[PAUSE]

To say "Allah allowed this" or "Allah forbade this" without knowledge is not merely bad method. It is trespassing into divine authority. It puts the ego in the position of distributing sacred verdicts as though revelation were a tool in its hand rather than a limit over its speech.

This should make the believer cautious, humble, and slow. Not paralyzed from clarity when Allah has spoken, but fearful of borrowing His authority when He has not.

### Theme 2: Ingratitude Often Corrupts Provision Before It Corrupts Praise

We usually imagine shukr as saying thank you after receiving a blessing. But these ayahs reveal a deeper form of gratitude: honoring Allah's provision on Allah's terms.

[PAUSE]

If Allah provides and we respond by seizing interpretive control - declaring, editing, permitting, forbidding without warrant - then the ingratitude is already operating before any verbal praise or lack of it. We have taken gift and used it to stage autonomy.

That is why the ayah ends with favor and shukr. The real question is not only whether provision is present. It is whether provision turns the soul toward humility before Allah's right to define it.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs confront people who take Allah's sent-down provision and then divide it into halal and haram on their own authority. The Qur'an narrows the matter sharply: either Allah gave permission, or this is fabrication against Him. Then it asks what such fabricators imagine about the Day of Resurrection and closes by exposing the deeper disease beneath the distortion: Allah is full of favor toward mankind, but most do not meet that favor with gratitude.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask: where do I speak too confidently about what Allah wants, permits, or forbids without enough reverence and proof? In what areas have I turned blessing into control instead of gratitude? And when I receive Allah's provision, do I first ask what He authorized, or do I quietly start authorizing myself?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the passage lands here: **provision comes down from Allah as favor, but when the human being starts assigning sacred labels without divine permission, he is no longer simply handling goods in the world, he is speaking about Allah - and that is why ingratitude here becomes fabrication.**
