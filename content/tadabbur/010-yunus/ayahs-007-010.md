# The Difference Between Hearts Is What They Call Home

## Surah Yunus (10:7-10)

[ayah:10:7] إِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ لَا يَرْجُونَ لِقَآءَنَا وَرَضُوا۟ بِٱلْحَيَوٰةِ ٱلدُّنْيَا وَٱطْمَأَنُّوا۟ بِهَا وَٱلَّذِينَ هُمْ عَنْ ءَايَـٰتِنَا غَـٰفِلُونَ

[ayah:10:8] أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ مَأْوَىٰهُمُ ٱلنَّارُ بِمَا كَانُوا۟ يَكْسِبُونَ

[ayah:10:9] إِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ وَعَمِلُوا۟ ٱلصَّـٰلِحَـٰتِ يَهْدِيهِمْ رَبُّهُم بِإِيمَـٰنِهِمْ ۖ تَجْرِى مِن تَحْتِهِمُ ٱلْأَنْهَـٰرُ فِى جَنَّـٰتِ ٱلنَّعِيمِ

[ayah:10:10] دَعْوَىٰهُمْ فِيهَا سُبْحَـٰنَكَ ٱللَّهُمَّ وَتَحِيَّتُهُمْ فِيهَا سَلَـٰمٌ ۚ وَءَاخِرُ دَعْوَىٰهُمْ أَنِ ٱلْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ رَبِّ ٱلْعَـٰلَمِينَ

*Inna alladhina la yarjuna liqa'ana wa radu bi-al-hayati al-dunya wa itma'annu biha wa alladhina hum 'an ayatina ghafilun.*

*Ula'ika ma'wahumu al-naru bima kanu yaksibun.*

*Inna alladhina amanu wa 'amilu al-salihati yahdihim rabbuhum bi-imanihim. Tajri min tahtihimu al-anharu fi jannati al-na'im.*

*Da'wahum fiha subhanaka Allahumma wa tahiyyatuhum fiha salam. Wa akhiru da'wahum ani al-hamdu li-Allahi rabbi al-'alamin.*

**Indeed, those who do not hope for meeting Us, are satisfied with the worldly life, and feel settled in it, and those who are heedless of Our signs, those, their refuge is the Fire because of what they used to earn. Indeed, those who believe and do righteous deeds, their Lord guides them by their faith. Rivers flow beneath them in Gardens of Delight. Their call therein is: Glory be to You, O Allah. Their greeting therein is peace. And the end of their call is that all praise belongs to Allah, Lord of the worlds.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

The Qur'an often describes disbelief not first as argument, but as settlement.

Some people do not merely sin in the world. They relax into it. They make peace with a horizon that ends at the visible life. They stop straining toward meeting Allah and begin arranging themselves comfortably inside the present surface. That inner settling is what Yunus names here with painful precision.

[PAUSE]

Then the passage turns and gives the opposite scene. Not only do believers reach gardens. Their speech itself is healed. Their first language is tasbih. Their social atmosphere is salam. Their final language is hamd. The contrast is profound: one people settle into dunya until heedlessness becomes their native climate. Another people are guided by their faith until even paradise sounds like remembrance.

The hook of the passage is this: **Yunus teaches that the great divide between hearts is not simply belief versus disbelief in the abstract, but where the soul settles, what it longs to meet, and what kind of speech its deepest home finally produces.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **La yarjuna liqa'ana**: They Do Not Hope for the Meeting

The passage begins:

**la yarjuna liqa'ana**.

They do not hope for meeting Us.

[PAUSE]

This is deeper than saying they deny information. The issue is their horizon of expectation. They do not live oriented toward encounter with Allah. If the meeting disappears from the imagination of the heart, the world immediately gets heavier, louder, and more final than it really is.

### 2. **Radu ... wa itma'annu biha**: Satisfaction Deepens into Settled Rest

Then:

**radu bi-al-hayati al-dunya** and **itma'annu biha**.

They are satisfied with the worldly life and feel settled in it.

[PAUSE]

The movement matters. First contentment, then settled rest. Dunya is no longer treated as a crossing-place but as home. This is one of the most dangerous spiritual anesthesias: not wild indulgence, but calm accommodation to a life too low for the soul's true design.

### 3. **'An ayatina ghafilun**: Heedlessness of the Signs Is the Companion of Dunya-Settlement

Then:

**'an ayatina ghafilun**.

They are heedless of Our signs.

[PAUSE]

That is what makes the previous verses in Yunus so important. The signs are everywhere. Night, day, sun, moon, return, governance, truth. So if a person is heedless, it is not because the world is silent. It is because dunya-settlement has dulled his reading of the world.

### 4. **Yahdihim rabbuhum bi-imanihim**: Faith Itself Becomes a Means of Guidance

Then the contrast:

**yahdihim rabbuhum bi-imanihim**.

Their Lord guides them by their faith.

[PAUSE]

This is beautiful. Faith is not only the starting point. It becomes an instrument by which further guidance is granted. Belief opens into more seeing. Trust opens into more direction. The one who responds to Allah with iman is not left carrying himself alone. His Lord guides him by the very faith he carries.

### 5. **Da'wahum ... tahiyyatuhum ... akhiru da'wahum**: Paradise Is Described Through Speech

Then verse 10 unfolds the soundscape of Paradise:

**da'wahum**, **tahiyyatuhum**, **akhiru da'wahum**.

Their call, their greeting, the end of their call.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the gentlest descriptions of Jannah in the Qur'an. The verse is not focused only on architecture or reward-object. It is focused on atmosphere. The people there speak in tasbih, meet each other in salam, and end in hamd. Their language has become entirely clean.

That means Paradise is not only a place you enter. It is a state where the soul finally speaks without distortion.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Soul Becomes Like What It Calls Rest

If your heart treats dunya as a waiting room, its trials hurt differently. If your heart treats dunya as home, every threat to comfort feels apocalyptic. These verses expose that deep emotional theology.

[PAUSE]

The issue is not merely loving blessings. The issue is mistaking temporary lodging for final abode.

### Theme 2: Faith Is Meant to Heal Your Inner Language

The speech of Paradise in verse 10 is not random. Tasbih, salam, hamd. This is the purified vocabulary of a soul that sees Allah rightly, lives with others rightly, and receives everything from Him rightly.

[PAUSE]

So one way to measure whether faith is ripening in us is to listen to our own speech: is it moving toward remembrance, peace, gratitude? Or is our tongue still mostly shaped by complaint, ego-defense, and dunya-saturation?

## Closing Synthesis

These verses describe those who do not hope for meeting Allah, who become satisfied with dunya and settled in it, and who are heedless of the divine signs around them. Their refuge is the Fire. In contrast stand those who believe and do righteous deeds: their Lord guides them by their faith, rivers flow beneath them in Gardens of Delight, and the speech of Paradise itself is made of tasbih, salam, and hamd.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask: where has my soul started calling dunya "enough"? What have I emotionally settled into that should still feel provisional? And if my speech were a preview of my inner home, would it sound more like restlessness in the world or more like the early grammar of tasbih, salam, and hamd?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the passage lands here: **people are divided not only by doctrines but by where they emotionally settle, and the heart that lives hoping for Allah's meeting is slowly guided out of heedlessness until even its speech begins to resemble the atmosphere of Paradise.**
