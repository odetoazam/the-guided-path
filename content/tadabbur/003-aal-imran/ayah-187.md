# When Truth Is Hidden for a Small Price

## Surah Aal-Imran (3:187)

[ayah:3:187] وَإِذْ أَخَذَ ٱللَّهُ مِيثَـٰقَ ٱلَّذِينَ أُوتُوا۟ ٱلْكِتَـٰبَ لَتُبَيِّنُنَّهُۥ لِلنَّاسِ وَلَا تَكْتُمُونَهُۥ فَنَبَذُوهُ وَرَآءَ ظُهُورِهِمْ وَٱشْتَرَوْا۟ بِهِۦ ثَمَنًا قَلِيلًا ۖ فَبِئْسَ مَا يَشْتَرُونَ

*Wa idh akhadha Allahu mithaqa alladhina utu al-kitaba la-tubayyinunnahu li-al-nasi wa la taktumunahu. Fa-nabadhuhu wara'a zuhurihim wa ishtaraw bihi thamanan qalilan. Fa-bi'sa ma yashtarun.*

**And when Allah took the covenant of those who were given the Scripture: "You must make it clear to the people and not conceal it." But they threw it behind their backs and sold it for a small price. Miserable indeed is what they purchased.**

[PAUSE]

## The World This Arrived Into

Not every betrayal looks like open rebellion.

Some betrayals happen while still holding the book.

That is the atmosphere of this ayah. The verse is not speaking first about people who never received revelation. It is speaking about people who were entrusted with it. They were given the Book, bound by covenant, charged with explaining it to people, and forbidden from hiding it. So the tragedy here is not distance from truth. It is intimacy with truth without fidelity to truth.

And that is always more frightening.

Because the one far away from revelation may still be seeking it. But the one entrusted with revelation can corrupt entire communities if he begins treating divine trust as a tool of self-protection, social standing, or gain.

The hook of the ayah is this: **the gravest religious betrayal is not merely to disbelieve the truth, but to know it, hold it, and then hide it when truth becomes costly.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Akhadha Allahu mithaqa**: Revelation Comes With Binding Responsibility

The verse begins: **wa idh akhadha Allahu mithaqa**.

Allah took a **mithaq**. The root **w-th-q** carries the sense of firmness, fastening, binding securely. A **mithaq** is not a casual reminder or soft preference. It is a weighty covenant, a trust tied down with moral seriousness.

And that matters because the ayah is correcting a distortion many religious people fall into: we love the honor of being given sacred knowledge, but we resist the burden that comes with it.

[PAUSE]

To receive revelation is not only to be dignified. It is to be obligated. The Book is never a private ornament. It arrives as guidance for people, and anyone entrusted with it is immediately responsible for what they do with that trust.

That is why the verse speaks of those **who were given the Book** rather than those who merely read it. To be given something by Allah is to be answerable for it before Him.

### 2. **La-tubayyinunnahu li-al-nas wa la taktumunahu**: Clarify, Do Not Conceal

Then the covenant itself is stated in two paired commands: **la-tubayyinunnahu li-al-nas wa la taktumunahu**.

You must make it clear to the people, and you must not conceal it.

The verb **tubayyinunnahu** from the root **b-y-n** means to clarify, make distinct, bring out of ambiguity. The charge is not merely to preserve the text physically. It is to make its truth plain.

Then comes the negation: **la taktumunahu**, from the root **k-t-m**, to conceal, hide, keep something covered inside.

[PAUSE]

This pairing is beautiful and severe.

Religious fidelity is not fulfilled only by not destroying the text. It requires active clarification and active refusal to hide. Silence itself can become betrayal when silence protects falsehood from being exposed or protects the self from the cost of honesty.

That should stop us.

How often do people imagine they are innocent because they did not explicitly lie, while they carefully withheld the truth that would have disrupted comfort, power, or popularity? This verse says concealment can be its own disobedience.

### 3. **Fa-nabadhuhu wara'a zuhurihim**: They Did Not Lose the Trust; They Threw It Away

Then the verse describes the betrayal with startling physicality: **fa-nabadhuhu wara'a zuhurihim**.

They threw it behind their backs.

The verb **nabadh** is not forgetfulness. It is casting away, tossing aside. And **wara'a zuhurihim** intensifies the image. Behind their backs. Out of sight. Treated as something no longer allowed to stand before them and govern them.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the Quran's most haunting images of religious abandonment.

Notice that the Book is not said to have slipped from their hands. It was not misplaced accidentally. It was thrown behind them. A deliberate relocation of guidance from the place in front of the face to the place behind the self.

And that is still how many betrayals happen. We do not always deny revelation verbally. We simply move it out of the position where it gets to confront us.

### 4. **Ishtaraw bihi thamanan qalilan**: They Traded the Infinite for the Cheap

Then comes the economic metaphor: **wa ishtaraw bihi thamanan qalilan**.

They purchased with it a small price.

The verb from **sh-r-y** is the language of transaction again. Revelation keeps exposing moral corruption as a bargain. Something is being exchanged. Something of immense worth is being converted into something paltry.

And the word **qalilan** - small, little, meager - is devastating. Even if the worldly gain looked large in human eyes, the ayah calls it small. Why? Because anything purchased at the cost of betrayed revelation is small by definition.

[PAUSE]

This is how the Quran rescales corruption. The price may be position, applause, access, comfort, money, safety, the avoidance of controversy, the maintenance of a constituency. But compared to what was traded away, it is still **thamanan qalilan**.

Then the verse closes with disgust: **fa-bi'sa ma yashtarun**. Miserable indeed is what they purchase.

Not clever. Not strategic. Miserable.

[PAUSE]

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Religious Concealment Often Begins With Fear of Cost

Most people do not wake up wanting to betray revelation.

The process is usually quieter.

If I say this clearly, people will be upset.
If I name this honestly, I may lose status.
If I teach the full truth here, my place in this room may become fragile.
If I clarify what Allah said, I may no longer be rewarded by those who prefer ambiguity.

[PAUSE]

That is where concealment starts: not with total rejection, but with the ego calculating cost.

And once cost becomes the governing lens, the Book has already begun moving behind the back. Because now truth will be shown only when convenient, and hidden when expensive.

This ayah warns us that such concealment is not neutral caution. It is a covenant breach. The one entrusted with truth is not free to behave as though divine speech were private capital to deploy selectively for self-preservation.

### Theme 2: We Still Sell Truth Cheaply in Modern Forms

This verse is painfully current.

People still sell truth for small prices. The prices have different names now, but the market is the same.

Praise.
Platform.
Institutional comfort.
Cultural approval.
Avoidance of backlash.
The preservation of a brand.
The desire to remain invited everywhere.

[PAUSE]

And again, the issue is not wisdom in timing or gentleness in delivery. Revelation itself teaches hikmah. The issue is something darker: when a servant already knows what must be made clear, but chooses concealment because the worldly transaction feels attractive.

That is why the ayah stays frightening. It is not only about "them." It is about any heart that begins to see truth as negotiable and cost as ultimate.

The question for us is not merely whether we have the truth. The question is whether we are willing to leave it in front of us when it threatens our comfort.

[PAUSE]

## Closing Synthesis

This ayah begins with covenant, moves into the obligation to clarify rather than conceal, then shows the betrayal as a physical act of throwing the trust behind the back and an economic act of trading it for a meager price.

So the verse reveals two things about religious corruption.

It is directional.
Truth gets moved behind the self.

And it is transactional.
Truth gets exchanged for something cheap.

[PAUSE]

### Questions to Carry

1. Where have I treated truth as something to manage strategically rather than something I am bound to honor?
2. What "small price" am I most tempted to protect by keeping difficult truths unclear?
3. Is revelation in front of me, confronting me, or have I quietly moved parts of it behind my back?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**This ayah teaches that revelation is a covenantal trust to be clarified, not concealed, and that whenever truth is hidden for worldly gain, the gain is always small and the purchase always miserable.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, keep Your Book in front of us and never let us move it behind our backs. Protect us from concealing truth out of fear, greed, vanity, or the desire to stay comfortable. Make us people who honor the trust of revelation, speak it with wisdom and courage, and never sell what came from You for a price You have already called small. Ameen.
