# We Heard the Caller, So Forgive What Still Remains

## Surah Aal-Imran (3:193)

[ayah:3:193] رَّبَّنَآ إِنَّنَا سَمِعْنَا مُنَادِيًا يُنَادِى لِلْإِيمَـٰنِ أَنْ ءَامِنُوا۟ بِرَبِّكُمْ فَـَٔامَنَّا ۚ رَبَّنَا فَٱغْفِرْ لَنَا ذُنُوبَنَا وَكَفِّرْ عَنَّا سَيِّـَٔاتِنَا وَتَوَفَّنَا مَعَ ٱلْأَبْرَارِ

*Rabbana innana sami'na munadiyan yunadi li-al-imani an aminu bi-rabbikum fa-amanna. Rabbana fa-ghfir lana dhunubana wa kaffir 'anna sayyi'atina wa tawaffana ma'a al-abrar.*

**Our Lord, indeed we heard a caller calling to faith, "Believe in your Lord," so we believed. Our Lord, then forgive our sins, erase from us our misdeeds, and take us in death with the righteous.**

[PAUSE]

## The World This Arrived Into

The prayer at the end of Aal-Imran keeps moving inward.

First, the believers looked at creation and said it was not made in vain. Then they begged protection from the Fire. Then they named entry into the Fire as disgrace and the fate of the wrongdoers as helperless.

Now they remember something else: they were not left to figure this road out alone. They were called.

That matters, because faith is never described here as self-manufactured heroism. The believers do not say, "We found the truth through our own brilliance." They say: we heard a caller. We were addressed. We were summoned to iman. And having answered that call, they still do not ask for a certificate of perfection. They ask for forgiveness.

The hook of the ayah is this: **real believers do not mistake answering the call of faith for having become pure enough to no longer need mercy.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Sami'na munadiyan yunadi li-al-iman**: Faith Begins as a Heard Summons

The verse says: **innana sami'na munadiyan yunadi li-al-iman**.

Indeed, we heard a caller calling to faith.

The root **s-m-'** is hearing, and the word **munadi** from **n-d-y** is a caller, one who raises a summons publicly, one whose voice reaches others and gathers them.

[PAUSE]

This is beautiful because it gives iman the texture of response.

Faith is not portrayed here as something generated in sealed isolation. It is hearing and answering. A voice reached us. A call entered our awareness. The soul was addressed and then moved.

That means humility is built into the verse from the beginning. We believe, yes, but our belief is already framed as response to a grace that came toward us first.

And notice the phrase **li-al-iman**. The caller is not calling to himself, not to a tribe, not to prestige, not to a vague spirituality. He is calling toward iman itself: entrusting oneself to the Lord, entering into the security of truth.

### 2. **An aminu bi-rabbikum fa-amanna**: The Answer Is Immediate, But Not Self-Sufficient

Then the call is quoted: **an aminu bi-rabbikum**. Believe in your Lord.

This is not simply "believe in God" in an abstract sense. It is **bi-rabbikum** - in your Lord, the One who raised, sustained, shaped, and cared for you. The command to iman is rooted in relationship.

Then comes the believers' answer: **fa-amanna**. So we believed.

[PAUSE]

The **fa** matters. It gives immediacy. The call came, so belief followed. There is beauty in that directness. The verse is honoring the obedience of response.

But then something even more beautiful happens. They do not stop at "we believed," as if that were the end of the matter. They return immediately to **Rabbana** and begin asking for forgiveness. Which means the verse protects us from two errors at once:

One error is delaying response forever.
The other is imagining that response itself removes our need for mercy.

True faith answers promptly, then bows more deeply.

### 3. **Fa-ghfir lana dhunubana wa kaffir 'anna sayyi'atina**: Forgiveness and Erasure Are Asked Together

Then the du'a unfolds: **fa-ghfir lana dhunubana wa kaffir 'anna sayyi'atina**.

Forgive for us our sins, and efface from us our misdeeds.

The root **gh-f-r** carries the sense of covering, forgiving, shielding. It is not mere legal cancellation. It carries mercy, concealment, and protection from the consequences of what would otherwise expose us.

Then **kaffir 'anna** from **k-f-r** in this verbal form means to cover over, efface, wipe away. The pairing is remarkable.

[PAUSE]

And the objects are also distinct:

**dhunub** - sins, with the sense of burdens and liabilities that cling.

**sayyi'at** - evil deeds, ugly actions, the actual bads we have done.

The believers are not vague here. They know that even after iman, there are still things that need covering, effacing, cleaning, carrying away.

This is the realism of the Qur'an. It does not flatter the believer into forgetting his stains. Nor does it crush him with them. It teaches him to name them before a Lord whose mercy is still being sought precisely because faith is alive.

### 4. **Wa tawaffana ma'a al-abrar**: The Prayer Ends With the Company of the Righteous

Then the du'a rises to its final request: **wa tawaffana ma'a al-abrar**.

Take us fully in death with the righteous.

The root **w-f-y** in **tawaffana** carries completeness: to take fully, to gather the soul at death in full taking. And **al-abrar** from **b-r-r** are the truly righteous, the people of deep moral goodness, breadth of virtue, and truthful alignment.

[PAUSE]

This is so moving because the verse does not ask only for survival, only for pardon, only for escape. It asks for companionship in the final station. Do not let us die alone in moral fragmentation. Let us be gathered with the people whose lives were true.

The du'a therefore teaches us that salvation is not merely about isolated self-preservation. It is about belonging to the company of truth when the soul is finally taken.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Hearing the Call Does Not Mean the Journey Is Finished

Many people imagine religion as though there were one decisive moment that settles everything.

I believed, so now I am done.
I committed, so now I can think of myself as secure.
I answered the call, so now my story is mainly about arrival.

[PAUSE]

This ayah refuses that shallowness.

Yes, the believers heard the call.
Yes, they responded.
Yes, they say, "So we believed."

And then immediately they ask for forgiveness, expiation, and a righteous death.

That is the psychology of the sincere believer. Faith does not make him complacent; it makes him more lucid. The closer he comes to Allah, the less impressed he is by himself and the more urgent his need for mercy becomes.

### Theme 2: The Healthiest Faith Is Neither Self-Congratulating Nor Despairing

There is a beautiful balance in this verse that many of us need desperately.

It does not speak the language of self-congratulation.
But it also does not speak the language of despair.

[PAUSE]

The believers do not say, "We are nothing, our response means nothing, there is no point speaking of faith." No, they honor the grace of response: **fa-amanna**.

But neither do they say, "We believed, therefore our sins are beneath mention." No, they ask: forgive our sins, erase our misdeeds, take us with the righteous.

This is mature iman.

It remembers grace without becoming proud.
It remembers sin without becoming hopeless.

And perhaps this is one of the deepest marks of the people of inner core: they know how to inhabit the space between response and need, between obedience and pleading, between being grateful that they heard and trembling at what still remains to be cleaned.

[PAUSE]

## Closing Synthesis

This ayah moves from hearing the caller to iman, to answering that call, to asking for forgiveness, expiation, and a righteous end.

So it teaches that the believer's life is not a straight line from call to self-confidence. It is a living rhythm:

summons,
response,
mercy,
hope for a good ending.

[PAUSE]

### Questions to Carry

1. Do I still remember my faith as a response to a call that reached me, or have I started narrating it mainly as my own accomplishment?
2. Where has my answer to the call become self-satisfaction instead of deeper dependence on Allah?
3. If I asked tonight to be taken with the righteous, what unfinished repentance would I not want to delay?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**This ayah teaches that true believers hear and answer the call to iman, then turn immediately to mercy, because responding to Allah never removes the need to be forgiven, cleansed, and given a righteous end.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, keep the first tenderness of hearing Your call alive in us. Do not let our faith become a story we tell about ourselves instead of a mercy we keep thanking You for. Forgive our sins, erase our misdeeds, and when our souls are finally taken, take us with the righteous. Ameen.
