# What Is With Allah Is Better for the Righteous

## Surah Aal-Imran (3:198)

[ayah:3:198] لَـٰكِنِ ٱلَّذِينَ ٱتَّقَوْا۟ رَبَّهُمْ لَهُمْ جَنَّـٰتٌ تَجْرِى مِن تَحْتِهَا ٱلْأَنْهَـٰرُ خَـٰلِدِينَ فِيهَا نُزُلًا مِّنْ عِندِ ٱللَّهِ ۗ وَمَا عِندَ ٱللَّهِ خَيْرٌ لِّلْأَبْرَارِ

*Lakini alladhina ittaqaw rabbahum lahum jannatun tajri min tahtiha al-anharu khalidina fiha nuzulan min 'indi Allah. Wa ma 'inda Allahi khayrun li-al-abrar.*

**But those who were mindful of their Lord will have gardens beneath which rivers flow, abiding therein, as a welcoming gift from Allah. And what is with Allah is better for the righteous.**

[PAUSE]

## The World This Arrived Into

After warning the believer not to be deceived by the movement of those who disbelieve through the lands, the surah does not leave us only with negation.

It gives us the alternative.

That matters. The Quran is not content merely to take away false glamour. It wants to replace it with something truer and more beautiful. So after saying that the disbelievers' circulation is only a little enjoyment and that their refuge is Jahannam, this verse says: **but**. But those who had taqwa of their Lord have something else entirely.

The hook of the ayah is this: **the way the Quran heals envy is not only by exposing the smallness of what dazzled you, but by reintroducing you to what is actually better.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Lakini alladhina ittaqaw rabbahum**: The Contrast Begins in the Heart

The verse opens with **lakin** - but, however, in contrast.

This is the word that rescues the believer from staring too long at the previous scene. The disbelievers' worldly movement is not the whole picture. There is another people, another path, another end.

And that other people are defined first not by what they visibly had, but by what they inwardly carried: **ittaqaw rabbahum**.

They had taqwa of their Lord.

The root **w-q-y** is protection, guarding, shielding oneself. Taqwa is not merely fear in a flat sense. It is a guarded nearness to Allah, a life shaped by serious awareness of Him.

[PAUSE]

This is significant. The verse does not begin the contrast with status, heroism, platform, or even outward suffering. It begins with an inner posture before the Lord. The real difference between these two destinies lies first in relation to Allah.

### 2. **Lahum jannatun tajri min tahtiha al-anhar**: Lasting Abundance Replaces Brief Enjoyment

Then the verse names what is for them: **lahum jannat**.

For them are gardens.

Not one enclosed, meager patch, but gardens - abundance, fertility, shaded life, beauty that does not wither under the sun of time. Then: **tajri min tahtiha al-anhar** - rivers flow beneath them.

[PAUSE]

This is the direct answer to **mata'un qalil** in the previous passage.

Brief enjoyment is answered with enduring garden.
Restless movement through the lands is answered with a place of true settlement.
Apparent worldly spread is answered with a gift from Allah that does not decay.

The Qur'an often heals the soul by contrast. It does not merely tell you that one thing is wrong. It lets you taste the difference between the cheap and the real.

### 3. **Khalidina fiha nuzulan min 'indi Allah**: This Is Not Bare Survival; It Is Hospitality

Then comes **khalidina fiha** - abiding therein, remaining without expiration.

And then a very beautiful word: **nuzulan**.

From the root **n-z-l**, it carries the sense of what is laid out for a guest, a hospitable welcome, an arrival gift prepared for one who has come and is being received with honor.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the verse's most tender turns.

Jannah here is not described only as property or reward in a transactional sense. It is described as hospitality from Allah. The righteous do not merely survive into the Hereafter. They are received.

That changes the emotional tone of the verse entirely. The one who lived with taqwa in a world that may not have understood him is not just compensated. He is welcomed.

And the phrase **min 'indi Allah** intensifies that welcome. From the presence of Allah. From near Him. The value of the gift is not only in what it is, but in whose giving it comes from.

### 4. **Wa ma 'inda Allahi khayrun li-al-abrar**: Nearness to Allah Is the Real Measure of Better

Then the verse closes: **wa ma 'inda Allahi khayrun li-al-abrar**.

What is with Allah is better for the righteous.

The word **khayr** - better, خير - matters because it is comparative. The verse knows there is something in the dunya that can attract, please, and impress. But it says what is with Allah is better.

[PAUSE]

That means the cure for spiritual deception is not pretending the world has nothing alluring in it. The cure is comparative clarity. Yes, there is something there. But what is with Allah is better.

And for whom? **Li-al-abrar** - for the righteous, the deeply truthful and good. The verse ends not merely by praising the gift, but by naming the kind of people who will be able to receive it: those whose lives had inward and outward truth in them.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Envy Dies When the Heart Recovers the Better

Envy survives by amnesia.

It works by making the immediate thing feel ultimate, the visible thing feel decisive, the worldly thing feel like the only available sweetness. Once that spell takes hold, the heart becomes restless and resentful.

[PAUSE]

This ayah breaks the spell not only by criticizing what others have, but by restoring the category of **better**.

What is with Allah is better.

That sentence is enough to rescue a believer from many humiliating comparisons if he lets it sink deeply enough. Not because the dunya stops existing, but because it stops being the highest available horizon.

The righteous person is not someone who felt no temptation. He is someone who kept returning to the better.

### Theme 2: The Hereafter Is Described Here as Being Received, Not Merely Paid

The word **nuzul** should stay with us.

Many people imagine the Hereafter in moral and legal terms alone: judgment, scales, reward, punishment. All of that is true. But this verse adds another register: welcome.

[PAUSE]

To the one who lived with taqwa, Jannah is not merely "what he earned." It is what Allah receives him into. There is warmth in that. Nearness. Honor. The end of being spiritually homeless in a world that was never the final home to begin with.

And perhaps that is why the verse is such a needed contrast after the previous warnings. The believer is not only being told, "Do not envy their road." He is being shown, "Look at how you will be received if you keep walking yours."

## Closing Synthesis

This verse contrasts directly with the brief, deceptive enjoyment of the previous passage. It begins with taqwa, unfolds into abiding gardens and flowing rivers, deepens into the language of divine hospitality, and then seals the whole comparison with one decisive sentence: what is with Allah is better for the righteous.

So the verse is not only a promise. It is a recalibration of desire.

[PAUSE]

### Questions to Carry

1. What forms of worldly ease still tempt me because I have not kept the "better" of Allah vivid enough in my heart?
2. How would my patience change if I remembered not only reward, but the hospitality of being received by Allah?
3. In my daily choices, am I really orienting myself toward what is with Allah as better, or only saying that I am?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**This ayah teaches that the righteous are not merely denied the world's brief illusions; they are being led toward something better - lasting, welcoming, and from the very presence of Allah.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, keep the better of what is with You alive in our hearts until the brief shine of this world loses its power to enchant us. Make us people of taqwa who are walking toward Your welcome, not people distracted by temporary circulation and fading praise. Receive us among the righteous with the hospitality You promised. Ameen.
