# Why Do You Bend the Path?

## Surah Aal-Imran (3:99)

[ayah:3:99] قُلْ يَـٰٓأَهْلَ ٱلْكِتَـٰبِ لِمَ تَصُدُّونَ عَن سَبِيلِ ٱللَّهِ مَنْ ءَامَنَ تَبْغُونَهَا عِوَجًا وَأَنتُمْ شُهَدَآءُ ۗ وَمَا ٱللَّهُ بِغَـٰفِلٍ عَمَّا تَعْمَلُونَ

*Qul ya ahla al-kitabi lima tasudduna 'an sabili Allahi man amana tabghunaha 'iwajan wa antum shuhada'u wa ma Allahu bi-ghafilin 'amma ta'malun.*

**Say: O People of the Book, why do you turn away from the path of Allah those who believe, seeking to make it crooked while you are witnesses? And Allah is not unaware of what you do.**

[PAUSE]

## The World This Arrived Into

There is a grave difference between being lost yourself and becoming an architect of someone else's detour.

That is the escalation in this ayah.

The previous verse asked: why do you disbelieve in Allah's signs while Allah is Witness over what you do? This verse goes further. Now the problem is not only that some among the People of the Book reject what they know. The problem is that they become active pressure against the path itself. They do not merely fail to walk. They try to make walking harder for others.

That is why the verse feels morally heavier.

It addresses a people associated with scripture and witness and asks why they are now engaged in obstruction: turning people away from Allah's path, wanting that path to appear crooked, and doing all of this while standing in possession of enough witness to know exactly what they are handling.

The hook of the ayah is this: **private resistance becomes far darker when it matures into public distortion.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Tasuddun 'an sabil Allah**: Not just refusing the path, but blocking it

The verse says: **lima tasuddun 'an sabil Allah**.

The verb **tasuddun** comes from the root **s-d-d**, which carries the sense of blocking, repelling, turning away, creating obstruction. This is more aggressive than simply not following. It is the language of interference.

That matters. The ayah is not describing a private crisis hidden in the chest. It is describing a social act. People are being pushed away, discouraged, confused, or fenced off from the path of Allah by those who should have been among the first to recognize it.

[PAUSE]

And then the verse says **sabil Allah**. The path of Allah.

A **sabil** is not only a destination. It is a traversable route, a way one can walk, a passage opened toward arrival. To obstruct a sabil is therefore to interfere with movement itself. The offense here is not merely holding a wrong opinion. It is making obedience harder for another human being.

That should shake us.

Because one of the easiest sins to underestimate is the sin of becoming friction in somebody else's return to Allah. Not because we announced ourselves as enemies of faith, but because we mocked, delayed, complicated, discredited, shamed, or strategically confused the person who was trying to move toward truth.

[PAUSE]

### 2. **Tabghunaha 'iwajan**: Wanting the straight thing to look bent

Then comes one of the ayah's sharpest phrases: **tabghunaha 'iwajan**.

The verb **tabghun** comes from the root **b-gh-y**, a root of seeking, pursuing, wanting, sometimes with the flavor of overreaching or wrongful desire depending on context. Here it is not a casual accident. There is intent. They are seeking something.

And what are they seeking? **'iwajan**.

The noun **'iwaj** is crookedness, deviation, bend, distortion. Something that should direct clearly has been made to appear bent, warped, unreliable, difficult to trust.

[PAUSE]

This is devastating because the verse does not merely accuse them of missing the path. It accuses them of wanting the path itself to look misshapen.

That is a darker moral posture. It is one thing to feel unable to walk. It is another to desire that the road be seen as defective. To want the truth to seem inconsistent. To want the straight way to feel embarrassing, impractical, or unstable in the eyes of others.

How often does distortion work exactly like this? Not by producing a completely new road, but by subtly bending the real one in public perception. A little mockery here. A little suspicion there. A strategic half-truth. A burden added where Allah gave ease. A doubt introduced without sincerity. A tone of superiority. Soon the path still exists, but to the new believer it looks bent.

[PAUSE]

### 3. **Wa antum shuhada'**: The tragedy is intensified by witness

Then comes the ayah's most painful clause: **wa antum shuhada'**. While you are witnesses.

The word **shuhada'** comes from the root **sh-h-d**, witnessing, testifying, being present to what one knows. The offense is not happening in innocence. It is happening in the presence of witness.

[PAUSE]

That is what makes the verse so grave. The obstruction here is not ignorance operating in darkness. It is witness corrupted into opposition.

This is one of the Quran's recurring moral principles: when knowledge is real, accountability is heavier. To know enough to testify and then use that position to bend the path for others is not merely personal failure. It is betrayal of trust.

And the ending seals it: **wa ma Allahu bi-ghafilin 'amma ta'malun**. Allah is not unaware of what you do.

**Ghaflah** is heedlessness, inattentiveness, letting a matter slip outside awareness. The verse negates that entirely for Allah. Nothing about these distortions escapes Him. Not the outward obstruction, not the inward intention, not the subtle reshaping of perception, not the damage done to those trying to believe.

[PAUSE]

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: There is a special ugliness in making tawbah harder for someone else

Most people can recognize the ugliness of open unbelief. Fewer recognize the ugliness of becoming a barrier.

But the Quran recognizes it immediately.

To stand between a soul and Allah's path is a profound violence, even when it is done with polished language. The person trying to return may already be fragile, uncertain, ashamed, and easily shaken. A single mocking comment, a strategic insinuation, a public distortion, or a cynical reading from someone who sounds authoritative can introduce a bend into the road right when the person needed straightness most.

[PAUSE]

This means the ayah does not belong only to ancient polemic. It belongs to us.

Have we ever made obedience look foolish because our ego needed company?
Have we ever treated someone's sincere return as naivete?
Have we ever exaggerated the harshest version of religion to make the path seem unwalkable?
Have we ever used knowledge not to guide, but to dominate?

Whenever that happens, the verse moves uncomfortably close. The problem is no longer just what we believe. The problem is what kind of environment our presence creates around those trying to believe.

[PAUSE]

### Theme 2: Witness should make a person gentle, not manipulative

The clause **wa antum shuhada'** carries a terrible irony. Witness should have humbled them. It should have made them careful, responsible, soft before truth. Instead, in the verse's indictment, witness has become material for manipulation.

That danger remains alive in every religious setting.

Knowledge can either make a person more transparent before Allah or more skillful at bending appearances. Scholarship can either become amanah or become leverage. Familiarity with sacred language can either straighten the path for others or furnish better tools for making crookedness sound principled.

[PAUSE]

That is why the ending matters so much: **Allah is not unaware**. Human beings may miss the maneuver. Audiences may applaud the speaker. Communities may inherit the distortion and call it tradition. But Allah is not negligent toward any of it.

There is relief in that, too.

If you have ever been confused by those who seemed knowledgeable yet made the road to Allah feel more twisted than clear, this verse reminds you that divine sight is not fooled by religious performance. The One who knows the straight path also knows who is straightening it and who is bending it.

[PAUSE]

## Closing Synthesis

This ayah exposes an escalation in moral failure.

First comes the address: **O People of the Book**.
Then comes the charge: **why do you obstruct from Allah's path those who believe?**
Then comes the deeper diagnosis: **you seek to make that path crooked**.
Then comes the aggravating condition: **while you are witnesses**.
And finally the closure: **Allah is not unaware of what you do**.

So the verse is not only about disbelief. It is about what disbelief becomes when it turns strategic, social, and distortive. It is about the sin of interfering with someone else's straight line to Allah.

### Questions to Carry

1. Have I ever made the path to Allah look more bent, harsh, or confusing than Allah Himself made it?
2. Does my knowledge make the road clearer for people, or does it make me more capable of controlling them?
3. If Allah reviewed the effect of my words on other people's faith, would I be relieved or afraid?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**This ayah condemns the person who not only resists Allah's path, but tries to bend that path in the eyes of others while already knowing enough to witness the truth.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, never let us become obstacles between Your servants and Your path. Make our knowledge a trust that clarifies rather than confuses, softens rather than hardens, and guides rather than bends. Protect us from using witness as leverage, and make us among those who straighten the road to You for ourselves and for others. Ameen.
