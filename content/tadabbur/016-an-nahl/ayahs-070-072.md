# He Created You, Takes You Back Through Weakness, and Still You Deny His Blessings

## Surah An-Nahl (16:70-72)

[ayah:16:70] وَٱللَّهُ خَلَقَكُمْ ثُمَّ يَتَوَفَّىٰكُمْ ۚ وَمِنكُم مَّن يُرَدُّ إِلَىٰٓ أَرْذَلِ ٱلْعُمُرِ لِكَىْ لَا يَعْلَمَ بَعْدَ عِلْمٍ شَيْـًٔا ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ عَلِيمٌ قَدِيرٌ

## Introduction

It is easy to feel permanent while we are strong.

It is easy to speak as though what we have, know, or control naturally belongs to us.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs dismantle that illusion from several angles at once.

Allah created you.

Allah takes you in death.

Some of you are even returned, before death, to a state in which knowledge itself begins to unravel.

Then the surah turns to provision, family, and ordinary goodness. People accept unequal shares of rizq in the world, but would never accept their servants as equal partners in what they possess. Yet somehow they still make partners for Allah. Then the surah names spouses, children, descendants, and wholesome provision as His gifts and asks the humiliating question straight: is it falsehood they believe in, while Allah's favor they keep denying?

The hook of the passage is this: **An-Nahl teaches that human strength, knowledge, wealth, and family are all signs of radical dependence on Allah, and that ingratitude often begins when we start treating temporary entrusted gifts as though they were proof of independence.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Khalaqakum ... Yatawaffakum ... Yuraddu ila Ardhali al-'Umur**: The Human Being Is Held Entirely Within Allah's Control

The first ayah opens with terrifying simplicity:

**wa Allahu khalaqakum thumma yatawaffakum**

Allah created you, then He takes you in death.

[PAUSE]

The verse does not begin with what we do. It begins with what was done to us and for us. We were created. We are taken. The whole arc is already under His action.

Then it adds an unsettling middle scene:

**wa minkum man yuraddu ila ardhali al-'umur**

Among you is one who is returned to the lowest, most feeble stage of life.

`Yuraddu` matters here. He is returned. This condition is not self-authored. And `ardhal al-'umur` is not merely old age in an honorable abstract sense. It is the most depleted edge of human weakness.

Then comes the heartbreak:

**li-kay la ya'lama ba'da 'ilmin shay'an**

so that after knowledge, he knows nothing.

[PAUSE]

The Quran is forcing us to see knowledge itself as contingent. The mind we trust, the memory we lean on, the clarity we quietly take pride in, all of it can be thinned out before our eyes.

And then the close:

**inna Allaha 'alimun qadir**

Surely Allah is All-Knowing, All-Powerful.

Human knowing rises and falls. His does not. Human capacity weakens and decays. His power does not.

### 2. **Fa-Ma alladhina Fuddilu bi-Raddi Rizqihim**: People Refuse Equality in Their Own Wealth, Yet Invent It in Allah's Dominion

Then the surah moves to provision:

**wa Allahu faddala ba'dakum 'ala ba'din fi al-rizq**

Allah has favored some of you over others in provision.

This is not presented first as a social theory. It is presented as a fact of created life under divine distribution.

Then comes the argument:

**fa-ma alladhina fuddilu bi-raddi rizqihim 'ala ma malakat aymanuhum fa-hum fihi sawa'**

Those who were given more would not hand their provision over to those under their authority such that they would become equal in it.

[PAUSE]

The point is not to praise social hierarchy. The point is to expose hypocrisy through a comparison they themselves understand. You refuse equality in your own controlled property. You do not accept your dependents as co-owners of what you possess. So how do you then imagine that Allah should have partners in His dominion?

The ending sharpens the wound:

**a-fa-bi-ni'mati Allahi yajhadun**

Is it then the favor of Allah that they deny?

`Yajhadun` is more aggressive than ordinary forgetfulness. It carries the force of denial, refusal, covering over what is known.

### 3. **Ja'ala Lakum min Anfusikum Azwajan**: The Most Intimate Human Comforts Are Also Named as Divine Gift

Then the surah turns to the home:

**wa Allahu ja'ala lakum min anfusikum azwajan**

Allah made for you from yourselves spouses.

[PAUSE]

`Min anfusikum` is tender. From yourselves. Of your own kind. The gift is not only that spouses exist, but that they are made in a way that human beings can find nearness, recognition, and settled companionship with them.

Then:

**wa ja'ala lakum min azwajikum banina wa hafadah**

And He made for you from your spouses children and descendants.

The verse keeps moving outward from the intimate pair to the widening family line.

Then:

**wa razaqakum mina al-tayyibat**

He provided you with good and wholesome things.

The structure is beautiful: companionship, offspring, provision. The architecture of ordinary life is being named as ni'mah.

Then the final exposure:

**a-fa-bi-al-batili yu'minuna wa bi-ni'mati Allahi hum yakfurun**

Is it then in falsehood that they believe, while in Allah's favor they disbelieve?

[PAUSE]

The contrast is devastating. `Al-batil` is embraced. `Ni'mat Allah` is denied. That is the reversal the surah wants to shame.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Human Competence Is More Fragile Than We Like to Admit

Most people are comfortable saying they will die someday.

Far fewer are prepared to think seriously about losing their strength, memory, or clarity before they die.

[PAUSE]

This ayah makes us sit inside that possibility. After knowledge, nothing. After fluency, confusion. After capability, dependence.

Why does the Quran make us look there?

Because the nafs builds arrogance not only from wealth or status, but from mental steadiness itself. We begin to trust our own continuity. We treat our capacity as though it were a natural possession instead of a sustained gift.

But human excellence is precarious. Even our best seasons are borrowed.

That is not meant to make us cynical about aging or cruel toward weakness. Quite the opposite. It is meant to produce humility, mercy, and realism. The person you may one day grow impatient with is a sign of what you are made of. The clarity you are proud of today is not your private creation. And the dignity of the weak must never be measured by how useful they remain.

It also teaches us something about our relationship to knowledge. If Allah can let knowledge thin out after years of accumulation, then knowledge was never ours in an absolute sense. It was amanah all along. That should make us grateful while we have it, gentle when others lose it, and less impressed by ourselves in the meantime.

### Theme 2: Ingratitude Often Looks Like Treating Gifts as Normal, Deserved, or Self-Generated

The passage moves from life-span to provision to family, but the disease it keeps exposing is one: juhud and kufr toward ni'mah.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the quietest spiritual disasters. A person may not deny Allah verbally at every moment. He may even speak religiously. Yet his inner posture toward blessings becomes practical denial.

How?

By treating them as baseline.

By acting as though his mind, income, household, children, or daily provisions simply belong to the furniture of life.

By being shocked at loss, but never shocked at gift.

By arguing for false loyalties while continuing to live off favors that came only from Allah.

That is why the verse's questions land so hard. You won't accept rivals in your own possession, but you assign rivals to Allah. You live by His favors, but you put your trust in falsehood. You are held up by gifts you did not manufacture, yet you narrate yourself as though you stand independently.

The cure is not merely saying "alhamdulillah" more often, though that matters. The deeper cure is to relearn the ontology of gifts: this was given, this was arranged, this was eased, this was sustained. My life is not self-standing. It is continuously received.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs take apart the myth of human self-sufficiency with great tenderness and force.

Allah created you.

Allah will take you.

Some of you are returned, even before death, to a stage where knowledge itself grows thin.

Then the surah shows how irrational ingratitude really is. Provision is distributed by Allah, yet people refuse equality in their own possessions while imagining partners for Him. Spouses, children, descendants, and wholesome sustenance all come as His gifts, yet people keep believing in falsehood and covering over the very favor by which they live.

[PAUSE]

So the architecture is clear: created life, fading knowledge, distributed rizq, intimate blessings, then the indictment of denial. Everything we are tempted to treat as "mine" has already been named as His gift. The only sane response is humility, gratitude, and worship free of partners.

### Questions to Carry

- What part of my strength or knowledge have I begun treating as though it were inherently mine?
- Does the fragility of age make me more merciful toward the weak, or do I still measure people by usefulness?
- Which blessings in my life have become so normal to me that I no longer feel them as gifts?
- Where might I be leaning on false narratives of independence while living entirely off Allah's favor?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs teach that our life, knowledge, provision, and family are all continuously received gifts from Allah, and that ingratitude begins whenever we start treating borrowed blessings as proof that we stand by ourselves.**

### Du'a

*Allahumma, do not let our knowledge make us proud, our provision make us heedless, or our families make us forget their Giver. Keep us grateful while we are strong, gentle toward those returned to weakness, and truthful about our complete dependence on You.*
