# They Assign to Allah What They Themselves Cannot Bear

## Surah An-Nahl (16:56-60)

[ayah:16:56] وَيَجْعَلُونَ لِمَا لَا يَعْلَمُونَ نَصِيبًا مِّمَّا رَزَقْنَـٰهُمْ ۗ تَٱللَّهِ لَتُسْـَٔلُنَّ عَمَّا كُنتُمْ تَفْتَرُونَ

## Introduction

There are errors so ugly that the Quran exposes them not only as false, but as morally deformed.

This is one of those passages.

It begins with people taking what Allah provided and allocating a portion of it to beings they do not truly know. Then it presses deeper into a more shameful contradiction: they assign daughters to Allah while reserving for themselves what they desire. And when one of them is told he has been given a daughter, his face darkens, he chokes inwardly, hides from people, and contemplates whether to keep her in humiliation or bury her in the dust.

[PAUSE]

The surah is not only condemning a pre-Islamic custom. It is exposing a whole diseased inner architecture: false theology, distorted value, selective honor, and a vision of the Hereafter so absent that the soul becomes capable of both lying about Allah and crushing the vulnerable.

Then Allah names the root with terrifying clarity: those who do not believe in the Hereafter carry **the likeness of evil**. As for Allah, His is the highest likeness.

The hook of the passage is this: **An-Nahl teaches that corrupted belief about Allah and corrupted treatment of human vulnerability spring from the same diseased heart, and that denial of the Hereafter produces a moral ugliness the Quran names as the likeness of evil itself.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Yaj'aluna Lima La Ya'lamuna Nasiban**: They Take Allah's Provision and Allocate It to What They Do Not Truly Know

The passage begins:

**wa yaj'aluna lima la ya'lamuna nasiban mimma razaqnahum**

They assign to what they do not know a share from what We provided them.

[PAUSE]

This is already devastating.

First, the provision is named as **mimma razaqnahum** - from what We provided them. So even before the critique begins, Allah reminds them that the wealth, animals, crops, or offerings in question are not self-originated.

Then the beings receiving those allotted shares are described as:

**lima la ya'lamun**

to what they do not know.

That phrase is sharper than simply saying "their idols." The Quran is exposing epistemic emptiness. They are structuring acts of reverence and allocation around entities they do not truly know in any meaningful sense as deserving lordship.

Then comes the oath:

**ta-Allahi la-tus'alunna 'amma kuntum taftarun**

By Allah, you will surely be questioned about what you used to fabricate.

[PAUSE]

`Taftarun` shows that this is not innocent symbolism. It is fabrication against Allah.

### 2. **Wa Yaj'aluna li-Allahi al-Banat ... wa Lahum Ma Yashtahun**: Their Theology Mirrors Their Desires

Then the surah intensifies the indictment:

**wa yaj'aluna li-Allahi al-banat**

They assign daughters to Allah.

Immediately:

**subhanahu**

Exalted is He.

[PAUSE]

The tasbih lands like a cleansing interruption. What they just said is too corrupt to be left standing even for a breath without declaring Allah far above it.

Then:

**wa lahum ma yashtahun**

and for themselves is what they desire.

This is one of the Quran's most humiliating exposures of hypocrisy. They give Allah the category they themselves disdain, while reserving for themselves what they most prefer.

Their theology is not accidental. It is shaped by their desires.

### 3. **Zalla Wajhuhu Muswaddan wa Huwa Kazim**: The Quran Paints the Moment of Moral Exposure on the Face

Then the scene becomes painfully human:

**wa idha bushshira ahaduhum bi-al-untha**

When one of them is given news of a female.

**zalla wajhuhu muswaddan**

his face remains darkened.

**wa huwa kazim**

and he is inwardly choking, suppressing rage and grief.

[PAUSE]

The Quran places the sickness on the face and in the chest. The outward discoloration and the inward pressure belong to the same disease.

Then:

**yatawara mina al-qawm**

he hides himself from the people.

He is ashamed, not because he has thought evil, but because he has received a daughter.

Then the ayah lets us hear the internal courtroom:

**a-yumsikuhu 'ala hunin am yadussuhu fi al-turab**

Will he keep her in humiliation, or bury her in the dust?

[PAUSE]

That is one of the Quran's most devastating questions. It reduces a whole social ethic to the point of moral horror. The vulnerable child is evaluated through shame, not mercy; through social optics, not truth; through pride, not trust in Allah.

And then the verdict:

**ala sa'a ma yahkumun**

Unquestionably evil is what they judge.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: What We Believe About Allah Leaks Into How We Treat the Weak

This passage refuses the split between theology and ethics.

They lie about Allah.

Then they mistreat daughters.

The Quran presents these as connected.

[PAUSE]

Why?

Because once a heart becomes comfortable fabricating about Allah, it becomes comfortable distorting value everywhere else. When the highest truth is manipulated, lower relationships soon follow.

And notice the direction of the harm. The strong fabricate. The weak suffer.

That remains true far beyond this historical form. Whenever people use religion to protect ego, honor hierarchy, or justify selective dignity, the distortion rarely lands first on the powerful. It lands on the vulnerable.

### Theme 2: Desire Can Secretly Be the Architect of False Theology

The verse says:

They assign daughters to Allah,

and for themselves is what they desire.

[PAUSE]

This should make us suspicious of our own religious instincts in the best possible way.

How often do people clothe preference in principle?

How often does the nafs move first, and then the intellect builds a respectable argument afterward?

Their theology was not a detached metaphysical system. It was organized around what they wanted for themselves and what they were unwilling to bear.

That is not an old pagan problem only. It is a human problem. We are always tempted to construct an image of religion that leaves us with the honors we desire, the fears we want avoided, and the sacrifices we do not want demanded.

The ayah teaches us to examine whether our beliefs have quietly become flattering arrangements of our appetites.

### Theme 3: Denial of the Hereafter Produces a Distinct Moral Ugliness

The final ayah does something enormous:

**lilladhina la yu'minuna bi-al-akhirati mathalu al-saw'**

For those who do not believe in the Hereafter is the likeness of evil.

[PAUSE]

The passage does not say only that they do evil things. It says they carry the likeness, pattern, or profile of evil.

Why connect this to the Hereafter?

Because belief in the Hereafter disciplines value. It teaches that honor is not what society applauds, that weakness is not worthlessness, that hidden cruelty will be accounted for, and that Allah's judgment is truer than the tribe's embarrassment.

When akhirah drops out, the soul becomes frighteningly available to distorted scales. Pride becomes rational. Violence becomes thinkable. Shame moves from sin to vulnerability. That is exactly what these ayahs show.

Then comes the opposite:

**wa li-Allahi al-mathalu al-a'la**

And for Allah is the highest likeness.

[PAUSE]

Allah is not measured by the ugly scales of people who deny Him and deny the next life. His names, attributes, and perfections stand above every degrading projection.

The ending with **al-'Aziz al-Hakim** is perfect. He is mighty enough that none of these lies diminish Him, and wise enough that every false judgment will be answered with its proper due.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs begin with fabricated allocations of provision and end with the highest theological contrast in the block. Between those two points the surah exposes a whole moral world.

People take Allah's رزق and assign a portion to what they do not truly know. They fabricate against Him. They assign daughters to Allah while reserving for themselves what they desire. Then the surah shows the fruit of that distorted value-system in human life: the father whose face darkens at the news of a daughter, who hides in shame and contemplates humiliation or burial. The verdict falls: evil is what they judge.

[PAUSE]

Then the whole scene is interpreted from above. Those who do not believe in the Hereafter carry the likeness of evil. Their ugliness is not accidental. It emerges from an unseen denial. But Allah's is the highest likeness. He is above their projections, above their desires, above their moral deformities. He is the Mighty, the Wise.

### Questions to Carry

- Where might desire be quietly shaping what I find easy to believe or hard to submit to?
- Do my beliefs about Allah make me more truthful and merciful toward the vulnerable, or do they leave ego untouched?
- What forms of social shame or honor have I absorbed that the Hereafter should have corrected?
- In what ways do I need belief in akhirah to purify how I judge worth, dignity, and responsibility?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs show that lying about Allah, valuing the vulnerable through ego and shame, and denying the Hereafter all belong to one diseased moral world, while Allah alone possesses the highest likeness beyond every false projection.**

### Du'a

*Allahumma, purify our hearts from false judgments, distorted desires, and every lie spoken against You. Make belief in the Hereafter alive enough in us that it reforms how we value every human being, especially the vulnerable, and keep us under the light of Your highest perfection.*
