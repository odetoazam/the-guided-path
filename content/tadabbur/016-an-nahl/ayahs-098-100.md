# Seek Refuge Before the Quran: Shaytan Has No Authority Over Trusting Believers

## Surah An-Nahl (16:98-100)

[ayah:16:98] فَإِذَا قَرَأْتَ ٱلْقُرْءَانَ فَٱسْتَعِذْ بِٱللَّهِ مِنَ ٱلشَّيْطَـٰنِ ٱلرَّجِيمِ

[ayah:16:99] إِنَّهُۥ لَيْسَ لَهُۥ سُلْطَـٰنٌ عَلَى ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ وَعَلَىٰ رَبِّهِمْ يَتَوَكَّلُونَ

[ayah:16:100] إِنَّمَا سُلْطَـٰنُهُۥ عَلَى ٱلَّذِينَ يَتَوَلَّوْنَهُۥ وَٱلَّذِينَ هُم بِهِۦ مُشْرِكُونَ

*Fa-idha qara'ta al-Qur'ana fa-sta'idh bi-Allahi mina al-shaytani al-rajim.*

*Innahu laysa lahu sultanun 'ala alladhina amanu wa 'ala rabbihim yatawakkalun.*

*Innama sultanuhu 'ala alladhina yatawallawnahu wa alladhina hum bihi mushrikun.*

**So when you recite the Quran, seek refuge with Allah from the accursed Shaytan. Surely he has no authority over those who believe and who place their trust in their Lord. His authority is only over those who take him as an ally and those who, through him, associate partners.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

The Quran does not only tell us what to read.

It teaches us how to arrive.

[PAUSE]

That matters because revelation is not encountered in a vacuum. We do not come to the Book as neutral minds floating above appetite, distraction, ego, resentment, woundedness, performance, or whispering. We come carrying an inner weather. And the ayah teaches that one of the first acts of honesty before the Quran is to admit that we need protection before we open our mouths.

This short passage is therefore far bigger than a formula of etiquette. It is a map of spiritual power. Seek refuge with Allah before the Quran. Why? Because Shaytan has no real authority over believers who lean their trust upon their Lord. His access is not universal and unstoppable. It concentrates where people grant him loyalty, follow him, and let their relationship with Allah be corrupted by him.

The hook of the passage is this: **An-Nahl teaches that approaching the Quran properly begins with fleeing to Allah from Shaytan, and that the devil's influence is not an unavoidable destiny but a relational consequence of whom the heart trusts, follows, and takes as ally.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Fa-Sta'idh bi-Allahi**: Refuge Before Recitation Is an Admission That Quran Reading Is a Spiritual Encounter

The opening command is simple:

**fa-idha qara'ta al-Qur'ana fa-sta'idh bi-Allahi**

When you recite the Quran, then seek refuge with Allah.

[PAUSE]

The command comes before the act of protected reading in our lived sequence, even if the wording is attached to the moment of recitation. The tafsir tradition here is clear: when the servant is about to read, he is taught to begin by taking shelter.

That alone teaches us something beautiful. The Quran is not approached with self-confidence. It is approached with isti'adhah.

`Ista'idh` is a request to enter safety, to take cover, to place oneself under protection. And that protection is not from generic distraction. It is specified:

**mina al-shaytani al-rajim**

from the accursed Shaytan.

`Al-rajim` carries the sense of one cast away, driven off, pelted, expelled from mercy. The reader is being taught to identify the enemy clearly and then to refuse facing him alone.

### 2. **Laysa Lahu Sultan**: Shaytan's Reach Is Not Sovereign

Then comes the reassurance:

**innahu laysa lahu sultanun 'ala alladhina amanu**

He has no `sultan` over those who believe.

`Sultan` is rich language. It can carry the sense of power, authority, sway, even proof or decisive leverage.

[PAUSE]

The ayah is not saying the believer will never be whispered to. It is saying Shaytan does not possess rightful mastery, binding proof, or uncontested dominion over the one whose core stance is iman.

That is spiritually crucial. Many people live as though temptation were fate and inner sabotage were inevitable rulership. This ayah breaks that psychological exaggeration. Shaytan is real, but he is not lord.

Then the verse adds the second description:

**wa 'ala rabbihim yatawakkalun**

and upon their Lord they rely.

Iman here is not left abstract. The believer is described by tawakkul. The heart does not only affirm Allah. It leans on Him.

### 3. **Yatawallawnahu ... Bihi Mushrikun**: Influence Deepens Where Loyalty Has Already Been Given Away

Then the contrast:

**innama sultanuhu 'ala alladhina yatawallawnahu**

His authority is only over those who take him as ally.

`Yatawallawnahu` is serious language. This is not a passing brush with temptation. It is a movement of alliance, turning toward, granting relational loyalty.

Then:

**wa alladhina hum bihi mushrikun**

and those who, through him, are associators.

[PAUSE]

The ayah exposes a terrifying progression. Shaytan's influence is not random. It grows where a person repeatedly gives him practical allegiance until that allegiance matures into forms of shirk, whether by obeying him against Allah, preferring his call over revelation, or allowing his whispers to reconfigure the heart's loyalties.

So the passage names two spiritual geographies:

one heart seeks refuge before recitation and trusts its Lord,
the other keeps granting the enemy more room and then wonders why the inner space feels occupied.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: We Often Underestimate How Serious Quran Recitation Really Is

Many of us approach the Quran with far less seriousness than we approach worldly meetings that matter to us.

We prepare for presentations.

We adjust our tone before important calls.

We rehearse before difficult conversations.

But when it comes to reciting the speech of Allah, we can drift in casually, unguarded, carrying noise, vanity, haste, old grudges, and the desire merely to "get through" the portion.

[PAUSE]

This command interrupts that carelessness.

It teaches that recitation is contested ground. Not because the Quran is weak, but because it is strong. Shaytan has a stake in how we hear it. He would rather the Quran pass our lips without entering the heart, our ears without producing humility, our mind without producing obedience. He is content even with religious performance so long as the recitation never becomes surrender.

That is why isti'adhah matters. It is not a decorative preface. It is the servant saying: "My Lord, I do not trust myself to meet Your words without Your protection."

And that makes the recitation more honest from the first breath.

### Theme 2: Shaytan's Authority Is Not Primarily a Story of His Strength, but of Human Alliance and Weak Trust

This passage is deeply empowering and deeply sobering at the same time.

It empowers because it says Shaytan has no `sultan` over those who believe and rely on their Lord. The believer is not spiritually abandoned. He is not defenseless if he is truly with Allah.

But it sobers because the next ayah says the devil's authority attaches to those who take him as ally.

[PAUSE]

That means a great deal of what we call being "overcome" is also about what we have repeatedly befriended.

Whispers become patterns.

Patterns become permissions.

Permissions become loyalties.

And loyalties slowly reshape worship.

So the ayahs call us back to responsibility without denying vulnerability. Yes, seek refuge. Yes, Shaytan is an enemy. But no, his influence is not some mysterious force detached from how the heart is living. Tawakkul reduces his reach. Alliance extends it.

That should change how we diagnose our own inner battles. Not only: "Why is Shaytan attacking me?" But also: "What doors am I still holding open for him? Where have I granted him recurring access by habit, indulgence, resentment, arrogance, or practical obedience?"

## Closing Synthesis

These three ayahs begin with the mouth and end with allegiance.

That too is exact.

The mouth opens to recite the Quran.

But what matters is which unseen relationship stands behind that recitation: refuge in Allah, or quiet friendship with the enemy.

[PAUSE]

So the architecture is simple and profound: seek refuge, remember the devil has no true authority over trusting believers, and fear becoming the kind of person who gradually hands him that authority by taking him as ally.

The Quran is not merely to be pronounced.

It is to be entered under protection.

### Questions to Carry

- Do I treat isti'adhah as a real act of refuge, or only as a memorized prelude?
- When I recite Quran, what inner states am I bringing with me unexamined?
- In what recurring habits or loyalties have I been giving Shaytan more access than I admit?
- What would stronger tawakkul on Allah look like in my actual spiritual battles?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs teach that proper recitation begins by fleeing to Allah from Shaytan, and that the devil's influence has no binding authority over trusting believers but grows where people repeatedly grant him loyalty.**

### Du'a

*Allahumma, make us people who enter Your Book under Your protection. Keep Shaytan far from our recitation, our hearing, and our hearts, strengthen our tawakkul upon You, and do not let us give our enemy the loyalty that belongs only to our Lord.*
