# Look at Its Fruit When It Bears

## Surah Al-Anam (6:95-99)

[ayah:6:95] إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ فَالِقُ ٱلْحَبِّ وَٱلنَّوَىٰ ۖ يُخْرِجُ ٱلْحَىَّ مِنَ ٱلْمَيِّتِ وَمُخْرِجُ ٱلْمَيِّتِ مِنَ ٱلْحَىِّ ۚ ذَٰلِكُمُ ٱللَّهُ ۖ فَأَنَّىٰ تُؤْفَكُونَ

## Introduction

Some ayat do not argue with us first. They retrain our eyes.

We rush past the world as if it were background. Dawn is just the hour before work. Night is just the time to collapse. Stars are hidden by city light. Fruit is something already washed and stacked under supermarket mist. Even our own existence becomes abstract. We know we came from somewhere, but we do not sit with the strangeness of being brought forth, held for a while, and then returned.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs slow us down and make us look again. Not at rare miracles, but at familiar things so familiar that we no longer feel their weight. A grain splits. A pit breaks. Dawn opens. Night settles. The sky becomes a map. Human life emerges from one shared origin and moves between holding places we barely understand. Rain descends, green shoots break open, fruit appears, fills, ripens. The Qur'an is not simply saying that Allah made these things. It is teaching us how creation is constantly speaking His names.

[PAUSE]

The hook of this passage is this: **heedlessness is not only forgetting the next world; it is walking through this world without really seeing the One who keeps bringing life out, opening things up, and guiding us through every darkness.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. Allah Is Named as the One Who Splits Things Open

The passage opens:

*Inna Allaha faliqu al-habbi wa al-nawa.*

Indeed Allah is the splitter of the grain and the kernel.

[PAUSE]

The word *faliq* comes from the root *f-l-q*: to split, to cleave open, to break something so that what was hidden inside emerges. The image is not of destruction but of release. A hard shell is broken so life can begin its visible journey.

Then the ayah immediately widens:

*Yukhriju al-hayya mina al-mayyiti wa mukhriju al-mayyiti mina al-hayy.*

He brings the living out of the dead, and brings the dead out of the living.

This is botanical and more than botanical. Seeds split. Birds emerge from eggs. New life emerges from what appears closed, still, or finished. And the reverse also remains true: what lives becomes what dies. The whole created order moves under His bringing-forth.

Then the ayah cuts through abstraction:

*Dhalikumu Allah.*

That is Allah.

[PAUSE]

Not merely: that is a process, that is nature, that is biology, that is seasonal rhythm. No. That is Allah.

Then comes the shock:

*Fa-anna tu'fakun.*

So how are you turned away?

The problem is not lack of signs. The problem is being diverted from the One to whom the signs keep pointing.

### 2. The Splitting of Grain Is Paired with the Splitting of Dawn

The next ayah begins with the same name-pattern:

*Faliqu al-isbah.*

Splitter of dawn.

The pairing is beautiful. The One who splits open the seed in the earth is the same One who splits open light across the sky. Hidden life in the soil and hidden light behind the horizon both break open by His command.

[PAUSE]

Then:

*Wa ja'ala al-layla sakanan.*

He made the night a repose, a settling-place, a stillness.

*Sakan* is not mere darkness. It is rest, quiet habitation, the easing of motion. Night is not a void we endure until morning. It is a mercy designed for the creature who cannot remain in constant outward expenditure.

Then:

*Wa al-shamsa wa al-qamara husbanan.*

The sun and the moon as *husbanan*.

This word carries the sense of precise reckoning, measured calculation, exact ordering. Creation is not only beautiful. It is calibrated. The great lights above us are not wandering ornaments. They are part of a measured order.

Then the conclusion:

*Dhalika taqdiru al-'Azizi al-'Alim.*

That is the determination of the Mighty, the Knowing.

Power and knowledge come together here. The order of creation is not brute force, and it is not cold information. It is measured by the One who cannot be overcome and who misses nothing.

### 3. The World Is Not Only Beautiful; It Is Meant to Guide

Then the surah says:

*Wa huwa alladhi ja'ala lakumu al-nujuma li-tahtadu biha fi zulumati al-barri wa al-bahr.*

He is the One who made for you the stars so that you may be guided by them through the darknesses of land and sea.

[PAUSE]

Notice the tenderness of *lakum* - for you. The stars are not mentioned here first as objects of wonder but as provisions of guidance.

And the Qur'an says *fi zulumati al-barri wa al-bahr* - the darknesses, plural, of land and sea. Darkness is layered. Confusion comes in more than one form. There is the darkness of not knowing where you are. There is the darkness of being unable to read what surrounds you. There is the darkness of vulnerability, exposure, fear.

Allah does not merely create a world. He places signs within it by which travelers may find their way.

Then comes the refrain:

*Qad fassalna al-ayati li-qawmin ya'lamun.*

We have detailed the signs for a people who know.

The signs are not sparse. They have been *fassalna* - laid out distinctly, clarified, articulated in detail. The shortage is rarely in signs. It is in seeing.

### 4. From Cosmic Guidance to Human Origin

Then the gaze moves from sky to self:

*Wa huwa alladhi ansha'akum min nafsin wahidah.*

He is the One who brought you forth from a single soul.

[PAUSE]

*Ansha'a* gives a sense of originating, causing to grow, bringing into developed existence. We did not author ourselves. We were brought forth.

And from where? From *nafsin wahidah* - a single soul. Whatever hierarchies we construct later, whatever arrogance we build on wealth, lineage, color, tribe, intellect, or power, this ayah quietly levels us all at the root.

Then:

*Fa-mustaqarrun wa mustawda'.*

Then a place of settling and a place of deposit.

The language is compact on purpose. Human life is passing through stations. There is somewhere you are held for a time, and somewhere you are placed in trust. The tafasir explore these as layers of earthly and post-earthly dwelling, but the spiritual effect is immediate even before one resolves every referent: you are not self-possessed. You are situated, carried, lodged for a while.

Then the refrain shifts slightly:

*Qad fassalna al-ayati li-qawmin yafqahun.*

We have detailed the signs for a people who understand deeply.

Knowledge becomes *fiqh*. The signs ask not only for observation, but for penetration beneath the surface.

### 5. The Passage Ends by Commanding Us to Look

Then comes the long unfolding of rain, growth, greenery, grain, palms, grapes, olives, and pomegranates:

*Fa-akhrajna bihi nabata kulli shay'.*

By it We brought forth the growth of everything.

Then:

*Fa-akhrajna minhu khadiran nukhriju minhu habban mutarakiban.*

We brought out from it green growth from which We bring forth layered grain.

The imagery is abundance, texture, sequence. Life comes out in stages. What looks simple from far away is intricate up close.

Then the ayah lingers over nearness:

*Qinwanun daniyah.*

Low-hanging clusters.

The provision is not only created. It is brought near.

Then the passage names fruits that are alike in some ways and unlike in others:

*Mushtabihan wa ghayra mutashabih.*

Resembling and not resembling.

Even within similarity, Allah places distinction. Uniformity is not the mark of divine creativity. Pattern and variation coexist.

Then the command:

*Unzuru ila thamarihi idha athmara wa yan'ihi.*

Look at its fruit when it bears and at its ripening.

[PAUSE]

This is the heart of the passage. Not merely eat it. Not merely use it. Look. Attend. Stay with the process from emergence to maturity.

The Qur'an here turns noticing into worship.

Then the close:

*Inna fi dhalikum la-ayatin li-qawmin yu'minun.*

Indeed in that are signs for a people who believe.

Iman is not only believing despite the world. It is believing through a world whose layers are read properly.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Allah Often Reveals Himself Through Openings

These ayahs are full of openings: grain split, dawn split, pathways through darkness, the emergence of human life, the breaking forth of green growth, the appearance of fruit.

That matters for the soul because we often think Allah is absent when we are still inside the closed shell. We judge by the hardness of the moment. We see confinement, delay, burial, darkness, pressure. But one of Allah's recurring signatures in creation is that He opens what looks sealed and brings forth what was hidden.

[PAUSE]

Sometimes a heart feels like a seed under soil: buried, compacted, unseen. Sometimes a life stage feels like night with no contour. Sometimes guidance feels impossible because every direction looks dark. These ayahs do not offer cheap reassurance. They train us to recognize a pattern in the Lord's action. He is *faliq*. He opens. He brings out. He guides through darknesses. He ripens what began as a hidden potential.

One way to sit with this is to ask: where in my life have I mistaken a shell for an ending, when Allah may be preparing it as a beginning?

### Theme 2: The Difference Between Consuming the World and Reading It

Most of us interact with creation mainly through use. We use time, food, land, weather, light, and our own bodies. The Qur'an keeps returning us to another posture: reading.

Night is not only for sleep; it is *sakan*. Stars are not only far away; they are signs of guidance. Human origin is not only biology; it is a reminder of shared dependence. Fruit is not only nourishment; it is an unfolding ayah from first bearing to ripening.

[PAUSE]

The modern habit is to master processes while becoming blind to meaning. We can explain pollination, photosynthesis, celestial mechanics, embryonic development, and agricultural cycles, yet still be spiritually illiterate before them. The Qur'an does not ask us to abandon explanation. It asks us not to let explanation become a veil.

So when was the last time we actually obeyed this command?

*Unzuru ila thamarihi idha athmara wa yan'ihi.*

Look at its fruit when it bears and at its ripening.

Not glance. Not extract. Look.

There is a kind of iman that grows not mainly through argument, but through disciplined noticing. Through standing still long enough for the ordinary to become transparent to Allah.

## Closing Synthesis

This passage begins with grain and ends with fruit, but in between it quietly remaps our whole existence.

The One who opens the seed opens the dawn. The One who orders the heavens guides the lost traveler. The One who originated us from a single soul knows every station in which we are lodged. The One who sends rain does not merely create provision; He stages an education in seeing. And after all of this, the question still remains over the distracted heart:

*Dhalikumu Allahu fa-anna tu'fakun.*

That is Allah. So how are you turned away?

[PAUSE]

Perhaps one of the simplest acts of repair is to slow down before what Allah keeps opening. Dawn. Night. Sky. Breath. Food. Fruit. Our own fragile life. To let the world stop being a wall of things and become, once again, ayat.

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the passage lands here: **the believer is not merely the one who knows that Allah created the world, but the one who has learned to look at the world until creation becomes guidance, provision becomes witness, and ordinary ripening becomes a path back to iman.**
