# Do Not Let the Majority Decide the Truth

## Surah Al-Anam (6:114-117)

[ayah:6:114] أَفَغَيْرَ ٱللَّهِ أَبْتَغِى حَكَمًا وَهُوَ ٱلَّذِىٓ أَنزَلَ إِلَيْكُمُ ٱلْكِتَـٰبَ مُفَصَّلًا ۚ وَٱلَّذِينَ ءَاتَيْنَـٰهُمُ ٱلْكِتَـٰبَ يَعْلَمُونَ أَنَّهُۥ مُنَزَّلٌ مِّن رَّبِّكَ بِٱلْحَقِّ ۖ فَلَا تَكُونَنَّ مِنَ ٱلْمُمْتَرِينَ

## Introduction

When pressure rises, one of the first things we start craving is another judge.

Someone who will tell us what is reasonable. Someone who will reassure us that the crowd cannot be that wrong. Someone who will soften the sharpness of revelation until it feels easier to inhabit. The soul under social pressure starts looking sideways for permission to dilute what it already knows.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs close that door. Why seek any judge besides Allah when He is the One who sent down the Book *mufassalan* - laid out in detail? Then the passage locks the believer's confidence in place by saying Allah's word is complete in truth and justice, impossible to alter. Only after that internal anchor is secured does the surah confront one of the greatest psychological forces in religion and public life: the majority. If you obey most of those on earth, the ayah says, they will lead you away from Allah's path, because large numbers often move by conjecture, not certainty.

[PAUSE]

The hook of this passage is this: **truth is not stabilized by numbers, mood, or social consensus, but by the completed word of Allah, which remains true and just even when the majority moves elsewhere.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Question of Judgment Is Settled at the Source

The passage begins:

*Afa-ghayra Allahi abtaghi hakaman?*

Shall I seek other than Allah as judge?

[PAUSE]

The verb *abtaghi* from *b-g-y* is to seek out, pursue, want as one's object. The point is not merely that another judge happens to appear. It is that the soul may go searching for one.

And the noun *hakaman* is not just any opinion-giver. It is an arbiter, one whose judgment settles matters.

Then the reason the question is absurd:

*Wa huwa alladhi anzala ilaykumu al-kitaba mufassalan.*

He is the One who sent down the Book detailed, distinguished, set out with clarity.

*Mufassalan* matters. This is not an indistinct word requiring the crowd to finish it for us. Allah already sent down a Book with clarified articulation.

Then the ayah adds supporting testimony:

*Wa alladhina ataynahumu al-kitaba ya'lamuna annahu munazzalun min rabbika bi-al-haqq.*

Those given scripture know it is sent down from your Lord in truth.

And then the personal command:

*Fa-la takunanna mina al-mumtarin.*

Do not be among the doubters.

This is not because the Prophet actually doubted, but because revelation often addresses the Messenger in a form that disciplines everyone listening through him.

### 2. Allah's Word Is Complete in Two Ways

Then comes one of the most balanced descriptions in the Qur'an:

*Wa tammat kalimatu rabbika sidqan wa 'adlan.*

The word of your Lord has been completed in truth and justice.

[PAUSE]

*Tammat* means completed, brought to fullness, perfected.

Then the two anchor terms:

*Sidqan wa 'adlan.*

Truth and justice.

Its reports are true. Its commands are just. Its descriptions do not lie. Its rulings do not oppress. These two words together give the believer epistemic and moral security at once.

Then:

*La mubaddila li-kalimatih.*

There is no changer of His words.

The revelation is not vulnerable to social override. No majority vote revises it. No emotional season edits it. No later pressure improves on it.

Then the names:

*Wa huwa al-Sami'u al-'Alim.*

The All-Hearing, the All-Knowing.

He hears what is said around His revelation and knows what is being done with it.

### 3. Majority Is Not a Criterion of Truth

Then the surah confronts a dangerous instinct:

*Wa in tuti' akthara man fi al-ard yudilluka 'an sabili Allah.*

If you obey most of those on earth, they will lead you away from the path of Allah.

[PAUSE]

The danger is not simply that "many people are wrong" in the abstract. The ayah specifically warns against *tuti'* - obeying, yielding, letting their direction become yours.

Then it tells us why:

*In yattabi'una illa al-zann.*

They follow nothing but conjecture.

*Zann* here is not disciplined inference. It is unreliable assumption standing where certainty should stand.

Then:

*Wa in hum illa yakhrusun.*

They do nothing but guess, estimate, and speak without solid knowledge.

*Yakhrusun* carries the sense of conjectural speech, speaking by approximation, sometimes even fabrication. The ayah exposes how much public certainty is built on private guesswork.

### 4. Final Knowledge of Guidance and Misguidance Belongs to Allah

Then the passage ends by returning the matter upward:

*Inna rabbaka huwa a'lamu man yadillu 'an sabilihi wa huwa a'lamu bi-al-muhtadin.*

Your Lord knows best who strays from His path, and He knows best the guided.

[PAUSE]

This closes two doors at once. It closes the door of crowd-definition, where numbers decide truth. And it closes the door of arrogant self-certification, where we imagine ourselves the ultimate readers of hearts.

Allah knows the astray. Allah knows the guided. The believer's job is fidelity, not replacing divine omniscience.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Social Consensus Exerts a Spiritual Pressure We Often Underestimate

Many people do not consciously reject revelation because they have disproved it. They simply absorb the atmosphere around them until the majority begins to feel like reality itself.

What most people normalize starts to feel obvious. What most people mock starts to feel embarrassing. What most people repeat starts to sound true by sheer repetition. These ayahs rescue the believer from that hypnosis.

[PAUSE]

The Qur'an is not telling us to become arrogant contrarians who assume the minority is always right. It is warning us against making mass acceptance into a source of authority. Numbers can describe prevalence. They cannot establish truth.

That is why the passage first anchors us in Allah's detailed Book and completed word. Without that anchor, "most people think" becomes spiritually devastating.

### Theme 2: Allah's Word Heals Two Deep Human Fears

The phrase *sidqan wa 'adlan* is enough to nourish the soul for a long time.

We fear being misled. We also fear being wronged.

Truth answers the first fear. Justice answers the second.

[PAUSE]

If Allah's word is *sidq*, then it will not deceive us about reality. If it is *'adl*, then it will not wrong us in command. So the servant can surrender without needing to hold back a hidden reserve of suspicion.

That may be one of the reasons modern hearts struggle so much with submission: we are afraid that if we yield, we will either be fooled or constrained unfairly. This ayah answers both anxieties at once. Allah's completed word is true and just.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs rebuild intellectual courage.

Do not go searching for another arbiter once Allah has sent down the detailed Book. Do not let numbers decide what revelation already settled. Do not imagine that repeated conjecture becomes truth through scale. And do not forget that Allah's word reaches completion in exactly the two ways the fearful soul most needs: truth and justice.

[PAUSE]

Perhaps one way to sit with this passage is to ask: where has the weight of "most people" quietly become more persuasive to me than the completed word of my Lord?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the passage lands here: **the believer stays upright when he stops outsourcing judgment to the crowd, trusts that Allah's word is already complete in truth and justice, and refuses to let widespread conjecture outrank revealed certainty.**
