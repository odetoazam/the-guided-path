# In Real Distress, You Call Only Him

## Surah Al-Anam (6:40-41)

[ayah:6:40] قُلْ أَرَءَيْتَكُمْ إِنْ أَتَىٰكُمْ عَذَابُ ٱللَّهِ أَوْ أَتَتْكُمُ ٱلسَّاعَةُ أَغَيْرَ ٱللَّهِ تَدْعُونَ إِن كُنتُمْ صَـٰدِقِينَ

[ayah:6:41] بَلْ إِيَّاهُ تَدْعُونَ فَيَكْشِفُ مَا تَدْعُونَ إِلَيْهِ إِن شَآءَ وَتَنسَوْنَ مَا تُشْرِكُونَ

*Qul a-ra'aytakum in atakum 'adhabu Allahi aw atatkumu al-sa'ah a-ghayra Allahi tad'un in kuntum sadiqin.*

*Bal iyyahu tad'un fa-yakshifu ma tad'una ilayhi in sha'a wa tansawna ma tushrikun.*

**Say, "Have you considered: if there came to you the punishment of Allah or there came to you the Hour - is it other than Allah you would call upon, if you should be truthful?" Rather, it is Him alone you call upon, and He removes that for which you called upon Him, if He wills, and you forget whatever you associate with Him.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

There are truths the soul argues against in comfort and confesses under pressure.

As long as life feels manageable, a person can distribute his trust widely.
He can speak as though many things hold him up.
He can give his fear, hope, dependence, and emotional surrender to a whole network of smaller gods.
He can even persuade himself that this scattered loyalty is normal.

[PAUSE]

But when real helplessness breaks in, something hidden is exposed.

That is the scene of these ayahs. The surah does not begin here with a theological essay. It asks a question. If Allah's punishment came to you, or if the Hour itself arrived, would you really call anyone besides Allah? If you are truthful, answer honestly. Then the Qur'an answers on behalf of the human fitrah: no. In the moment of genuine collapse, it is Him alone you call. And if He wills, He alone removes the harm. The partners once defended so confidently in ease are forgotten when reality becomes severe.

[PAUSE]

The hook of this passage is this: **shirk often survives in the artificial security of comfort, but real distress exposes that the heart knows, beneath all its evasions, that only Allah truly answers the desperate call.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Ayah Forces an Honest Answer Through Question

The passage begins:

*Qul a-ra'aytakum...*

Say: have you considered?

[PAUSE]

This is not a request for abstract speculation. It is a forcing device. The Qur'an makes the listener imagine himself inside an unavoidable scene and then answer from there rather than from borrowed slogans.

Then the two possibilities:

*In atakum 'adhabu Allahi aw atatkumu al-sa'ah.*

If Allah's punishment came to you, or if the Hour came to you.

One possibility is immediate punishment. The other is the final unveiling of all reality. Between them, the whole field of ultimate threat is covered.

Then the question sharpens:

*A-ghayra Allahi tad'un?*

Is it other than Allah that you would call upon?

The placement matters. *A-ghayra Allahi* comes forward in a way that makes the very possibility feel strange. Other than Allah? At that moment?

And then:

*In kuntum sadiqin.*

If you are truthful.

The ayah does not let a person hide in inherited claims. It presses him to answer according to what he would actually do when stripped of performance.

### 2. "Rather, Him Alone" Exposes the Hidden Knowledge of the Heart

Then comes the decisive correction:

*Bal iyyahu tad'un.*

Rather, it is Him alone you call.

[PAUSE]

*Bal* overturns the false possibility entirely. And *iyyahu* is emphatic. Him. Him alone. The ayah is naming the truth the soul discovers when illusions stop being emotionally serviceable.

This is one of the Qur'an's most penetrating arguments against shirk. It does not only say that Allah deserves exclusive devotion in theory. It shows that the heart itself knows where it runs when all supports collapse.

That instinctive flight in crisis is itself a witness. The question then becomes: if you know where ultimate dependence really belongs in terror, why do you distribute your dependence elsewhere in ease?

### 3. Removal Belongs to Him Too, and Even Relief Is Conditional on His Will

The verse continues:

*Fa-yakshifu ma tad'una ilayhi in sha'a.*

He then removes what you called upon Him about, if He wills.

[PAUSE]

The verb *yakshifu* is beautiful here. It carries the sense of uncovering, lifting away, removing what has settled over the person as affliction or constriction. Distress lies on the soul like a cover, and Allah is the One who can peel it back.

But the ayah also keeps adab intact:

*In sha'a.*

If He wills.

Even in desperate du'a, Allah is not turned into a mechanism. He is the Lord whose wisdom and will govern relief itself. We call upon Him because He alone hears and can remove, not because we control the timing or form of His answer.

### 4. The False Partners Are Forgotten in the Hour of Truth

Then the end of the ayah:

*Wa tansawna ma tushrikun.*

And you forget what you associate with Him.

[PAUSE]

This is devastating. What was once loudly defended becomes forgettable under real pressure.

The things treated as indispensable in comfort disappear from practical devotion in crisis. The heart stops performing its distributed theology. It drops the pose and runs to the One it actually knows can answer.

That forgetting is itself an exposure. It means the associated things were never truly ultimate to begin with. They were props of delusion, not real refuges.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Distress Reveals What Ease Allows Us to Hide

These ayahs invite a painful but honest self-reading.

What do I rely on when life feels stable?
What do I speak about as if it secures me?
What do I emotionally treat as decisive?

[PAUSE]

Many forms of subtle shirk survive because ordinary life gives them room. We rely on status, money, approval, networks, planning, competence, or other people in ways that slowly train the heart away from naked dependence upon Allah. We may still say the right things, but the lived center of trust starts drifting.

Then crisis comes, and suddenly the heart cuts through its own theater. It calls Allah. Not because it became scholarly in that moment. Because the fitrah underneath the distortion still knew.

That should humble us. It should also invite us to bring our comfort-life into line with what our distress-life accidentally confesses.

### Theme 2: Tawhid Is Proven Not Only in Worship but in Instinctive Refuge

One of the strongest lessons here is that tawhid is not merely a statement about formal ritual. It is about where the soul seeks refuge when stripped bare.

That is why this passage is so powerful. It reveals practical theology.

[PAUSE]

Whatever you run to instinctively when there is no time for image management tells the truth about your heart. The Qur'an is telling the mushrik: you already know, in the deepest emergency register of your being, that only Allah is the One who finally hears, can remove, and is worth calling upon.

For us, the challenge is gentler but no less real. Do our daily patterns of reliance match what our emergency instincts know? Or do we live with a split heart: one theology in danger and another in comfort?

### Theme 3: Even Relief Must Deepen Surrender, Not Restore Forgetfulness

There is another subtle lesson in the phrase *in sha'a*. Allah may remove what we call upon Him about, if He wills. That means every relief is both mercy and test.

When the pressure lifts, what happens next?

[PAUSE]

Do we remain with the Allah we called in helplessness?
Or do we quietly return to the same scattered loyalties as soon as life feels manageable again?

The danger is not only calling others in crisis. The danger is forgetting Allah once the crisis has passed, even after the crisis itself forced us to witness His exclusive nearness.

So these ayahs ask not only about the du'a of desperation, but about the integrity of life after relief. Real tawhid means that the sincerity of helplessness should become the sincerity of ordinary days too.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs are brief, but they cut straight to the center.

The Qur'an asks the denier what he would really do if punishment came or the Hour arrived.
It strips away theoretical answers and forces honesty.
Then it declares that in such a moment he would call Allah alone.
Allah alone, if He wills, would remove the distress.
And the false partners so loudly defended in ease would fall away from remembrance in the hour of genuine helplessness.

[PAUSE]

That is the mercy of the passage: it exposes a hidden witness inside the soul. Beneath habits of shirk, beneath distributed trust, beneath all the social and psychological structures that make false dependence feel normal, the heart still knows who the Lord is when the masks tear. The tragedy would be to wait for catastrophe to live by that knowledge. The gift is to bring that emergency clarity into our ordinary life now, so that the Allah we call in desperation is also the Allah we rely on in calm.

### Questions to Carry

1. What do my reflexes in real fear reveal about whom I actually believe can save, carry, and answer me?
2. Where has comfort allowed me to scatter my trust in ways that distress would immediately expose as false?
3. After relief comes, do I stay near Allah or quietly drift back to the same practical shirk of ordinary days?
4. What would it look like for my ease-life to match the tawhid my crisis-life already confesses?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs teach that real distress strips away false partners and exposes that the desperate heart already knows only Allah can truly be called upon and only He can remove what covers it.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, make the tawhid we confess in fear the tawhid we live by in ease. Do not let comfort scatter the trust that hardship gathers back to You. Teach our hearts to rely on You before distress forces them to, and let every answered call deepen our surrender rather than return us to forgetfulness. Amin.
