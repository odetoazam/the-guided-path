# Even If Every Sign Came

## Surah Al-Anam (6:109-111)

[ayah:6:109] وَأَقْسَمُوا۟ بِٱللَّهِ جَهْدَ أَيْمَـٰنِهِمْ لَئِن جَآءَتْهُمْ ءَايَةٌ لَّيُؤْمِنُنَّ بِهَا ۚ قُلْ إِنَّمَا ٱلْـَٔايَـٰتُ عِندَ ٱللَّهِ ۖ وَمَا يُشْعِرُكُمْ أَنَّهَآ إِذَا جَآءَتْ لَا يُؤْمِنُونَ

[ayah:6:110] وَنُقَلِّبُ أَفْـِٔدَتَهُمْ وَأَبْصَـٰرَهُمْ كَمَا لَمْ يُؤْمِنُوا۟ بِهِۦٓ أَوَّلَ مَرَّةٍ وَنَذَرُهُمْ فِى طُغْيَـٰنِهِمْ يَعْمَهُونَ

But most of them are ignorant.**

## Introduction

One of the oldest excuses of the resisting heart is this: I would believe, if only I had more proof.

If only something undeniable happened. If only the heavens split in front of me. If only I saw what others only describe. If only revelation came packaged in a way that removed all need for surrender. Then, surely, I would submit.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs expose that excuse with unsettling precision. The deniers swear with their strongest oaths that they would believe if a sign came. But Allah answers that signs belong to Him alone, and then the passage reveals something harder to accept: the issue is not merely what stands outside the person, but what has already happened inside him. Hearts and sights can be turned. A first refusal has consequences. So the surah goes to the furthest imaginable extreme: even if angels descended, the dead spoke, and every thing were gathered before them face to face, they still would not believe unless Allah willed.

[PAUSE]

The hook of this passage is this: **faith does not finally fail because truth lacked spectacle, but because a heart that repeatedly resists clarity becomes unable to receive even overwhelming signs as guidance.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Oath Sounds Stronger Than the Heart Behind It

The opening says:

*Wa aqsamu bi-Allahi jahda aymanihim.*

They swore by Allah with the utmost strain of their oaths.

[PAUSE]

*Aqsamu* is formal oath-taking. *Jahda aymanihim* intensifies it: the full force, effort, earnestness, and extremity of their swearing. Outwardly, it sounds emphatic. The rhetoric is heavy with certainty.

Then:

*La-in ja'at-hum ayatun la-yu'minunna biha.*

If a sign came to them, they would surely believe in it.

This is not modest hesitation. It is confident self-report. They claim to know exactly what would unlock faith in them.

Then Allah answers:

*Qul innama al-ayatu 'inda Allahi.*

Say: the signs are only with Allah.

The ayat are not bargaining chips in the hands of the crowd. They are not issued on demand. Their timing, form, and purpose belong to Allah alone.

### 2. The Real Question Is Not Whether a Sign Comes, but What the Heart Does With It

Then comes a startling line:

*Wa ma yush'irukum annaha idha ja'at la yu'minun.*

What will make you perceive that even if it came, they would not believe?

[PAUSE]

The verb *yush'irukum* from the root of awareness and perception cuts at assumption. What makes you so sure that the arrival of a sign would produce faith? The ayah breaks the easy causal chain between spectacle and submission.

That matters because we often imagine that certainty is mostly an evidential problem. The Qur'an agrees that signs matter deeply. But it refuses the naivete of thinking signs work mechanically on a resistant soul.

### 3. Hearts and Eyes Can Be Turned

Then Allah says:

*Wa nuqallibu af'idatahum wa absarahum kama lam yu'minu bihi awwala marratin.*

We turn over their hearts and their sights just as they did not believe in it the first time.

[PAUSE]

The verb *nuqallibu* comes from the root *q-l-b*, to turn, invert, flip, rotate. It is disturbing language because it tells us that repeated refusal is not static. It alters a person.

And notice the pairing:

*Af'idatahum wa absarahum.*

Their hearts and their sights.

Not only inner feeling, and not only external seeing. The whole interpretive apparatus of the human being is involved.

Then:

*Awwala marratin.*

The first time.

That phrase is weighty. There was an earlier moment of encounter, an initial opening, an original exposure to truth. The refusal of that first opening has consequences.

Then the ayah continues:

*Wa nadharuhum fi tughyanihim ya'mahun.*

And We leave them in their transgression, wandering blindly.

*Tughyan* is overflowing rebellion, crossing bounds. *Ya'mahun* is not merely walking in darkness but stumbling in bewildered blindness, unable to orient oneself rightly.

### 4. Even Maximal Spectacle Cannot Force Faith

Then the passage reaches its climax:

*Wa law annana nazzalna ilayhimu al-mala'ikata wa kallamahumu al-mawta wa hasharna 'alayhim kulla shay'in qubulan...*

Even if We sent down the angels to them, and the dead spoke to them, and We gathered before them every thing face to face...

[PAUSE]

The sequence is deliberate. A heavenly sign. A breached boundary with the dead. A total gathering of evidence before them. The ayah stacks impossibilities into a mountain.

Then comes the verdict:

*Ma kanu li-yu'minu illa an yasha'a Allah.*

They would not be ones to believe unless Allah willed.

This does not trivialize human responsibility. It returns us to tawhid at the level of guidance itself. No created sign independently manufactures iman. Faith is a grace Allah opens in a servant who does not keep hardening himself against the truth.

Then the closing diagnosis:

*Wa lakinna aktharahum yajhalun.*

But most of them are ignorant.

Their ignorance is not simply lack of information. It is ignorance of the nature of guidance, of themselves, and of how badly the resisting heart misunderstands its own condition.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: We Often Overestimate Spectacle and Underestimate Moral Refusal

The modern imagination is still tempted by the same illusion as the Quraysh: if only the evidence were more dramatic, belief would naturally follow.

But the Qur'an keeps insisting that the human problem is deeper than informational shortage. There is pride, appetite, inherited loyalty, fear of surrender, and love of moral autonomy. A person can stand before intense evidence while secretly seeking only a way not to bow.

[PAUSE]

That is why these ayahs are so unsettling. They tell us that faith is not simply the result of accumulating external force. If the heart has trained itself to resist the implications of truth, then even the extraordinary may be absorbed as one more occasion for refusal.

This should make us gentler in one direction and more afraid in another. Gentler, because we stop imagining that we can engineer guidance in people through sheer impressiveness. More afraid, because we begin to see that our own small refusals matter. Every ignored opening has a cost.

### Theme 2: The First Response to Truth Matters More Than We Think

The phrase *awwala marratin* deserves to be sat with slowly.

There is often a first moment when the truth comes near: the first time the ayah lands, the first time guilt clarifies, the first time tawbah feels possible, the first time a false attachment is exposed. In that moment, the heart is being invited. But if the person repeatedly pushes that moment away, the internal landscape changes.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the most serious realities in spiritual life. Refusal is not neutral. Delay is not always harmless. A heart can become harder, vision can become less trustworthy, and the soul can become more practiced in wandering.

So one way to sit with this passage is not mainly to ask why others do not believe. It is to ask: what have I done with the first clarities Allah already gave me?

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs dismantle the fantasy that salvation lies in one more dramatic proof.

Signs belong to Allah. Hearts are not machines. A first refusal can bend the entire inner life. And even the most overwhelming created evidence cannot substitute for the divine grace by which Allah opens a servant to faith.

[PAUSE]

That leaves us in a humbler and more urgent place. We ask Allah for guidance, because He alone gives it. We stop pretending that spectacle can do the work of surrender. And when truth first comes near, we do not toy with it.

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the passage lands here: **the soul is not saved by demanding bigger signs, but by receiving the signs already given before repeated refusal turns the heart and sight away from the very guidance they were asking for.**
