# No Sight Encloses Him

## Surah Al-Anam (6:100-104)

## Introduction

Human beings do not merely deny Allah. We shrink Him.

We make Him smaller than His creation, then start speaking about Him as though He were one being among other beings, needing lineage, surrounded by rival powers, explainable in creaturely terms, measurable by the same categories that govern us. The Qur'an treats that not only as doctrinal error but as a collapse in spiritual proportion.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs answer that collapse in layers. They expose the ugliness of speaking about Allah without knowledge. They answer fabricated kinship myths by naming Him *Badi' al-samawat wal-ard* - the utterly original originator of the heavens and the earth. They gather the whole argument into one creed sentence: He is your Lord, there is no god but Him, He is creator of every thing, so worship Him. Then the passage rises into one of the great lines of the surah: sight does not encompass Him, while He fully encompasses sight. Finally, the argument becomes personal. The *basa'ir* have already come. Now the question is whether we will see.

[PAUSE]

The hook of this passage is this: **tawhid is not only to say that Allah exists, but to let Him remain utterly unlike the creatures our minds keep trying to reduce Him to, and then to receive revelation as the sight-correction that heals our inner vision.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. Fabrication Begins Where Knowledge Ends

The opening is brutal:

*Wa ja'alu lillahi shuraka'a al-jinna wa khalaqahum.*

They made for Allah partners from the jinn, though He created them.

[PAUSE]

The contradiction is exposed in a few words. How can a created being stand beside the Creator as a partner when it exists only because He created it?

Then:

*Wa kharaqu lahu banina wa banatin bi-ghayri 'ilm.*

They fabricated for Him sons and daughters without knowledge.

The verb *kharaqu* carries the sense of inventing, tearing out, forging something false onto reality. It is not honest confusion. It is reckless attribution.

And the ayah names the disease plainly:

*Bi-ghayri 'ilm.*

Without knowledge.

That phrase matters. Much corruption in religion is not produced by careful rebellion. It is produced by speaking about Allah beyond what one knows.

Then the corrective:

*Subhanahu wa ta'ala 'amma yasifun.*

Exalted is He and far above what they describe.

*Subhan* is tanzih, declaring Allah free of every unworthy attribution. *Ta'ala* intensifies transcendence: He is high above these creaturely descriptions.

### 2. Badi' Breaks the Creaturely Analogy

Then comes one of the most arresting divine descriptions in the surah:

*Badi'u al-samawati wa al-ard.*

The marvelous originator of the heavens and the earth.

[PAUSE]

The root *b-d-'* carries the sense of bringing forth in an unprecedented way, originating without a prior model. Allah is not a maker working with inherited material inside an already existing order. He is the one from whom the order itself begins.

Then the argument:

*Anna yakunu lahu waladun wa lam takun lahu sahibah?*

How could He have a child when He has had no consort?

The Qur'an is dismantling creature-thinking by refusing false analogy. Children belong to the conditions of created life, dependence, pairing, sequence, and kind. Allah is not entered through those categories at all.

Then the ayah closes the door completely:

*Wa khalaqa kulla shay'. Wa huwa bi-kulli shay'in 'alim.*

He created every thing, and He is knowing of every thing.

If everything besides Him is created, then any supposed child, partner, or rival would also be inside the domain of His creating. The claim collapses under its own absurdity.

### 3. Theology Becomes Worship

Then the passage condenses creed into command:

*Dhalikumu Allahu rabbukum. La ilaha illa huwa. Khaliqu kulli shay'in fa'buduhu.*

That is Allah, your Lord. There is no god except Him. Creator of every thing, so worship Him.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the Qur'an's constant moves: true description of Allah is never left in abstraction. It ends in *fa'buduhu* - therefore worship Him.

If He alone is Lord, if He alone is God, if He alone creates every thing, then worship is not one option among many. It is the only fitting response.

Then:

*Wa huwa 'ala kulli shay'in wakil.*

He is over every thing as guardian, disposer, caretaker, trustee.

The soul does not need rival protectors once it understands *wakil* properly. Allah is enough over what He made.

### 4. Vision Has a Limit; Allah Does Not

Then comes the towering line:

*La tudrikuhu al-absar wa huwa yudriku al-absar.*

Sight does not encompass Him, while He encompasses sight.

[PAUSE]

The verb *tudrikuhu* is stronger than simply "see." It carries the sense of fully catching up to, grasping, comprehending, enclosing in perception. The ayah does not negate Allah's reality; it negates creaturely enclosure of Him.

Human sight is bounded. Allah is not.

And then the reversal:

*Wa huwa yudriku al-absar.*

He fully encompasses sight.

He knows vision from above it, within it, beyond it. He knows what eyes see, what they miss, what they seek, and what they avoid.

Then the names:

*Wa huwa al-Latifu al-Khabir.*

The Subtle, the All-Aware.

*Latif* carries delicacy, hidden penetration, a mode of care and knowledge too fine for coarse perception. *Khabir* is the one with intimate awareness of realities from within. The names fit the ayah perfectly. The One we cannot encompass is precisely the One whose knowing penetrates every inward and outward subtlety.

### 5. Revelation Comes as Inner Eyesight

Then the passage lands in human responsibility:

*Qad ja'akum basa'iru min rabbikum.*

Clear inner sights, perceptive evidences, eye-opening insights have come to you from your Lord.

[PAUSE]

This is stronger than information. *Basa'ir* are the kinds of signs that give sight to the heart.

Then:

*Fa-man absara fa-li-nafsih. Wa man 'amiya fa-'alayha.*

Whoever sees, it is for his own soul; and whoever goes blind, it is against it.

Seeing here is moral and spiritual reception, not just optical function. The benefit rebounds to the soul itself. Blindness harms the self before it harms anyone else.

And the Messenger is told to end with boundary:

*Wa ma ana 'alaykum bi-hafiz.*

I am not over you a keeper.

The Prophet delivers *basa'ir*. He does not control acceptance.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Most Theological Corruption Begins by Making Allah Creature-Sized

Why do human beings keep producing distorted speech about Allah? Because we are constantly tempted to think upward from ourselves instead of receiving Him as He names Himself.

We know kinship, so we imagine divine kinship. We know protection through networks, so we imagine divine partners. We know beings through categories of gender, reproduction, sequence, and visibility, so we carry those habits into theology. The Qur'an breaks that habit sharply. Allah is *Badi'*. He is not one item inside the chain of originated things. He is the one from whom the chain begins.

[PAUSE]

That matters beyond formal creed. Any time we imagine Allah as if He were basically like us but bigger, our worship shrinks. Our du'a shrinks. Our awe shrinks. Our trust shrinks. We start negotiating with Him as though He were another actor inside the same limited field we inhabit.

The ayah restores proportion. No sight encloses Him. No analogy masters Him. No fabricated story explains Him. We know Him only because He lets Himself be known.

### Theme 2: Revelation Does for the Heart What Eyes Cannot Do Alone

There is a striking movement here. First the passage tells us that sight cannot encompass Allah. Then immediately it tells us that *basa'ir* have come from your Lord.

That means revelation is not a replacement for reality; it is the correction of how we perceive reality. The eye sees forms. Revelation teaches meaning. The eye sees the world. Revelation teaches what the world is saying.

[PAUSE]

And the responsibility is personal:

*Fa-man absara fa-li-nafsih.*

Whoever truly sees, it is for himself.

No one can see on your behalf. No one can outsource your surrender. No one can inherit inner vision for you by proximity.

This is especially important in religious environments where people can become fluent in inherited language without actual sight. We may know the formulas, repeat the creeds, defend the arguments, and yet not receive the *basa'ir* as living clarity from our Lord. The Qur'an presses us further. Has revelation actually made us see?

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs begin with false descriptions and end with true sight.

In between, the surah rebuilds the whole map. Allah is free of fabricated kinship. He is the original originator of the heavens and the earth. He alone creates every thing, so He alone deserves worship. He cannot be enclosed by sight, while all sight is enclosed by Him. And because He knows our perceptual poverty, He sent *basa'ir* from our Lord.

[PAUSE]

So the question is no longer whether the signs are there. The question is whether we will let them heal our vision.

Perhaps one way to sit with this passage is to ask: where have I quietly reduced Allah to categories small enough for my mind to manage, and where is revelation trying to break those limits and give me real sight again?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the passage lands here: **tawhid matures when we stop trying to contain Allah inside creaturely imagination, let His transcendence humble us, and receive revelation as the inner eyesight by which the soul finally learns to see truly.**
