# Your Lord Is Possessed of Vast Mercy

## Surah Al-Anam (6:145-147)

[ayah:6:145] قُل لَّآ أَجِدُ فِى مَآ أُوحِىَ إِلَىَّ مُحَرَّمًا عَلَىٰ طَاعِمٍ يَطْعَمُهُۥٓ إِلَّآ أَن يَكُونَ مَيْتَةً أَوْ دَمًا مَّسْفُوحًا أَوْ لَحْمَ خِنزِيرٍ فَإِنَّهُۥ رِجْسٌ أَوْ فِسْقًا أُهِلَّ لِغَيْرِ ٱللَّهِ بِهِۦ ۚ فَمَنِ ٱضْطُرَّ غَيْرَ بَاغٍ وَلَا عَادٍ فَإِنَّ رَبَّكَ غَفُورٌ رَّحِيمٌ

[ayah:6:146] وَعَلَى ٱلَّذِينَ هَادُوا۟ حَرَّمْنَا كُلَّ ذِى ظُفُرٍ ۖ وَمِنَ ٱلْبَقَرِ وَٱلْغَنَمِ حَرَّمْنَا عَلَيْهِمْ شُحُومَهُمَآ إِلَّا مَا حَمَلَتْ ظُهُورُهُمَآ أَوِ ٱلْحَوَايَآ أَوْ مَا ٱخْتَلَطَ بِعَظْمٍ ۚ ذَٰلِكَ جَزَيْنَـٰهُم بِبَغْيِهِمْ ۖ وَإِنَّا لَصَـٰدِقُونَ

[ayah:6:147] فَإِن كَذَّبُوكَ فَقُل رَّبُّكُمْ ذُو رَحْمَةٍ وَٰسِعَةٍ وَلَا يُرَدُّ بَأْسُهُۥ عَنِ ٱلْقَوْمِ ٱلْمُجْرِمِينَ

*Qul la ajidu fi ma uhiya ilayya muharraman 'ala ta'imin yat'amuhu illa an yakuna maytatan aw daman masfuhan aw lahma khinzirin fa-innahu rijsun aw fisqan uhilla li-ghayri Allahi bihi. Fa-mani idturra ghayra baghin wa la 'adin fa-inna rabbaka ghafurun rahim.*

*Wa 'ala alladhina hadu harramna kulla dhi zufurin wa mina al-baqari wa al-ghanami harramna 'alayhim shuhumahoma illa ma hamalat zuhuruhuma awi al-hawaya aw ma ikhtalata bi-'azmin. Dhalika jazaynahum bi-baghyihim wa inna la-sadiqun.*

*Fa-in kadhdhabuka fa-qul rabbukum dhu rahmatin wasi'atin wa la yuraddu ba'suhu 'ani al-qawmi al-mujrimin.*

**Say, "I do not find within what has been revealed to me anything forbidden to one who would eat it unless it be dead meat, or spilled blood, or the flesh of swine - for indeed it is defilement - or a sinful offering dedicated to other than Allah. But whoever is compelled, neither desiring nor transgressing, then indeed your Lord is Forgiving and Merciful." And upon those who were Jews We forbade every animal having an undivided hoof; and of the cattle and the sheep We forbade to them their fat, except what their backs carry, or the entrails, or what is mixed with bone. That We repaid them for their rebellion, and indeed We are truthful. So if they deny you, then say, "Your Lord is possessed of vast mercy, but His punishment will not be turned away from the criminal people."**

## Introduction

After several ayahs exposing made-up prohibitions, the surah finally says: here is what was actually forbidden.

That shift matters. The Qur'an does not merely critique religious fabrication in the abstract. It replaces fabrication with revelation. It does not leave us in suspicion toward all boundaries. It teaches us which boundaries are truly from Allah, which ones were imposed on a specific community as recompense, and how mercy remains present even where law and punishment are being spoken about.

[PAUSE]

The first ayah names a restrained set of forbidden foods in what was revealed to the Prophet. Then it immediately opens a door for real human necessity: whoever is compelled, without rebellious desire or transgressing the need, finds Allah Forgiving and Merciful. The next ayah distinguishes this from particular restrictions imposed on the Jews because of their *baghy* - their wrongdoing and rebellion. Then the passage closes with a sentence that holds two seemingly opposite truths together without letting either cancel the other: your Lord is possessed of vast mercy, and His punitive force is not turned away from the criminal people.

[PAUSE]

The hook of this passage is this: **Allah's law is neither the cruelty of invented restriction nor the chaos of no restriction at all; it is truthful boundary held inside divine mercy, with room for necessity and with justice calibrated to real transgression.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. Revelation Names the Boundary With Restraint, Not With Endless Severity

The passage opens:

*Qul la ajidu fi ma uhiya ilayya muharraman...*

Say: I do not find in what has been revealed to me anything forbidden...

[PAUSE]

This is a striking way to begin. The tone is restrained. The Prophet is not told to speak as though everything is dangerous until proven otherwise. He is told to state what revelation actually forbids.

Then the ayah names the known categories: carrion, spilled blood, swine flesh, and what has been consecrated to other than Allah. The structure matters because it restores sobriety to the conversation. After so many human inventions in the preceding verses, revelation narrows the field back down to what Allah actually named.

One phrase stands out:

*Daman masfuhan.*

Spilled blood.

The ayah is specific. This is not vague ritual panic. It is named with precision, as are the other categories.

Then:

*Fa-innahu rijsun aw fisqan uhilla li-ghayri Allahi bihi.*

For indeed it is filth, or a sinful offering dedicated to other than Allah.

So some prohibitions are about impurity, and some are about violated allegiance. Food is never just food in the Qur'an. It also reveals whose name governs our receiving.

### 2. Necessity Does Not Cancel the Law, But Mercy Meets the Constrained Person Inside It

Then comes one of the most merciful legal clauses in the Qur'an:

*Fa-mani idturra ghayra baghin wa la 'adin...*

Whoever is compelled, neither desiring nor transgressing...

[PAUSE]

The verb of compulsion acknowledges human extremity. Circumstances can tighten around a person until survival itself becomes the pressing fact. But even here the ayah guards the heart: not out of rebellious craving, not by stepping beyond what the necessity actually requires.

Then:

*Fa-inna rabbaka ghafurun rahim.*

Indeed your Lord is Forgiving, Merciful.

Law is still there. Need is still real. And mercy is not absent from either.

### 3. Not Every Restriction Is Universal; Some Were a Recompense for Rebellion

Then the surah clarifies another crucial issue:

*Wa 'ala alladhina hadu harramna...*

And upon those who were Jews We forbade...

[PAUSE]

This is an important distinction. The surah is teaching us not to flatten every restriction into a universal default. Some limits belonged to a specific people under a specific history.

Then after the details comes the key:

*Dhalika jazaynahum bi-baghyihim.*

That We repaid them for their rebellion.

The restriction here is not random dietary complexity. It is a recompense tied to wrongdoing. That protects us from confusing punitive history with the original generosity of Allah's provision.

Then:

*Wa inna la-sadiqun.*

And indeed We are truthful.

The divine speech is underlining that this account is exact. No distortion. No fabrication. This is revelation telling the truth about why these limits were there.

### 4. Vast Mercy and Unstoppable Punishment Stand Side by Side

Then comes the close:

*Fa-in kadhdhabuka fa-qul rabbukum dhu rahmatin wasi'ah.*

If they deny you, then say: your Lord is possessed of vast mercy.

[PAUSE]

Not small mercy. Not occasional mercy. Vast mercy. Mercy with breadth, room, extension, reach.

Then immediately:

*Wa la yuraddu ba'suhu 'ani al-qawmi al-mujrimin.*

And His punishment is not turned away from the criminal people.

This pairing is spiritually essential. Some people hear mercy and imagine impunity. Others hear punishment and imagine harshness without gentleness. The Qur'an gives neither distortion room to survive. Allah's mercy is vast. His punishment is real. And the criminal people should not treat His mercy as protection for their جرم.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Real Piety Does Not Invent Hardship Beyond What Allah Revealed

One of the great lessons of this passage is that religion is not made holier by being made harsher.

The mushrikin had been inventing prohibitions. The Qur'an does not answer them by abolishing all boundaries. It answers them by naming the true boundaries. That distinction matters deeply. We are not more faithful when we are stricter than revelation. We are more faithful when we stop exactly where Allah stopped, and we fear lying about Him either by permitting what He forbade or forbidding what He permitted.

[PAUSE]

This should make us suspicious of religious environments that constantly generate extra anxieties and extra taboos without evidence. It should also make us grateful that revelation is not chaotic. Allah did not leave us to appetite, but He also did not surrender us to superstition.

### Theme 2: Divine Mercy Is Vast, But It Must Not Be Recruited Into Denial

The final ayah is one of those balances the heart needs again and again.

Some people only know how to speak of Allah in terms of punishment, and so religion becomes suffocating. Others only want to speak of mercy, and so religion becomes morally weightless. But the ayah refuses both reductions. *Dhu rahmatin wasi'ah.* Vast mercy. *Wa la yuraddu ba'suhu.* Punishment not turned away.

[PAUSE]

This means the believer lives in a serious hope. We do not despair when we are weak, compelled, cornered, or returning. Verse 145 has already shown us that Allah meets necessity with mercy. But we also do not weaponize that mercy against the truth. Persistent denial, جرم, and rebellion should not expect divine gentleness to function as a shield for ongoing corruption.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs restore moral proportion after the chaos of invented religion.

Allah names the real prohibitions. He makes room for the compelled person without turning emergency into appetite. He explains that some restrictions were a recompense for rebellion, not the original shape of provision. And when denial persists, He reminds the Messenger to speak with both truths intact: your Lord's mercy is vast, and His punishment does reach the criminal.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask: do I imagine piety as stricter than revelation, or mercy as softer than justice, instead of letting Allah define both?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of the passage lands here: **Allah's revealed limits are truthful and measured, His mercy reaches the sincerely constrained, and His justice still stands against criminal persistence, so the heart is asked to live neither in fabricated severity nor in presumptuous ease, but in obedient trust.**
