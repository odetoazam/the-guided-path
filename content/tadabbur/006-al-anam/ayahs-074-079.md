# I Have Turned My Face to the One Who Split Open the Heavens

## Surah Al-Anam (6:74-79)

[ayah:6:74] ۞ وَإِذْ قَالَ إِبْرَٰهِيمُ لِأَبِيهِ ءَازَرَ أَتَتَّخِذُ أَصْنَامًا ءَالِهَةً ۖ إِنِّىٓ أَرَىٰكَ وَقَوْمَكَ فِى ضَلَـٰلٍ مُّبِينٍ

## Introduction

After more than seventy ayahs of argument, Al-An’am suddenly gives the argument a human face.

Not a prophet repeating inherited slogans.
Not a man hiding inside tribal consensus.
But Ibrahim, standing inside a world full of visible objects of awe and refusing to let awe become worship.

[PAUSE]

The passage begins at home, with his father and idols. Then Allah shows him the *malakut* of the heavens and the earth so he may be among the people of certainty. Then night falls, and the sequence begins: star, moon, sun. Each one appears radiant enough to tempt the soul that wants something visible to bow to. Each one also sets. And that single fact becomes devastating. What disappears cannot be Lord. What withdraws cannot be the One on whom all things depend. By the end, Ibrahim is no longer merely rejecting false gods. He is positively turned: face, direction, and devotion aimed toward the One who *fatara* the heavens and the earth.

[PAUSE]

The hook of this passage is this: **Ibrahim reaches tawhid by following impermanence all the way to its conclusion, until every setting thing is disqualified and only the One who originated all things remains worthy of the heart's full turning.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Story Begins with a Refusal to Let Idols Hide Behind Normalcy

The first scene is direct:

*A-tattakhidhu asnaman alihah?*

Do you take idols as gods?

[PAUSE]

The question is destabilizing because it drags the practice out into the open. *Asnam* are carved objects, formed things, made things. *Tattakhidhu* suggests active adoption, taking, appointing. So Ibrahim is forcing the absurdity to appear in daylight: you are taking what is made and treating it as divine.

Then he says:

*Inni araka wa qawmaka fi dalalin mubin.*

I see you and your people in manifest error.

*Dalal mubin* is not slight miscalculation. It is clear deviation, visible wandering. The ayah begins by refusing the politeness that treats shirk as an alternative path of equal dignity.

### 2. Allah Shows Ibrahim the Kingdom Behind the Visible World

Then:

*Wa kadhalika nuri Ibrahima malakuta al-samawati wa al-ard.*

Thus We show Ibrahim the kingdom of the heavens and the earth.

[PAUSE]

*Malakut* is an intensified word from the field of dominion and sovereignty. Not merely the existence of the heavens and earth, but the deeper reality of their ownedness, governedness, and creatureliness under Allah.

And why?

*Wa li-yakuna mina al-muqinin.*

So that he may be among the people of certainty.

The story is not a tale of cleverness alone. Certainty is also shown, granted, deepened by Allah's opening of vision.

### 3. The Setting of the Star Becomes a Criterion

Then the night scene:

*Fa-lamma janna 'alayhi al-layl ra'a kawkaban.*

When the night covered him, he saw a star.

[PAUSE]

The night does not simply arrive. *Janna* carries covering. Darkness wraps the scene, and inside that covering a bright object becomes striking.

Then Ibrahim says:

*Hadha rabbi.*

This is my lord.

The passage is staging the argument from inside the psychology of idolization. What shines in the dark easily tempts attachment.

But then:

*Fa-lamma afala qala la uhibbu al-afilin.*

When it set, he said: I do not love those that set.

*Afala* is the key verb here: to disappear, go down, sink out of sight. Ibrahim does not merely say, "This is logically weak." He says, "I do not love those that set." That is deeper than syllogism. The heart itself is being educated. True lordship cannot belong to what departs.

### 4. Moon and Sun Intensify the Argument, Not Replace It

Then the scene escalates:

*Fa-lamma ra'a al-qamara bazighan...*

When he saw the moon rising.

Then:

*Fa-lamma ra'a al-shamsa bazighatan... hadha akbar.*

When he saw the sun rising... this is greater.

[PAUSE]

*Bazigh* suggests emerging, breaking forth, appearing with force. Each object seems more persuasive than the last. The argument is not random celestial sightseeing. It is an ascent through stronger and stronger candidates from the point of view of a soul trained to worship what dazzles.

And yet the criterion remains the same: they set.

The moon sets.
The sun sets.

The stronger the candidate, the more devastating its failure becomes.

By the time the sun disappears, the whole architecture collapses.

Then Ibrahim says:

*Ya qawmi inni bari'un mimma tushrikun.*

O my people, I am free from what you associate.

This is now open dissociation. He does not merely reject one object. He severs himself from the entire logic of shirk.

### 5. Tawhid Is Described as Turning the Face

Then the culmination:

*Inni wajjahtu wajhiya lilladhi fatara al-samawati wa al-ard hanifan.*

I have turned my face toward the One who split open the heavens and the earth, inclining uprightly.

[PAUSE]

The doubled language of *wajjahtu wajhiya* is powerful. I turned my face. This is not abstract assent. It is directional totality. The face is where attention, identity, and orientation gather.

Then the verb:

*Fatara.*

Not merely created in a generic sense, but originated, split open, brought forth with primal force. Ibrahim does not land on a bigger object inside the cosmos. He lands on the One who originated the cosmos.

Then:

*Hanifan.*

Inclining away from crookedness into upright devotion. Hanifiyyah is not neutral monotheism. It is a decisive bend away from every rival and toward Allah alone.

And the ending seals it:

*Wa ma ana mina al-mushrikin.*

I am not of those who associate partners.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Heart Is Always in Danger of Worshipping What Shines in Its Darkness

This story is not just about ancient star-worshipers.

It is about the permanent temptation to let what appears luminous in our darkness become functionally lord over us.

[PAUSE]

When we are confused, lonely, frightened, ambitious, or impressed, many things can begin to glow with false ultimacy. A person. A career. A movement. A public image. A desire. A source of power. A system of meaning that gives immediate emotional lift.

The Qur'an does not deny that these things can appear bright. The star really shines. The moon really rises. The sun really is greater. The issue is not whether they can impress. The issue is whether they can stay. Whether they can sustain. Whether they can be Lord.

Ibrahim teaches the soul a ruthless question: what happens when this sets? If the thing loses its power, leaves your life, stops answering, fades from your horizon, or proves itself creaturely, what then? Anything that can set cannot be your final trust.

### Theme 2: Tawhid Is Not Only Rejecting False Gods, but Reorienting the Self

The end of the passage matters as much as the collapse of the false candidates.

Ibrahim does not stop at "these are not gods."
He says: *wajjahtu wajhiya*.

[PAUSE]

This is crucial. Many people know how to deconstruct false absolutes intellectually, but their faces are still not turned. They are skeptical without being surrendered. Disenchanted without being devoted. The Qur'anic goal is not bare negation. It is reorientation.

And that reorientation must be *hanifan*: a real bending away from crooked rivalries. The heart cannot remain facing everything at once. It has to turn.

So the story invites us to ask not only what false lords we have intellectually outgrown, but where our faces are actually pointed now. What organizes our awe? What governs our longing? What do we instinctively treat as too important to lose?

## Closing Synthesis

In these ayahs, Ibrahim becomes the embodied argument of Al-An’am.

He confronts idols at home.
Allah shows him the deeper kingdom of the heavens and earth.
The star rises and sets.
The moon rises and sets.
The sun rises and sets.
Every candidate impressive enough to seduce the eye is disqualified by impermanence.
Then Ibrahim openly severs himself from shirk and turns his whole face toward the One who originated the heavens and the earth in upright devotion.

[PAUSE]

That is how tawhid is born here: not from inherited passivity, but from watching the world carefully enough to realize that everything inside it is too contingent to be Lord. The setting of things is not a side detail. It is the argument. And when the argument finishes, the only faithful human response is to turn your face wholly toward the One who never sets.

### Questions to Carry

1. What in my life shines strongly enough that I am tempted to treat it as more ultimate than it really is?
2. Where is my face actually turned when I strip away my slogans and look at what governs my awe, fear, and hope?
