# The Owner Who Wrote Mercy

## Surah Al-Anam (6:12-13)

[ayah:6:12] قُل لِّمَن مَّا فِى ٱلسَّمَـٰوَٰتِ وَٱلْأَرْضِ ۖ قُل لِّلَّهِ ۚ كَتَبَ عَلَىٰ نَفْسِهِ ٱلرَّحْمَةَ ۚ لَيَجْمَعَنَّكُمْ إِلَىٰ يَوْمِ ٱلْقِيَـٰمَةِ لَا رَيْبَ فِيهِ ۚ ٱلَّذِينَ خَسِرُوٓا۟ أَنفُسَهُمْ فَهُمْ لَا يُؤْمِنُونَ

[ayah:6:13] ۞ وَلَهُۥ مَا سَكَنَ فِى ٱلَّيْلِ وَٱلنَّهَارِ ۚ وَهُوَ ٱلسَّمِيعُ ٱلْعَلِيمُ

*Qul liman ma fi al-samawati wa al-ard. Qul lillah. Kataba 'ala nafsihi al-rahmah. La-yajma'annakum ila yawmi al-qiyamati la rayba fihi. Alladhina khasiru anfusahum fa-hum la yu'minun.*

*Wa lahu ma sakana fi al-layli wa al-nahar. Wa huwa al-sami'u al-'alim.*

**Say, "To whom belongs whatever is in the heavens and the earth?" Say, "To Allah." He has written mercy upon Himself. He will surely gather you to the Day of Resurrection, about which there is no doubt. Those who have lost their own selves are the ones who do not believe. And to Him belongs whatever settles in the night and the day, and He is the All-Hearing, the All-Knowing.**

## Introduction

There are moments when the Qur'an does not argue by multiplying details. It argues by asking one question so large that everything false has nowhere left to stand.

That is what happens here.

To whom belongs whatever is in the heavens and the earth?

Once that question is answered truthfully, a whole chain of realities follows naturally. If it all belongs to Allah, then mercy is His to bestow, gathering is His to decree, judgment is His to bring, and every creature passing through the stillness of night and the movement of day already lives inside His ownership and knowledge.

[PAUSE]

What is especially striking is that the ayah does not move from ownership to terror first. It moves from ownership to mercy:

*Kataba 'ala nafsihi al-rahmah.*

He has written mercy upon Himself.

That is one of the most hope-giving turns in the Qur'an. The One who owns everything is not introduced here as arbitrary. He is the Lord who has made mercy a written commitment upon Himself. But the verse does not let us sentimentalize that mercy into forgetfulness. The same sentence moves straight toward Resurrection. He will gather you. There is no doubt in that Day. So mercy is not a denial of accountability. It is the atmosphere in which accountability is being announced.

The hook of these ayahs is this: **once everything is returned to Allah's ownership, the soul discovers that the One who will certainly gather it has already bound Himself to mercy, and that nothing settling through the whole rhythm of night and day lies outside His hearing and knowledge.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Ayah Makes the Listener Answer the Question of Ownership

It opens with a command:

*Qul liman ma fi al-samawati wa al-ard.*

Say: to whom belongs whatever is in the heavens and the earth?

[PAUSE]

This is one of the Qur'an's recurring methods. It makes the listener speak the truth aloud, because once a truth is admitted, other evasions begin to collapse under its weight.

And notice the sweep:

*ma fi al-samawati wa al-ard* - whatever is in the heavens and the earth.

Not only the heavens and the earth as abstract spaces, but whatever is in them. Creatures, forces, histories, souls, powers, signs, provisions, judgments, and hidden lives. The question is total.

Then comes the answer:

*Qul lillah.*

Say: to Allah.

Short. Final. No rival claim left standing.

The verse does not treat this as an interesting theological proposition. It treats it as the master-key. Once that is said sincerely, the rest of life has to be re-read.

### 2. The Owner of All Things Has Written Mercy Upon Himself

Then comes one of the most tender lines in the surah:

*Kataba 'ala nafsihi al-rahmah.*

He has written mercy upon Himself.

[PAUSE]

The verb *kataba* gives weight. This is not passing sentiment. It is something written, established, set down with deliberateness and firmness. And the phrase *'ala nafsihi* is awe-inspiring. Allah, in His majesty, speaks of having written mercy upon Himself. Not because anything compels Him from outside, but because this is how He has chosen to relate to His creation.

This does not make Allah dependent. It makes the servant hopeful. The Lord of total ownership is the Lord who has made mercy part of the very way He wishes to be known and encountered.

That is why this line heals something deep in the human soul. Many of us can imagine power more easily than mercy. We know how ownership can become harsh in the human world. But the Qur'an says the true Owner has written mercy.

### 3. Mercy Does Not Cancel Gathering - It Grounds It

Then the verse continues immediately:

*La-yajma'annakum ila yawmi al-qiyamati la rayba fihi.*

He will surely gather you to the Day of Resurrection, no doubt in it.

[PAUSE]

The emphatic form matters. This is not tentative. The gathering is certain.

And the word *yajma'annakum* is powerful after the earlier themes of ownership. The One who owns all things can gather all things. Your life does not scatter beyond His reach. Death does not dissolve you out of His dominion. The Day of Resurrection is simply the day scattered human histories are recollected before their Owner.

Then:

*La rayba fihi.*

No doubt in it.

Not because every human being feels equally persuaded at every moment, but because the reality itself is firm regardless of how people wobble before it. The certainty belongs to the Day, not to the fluctuations of our psychology.

Then comes a devastating diagnosis:

*Alladhina khasiru anfusahum fa-hum la yu'minun.*

Those who have lost their own selves are the ones who do not believe.

This is such a penetrating way to describe unbelief. The greatest loss is not first of wealth, status, or opportunity. It is the loss of the self. A person can walk around appearing intact while spiritually trading away the very soul that was meant to survive into the Day being named.

### 4. Night, Day, Stillness, and Total Knowing

Then the next ayah adds a quieter but equally total image:

*Wa lahu ma sakana fi al-layli wa al-nahar.*

To Him belongs whatever settles in the night and the day.

[PAUSE]

This is beautiful. The ayah does not only say what moves through night and day. It says what *sakana* - what settles, comes to rest, dwells, grows still within them.

That wording creates a gentler atmosphere than cosmic thunder. It includes creatures in their shelters, hearts in their hidden hours, lives in their unobserved rhythms, the whole settled population of existence across darkness and light. Even what seems tucked away, quiet, and unnoticed already belongs to Him.

Then the closing names:

*Wa huwa al-sami'u al-'alim.*

And He is the All-Hearing, the All-Knowing.

The names fit the passage perfectly. Ownership is not blind possession. Mercy is not uninformed softness. Gathering is not arbitrary force. He hears and He knows. The whole moral and emotional texture of your life unfolds before One whose knowledge is complete and whose hearing misses no call, no confession, no hidden grief, and no unspoken defiance.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Deepest Relief Begins When Ownership Is Settled

A great deal of our unrest comes from living inside half-settled ownership.

We say Allah owns everything, but we emotionally behave as though certain territories remain privately ours: our future, our reputation, our grief, our loved ones, our inner life, our timeline, our safety. We do not fully admit it, but our panic often reveals it.

[PAUSE]

This ayah is a mercy because it presses the matter to completion. To whom belongs whatever is in the heavens and the earth?

If the answer is truly Allah, then your story is not ownerless.
Your hidden night thoughts are not ownerless.
Your Resurrection is not ownerless.
Even your fear of being gathered is not ownerless.

That does not remove responsibility. It removes the terror of imagining that reality is being managed by fractured, competing hands. There is one Owner. And once that is settled, the soul can finally begin to breathe correctly.

### Theme 2: Divine Mercy Is Not an Escape from Accountability but the Reason Accountability Is Bearable

The second great healing in this passage is the order of its ideas.

Ownership.
Mercy written upon Himself.
Then gathering to Resurrection.

[PAUSE]

That order matters. If a person hears only about Resurrection without knowing the character of the One who will gather him, fear may turn into despair. But if he hears only about mercy while refusing the Day, hope decays into heedlessness.

The Qur'an brings them together.

You will be gathered.
There is no doubt in that.
But the One gathering you has written mercy upon Himself.

That is not permission to be casual. It is permission not to despair. It means the right response is not denial of the Day, nor paralysis before it, but returning to Allah with a heart that fears and hopes together.

### Theme 3: Losing the Self Is the Most Catastrophic Loss

The phrase *khasiru anfusahum* deserves to stay with us.

Most human beings are deeply afraid of losing things around them.
Money.
Place.
Relationships.
Influence.
Time.

[PAUSE]

But the Qur'an says there is a deeper loss that can hide beneath all of those anxieties: losing the self itself.

That happens when a person keeps feeding the outer life while starving the soul that must stand at Resurrection. It happens when we protect image and appetite at the expense of iman. It happens when disbelief is treated as intellectual independence rather than self-ruin.

So these ayahs ask an urgent question: what if the real catastrophe in your life would not be losing what you fear losing, but living in a way that quietly loses *you*?

And the answer is hidden in the passage itself. Return ownership to Allah. Receive the fact that mercy is written. Prepare for the gathering. Live under the hearing and knowledge of the One to whom every settled thing already belongs.

## Closing Synthesis

These two ayahs move through a breathtaking sequence.

They begin by making the listener answer the question of ownership.
They reveal that the Owner has written mercy upon Himself.
They announce the certainty of the Gathering.
They diagnose unbelief as self-loss.
Then they widen the horizon again: everything settled in night and day belongs to Him, and He hears and knows all.

[PAUSE]

That is the architecture: ownership, mercy, resurrection, total knowing.

And in that architecture the heart finds both sobriety and shelter. You are not outside His dominion. You are not outside His mercy. You are not outside His gathering. And you are certainly not outside His hearing or His knowledge. The only real question left is whether you will live as someone who belongs to that Lord before the Day arrives when all belonging becomes impossible to deny.

### Questions to Carry

1. What in my life do I still cling to as though it were privately mine rather than something already belonging to Allah?
2. Does the certainty of Resurrection lead me toward repentance and hope, or do I keep using distraction to postpone feeling its weight?
3. In what ways might I be protecting outer parts of life while quietly losing my own self?
4. How would my nights and days change if I really remembered that whatever settles in them already belongs to the One who hears and knows everything?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These ayahs teach that the One who owns everything in the heavens and earth has written mercy upon Himself, will certainly gather us to a doubtless Resurrection, and already possesses and fully knows all that settles through the whole cycle of night and day.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, settle our hearts by settling ownership in us. Let us know deeply that we belong to You, that Your mercy is written, that our gathering before You is certain, and that our nights and days unfold under Your hearing and knowledge. Protect us from the loss of our own selves and bring us back to You before the Day we are gathered. Amin.
