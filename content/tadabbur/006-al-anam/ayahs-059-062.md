# Not a Leaf Falls Outside His Knowledge

## Surah Al-Anam (6:59-62)

[ayah:6:59] ۞ وَعِندَهُۥ مَفَاتِحُ ٱلْغَيْبِ لَا يَعْلَمُهَآ إِلَّا هُوَ ۚ وَيَعْلَمُ مَا فِى ٱلْبَرِّ وَٱلْبَحْرِ ۚ وَمَا تَسْقُطُ مِن وَرَقَةٍ إِلَّا يَعْلَمُهَا وَلَا حَبَّةٍ فِى ظُلُمَـٰتِ ٱلْأَرْضِ وَلَا رَطْبٍ وَلَا يَابِسٍ إِلَّا فِى كِتَـٰبٍ مُّبِينٍ

## Introduction

There are moments when the soul feels abandoned by scale.

The world seems too large, too scattered, too full of hidden things. So much is happening beyond us, beneath us, behind us, above us. We do not know what is coming. We do not even fully know what is already happening inside the hour we are living.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs come into that anxiety with overwhelming sovereignty.

The keys of the unseen are with Allah alone. He knows what is in the land and sea. Not a leaf falls outside His knowledge. Not a seed disappears into the darkness of the earth unnoticed. Then the surah narrows from the cosmos to your own nightly sleep, your daily earning, your appointed term, the angels who keep watch, the exact moment of death, and the return to Allah as the true Master whose judgment no one can overrule.

This is not only a passage about information. It is a passage about ownership, custody, and return. The unseen is His. Your sleep is in His hand. Your days are under His knowledge. Your death is not neglected. Your return is not optional. Your account is already perfectly held.

[PAUSE]

The hook of this passage is this: **Allah's knowledge is not a cold database of facts but the total sovereignty of the One in whose hand the unseen, your nights, your deeds, your death, and your final return have always been.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Keys of the Unseen Are With Him Alone

The opening line is one of the great Qur'anic declarations of divine exclusivity:

*Wa 'indahu mafatihu al-ghayb la ya'lamuha illa huwa.*

With Him are the keys of the unseen. None knows them except Him.

[PAUSE]

*Mafatih* can carry the sense of keys, and the classical tafsir also carries the sense of خزائن, storehouses, treasuries. Either way, the point is possession and access. The unseen is not a field lying open to created mastery. Its entry-points are with Him.

Then the ayah expands:

*Wa ya'lamu ma fi al-barri wa al-bahr.*

He knows what is in the land and sea.

Not only the abstract unseen, but the spread of the visible world as well. Dry land and open sea function here like total horizon language. Wherever you can stand and wherever you can be lost, His knowledge already surrounds it.

Then the ayah becomes startlingly intimate:

*Wa ma tasqutu min waraqatin illa ya'lamuha.*

Not a leaf falls except that He knows it.

The image is not grand in a theatrical way. It is small, ordinary, almost quiet. A leaf loosens, turns, falls, lands. And the ayah says even that is not outside divine knowledge.

Then deeper still:

*Wa la habbatin fi zulumati al-ardi.*

Nor a grain in the darknesses of the earth.

The plural *zulumat* matters. Layers of darkness. Covered, buried, unreachable to human sight. Yet no hiddenness removes the thing from Allah's knowing.

And then the pair:

*Wa la ratbin wa la yabisin illa fi kitabin mubin.*

Neither moist nor dry except that it is in a clear record.

The passage moves from unseen, to land and sea, to leaf, to seed, to every moist and dry thing. It is sweeping outward and inward at once until no category of overlooked reality remains.

### 2. Every Night Is a Rehearsal of Return

Then the surah turns from the world to us:

*Wa huwa alladhi yatawaffakum bi-al-layl.*

He is the One who takes you by night.

[PAUSE]

The verb *yatawaffa* is weighty. Classical tafsir names sleep here as a kind of lesser death, a taking. You lie down assuming continuity, but your waking is never self-owned. Every night is a surrender you cannot engineer from the inside.

Then:

*Wa ya'lamu ma jarahtum bi-al-nahar.*

And He knows what you earned by day.

*Jarahtum* literally carries the language of wounding and acquiring. The day leaves marks. Actions are not weightless. By the time evening comes, we are not simply tired. We are morally marked by what we have done.

Then:

*Thumma yab'athukum fihi li-yuqda ajalun musamman.*

Then He raises you in it so that an appointed term may be completed.

Sleep and waking become a daily parable of death and resurrection. You are taken. You are returned. Not randomly, but until a named term is fulfilled.

Then the ayah refuses to let us treat this as a biological cycle only:

*Thumma ilayhi marji'ukum thumma yunabbi'ukum bima kuntum ta'malun.*

Then to Him is your return. Then He will inform you of what you used to do.

The day was never only a day. It was evidence.

### 3. Above the Servants, Over Their Time, Exact in Their Taking

Then the passage rises again:

*Wa huwa al-qahiru fawqa 'ibadihi.*

He is the overpowering One above His servants.

[PAUSE]

*Al-Qahir* is not mere abstract superiority. It is irresistible dominance. He is not one force among forces. He is above the servants in a way that leaves every creature under His command.

Yet that transcendence is not distance without administration:

*Wa yursilu 'alaykum hafazatan.*

He sends over you guardians.

The angels are not decorative beings in the Qur'an. They are part of the moral seriousness of the world. Your life is watched, recorded, kept.

Then the endpoint:

*Hatta idha ja'a ahadakumu al-mawtu tawaffathu rusuluna wa hum la yufarrutun.*

Until, when death comes to one of you, Our messengers take him, and they do not neglect.

*La yufarritun.* They do not fail, delay, or lose track. Death is not an administrative mistake. No soul slips through the system. No appointed taking is forgotten.

### 4. We Return to the True Master

Then comes the final compression:

*Thumma ruddu ila Allahi mawlahumu al-haqq.*

Then they are returned to Allah, their true Master.

[PAUSE]

*Mawlahumu al-haqq* is a powerful ending to this chain. We spend so much of life behaving as if other masters will shield us: status, money, image, tribe, control. But at the end, the only true Mawla is the One to whom we are returned.

Then:

*Ala lahu al-hukmu.*

Unquestionably, His is the judgment.

The passage began with the keys of the unseen and ends with decisive judgment. Hidden things and final outcomes are both His.

Then the last phrase:

*Wa huwa asra'u al-hasibin.*

He is the swiftest of accountants.

Not because His accounting is rushed, but because nothing is missing. He does not need reconstruction, review, or recollection. The record was never absent from Him.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: We Live Inside a World That Is More Held Than It Feels

These ayahs are deeply healing for anyone exhausted by uncertainty.

The unseen is real.
Our ignorance is real.
The hiddenness of outcomes is real.

But hidden is not the same as unheld.

[PAUSE]

That is the distinction this passage gives the heart. We do not know, but He knows. We do not see, but He sees. We do not keep the record, but the record is already clear. And this includes realities so small they seem beneath mention: a leaf, a seed, something moist, something dry.

When the soul loses this, it begins to panic in the gaps. It starts thinking that what is hidden from us is therefore loose, chaotic, or abandoned. But these ayahs rebuild trust. Reality is not unraveling because you do not grasp it. It is already enclosed within the knowledge, decree, and custody of Allah.

### Theme 2: Sleep, Waking, and Death Are Daily Lessons in Servanthood

There is also a quiet humiliation in this passage, and it is mercy.

Every night, Allah takes you.
Every morning, He returns you.
Every day, you earn.
At death, you are taken exactly.
Then you are returned to Him finally.

[PAUSE]

That means the independent self we perform so confidently through the day is not nearly as self-standing as it imagines. You are not even the uninterrupted owner of your own consciousness. The night interrupts your illusion daily.

And perhaps that is why these ayahs feel so strong. They are not only telling us about the end. They are showing us that the end is being rehearsed all the time. If we paid attention to sleep properly, we would already understand something about surrender, vulnerability, and resurrection.

The heart that remembers this lives differently. It sins less casually. It wakes with more gratitude. It treats the day not as empty time to spend, but as marked ground under divine knowledge.

## Closing Synthesis

This passage gathers enormous realities into one movement.

The unseen belongs to Allah.
The world in land and sea lies under His knowledge.
Not a leaf falls unnoticed.
Not a hidden seed escapes Him.
Every night He takes you.
Every day He knows what you earned.
He raises you until your appointed term is fulfilled.
He is the overpowering One above His servants.
He sends guardians.
His messengers do not fail at death.
You are returned to Allah, your true Master.
His is the judgment.
His reckoning is swift because nothing was ever absent from Him.

[PAUSE]

So the soul does not need to control what it was never meant to control. It needs to remember whose world this is, whose record this is, whose night this is, whose death this is, and whose judgment waits at the end. There is immense sobriety here, but also immense relief. We are not lost in an unwatched universe. We are already inside the knowledge and dominion of Allah.

### Questions to Carry

1. What changes in my daily life if I remember that even my sleep and waking are acts occurring inside Allah's custody rather than my own control?
2. When I panic over what is hidden from me, do I know how to distinguish between what is unseen to me and what is perfectly known to Allah?
