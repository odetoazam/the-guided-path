# When Warning Becomes Wearisome to the Proud, They Demand the Threat and Nuh Returns the Matter to Allah

## Surah Hud (11:32-34)

[ayah:11:32] قَالُوا۟ يَـٰنُوحُ قَدْ جَـٰدَلْتَنَا فَأَكْثَرْتَ جِدَٰلَنَا فَأْتِنَا بِمَا تَعِدُنَآ إِن كُنتَ مِنَ ٱلصَّـٰدِقِينَ

[ayah:11:33] قَالَ إِنَّمَا يَأْتِيكُم بِهِ ٱللَّهُ إِن شَآءَ وَمَآ أَنتُم بِمُعْجِزِينَ

[ayah:11:34] وَلَا يَنفَعُكُمْ نُصْحِىٓ إِنْ أَرَدتُّ أَنْ أَنصَحَ لَكُمْ إِن كَانَ ٱللَّهُ يُرِيدُ أَن يُغْوِيَكُمْ ۚ هُوَ رَبُّكُمْ وَإِلَيْهِ تُرْجَعُونَ

*Qalu ya Nuhu qad jadalta-na fa-aktharta jidala-na fa'tina bima ta'iduna in kunta min al-sadiqin.*

*Qala innama ya'tikum bihi Allahu in sha'a wa ma antum bi-mu'jizin.*

*Wa la yanfa'ukum nushi in aradtu an ansaha lakum in kana Allahu yuridu an yughwiyakum. Huwa rabbukum wa ilayhi turja'un.*

**They said, "O Nuh, you have argued with us and prolonged argument with us, so bring us what you threaten us with, if you are among the truthful." He said, "Allah alone will bring it to you, if He wills, and you are not going to escape Him. And my counsel will not benefit you, even if I wanted to advise you sincerely, if Allah wills to leave you astray. He is your Lord, and to Him you will be returned."**

## Introduction

There is a stage of refusal more dangerous than open disagreement.

It is the stage where a person is no longer really asking whether the warning is true. They are simply tired of hearing it. The reminder has become emotionally inconvenient. The repeated call to repent has begun to feel, in their ears, like too much talking. And once the heart reaches that condition, it often says something reckless: if this is all true, then bring the consequence now.

[PAUSE]

That is the atmosphere here. Nuh's people are no longer engaging the substance of his call. They complain about its persistence. They are annoyed by the repeated pressure of truth. Nuh's answer is remarkable because he refuses to perform divine punishment on demand. He returns the whole matter to Allah's will, then says something even heavier: sincere counsel itself stops benefiting when a people are being left to the path of ruin they insisted on walking.

The hook of the passage is this: **Hud teaches that one of the signs of a hardened heart is that warning starts to feel like tedious argument, but the prophet never owns punishment or guidance; both matters remain with Allah, while counsel only benefits a heart that still wants to be saved.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Qad jadalta-na fa-aktharta jidala-na**: They Are Not Complaining About Weak Evidence, but About Sustained Pressure

They say:

**qad jadalta-na fa-aktharta jidala-na**.

You have argued with us, and you have greatly increased argument with us.

[PAUSE]

The root **j-d-l** carries dispute, back-and-forth, verbal contestation. But what matters here is not only the existence of dialogue. It is the complaint of abundance: **fa-aktharta** - you increased it, prolonged it, made it too much for us.

That is revealing. They do not say, "Your proof is unclear." They do not say, "Your argument has been refuted." They say, in effect, "Enough. We are tired of this continuing."

The nafs often prefers a false calm over a true disturbance. Real reminder disturbs the arrangements by which we have learned to live comfortably with disobedience. If someone keeps bringing us back to Allah, keeps reopening what we wanted closed, keeps refusing to leave us to our preferred illusions, we may start calling that pressure "argumentativeness" simply because it does not let our denial rest.

Then they say:

**fa'tina bima ta'iduna**.

Bring us what you threaten us with.

This is the language of a heart that wants finality without repentance. Instead of softening under warning, it dares the warning to become visible consequence.

### 2. **Innama ya'tikum bihi Allahu in sha'a wa ma antum bi-mu'jizin**: The Prophet Does Not Control the Threat

Nuh answers:

**innama ya'tikum bihi Allahu in sha'a**.

Allah alone will bring it to you, if He wills.

[PAUSE]

The word **innama** narrows and restricts. Only this. Only Allah. Nuh refuses the role they are trying to assign him. He is not a manager of punishment. He is not a stage director who can produce the promised consequence on cue.

This matters spiritually. We often want revelation to submit to our timetable. "If this warning is real, let me see the outcome now. Let me test Allah on my terms. Let me decide when the unseen becomes visible." The answer of prophecy is always humbling: the matter is not yours. It belongs to Allah.

Then:

**wa ma antum bi-mu'jizin**.

And you are not ones who can render Him unable.

The word **mu'jizin** comes from the field of **'ajz**, inability, incapacity. The meaning is not merely "you cannot run away fast enough." It is deeper: you cannot push Allah into helplessness. You cannot frustrate His decree. You cannot create a pocket of existence in which His will cannot reach you.

So Nuh's answer holds both truths at once: punishment is not in the prophet's control, but neither is safety in the control of the deniers.

### 3. **Wa la yanfa'ukum nushi... in kana Allahu yuridu an yughwiyakum**: Advice Benefits Only a Heart Still Open to Rescue

Ayah 34 deepens the scene:

**wa la yanfa'ukum nushi in aradtu an ansaha lakum**.

My counsel will not benefit you, even if I wanted to advise you sincerely.

[PAUSE]

The noun **nush** and the verb **ansaha** come from the field of sincere, well-wishing counsel. This is not argument for ego. This is not winning a debate. Nuh describes his speech as real concern for their good.

But then comes one of the heaviest lines in the passage:

**in kana Allahu yuridu an yughwiyakum**.

If Allah wills to leave you astray.

We have to sit with this carefully. The verse does not invite us to imagine arbitrary divine cruelty. One way the classical tradition helps us approach it is to see the terrible finality of a people who have resisted truth long enough that Allah leaves them to the direction they insisted on. They wanted the warning removed. They demanded consequence instead of repentance. At some point, being left to your chosen path becomes itself a judgment.

Then the ayah closes:

**huwa rabbukum wa ilayhi turja'un**.

He is your Lord, and to Him you will be returned.

[PAUSE]

That ending recenters everything. The One who controls punishment is not a distant force. He is your **Rabb** - your Lord, your Master, the One who owns, sustains, and judges your affair. And the matter ends not in your bravado, not in your irritation, not in your demand for immediacy, but in return.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: A Hardened Heart Often Calls Persistent Mercy "Too Much"

There are reminders we dislike not because they are false, but because they keep touching the wound we want to ignore.

Someone keeps telling us to repair the prayer we keep delaying. To make tawbah from the sin we keep renaming. To stop living off a compromise we have normalized. To restore a right we owe. To leave a relationship that is costing us our soul. And after enough reminders, the heart can become irritated. Not humbled, irritated.

[PAUSE]

At that point we may not say, "I don't want Allah." We say something more respectable sounding: "This is too much. You're always bringing this up. Why so much pressure?" But sometimes the repetition itself is mercy. If Allah keeps sending warning back to the same area of your life, it may be because He has not abandoned you there yet.

The frightening turn is when we begin preferring silence to rescue. That is the beginning of inward hardening.

### Theme 2: The Servant Does Not Own Results; He Owns Sincere Counsel

Nuh's answer is also a purification for anyone involved in teaching, parenting, leadership, friendship, or da'wah.

You are not asked to control outcomes. You are asked to be sincere.

That distinction saves the heart from two opposite corruptions. One is despair: "If they are not changing, I must have failed." The other is domination: "If they are not changing, I must force the matter harder." Nuh's speech rejects both. Punishment belongs to Allah. Guidance belongs to Allah. What belongs to the servant is **nush** - truthful, sincere concern expressed as faithfully as possible.

[PAUSE]

And there is another lesson here for the one receiving advice. If you keep asking Allah for help but reject every sincere person He sends to disturb your illusion, you may be praying for rescue while pushing away the boat.

The ayah is telling us that benefit from counsel is not automatic. Advice has to meet a heart that is willing, however tremblingly, to be moved.

## Closing Synthesis

Nuh's people reveal their condition by saying that he has argued too much and should now bring the threatened punishment if he is truthful. They are tired of warning, tired of moral interruption, tired of being called back.

[PAUSE]

Nuh answers by cleansing the scene of every illusion. Allah alone brings consequence, when He wills. No one can render Him unable. And sincere counsel itself does not benefit a people once they are being left to the ruin they insisted upon. The entire matter returns to the Lord, and the people themselves will return to Him as well.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with these ayahs is to ask: where in my life have reminders started to feel like irritating repetition rather than mercy? Do I secretly want Allah's warnings to stop more than I want my state to change? And when sincere counsel reaches me, am I still soft enough to benefit from it?

[PAUSE]

Hud leaves us with this severe mercy: **when warning becomes wearisome, the problem is often no longer the argument's clarity but the heart's resistance, and the only safe response is to soften before Allah leaves us to the path we were demanding to walk.**
