# Stand Straight, Do Not Lean toward Zulm, and Let Prayer Carry You into Patient Ihsan

## Surah Hud (11:112-115)

[ayah:11:112] فَٱسْتَقِمْ كَمَآ أُمِرْتَ وَمَن تَابَ مَعَكَ وَلَا تَطْغَوْا۟ ۚ إِنَّهُۥ بِمَا تَعْمَلُونَ بَصِيرٌ

[ayah:11:113] وَلَا تَرْكَنُوٓا۟ إِلَى ٱلَّذِينَ ظَلَمُوا۟ فَتَمَسَّكُمُ ٱلنَّارُ وَمَا لَكُم مِّن دُونِ ٱللَّهِ مِنْ أَوْلِيَآءَ ثُمَّ لَا تُنصَرُونَ

## Introduction

After all the stories, all the ruins, all the warnings about the Hereafter, and all the exposure of inherited falsehood, Surah Hud finally turns and tells us what all of that was supposed to produce in a life.

Not spectacle. Not religious fascination. Not historical curiosity.

**Istiqamah. Distance from zulm. Prayer. Sabr.**

[PAUSE]

This is one of the most searching turns in the surah. Allah does not leave the listener with ruined towns and final judgment as abstract theology. He converts the entire surah into a regimen: stand straight as commanded, do not cross the bounds, do not even lean toward wrongdoers, establish prayer at the edges of the day and in the nearnesses of the night, let good wear away the stains of evil, and keep going with patience because Allah never loses the reward of the people of ihsan.

The hook of the passage is this: **Hud teaches that the right response to all its severe stories is not paralysis but disciplined straightness - a life guarded from tilt toward zulm, repaired through prayer and good, and sustained by patient confidence that Allah preserves the reward of ihsan.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Fa-staqim kama umirta**: Straightness Is Not Self-Designed

The command opens with:

**fa-staqim kama umirta**.

So stand straight, remain upright, exactly as you were commanded.

[PAUSE]

The root behind **istiqamah** is qaf-waw-mim, the root of standing, rising, being upright. But the surah immediately protects the command from being turned into vague moral sincerity by adding **kama umirta** - as you were commanded.

That matters. Straightness in the Qur'an is not invented by personal taste. It is not "be authentic." It is not "follow your private sense of balance." It is commanded alignment. Allah decides the line; we are ordered to stand within it.

Then the command widens:

**wa man taba ma'ak**.

And whoever turned back with you.

This is not a prophetic burden only. Everyone who has returned to Allah with the Messenger is pulled into the same demand. Tawbah does not merely wash the past; it enrolls you into istiqamah.

Then comes the warning:

**wa la tatghaw**.

Do not transgress. Do not go beyond the bounds.

The surah does not only fear collapse to the left. It also fears excess beyond the line. Even religious energy can become spiritually destructive if it overruns what Allah actually commanded.

### 2. **La tarkanu**: The Qur'an Forbids the Lean before It Forbids the Fall

Then the passage says:

**wa la tarkanu ila alladhina zalamu**.

Do not incline toward those who wrong.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the most subtle warnings in the surah. The verb **tarkanu** is not full merger. It is leaning, inclining, resting toward, finding a kind of settled comfort in them. The Qur'an forbids the tilt before the complete alliance.

That is searching because many people imagine safety lies in avoiding outright participation in injustice while still permitting emotional, social, or strategic comfort with it. Hud says even the lean is dangerous.

Then the verse gives the consequence with startling directness:

**fa-tamassakumu al-nar**.

Lest the Fire touch you.

Not necessarily engulf at first. Touch. Even contact with Fire is too much. The verse makes the danger feel immediate and intimate.

### 3. Prayer, Remembrance, and the Hope of Repair

Then comes the practical mercy:

**wa aqimi al-salata tarafayi al-nahari wa zulafan mina al-layl**.

Establish the prayer at the two edges of the day and in the near hours of the night.

[PAUSE]

After commanding straightness and forbidding lean, the surah gives a rhythm by which the heart is repeatedly returned to Allah. Day begins and ends under prayer, and the night too is entered through nearnesses of worship.

Then comes one of the most tender promises in the surah:

**inna al-hasanati yudhhibna al-sayyi'at**.

Surely the good deeds carry away the bad deeds.

The verb **yudhhibna** comes from going away, removing, taking off. Good is not only recorded. It is active. By Allah's mercy, it works against the stain.

Then the ending gathers the whole passage:

**wa-sbir fa-inna Allaha la yudi'u ajra al-muhsinin**.

Be patient. Allah does not waste the reward of the people of ihsan.

The surah does not say merely "of the believers" here. It says **al-muhsinin** - those who strive to do what they do beautifully, faithfully, excellently before Allah.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Most Moral Collapse Begins with a Lean

Communities do not usually wake up one morning and decide to love injustice in its full form. The heart's movement is often smaller than that. It begins with comfort. With strategic softness. With admiration that goes unchallenged. With the sentence, "At least they are useful to keep close." With emotional rest near people whose core orientation is crooked.

[PAUSE]

That is why **la tarkanu** is so frightening. Allah forbids the small interior motion that later becomes complicity. The believer is not only asked to reject zulm when it becomes unbearable. He is asked not to let the soul settle toward it in the first place.

### Theme 2: Allah Did Not Only Warn You; He Gave You a Repair Rhythm

There is another mercy here. Hud is a severe surah. It has flood, wind, cry, overturning, marked stones, the gathered Day, the wretched and the blessed. A heart could leave such a surah feeling that the only faithful response is fear.

But Allah does not leave us there.

[PAUSE]

He gives prayer. He gives recurring good. He gives remembrance. He gives sabr. He gives the assurance that good can actually remove evil by His permission. And He ends by saying the reward of ihsan is never lost.

This is how the Qur'an disciplines fear so it does not become despair. The point of warning is not to crush you. It is to drive you into a life that keeps returning, cleaning, resisting tilt, and remaining under Allah's watch.

## Closing Synthesis

The answer to Hud's warnings is now clear. Stand straight exactly as commanded. Let tawbah mature into istiqamah. Do not exceed the bounds. Do not lean toward the people of zulm. Keep prayer alive across the day and into the night. Trust that good can remove the stain of evil by Allah's mercy. And stay patient, because the reward of those who live with ihsan is never wasted.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs turn the whole surah from narrated history into embodied practice. They tell us what to do after hearing about destroyed peoples and after trembling at the Hereafter: live a life of straightness, guarded loyalties, recurring prayer, practical repair, and durable patience.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this passage is to ask: where has my straightness become self-designed instead of command-shaped? What forms of zulm have I told myself I am not participating in, while my heart still leans toward them for comfort or advantage? Is my prayer actually functioning as a repair rhythm in my life? And do I believe deeply enough that Allah does not lose the reward of ihsan to keep going without applause?

[PAUSE]

Hud leaves us with a command cluster that can hold a whole life together: **stand straight, do not lean, keep the prayer, and be patient until Allah lets goodness outlast the stain.**
