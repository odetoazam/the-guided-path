# If I Fabricated It, Then My Crime Is on Me; I Am Clear of Yours

## Surah Hud (11:35)

[ayah:11:35] أَمْ يَقُولُونَ ٱفْتَرَىٰهُ ۖ قُلْ إِنِ ٱفْتَرَيْتُهُۥ فَعَلَىَّ إِجْرَامِى وَأَنَا۠ بَرِىٓءٌ مِّمَّا تُجْرِمُونَ

*Am yaquluna iftarahu. Qul ini iftaraytuhu fa-'alayya ijrami wa ana bari'un mimma tujrimun.*

**Or do they say, "He fabricated it"? Say: if I have fabricated it, then upon me is my crime, and I am free of what you commit.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

False accusation often expects panic.

It wants the truthful person to become flustered, over-explain themselves, and get dragged into a fog where the original clarity of the message disappears under defensive motion. One of the quiet miracles of this ayah is that it teaches a prophetic answer that is morally sharp and emotionally unpanicked.

[PAUSE]

The accusation is blunt: **iftarahu** - he made it up. He fabricated it. He forged revelation and attached it to Allah. But the answer does not spiral. It does not become theatrical self-vindication. It says something far more powerful: if I did fabricate it, then I bear that crime myself. And as for the crimes you are committing, I am clear of them.

The hook of the ayah is this: **Hud teaches that truth does not need frantic self-defense; it can calmly place liability where it belongs and refuse moral entanglement with the collective crimes of denial.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Iftarahu ... iftaraytuhu**: The Accusation Is Answered in Its Own Vocabulary

The ayah opens:

**am yaquluna iftarahu**.

Or do they say: he fabricated it?

[PAUSE]

The verb **iftara** is not casual mistake. It is invention with false ascription, especially in matters attributed to Allah. In the Qur'an this is among the gravest forms of speech-corruption: to manufacture something and then pin it onto divine authority.

What is striking is that the response repeats the same root:

**qul ini iftaraytuhu**.

Say: if I fabricated it...

He is taught not to flee the word. The accusation is brought into the open and answered directly. There is dignity in that. Sometimes falsehood gains strength because the truthful become afraid even to name the lie being told about them. The Qur'an teaches a steadier posture. Name it. Then place responsibility precisely.

### 2. **Fa-'alayya ijrami**: If I Lied, the Burden Is Mine

Then comes the phrase:

**fa-'alayya ijrami**.

Then upon me is my crime.

[PAUSE]

The word **ijram** comes from the field of **j-r-m**, جرم, the root of incurring offense, criminality, culpable violation. The response is astonishingly direct. If I am guilty, then I carry that guilt. If I have done what you claim, then I do not ask you to absorb it for me, excuse it for me, or blur it for me.

There is a moral confidence here that only truth can sustain. Liars usually want diffusion. They want fog, shared blame, procedural distraction, emotional smoke. Truth can afford clarity.

And notice the singular:

**ijrami** - my crime.

It is individualized. Account will not be collectivized. The burden of speech before Allah sits where it belongs: on the speaker.

### 3. **Wa ana bari'un mimma tujrimun**: Innocence Is Not Merely "I Didn't Do It"; It Is Moral Separation

Then the ayah completes the answer:

**wa ana bari'un mimma tujrimun**.

And I am clear of what you commit.

[PAUSE]

The word **bari'** carries the meaning of being free, absolved, disentangled, clear of association. This is more than denying one charge. It is a refusal to be woven into the criminal fabric of the accusers.

And now notice the shift from the singular to the plural. First:

**ijrami** - my crime, if I did it.

Then:

**mimma tujrimun** - from what you all are committing.

This is powerful. The ayah refuses false symmetry. It does not say, "If I lied and you lied, then we are all flawed." No. One hypothetical personal liability is set against the ongoing, multiple crimes of the deniers. Their problem is not merely one mistaken sentence. It is a whole pattern of جرم.

The Messenger is being taught to separate himself from that pattern with complete moral clarity.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Integrity Does Not Need Performative Panic

Many of us think that innocence must always sound frantic.

If someone misrepresents us, slanders us, questions our intention, or accuses us of hidden motives, we feel an almost immediate urge to over-defend. We want to explain every angle, narrate every detail, recruit witnesses, and somehow force everyone to feel our innocence at the same speed we feel their injustice.

[PAUSE]

This ayah teaches another posture. State the matter clearly. If I did what you say, I bear it before Allah. If not, then I do not need to drown in your theater. There is a serenity available to the truthful because they know that moral reality is not ultimately decided by suspicion campaigns.

That does not mean we never clarify. The ayah itself is a clarification. But it is not a clarification driven by panic. It is driven by accountability before Allah.

### Theme 2: One of the Hardest Forms of Taqwa Is Refusing Shared Criminal Atmosphere

The second half of the ayah reaches into another part of our lives. Sometimes we are not being accused personally. Sometimes we are being invited into a shared moral atmosphere.

Everyone around us is mocking something sacred. Normalizing a corruption. Repeating a slander. Smearing sincerity. Treating revelation as human invention. And the pressure is not always, "Say it too." Often it is subtler: laugh with us, nod with us, don't distance yourself from us, don't make our wrongdoing feel like wrongdoing.

[PAUSE]

The word **bari'** becomes medicine here. A believer must sometimes say with the heart, the tongue, or at least the posture of refusal: I am not with this. I will not lend myself to this جرم. I will not let belonging become partnership in offense.

This can be socially expensive. It may make you feel awkward, exposed, severe, or out of step. But every age has its forms of collective جرم, and one of the marks of faith is learning how not to dissolve into them.

And there is one more inward layer. Sometimes the collective atmosphere we need to separate from is not "out there." It is the conversation inside our own chest. The cynical inner voice. The reflex that treats divine command as human invention. The part of the self that wants to reduce revelation to psychology, habit, or culture so that surrender can be postponed.

This ayah can be read there too. I am not with that crime. I will not help that inner slander against Allah's truth.

## Closing Synthesis

The ayah opens with the accusation of fabrication and answers it with remarkable steadiness. If I fabricated it, then my crime is on me. I will answer for what I said. But I am clear of what you are committing.

[PAUSE]

The response is brief because the truth does not need sprawling theatrics to remain true. It places liability where it belongs, names innocence as moral separation, and refuses to let the accusation create a false partnership between messenger and denier.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this ayah is to ask: do I need endless human vindication before I can stay steady in the truth? Where am I being pressured into a shared atmosphere of جرم simply so I do not feel isolated? And when falsehood becomes socially normal around me, do I know how to remain **bari'** of it before Allah?

[PAUSE]

Hud leaves us with this calm moral discipline: **truthful speech can bear personal accountability without fear, and real innocence includes refusing any inward or outward alliance with the crimes that are being committed against Allah's truth.**
