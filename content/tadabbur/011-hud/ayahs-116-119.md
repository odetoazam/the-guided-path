# The Remnant That Resists Corruption, the Reform That Delays Ruin, and the Mercy That Saves from Difference

## Surah Hud (11:116-119)

[ayah:11:116] فَلَوْلَا كَانَ مِنَ ٱلْقُرُونِ مِن قَبْلِكُمْ أُو۟لُوا۟ بَقِيَّةٍ يَنْهَوْنَ عَنِ ٱلْفَسَادِ فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ إِلَّا قَلِيلًا مِّمَّنْ أَنجَيْنَا مِنْهُمْ ۗ وَٱتَّبَعَ ٱلَّذِينَ ظَلَمُوا۟ مَآ أُتْرِفُوا۟ فِيهِ وَكَانُوا۟ مُجْرِمِينَ

[ayah:11:118] وَلَوْ شَآءَ رَبُّكَ لَجَعَلَ ٱلنَّاسَ أُمَّةً وَٰحِدَةً ۖ وَلَا يَزَالُونَ مُخْتَلِفِينَ

## Introduction

After Surah Hud commands us to stand straight, not lean toward zulm, keep the prayer, and remain patient, it asks a devastating historical question.

Why were there not, in those earlier civilizations, people of moral remainder who kept standing up against corruption before it swallowed the land?

[PAUSE]

This is one of the most important passages in the surah for understanding why societies collapse. It is not only that corruption exists. It is that too few people retain enough inward residue of truth to confront it. Too few people remain with enough moral remainder to say no before fasad becomes normal.

Then the surah states a principle that protects the heart from cynical readings of history: Allah does not destroy towns unjustly while their people are **muslihun** - actively reforming. After that, the horizon broadens even further. Human beings will continue to differ, except those whom Allah shows mercy. And the passage ends by leaving us between two immensities: divine mercy and the fulfilled word that Jahannam will be filled by those who refuse it.

The hook of the passage is this: **Hud teaches that civilizations are preserved by people of moral remainder who resist corruption, that real reform matters before Allah, and that in a world where difference persists, mercy is the only true exit from collective ruin.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Ulu baqiyyah**: The People Who Still Have Something Left

The passage asks:

**fa-lawla kana mina al-quruni min qablikum ulu baqiyyatin yanhawna 'ani al-fasadi fi al-ard**.

Why were there not among the generations before you people of **baqiyyah** who forbade corruption in the earth?

[PAUSE]

That word is extraordinary. **Baqiyyah** comes from remaining, being left, enduring after loss. One careful way to sit with **ulu baqiyyah** is: people who still have something sound left in them, people in whom moral substance remains after a society has begun to decay.

The verse is not merely asking for loud activists or socially visible critics. It is asking for a remnant of inward durability. The kind of people who do not get completely dissolved by the atmosphere around them.

And what do these people do? **Yanhawna 'ani al-fasad**. They forbid corruption. Their remaining goodness does not stay private. It takes the form of resistance.

Then comes the grief:

**illa qalilan mimman anjayna minhum**.

Except a few of those We saved.

The remnant existed, but it was small. Small enough to become the exception named after the disaster.

### 2. **Ma utrifu fihi**: Luxury as a Path of Moral Capture

Then the ayah says:

**wa-ittaba'a alladhina zalamu ma utrifu fihi**.

Those who wronged followed what they had been given in luxury.

[PAUSE]

This is not a casual mention of comfort. The verse is naming one of the engines of civilizational ruin: people become morally loyal to the very state of ease that has softened their resistance to truth.

They followed what they had been indulged in. They did not merely enjoy it. They were led by it. Their pleasures became directional. Their luxury was not background; it was pedagogy. It taught them what not to challenge, what not to lose, and therefore what truths had to be suppressed.

Then the ayah lands the moral verdict:

**wa kanu mujrimin**.

They were criminals.

The Qur'an does not let luxury remain morally neutral once it becomes an accomplice to fasad.

### 3. **Muslihun** and the Mercy Hidden in the Principle of Non-Destruction

Then comes the principle:

**wa ma kana rabbuka li-yuhlika al-qura bi-zulmin wa ahluha muslihun**.

Your Lord would not destroy towns unjustly while their people are reforming.

[PAUSE]

Notice the choice of word. The verse does not only say **salihun** - personally righteous. It says **muslihun** - reforming, setting things right, actively repairing.

That is immense. The surah is teaching that Allah's way with societies is not detached from whether there are people still pushing back against disorder, still working repair into public life, still refusing to let corruption become the final language of the land.

This does not mean a society must be sinless. It means the line of islah must still be alive.

### 4. Difference, Mercy, and the Final Sorting

Then the passage widens beyond one civilization:

**wa law sha'a rabbuka la-ja'ala al-nasa ummatan wahidah wa la yazaluna mukhtalifin**.

Had your Lord willed, He could have made humanity one community. But they do not cease differing.

[PAUSE]

This gives perspective. Human difference is not happening outside Allah's sovereignty. He could have erased it. He did not. The world we inhabit is one in which divergence, fracture, and opposing paths persist by His wisdom.

Then comes the exception that matters more than the rule:

**illa man rahima rabbuk**.

Except the one your Lord has shown mercy.

The tafsir keeps us careful here: the truly rescued are those whom Allah's mercy gathers onto truth instead of leaving them scattered in ruinous difference.

Then the verse says:

**wa li-dhalika khalaqahum**.

And for that He created them.

One cautious way to carry this line, in light of the commentators, is that Allah created humanity within this wise unfolding of difference, sorting, mercy, and final distinction between the saved and the lost. The verse does not license speculation. It humbles us before divine wisdom.

And then the passage closes with terrifying finality:

**la-amla'anna jahannama mina al-jinnati wa al-nasi ajma'in**.

I will surely fill Jahannam with jinn and human beings altogether.

Mercy is real. Rescue is real. But so is the completed word against those who persist in rebellion.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Civilizations Often Die Because Too Few People Keep Enough Truth Left in Them

There is a kind of social failure deeper than corruption itself. It is the disappearance of the people who still feel responsible to confront it.

[PAUSE]

When a culture becomes saturated with ease, image, appetite, and collective self-protection, moral people are tempted into one of two retreats. Some go inward and keep their goodness private. Others remain publicly present but are slowly trained not to disturb the atmosphere. Both retreats create the same result: fasad keeps spreading because too few **ulu baqiyyah** remain.

Hud asks whether we are becoming the kind of people who still have enough left in us to interrupt decay. Not merely to observe it. Not merely to criticize it from a safe distance. But to actually stand in its way.

### Theme 2: What Protects a Society Is Not Innocence but Islah

The verse about destruction is profoundly hopeful. Allah does not say He only preserves a people who have no wrong at all. He says He does not destroy while they are **muslihun**.

[PAUSE]

That means a society may still be wounded, uneven, sinful, and struggling, yet remain under a different horizon if the line of repair is alive. If there are still people reconciling, forbidding harm, restoring justice, resisting corruption, and calling one another back to Allah, the surah tells us that such work is not marginal. It is one of the things that stands between a people and ruin.

This should change how we look at ordinary acts of repair. Honest correction, community care, teaching truth, resisting exploitation, returning people to prayer, refusing to flatter injustice - all of this belongs to **islah**. And Hud gives that work civilizational weight.

## Closing Synthesis

The earlier peoples were not only ruined by their sins; they were also ruined by the scarcity of people with enough moral remainder to forbid corruption before it took over. Those who wronged were carried by the luxury that had softened and captured them. Allah, however, does not destroy towns unjustly while their people are actively reforming. Human beings will continue to differ, except those whom Allah gathers under mercy, and the whole matter still ends under a fulfilled divine word: Jahannam will be filled by those who reject that mercy.

[PAUSE]

This passage leaves us with both burden and relief. The burden is that moral remainder must become moral action. The relief is that islah matters, mercy matters, and collapse is not narrated as random.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with these ayahs is to ask: in the environments I live in, what forms of corruption have become so normalized that people no longer expect resistance? What comforts have become pedagogies of silence? Am I preserving faith privately while withdrawing from islah publicly? And when I look at human difference, do I treat it as a puzzle to master, or as a condition in which I beg Allah for mercy to keep me on truth?

[PAUSE]

Hud leaves us with a hard, necessary hope: **a society is not preserved by prosperity alone, but by the remnant that still resists corruption and by the mercy of Allah that gathers people out of ruin and onto truth.**
