# Faith Ripens into Ikhbat, and the Two Ways of Perceiving Can Never Be the Same

## Surah Hud (11:23-24)

[ayah:11:23] إِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ وَعَمِلُوا۟ ٱلصَّـٰلِحَـٰتِ وَأَخْبَتُوٓا۟ إِلَىٰ رَبِّهِمْ أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ أَصْحَـٰبُ ٱلْجَنَّةِ ۖ هُمْ فِيهَا خَـٰلِدُونَ

[ayah:11:24] ۞ مَثَلُ ٱلْفَرِيقَيْنِ كَٱلْأَعْمَىٰ وَٱلْأَصَمِّ وَٱلْبَصِيرِ وَٱلسَّمِيعِ ۚ هَلْ يَسْتَوِيَانِ مَثَلًا ۚ أَفَلَا تَذَكَّرُونَ

*Inna alladhina amanu wa 'amilu al-salihati wa akhbatu ila rabbihim ula'ika ashabu al-jannah hum fiha khalidun.*

*Mathalu al-fariqayni ka-al-a'ma wa al-asammi wa al-basiri wa al-sami'. Hal yastawiyani mathalan. A-fa-la tadhakkarun.*

**Indeed, those who believe, do righteous deeds, and humble themselves inwardly to their Lord, those are the companions of Paradise; they will remain in it forever. The likeness of the two groups is like the blind and the deaf, and the seeing and the hearing. Are they equal in likeness? Will you not then take heed?**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

After severe warnings, the Qur'an often gives the heart a place to breathe.

Not by softening the truth, but by showing its opposite fulfilled in human form. After the people who block Allah's path, bend it, lose themselves, and arrive as the greatest losers, Hud now turns to another company entirely. The transition matters. The surah does not want us to live only in the shadow of what is ruined. It wants us to see what a rightly formed soul actually looks like.

[PAUSE]

That soul is not described by belief alone, nor by action alone. It is described by belief, righteous action, and then a third thing that reaches inward: **wa akhbatu ila rabbihim**. They made ikhbat to their Lord. They settled low, softened, and inclined inwardly toward Him. Then the whole contrast is made unforgettable by the parable of two kinds of perception: blind and deaf, seeing and hearing.

The hook of the passage is this: **Hud teaches that faith is completed by an inward humbled return to Allah, and that the difference between the people of truth and falsehood is not cosmetic but as radical as the difference between blindness and sight, deafness and hearing.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Amanu... 'amilu al-salihat... akhbatu ila rabbihim**: The Believing Self Does Not Only Agree; It Softens and Returns

Ayah 23 gives three marks:

**alladhina amanu** - those who believed.

**wa 'amilu al-salihat** - and did righteous deeds.

**wa akhbatu ila rabbihim** - and made ikhbat to their Lord.

[PAUSE]

The first two are familiar to us from many places in the Qur'an: faith and righteous action. But **akhbatu** deserves lingering attention. The root carries a sense tied to low, settled ground, and from that comes a spiritual meaning of humble quietness, loweredness, inward yielding, a calm submissiveness before Allah.

This is beautiful because it shows us that Islam is not complete at the level of doctrinal agreement and outward performance alone. There must also be a certain posture of the interior. Not swagger before Allah. Not transactional religiosity before Allah. Not merely correct language before Allah. Ikhbat.

It is the soul becoming receptive, unpretentious, no longer trying to stand tall against its Lord.

[PAUSE]

And notice the wording:

**ila rabbihim**.

To their Lord.

The movement is relational. They do not merely become meek in the abstract. Their hearts incline toward the One who nurtures, sustains, and owns them.

### 2. **Ashabu al-jannah hum fiha khalidun**: Paradise Is Given to a Certain Kind of Formed Person

Then the ayah says:

**ula'ika ashabu al-jannah**.

Those are the companions of Paradise.

[PAUSE]

The word **ashab** suggests belonging, company, settled association. Paradise is not being described as a random prize handed to disconnected people. It is the home of those whose souls have already been shaped for it by iman, righteous action, and ikhbat.

Then:

**hum fiha khalidun**.

They will remain there forever.

This permanence matters after the fragility of dunya and the collapse described earlier in the passage. Falsehood cannot hold. Invented securities vanish. But the people of faith and ikhbat are given a home that does not dissolve.

### 3. **Mathalu al-fariqayn**: The Difference Between the Two Groups Is a Difference in Perception

Ayah 24 then summarizes the whole contrast:

**mathalu al-fariqayni ka-al-a'ma wa al-asammi wa al-basiri wa al-sami'**.

The likeness of the two groups is like the blind and the deaf, and the seeing and the hearing.

[PAUSE]

This is striking. The Qur'an does not reduce the difference merely to legal status or tribal belonging. It makes the contrast perceptual. One group is cut off from reality at the level of receiving it. The other is awake to it.

Blindness and deafness here are not simply physical descriptions. They name modes of relation to truth. The blind one does not see the signs. The deaf one does not receive the warning. The seeing and hearing one is alive to what Allah is showing and saying.

Then the ayah asks:

**hal yastawiyani mathalan**.

Are they equal in likeness?

Of course not. The question is rhetorical, but it is also merciful. It presses the conscience to admit what the ego sometimes tries to blur.

And then:

**a-fa-la tadhakkarun**.

Will you not then take heed?

[PAUSE]

The goal of the contrast is remembrance, not mere categorization. We are supposed to let the image find us and ask which mode of perception we are nurturing in ourselves.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Heart Needs More Than Correctness; It Needs Ikhbat

Many people can imagine religion in two dimensions only: what to believe and what to do.

These are essential, but Hud adds a third dimension that may be even more diagnostic in some moments: what kind of inward posture does the soul hold before Allah?

[PAUSE]

A person may know the right things and even perform serious outward acts while still remaining internally hard, proud, argumentative with Allah, restless for recognition, or subtly resistant. Ikhbat is the opposite of that. It is a softened gravity of heart. A lowered interior. A willingness to be small before the Lord without humiliation, because the soul has found safety in belonging to Him.

This gives us a helpful test. When knowledge increases, does the heart become gentler before Allah or merely more armed? When deeds increase, does the soul become humbler or merely more self-aware? If faith is real, it should be ripening into ikhbat.

### Theme 2: The Ultimate Divide Between People Is Whether They Are Becoming More Able to Perceive Truth

Ayah 24 is also deeply practical. It tells us that the two groups are separated by what they can receive.

Some people keep moving through life with their senses spiritually deadened. Signs pass by them. Warnings do not enter. Beauty does not lead them to gratitude. Mortality does not lead them to seriousness. Revelation does not lead them to surrender.

[PAUSE]

Others become more seeing and more hearing over time. A verse reaches them. A warning wakens them. A mercy humbles them. A hardship clarifies them. A blessing deepens their thankfulness. They are not perfect, but they are perceptive.

That means our real question is not only "Which side am I on?" but "What is happening to my capacity to hear and see?" If a person is becoming more defensive, more numb, more evasive, more unable to receive the Qur'an, then something serious is happening no matter how respectable the outer form appears.

If a person is becoming more receptive, more tender, more remembering, more inwardly lowered to Allah, then that is a sign of life.

## Closing Synthesis

Hud answers the ruin of the previous ayahs by describing another kind of person altogether: people who believe, do righteous deeds, and make ikhbat to their Lord. Because their faith has reached the interior, they are named the companions of Paradise and promised abiding life there.

[PAUSE]

Then the surah reduces the whole moral contrast to a parable of perception: blind and deaf on one side, seeing and hearing on the other. They are not alike, and the Qur'an asks us to remember that difference before we flatten it away.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with these ayahs is to ask: has my religion reached the level of ikhbat, or am I still trying to remain inwardly tall before Allah? Am I becoming more able to hear and see the truth when it comes, or more numb to it? And what kind of person am I slowly being made into by the way I respond to revelation?

[PAUSE]

Hud leaves us with this promise and this mirror: **the people of iman are not only those who know and do, but those who soften themselves toward their Lord, and that inward yielding changes everything about what they are able to hear, see, and finally become.**
