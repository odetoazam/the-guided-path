# When Dunya and Its Adornment Are the Real Intention, the Deed May Pay Here and Still Arrive Empty There

## Surah Hud (11:15-16)

[ayah:11:15] مَن كَانَ يُرِيدُ ٱلْحَيَوٰةَ ٱلدُّنْيَا وَزِينَتَهَا نُوَفِّ إِلَيْهِمْ أَعْمَـٰلَهُمْ فِيهَا وَهُمْ فِيهَا لَا يُبْخَسُونَ

[ayah:11:16] أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ ٱلَّذِينَ لَيْسَ لَهُمْ فِى ٱلْـَٔاخِرَةِ إِلَّا ٱلنَّارُ ۖ وَحَبِطَ مَا صَنَعُوا۟ فِيهَا وَبَـٰطِلٌ مَّا كَانُوا۟ يَعْمَلُونَ

*Man kana yuridu al-hayata al-dunya wa zinataha nuwaffi ilayhim a'malahum fiha wa hum fiha la yubkhasun.*

*Ula'ika alladhina laysa lahum fi al-akhirati illa al-nar. Wa habita ma sana'u fiha wa batilun ma kanu ya'malun.*

**Whoever intends the worldly life and its adornment, We will fully repay them for their deeds in it, and they in it will not be deprived. Those are the ones for whom there is nothing in the Hereafter but the Fire. What they made there has come to nothing, and false is what they used to do.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

These ayahs are among the most frightening passages in the Qur'an about intention.

Not because they say worldly success is unreal. In one sense, they say the opposite. People may work, strive, build, achieve, and receive exactly what they were aiming at in this life. Their efforts can produce recognizable results. The door of worldly payout is not imaginary. The ayah even says they are not wronged in it. They are not cheated out of what their intention was actually pursuing.

[PAUSE]

That is what makes the passage so severe. The danger is not only in obvious failure. The danger is in succeeding at the wrong thing. The danger is getting what the self truly wanted and then discovering that what it wanted had no surviving weight before Allah.

Hud names the problem with startling precision: **man kana yuridu al-hayata al-dunya wa zinataha**. Whoever was wanting the worldly life and its adornment. The issue is not merely that a person lives in dunya. The issue is what they are really after through their deeds.

The hook of the passage is this: **Hud teaches that a deed can succeed fully in dunya and still arrive bankrupt in the akhirah if the real intention driving it was the worldly life and its adornment rather than Allah.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Yuridu al-hayata al-dunya wa zinataha**: The Problem Is the Will's True Direction

The ayah begins:

**man kana yuridu al-hayata al-dunya wa zinataha**.

Whoever was wanting the worldly life and its adornment.

[PAUSE]

The verb **yuridu** from the root **ر و د** is crucial. This is not just contact with dunya. It is directed wanting. Intention. Aim. Pursuit.

And what is pursued?

**al-hayata al-dunya** - the near life, the lower life, the immediate field.

**wa zinataha** - and its adornment, its beautified surface, its forms of prestige, attractiveness, polish, display, and desirability.

The verse is diagnosing a heart-level orientation. A person may do things that look externally noble, disciplined, sacrificial, or even religious, while the inner will is still bent toward dunya and its adornment. Applause. standing. relevance. recognition. influence. being seen a certain way. Having the beautiful form of success around the self.

[PAUSE]

That is why this passage is so unsettling. It is not aimed only at openly worldly people. It reaches the person whose actions could easily be mistaken for devotion while the will underneath them is still bargaining for dunya.

### 2. **Nuwaffi ilayhim a'malahum fiha**: Allah Does Not Wrong Them in the Field They Chose

Then the ayah says:

**nuwaffi ilayhim a'malahum fiha**.

We fully repay them their deeds in it.

[PAUSE]

The verb **nuwaffi** from **و ف ي** carries the sense of giving in full, completing, not reducing. The verse does not say Allah necessarily withholds all worldly fruit from deeds rooted in worldly intention. On the contrary, He may allow the person to reap exactly the kind of return they were laboring toward.

Then it makes the point even clearer:

**wa hum fiha la yubkhasun**.

And they in it are not deprived, not diminished, not shortchanged.

The passive verb **yubkhasun** from **ب خ س** means they are not cut short in the domain they chose. This is part of divine justice. If a person wanted dunya and worked for dunya, Allah may let them have dunya's payout without injustice.

[PAUSE]

This is spiritually shattering because it means worldly success is not proof of divine acceptance. Achievement can be real and still not mean what the ego wants it to mean. Doors opening are not always endorsements. Results are not identical to reward in the آخِرَة.

Some of the most dangerous deceptions in religion begin when visible fruit is mistaken for final approval.

### 3. **Laysa lahum fi al-akhirati illa al-nar**: The Real Loss Appears Where Intentions Are Finally Weighed

Then the next ayah turns the whole picture:

**ula'ika alladhina laysa lahum fi al-akhirati illa al-nar**.

Those are the ones for whom there is nothing in the Hereafter except the Fire.

[PAUSE]

The absolute force of this sentence is terrifying. All the fullness named in verse 15 is now followed by a void in the آخِرَة except for fire. The person was not deprived in dunya, but what they were gathering never crossed over.

Then the verse says:

**wa habita ma sana'u fiha**.

And what they made there has come to nothing.

The verb **habita** from **ح ب ط** is one of the Qur'an's most devastating verbs. It names collapse, nullification, the swelling corruption that leaves something ruined and without benefit. A thing can look full and turn out spoiled.

And notice the wording **ma sana'u** - what they made. Their construction, their crafted work, their produced form. The ayah is not saying they did nothing. It is saying what they made did not survive.

[PAUSE]

Then it closes:

**wa batilun ma kanu ya'malun**.

And false is what they used to do.

This is deeper than failed output. **Batil** means void, unsubstantial, without lasting truth. The whole action-complex becomes exposed as empty at the level that finally matters, because it was detached from Allah from the beginning.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Getting What You Wanted Can Be a Form of Judgment

One of the hardest spiritual truths to accept is that not every fulfilled desire is mercy in the deepest sense.

Sometimes the most frightening thing Allah can do for a person is to let them have, in this life, exactly what they truly wanted from their deeds. Reputation. success. wealth. influence. admiration. the beautiful outer form.

[PAUSE]

Why is that frightening? Because if the person mistakes that payout for proof that all is well, they may never ask the more dangerous question: what was I really doing this for?

Hud teaches us to fear success without sincerity.

There is a kind of failure that is obvious and humiliating, and because it is obvious it can drive a person back to Allah quickly.

Then there is a kind of "success" that feeds the nafs, stabilizes the illusion, and lets a person keep building on a false foundation until the آخِرَة exposes everything at once.

That is why these ayahs are not anti-world in a simplistic way. They are anti-idolatry of the world at the level of intention. Dunya is dangerous not only when it is loved grossly, but when it quietly becomes the hidden currency behind even our seemingly serious deeds.

### Theme 2: The Surviving Question Is Not Merely "What Did You Do?" but "What Were You Seeking Through It?"

The passage forces us to refine our moral imagination.

We often judge ourselves and others primarily by visible action. Did the project succeed? Did the person work hard? Did something beneficial happen? Did it look impressive? Was there sacrifice, discipline, output?

[PAUSE]

But Hud pushes deeper. What was being sought through the work?

That is a frightening question because it can expose impurities hidden under very respectable forms. A person can preach, build, teach, give, write, lead, serve, sacrifice, and still be quietly aiming at adornment.

And zinah is subtle. It is not always crude money or sensuality. Sometimes it is the adornment of being admired as sincere. Sometimes the adornment of being seen as indispensable. Sometimes the adornment of having a beautiful religious identity around the ego.

[PAUSE]

This is why the ending matters so much. **Habita** and **batil** tell us that the آخِرَة does not merely replay the visible appearance of deeds. It reveals their truth-value. It shows whether the work had Allah in it.

That should make us gentler with ourselves in one sense and harsher in another.

Gentler, because we stop panicking if something appears small in dunya while still hoping it may be immense with Allah.

Harsher, because we stop being easily soothed by visible success when the heart has not been honestly examined.

## Closing Synthesis

Hud says that whoever truly wants the worldly life and its adornment may be given the full payout of deeds in this world without being cheated. The success can be real. The results can be tangible. The appearance can be full.

[PAUSE]

Then the veil is removed. In the Hereafter they have nothing but the Fire. What they made has collapsed. What they used to do is exposed as batil. The same work that looked full in الدنيا is shown to be empty where final value is weighed.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with these ayahs is to ask: what do I actually want from the things I do? If Allah gave me the visible result but withheld His acceptance, would I still feel I had "won"? Where has zinah become the hidden companion of my effort? And what am I doing to purify intention before the آخِرَة names the truth of it for me?

[PAUSE]

Hud leaves us with this severe mercy: **a deed may be fully paid out in dunya and still perish in the آخِرَة if its real aim was the world and its adornment rather than Allah.**
