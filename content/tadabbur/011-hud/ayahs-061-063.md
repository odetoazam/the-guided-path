# Salih Calls Thamud Back to the Lord Who Raised Them from the Earth, but They Answer with Injured Familiarity

## Surah Hud (11:61-63)

[ayah:11:61] ۞ وَإِلَىٰ ثَمُودَ أَخَاهُمْ صَـٰلِحًا ۚ قَالَ يَـٰقَوْمِ ٱعْبُدُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ مَا لَكُم مِّنْ إِلَـٰهٍ غَيْرُهُۥ ۖ هُوَ أَنشَأَكُم مِّنَ ٱلْأَرْضِ وَٱسْتَعْمَرَكُمْ فِيهَا فَٱسْتَغْفِرُوهُ ثُمَّ تُوبُوٓا۟ إِلَيْهِ ۚ إِنَّ رَبِّى قَرِيبٌ مُّجِيبٌ

[ayah:11:62] قَالُوا۟ يَـٰصَـٰلِحُ قَدْ كُنتَ فِينَا مَرْجُوًّا قَبْلَ هَـٰذَآ ۖ أَتَنْهَىٰنَآ أَن نَّعْبُدَ مَا يَعْبُدُ ءَابَآؤُنَا وَإِنَّنَا لَفِى شَكٍّ مِّمَّا تَدْعُونَآ إِلَيْهِ مُرِيبٍ

[ayah:11:63] قَالَ يَـٰقَوْمِ أَرَءَيْتُمْ إِن كُنتُ عَلَىٰ بَيِّنَةٍ مِّن رَّبِّى وَءَاتَىٰنِى مِنْهُ رَحْمَةً فَمَن يَنصُرُنِى مِنَ ٱللَّهِ إِنْ عَصَيْتُهُۥ ۖ فَمَا تَزِيدُونَنِى غَيْرَ تَخْسِيرٍ

*Wa ila Thamuda akhahum Saliha. Qala ya qawmi u'budu Allaha ma lakum min ilahin ghayruhu. Huwa ansha'akum min al-ardi wa-sta'marakum fiha fa-staghfiruhu thumma tubu ilayh. Inna rabbi qaribun mujib.*

*Qalu ya Salihu qad kunta fina marjuwwan qabla hadha. A-tanhana an na'buda ma ya'budu aba'una wa innana lafi shakkin mimma tad'una ilayhi murib.*

*Qala ya qawmi ara'aytum in kuntu 'ala bayyinatin min rabbi wa atani minhu rahmatan fa-man yansuruni min Allahi in 'asaytuhu fa-ma tazidunani ghayra takhsir.*

**And to Thamud, their brother Salih. He said, "O my people, worship Allah; you have no god other than Him. He brought you forth from the earth and settled you in it, so seek His forgiveness, then turn to Him in repentance. Indeed, my Lord is near and responsive." They said, "O Salih, you had been among us one in whom hope was placed before this. Do you forbid us from worshipping what our fathers worshipped? Indeed, we are in troubling doubt about that to which you call us." He said, "O my people, have you considered: if I stand upon a clear proof from my Lord and He has given me mercy from Himself, then who could protect me from Allah if I disobeyed Him? So you would not increase me except in loss."**

## Introduction

Some rejections are openly hostile from the first line. Others are wounded.

Thamud's first answer to Salih is not only, "We reject you." It is, "We expected something else from you." That makes this exchange especially human. A familiar man, one once hoped in, begins to call his people away from inherited worship and back to Allah alone. Their resistance therefore carries not just argument, but the ache of disrupted expectation. Revelation has entered the circle of trust and undone what that circle assumed trust was for.

[PAUSE]

Salih's opening is beautiful because it grounds tawhid in origin, habitation, repentance, and divine nearness. Allah is the One who brought you forth from the earth, settled you in it, and remains near and responsive. But the people answer from ancestry and injured familiarity. Salih then lifts the matter back to bayyinah, rahmah, and the impossibility of obeying people by disobeying Allah.

The hook of the passage is this: **Hud teaches through Salih that the Lord who raised us from the earth and settled us within it deserves exclusive worship and repentant return, but communities often answer revelation through ancestral attachment and disappointed expectations rather than through surrendered reasoning.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Ansha'akum min al-ard wa-sta'marakum fiha**: Tawhid Is Grounded in Origin and Entrustment

Salih says:

**huwa ansha'akum min al-ard wa-sta'marakum fiha**.

He brought you forth from the earth and settled you in it.

[PAUSE]

This is a profound grounding for worship. Allah is not being introduced as a distant deity demanding abstract devotion. He is being named as the One who originated their very being from the earth and gave them habitation, cultivation, and life within it.

The verb **ansha'a** carries raising up, bringing into emergence. Then **ista'marakum** suggests causing them to inhabit, cultivate, build within the earth. Their entire civilizational life rests on a gift already given.

So tawhid here is not detached creed. It is gratitude disciplined into worship. The One who gave you origin and settlement is the One to whom your life should return.

### 2. **Fa-staghfiruhu thumma tubu ilayh. Inna rabbi qaribun mujib**: Repentance Is Invited by Nearness, Not Only by Threat

Then Salih says:

**fa-staghfiruhu thumma tubu ilayh**.

So seek His forgiveness, then turn to Him in repentance.

[PAUSE]

Again we see the pairing: forgiveness for what has passed, turning for what lies ahead.

But then comes one of the most tender endings to a prophetic call:

**inna rabbi qaribun mujib**.

Indeed, my Lord is near and responsive.

The names matter. **Qarib** - near. **Mujib** - answering, responsive to the one who calls.

This means repentance is not being preached into distance. The Lord they are being asked to return to is near enough to hear and responsive enough to answer.

### 3. **Qad kunta fina marjuwwan qabla hadha**: The Pain of Broken Social Expectation

The people say:

**qad kunta fina marjuwwan qabla hadha**.

You had been among us one in whom hope was placed before this.

[PAUSE]

This is revealing. They are not denying that Salih had stature, intelligence, or goodness among them. In fact, that is part of what unsettles them. They hoped in him - but hoped for what? Presumably for a future that would fit their inherited world, not overturn its worship.

Communities often welcome admirable people as long as their admirability serves continuity. The problem begins when the trusted person becomes a conveyor of revelation that confronts ancestral normality.

### 4. **A-tanhana an na'buda ma ya'budu aba'una**: Ancestral Worship as Emotional Argument

Then they say:

**a-tanhana an na'buda ma ya'budu aba'una**.

Do you forbid us from worshipping what our fathers worshipped?

[PAUSE]

Notice how the weight of the sentence rests on **aba'una** - our fathers. They are not leading with proof. They are leading with inheritance. The emotional charge comes from continuity, belonging, and identity.

Then:

**wa innana lafi shakkin mimma tad'una ilayhi murib**.

Indeed, we are in troubling doubt about that to which you call us.

The doubt is not presented as clean intellectual caution. **Murib** carries disturbance, troubling uncertainty, doubt that agitates.

### 5. **Fa-man yansuruni min Allahi in 'asaytuhu**: The Messenger Cannot Preserve Social Trust by Betraying Allah

Salih answers:

**in kuntu 'ala bayyinatin min rabbi wa atani minhu rahmatan fa-man yansuruni min Allahi in 'asaytuhu**.

If I stand upon a clear proof from my Lord and He has given me mercy from Himself, then who could protect me from Allah if I disobeyed Him?

[PAUSE]

This is a magnificent reorientation. The people's concern is social betrayal. Salih's concern is divine betrayal. They are asking, "How could you become this?" He is asking, "How could I disobey Allah once He has given clear proof and mercy?"

Then he closes:

**fa-ma tazidunani ghayra takhsir**.

So you would not increase me except in loss.

Your pressure does not offer gain. It only multiplies loss if I follow it.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: People Often Prefer the Familiar Good Person to the Truthful Messenger

There is a kind of approval communities love to give: "You are promising, admirable, reasonable, beneficial." But often that approval has hidden terms. Stay within the accepted script. Be good in a way that does not challenge inherited worship. Be noble without becoming revelatory.

[PAUSE]

That is why "You had been hoped in before this" can sound flattering while actually being a form of resistance. It means: we liked you when your goodness belonged to our expectations. We are troubled now that it belongs to Allah.

This pattern is painfully current. The moment a trusted person begins naming what revelation demands, people who once praised them may feel betrayed - not because the person became less moral, but because the moral clarity now threatens ancestral, cultural, or communal comfort.

### Theme 2: The Real Question in Moments of Pressure Is Whom Disobedience Would Actually Offend

Salih's answer is one of the great spiritual clarifiers in the Qur'an.

The people want him to fear social rupture. Salih fears disobeying Allah. That is the decisive difference between prophethood and popularity.

[PAUSE]

We live inside this tension constantly. Family expectation, communal identity, old loyalties, inherited customs, and emotional debts can all exert pressure. They make the costly path feel like betrayal. But the believer has to ask the Salih-question: if Allah has made something clear and granted mercy through guidance, then who will protect me from Him if I disobey Him just to preserve human approval?

That question cuts through immense confusion.

## Closing Synthesis

Salih calls Thamud to worship Allah alone, grounding the call in the Lord who brought them forth from the earth and settled them in it. He urges them toward istighfar and tawbah, and describes his Lord as near and responsive. They answer by telling him he had once been one hoped in, and by framing his call as an intolerable break from the worship of their fathers. Salih replies by returning everything to clear proof, divine mercy, and the impossibility of obeying people through disobedience to Allah.

[PAUSE]

The exchange is searching because it exposes how often revelation is resisted not only by crude denial, but by disappointed familiarity and inherited belonging. A prophet from among them becomes intolerable precisely because he refuses to leave the sacred center where the ancestors placed it.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with these ayahs is to ask: where have I confused ancestral comfort with truth? Do I only welcome religious voices that flatter expectations I already had? And when clear proof from Allah reaches me, do I feel more fear of upsetting people or of disobeying the Lord who is near and responsive?

[PAUSE]

Hud leaves us with this living question: **the Lord who gave us origin, habitation, and the open door of repentance deserves our worship above inherited habit, and once His bayyinah is clear, no human expectation is worth the loss that comes from disobeying Him.**
