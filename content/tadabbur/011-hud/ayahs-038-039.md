# He Builds While They Mock, but the Shame Will Soon Reverse

## Surah Hud (11:38-39)

[ayah:11:38] وَيَصْنَعُ ٱلْفُلْكَ وَكُلَّمَا مَرَّ عَلَيْهِ مَلَأٌ مِّن قَوْمِهِۦ سَخِرُوا۟ مِنْهُ ۚ قَالَ إِن تَسْخَرُوا۟ مِنَّا فَإِنَّا نَسْخَرُ مِنكُمْ كَمَا تَسْخَرُونَ

[ayah:11:39] فَسَوْفَ تَعْلَمُونَ مَن يَأْتِيهِ عَذَابٌ يُخْزِيهِ وَيَحِلُّ عَلَيْهِ عَذَابٌ مُّقِيمٌ

*Wa yasna'u al-fulka wa kullama marra 'alayhi mala'un min qawmihi sakhiru minhu. Qala in taskharu minna fa-inna naskharu minkum kama taskharun.*

*Fa-sawfa ta'lamuna man ya'tihi 'adhabun yukhzihi wa yahillu 'alayhi 'adhabun muqim.*

**And he was building the Ark, and every time an eminent group from his people passed by him, they mocked him. He said, "If you mock us, then we will mock you just as you mock. And you will soon know upon whom there comes a punishment that disgraces him and upon whom settles a lasting punishment."**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

Truth often looks strangest in its construction phase.

Before the ark floats, it looks absurd. Before the flood arrives, the wood and labor look misplaced. Before the unseen breaks open into sight, obedience can appear to the crowd as embarrassment. And the crowd, almost by instinct, laughs at what it cannot yet interpret.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs place us inside that long, exposed middle. Nuh is not only commanded to build; he is watched while building. The elite pass by again and again, and every pass becomes another occasion for mockery. Yet Nuh does not collapse into shame. He answers with a certainty that refuses to borrow its self-understanding from the laughter of the powerful. The ridicule, he says, is temporary. The shame will not remain where you think it is.

The hook of the passage is this: **Hud teaches that obedience under mockery is one of the clearest tests of certainty, because the project revealed by Allah can look ridiculous before it becomes obviously salvific, and the final reversal exposes who was truly laughable all along.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Wa yasna'u al-fulk**: Salvation Is Being Built in Public

The ayah opens:

**wa yasna'u al-fulk**.

And he is building the Ark.

[PAUSE]

The verb is in the ongoing form. We are made to watch the process, not only the completed result. The Qur'an could have moved quickly to the flood. Instead it slows down and shows us the obedient labor itself.

That matters. Many of us want the story of salvation without the season of exposed construction. We want the ark once it has been vindicated, not when it still looks impossible. But Allah wanted Nuh's building to be seen, because part of the trial was precisely this: would he keep obeying while the command still looked socially absurd?

And the object is **al-fulk** - the Ark, the ship, the vessel. Not a vague act of trust, but something concrete, structured, worked on piece by piece. Revelation does not always save us by bypassing means. Sometimes it tells us to build the means with our own hands.

### 2. **Wa kullama marra 'alayhi mala'un ... sakhiru minhu**: The Elite Turn Repetition into Ritualized Mockery

Then the ayah says:

**wa kullama marra 'alayhi mala'un min qawmihi sakhiru minhu**.

And every time an eminent group from his people passed by him, they mocked him.

[PAUSE]

The phrase **kullama** is important. Every time. Not once. Not as an isolated incident. Repeatedly. Their passing itself becomes a repeated theatre of ridicule.

And again it is the **mala'** - the notable class, the socially full people, those whose public opinion carries weight. Prophetic opposition is rarely only private disbelief. It often becomes performed contempt. The elite help teach the rest of society what should be laughed at.

Then the verb:

**sakhiru**.

They mocked. They ridiculed. They treated obedience as comedy.

This is one of the ego's favorite weapons because mockery lets it maintain superiority without honest engagement. If you can make something look silly, you do not have to answer whether it is true.

### 3. **In taskharu minna fa-inna naskharu minkum**: The Meaning of "We Mock You" Is the Coming Exposure of False Confidence

Nuh answers:

**in taskharu minna fa-inna naskharu minkum kama taskharun**.

If you mock us, then we mock you just as you mock.

[PAUSE]

We have to hear this correctly. This is not petty ego trying to trade insults. Nuh has already been taught to build under revelation. His heart is not being trained into vanity.

So what does the answer mean? It means: the reality you are laughing at will soon laugh at your ignorance. Your present ridicule rests on a catastrophic misreading of the situation. When the command of Allah unfolds, what looked foolish will be shown wise, and what looked confident will be shown deluded.

In that sense, the "mockery" here is the exposure of false mockery. It is the coming reversal in which worldly confidence discovers that it had been laughing at its own rescue.

### 4. **'Adhabun yukhzihi ... 'adhabun muqim**: Shame Falls, Then It Stays

Ayah 39 completes the reversal:

**fa-sawfa ta'lamun**.

You will soon know.

[PAUSE]

There is no frantic argument here. Only patient future certainty. Knowledge is coming, but not by debate. By event.

Then:

**man ya'tihi 'adhabun yukhzihi**.

Upon whom there comes a punishment that disgraces him.

The verb **yukhzihi** comes from the field of **khizy** - disgrace, humiliation, exposure. This is more than pain. It is shame unveiled before others. Their mockery was public. Their disgrace will be public too.

Then the second clause:

**wa yahillu 'alayhi 'adhabun muqim**.

And upon whom settles a lasting punishment.

The verb **yahillu** suggests descent and settling, something arriving and taking its place. And **muqim** gives the sense of abiding, remaining, not passing quickly. So the ayah moves from public shame to enduring consequence. The reversal is not momentary embarrassment. It is a moral reality that stays.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Building What Allah Commanded Often Looks Awkward Before It Looks Wise

There are obediences in life that the world honors immediately. But some of the most important ones enter public view in an awkward stage.

You turn down the career path everyone admires because it is feeding your soul poison. You restructure your home around prayer, study, or halal constraints that others see as impractical. You leave a glamorous sin for a quieter, more disciplined life. You start rebuilding trust through repentance and boundaries after a long period of drift. In the early stage, these things can look odd. Slower. Less impressive. Even embarrassing.

[PAUSE]

That is where many people stop building. Not because they lost conviction in private, but because they could not bear being watched in the unfinished phase.

Nuh teaches another kind of steadiness. Build anyway. Let the mockery happen while the ark is still wood in dry land. If Allah commanded it, then the awkward stage is not evidence against the project. It may be part of the test by which your certainty is purified from dependence on human approval.

### Theme 2: Ridicule Is Often a Strategy for Protecting False Confidence

These ayahs also expose what mockery usually hides.

The proud do not always laugh because something is genuinely laughable. Sometimes they laugh because laughter protects them from the terror of taking the truth seriously. If they paused long enough to consider that Nuh might actually be right, their whole world would need to be reorganized. Mockery is cheaper.

[PAUSE]

We do this too, even if more subtly. We joke about the disciplined person because their discipline rebukes our looseness. We dismiss the scrupulous believer as extreme because their caution unsettles our compromises. We caricature the sincere because sincerity threatens our performance. Laughter becomes a shield against repentance.

But the Qur'an says that this shield does not hold. **Fa-sawfa ta'lamun.** You will soon know. A day comes when the fog of social performance burns off and the reality of things stands there without costume.

And this can be read inwardly as well. Sometimes the voice mocking the ark is inside us. The part of the self that laughs at serious change, that treats repentance plans as unrealistic, that calls disciplined obedience "too much." Nuh's answer belongs there too. Keep building. The laugh is not proof.

## Closing Synthesis

Nuh is building the Ark, and every time the elite pass by, they mock him. He does not surrender the meaning of his work to their laughter. He tells them that the ridicule will reverse, because knowledge is coming through event, not through their present mood.

[PAUSE]

Soon they will know who the punishment comes to, who public disgrace actually belongs to, and on whom the lasting punishment settles. The one building under revelation was never ridiculous. The ridiculous thing was mocking salvation while it was being prepared in front of you.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with these ayahs is to ask: what ark has Allah told me to keep building even while it still looks unfinished and strange? Where have I let fear of ridicule interrupt obedience? And what forms of mockery do I still use - outwardly or inwardly - to protect myself from taking Allah's command fully seriously?

[PAUSE]

Hud leaves us with this strengthening truth: **the command of Allah may look awkward before it looks triumphant, but ridicule cannot finally define reality, and the shame that matters is the one exposed when truth arrives in full.**
