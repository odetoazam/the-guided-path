# The Human Being Swings Between Despair and Boasting Until Sabr and Righteous Action Steady the Soul

## Surah Hud (11:9-11)

[ayah:11:9] وَلَئِنْ أَذَقْنَا ٱلْإِنسَـٰنَ مِنَّا رَحْمَةً ثُمَّ نَزَعْنَـٰهَا مِنْهُ إِنَّهُۥ لَيَـُٔوسٌ كَفُورٌ

[ayah:11:10] وَلَئِنْ أَذَقْنَـٰهُ نَعْمَآءَ بَعْدَ ضَرَّآءَ مَسَّتْهُ لَيَقُولَنَّ ذَهَبَ ٱلسَّيِّـَٔاتُ عَنِّىٓ ۚ إِنَّهُۥ لَفَرِحٌ فَخُورٌ

[ayah:11:11] إِلَّا ٱلَّذِينَ صَبَرُوا۟ وَعَمِلُوا۟ ٱلصَّـٰلِحَـٰتِ أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ لَهُم مَّغْفِرَةٌ وَأَجْرٌ كَبِيرٌ

*Wa la-in adhaqna al-insana minna rahmatan thumma naza'naha minhu innahu la-ya'usun kafur.*

*Wa la-in adhaqnahu na'ma'a ba'da darra'a massat-hu la-yaqulanna dhahaba al-sayyi'atu 'anni. Innahu la-farihun fakhur.*

*Illa alladhina sabaru wa 'amilu al-salihati ula'ika lahum maghfiratun wa ajrun kabir.*

**And if We let the human being taste mercy from Us and then remove it from him, indeed he is surely despairing and ungrateful. And if We let him taste favor after hardship has touched him, he will surely say, "The evils have gone away from me." Indeed, he is exultant and boastful. Except for those who are patient and do righteous deeds; those will have forgiveness and a great reward.**

## Introduction

These ayahs describe a rhythm many of us know painfully well.

When mercy is present, we quietly start acting as though it will remain because it is ours. When it is removed, we fall into a darkness that behaves as though the mercy was never from Allah to begin with and will never return again. Then when ease comes back after difficulty, instead of becoming humble, grateful, and softer, we often become inflated. We speak as if hardship has been permanently expelled and as if our restored state proves something flattering about us.

[PAUSE]

Hud captures that entire instability with devastating economy. The human being tastes mercy, then loses it, and becomes **ya'us kafur**. Then he tastes blessing after hardship and becomes **farih fakhur**. Despair when deprived. Boastfulness when relieved. The ayahs diagnose the swing itself as part of the problem.

Then the Qur'an opens a door out of the cycle: **illa alladhina sabaru wa 'amilu al-salihat**. Except those who were patient and did righteous deeds. The exception is not an altered emotional temperament only. It is a formed moral life.

The hook of the passage is this: **Hud teaches that the undisciplined self swings between despair in loss and vanity in relief, and that only sabr joined to righteous action can steady a person into a form that receives forgiveness and great reward.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Adhaqna... rahmatan**: Mercy Is Described as Something Tasted

The first ayah says:

**wa la-in adhaqna al-insana minna rahmatan**.

If We let the human being taste mercy from Us.

[PAUSE]

The verb **adhaqna** from the root **ذ و ق** is important. The ayah does not simply say "if We gave him mercy." It says "if We let him taste it." Taste is intimate, immediate, experiential. Mercy here is not an abstract doctrine. It is something lived, felt, enjoyed.

That matters because when mercy becomes familiar, we stop experiencing it as mercy. Health becomes normal. Provision becomes expected. emotional stability becomes baseline. Relationships, access, energy, safety, opportunity - all of it starts to feel like the natural shape of life rather than a tasted gift from Allah.

Then the verse says:

**thumma naza'naha minhu**.

Then We pull it away from him.

The root **ن ز ع** carries the sense of pulling out, withdrawing, stripping away. The mercy is not merely "absent." It is felt as something removed. The wording lets us feel the violence of loss from the human side.

[PAUSE]

And how does the human being respond?

**innahu la-ya'usun kafur**.

Indeed he is surely despairing and ungrateful.

**Ya'us** names a heavy despair, a hopelessness that settles into the chest and stops expecting good. **Kafur** intensifies ingratitude. Not merely failing to say thank you, but responding to loss by becoming blind to the fact that what was removed had once been a gift.

This is such a precise diagnosis. The person does not only suffer pain. They interpret pain badly. They move from "this mercy was from Allah and has now been taken" to "there is no future mercy for me." Their reading of Allah changes inside the loss.

### 2. **Adhaqnahu na'ma'a ba'da darra'a**: Relief Can Rot the Soul Too

Then the next ayah mirrors the first:

**wa la-in adhaqnahu na'ma'a ba'da darra'a massat-hu**.

If We let him taste favor after hardship had touched him.

[PAUSE]

Again the verb is **taste**. Ease too is experiential, embodied, felt on the inside.

And notice the contrast between **na'ma'** and **darra'**. Favor and hardship. Expansiveness and constriction. The ayah is mapping the swing of lived human experience.

Then comes the revealing speech:

**la-yaqulanna dhahaba al-sayyi'atu 'anni**.

He will surely say: the evils have gone away from me.

At one level that sounds harmless, even understandable. But the ayah is exposing something deeper. The person speaks as if the difficulty has vanished in a final way and as if its removal authorizes a new self-reading. There is no caution, no gratitude, no sense of lingering dependence, no humility before the One who changed the state.

[PAUSE]

So the verse names what comes next:

**innahu la-farih fakhur**.

Indeed he is exultant and boastful.

**Farih** here is not healthy joy. It is the swelling kind of gladness that forgets proportion. **Fakhur** is boastfulness, self-magnifying pride, a heart that converts received favor into material for self-display.

This means the Qur'an is teaching that hardship is not the only test. Relief is also a test. In fact, relief may reveal diseases hardship kept hidden. A person who looked broken in pain may become inflated in ease.

### 3. The Mirror Between the Two States: Loss and Gain Expose the Same Untrained Nafs

These ayahs are beautifully symmetrical.

In the first, mercy is tasted and then removed.

In the second, favor is tasted after hardship.

In the first, the response is inward collapse: **ya'us**.

In the second, the response is inflated expansion: **farih fakhur**.

[PAUSE]

The point is not that human beings should feel nothing in either case. The Qur'an does not ask for emotional deadness. It asks for trained reading of Allah. The problem is not sadness at loss or gladness at relief. The problem is what the self becomes in those conditions when it is unmoored from sabr, shukr, and righteous action.

One state says: mercy is gone, therefore I have no horizon.

The other says: harm is gone, therefore I am secure and important.

Both are distortions of servanthood. In both states the self misreads its condition because it is centered on itself rather than on Allah.

### 4. **Illa alladhina sabaru wa 'amilu al-salihat**: The Exception Is Built, Not Merely Felt

Then comes the rescue:

**illa alladhina sabaru wa 'amilu al-salihat**.

Except those who were patient and did righteous deeds.

[PAUSE]

The exception is decisive. The Qur'an refuses to leave us inside the diagnosis.

And notice what it uses to break the cycle.

First: **sabaru**. Patience, endurance, disciplined staying, restraint before Allah. Sabr is not passive numbness. It is moral steadiness under pressure and delayed relief.

Second: **'amilu al-salihat**. They did righteous deeds. Not merely "they had good intentions." Their sabr became embodied in action.

This matters deeply. The way out of emotional instability is not only a better inner monologue. It is formed obedience. Sabr keeps the soul from collapsing in hardship. Salihat keep the soul from decomposing in ease.

[PAUSE]

Then the reward:

**ula'ika lahum maghfiratun wa ajrun kabir**.

Those will have forgiveness and a great reward.

Forgiveness comes first. The one who learns steadiness is not being told they were always flawless. They still need Allah to cover and pardon. Then comes the great reward. The ayah ends in generosity, not mere diagnosis.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Soul Often Misreads Both Pain and Relief

One of the strongest insights in this passage is that the soul can fail on both sides of the curve.

We usually think of spiritual danger as belonging mainly to hardship. We imagine the test is whether we can survive pain without breaking. But Hud shows something sharper. The soul can be ruined by loss through despair, and it can be ruined by relief through arrogance.

[PAUSE]

That means we should distrust simplistic self-readings.

When we are deprived, we may tell ourselves our sadness is just honesty, when in fact some of it is **ya's** - a hopelessness that has started making false claims about Allah's mercy.

When we are relieved, we may tell ourselves our happiness is just gratitude, when in fact some of it is **faraḥ** and **fakhr** - a swelling self-satisfaction that treats received favor as personal distinction.

The ayahs train us to ask a deeper question than "How do I feel right now?" They ask: "What is this state making of me before Allah?"

### Theme 2: Sabr Is What Stops Hardship From Defining You, and صالحات Stop Ease From Corrupting You

The exception in ayah 11 is beautifully balanced.

Sabr is what keeps the soul from disintegrating when mercy is withdrawn.

Righteous action is what keeps the soul from becoming vain when favor returns.

[PAUSE]

This pairing is so wise. Some people can endure hardship, but once Allah opens ease for them, they become careless, indulgent, and proud. Others can behave well in ease but collapse morally when tested. The Qur'an wants a more complete servant.

Sabr without righteous action can become a private stoicism with no outward fruit.

Righteous action without sabr can collapse the moment the soul is pinched by loss, delay, or disappointment.

Together they create steadiness.

And that steadiness is not emotionally glamorous. It is often quiet. It looks like not giving up on obedience when the chest is dark. It looks like not becoming insufferable when life opens up again. It looks like remembering Allah in both climates.

[PAUSE]

That is why the ending is so hopeful. The Qur'an does not say the unstable human being is doomed to stay unstable. It says there is an exception. There is a formed type of person who is not ruled by the swing.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs describe the raw human tendency with painful precision. When mercy is tasted and then removed, the untrained self becomes despairing and ungrateful. When favor is tasted after hardship, the same self becomes exultant and boastful. Loss and gain expose different symptoms of the same instability.

[PAUSE]

Then the Qur'an opens the exception: those who practiced sabr and did righteous deeds. The cycle is broken not by pretending pain is easy or by pretending blessing is dangerous in itself, but by learning how to remain Allah's servant in both states. That is why the passage ends not in condemnation only, but in **maghfirah** and **ajr kabir**.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with these ayahs is to ask: when mercy is pulled away, do I begin making hopeless claims about Allah? And when ease returns, do I quietly start reading myself as secure, special, or above the condition of need? Where do I need sabr, and where do I need righteous action to save me from my own swing?

[PAUSE]

Hud leaves us with this searching truth: **the self that is not trained by sabr and righteous action will collapse in loss and inflate in relief, but the servant who learns steadiness before Allah will find forgiveness and a great reward beyond both states.**
