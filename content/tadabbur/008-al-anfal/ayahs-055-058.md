# When Treachery Repeats, Clarity Comes First

## Surah Al-Anfal (8:55-58)

[ayah:8:55] إِنَّ شَرَّ ٱلدَّوَآبِّ عِندَ ٱللَّهِ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ فَهُمْ لَا يُؤْمِنُونَ

[ayah:8:56] ٱلَّذِينَ عَـٰهَدتَّ مِنْهُمْ ثُمَّ يَنقُضُونَ عَهْدَهُمْ فِى كُلِّ مَرَّةٍ وَهُمْ لَا يَتَّقُونَ

[ayah:8:57] فَإِمَّا تَثْقَفَنَّهُمْ فِى ٱلْحَرْبِ فَشَرِّدْ بِهِم مَّنْ خَلْفَهُمْ لَعَلَّهُمْ يَذَّكَّرُونَ

[ayah:8:58] وَإِمَّا تَخَافَنَّ مِن قَوْمٍ خِيَانَةً فَٱنۢبِذْ إِلَيْهِمْ عَلَىٰ سَوَآءٍ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ لَا يُحِبُّ ٱلْخَآئِنِينَ

*Inna sharra al-dawabbi 'inda Allahi alladhina kafaru fa-hum la yu'minun.*

*Alladhina 'ahadtta minhum thumma yanquduna 'ahdahum fi kulli marratin wa hum la yattaqun.*

*Fa-imma tathqafannahum fi al-harbi fa-sharrid bihim man khalfahum la'allahum yadhdhakkarun.*

*Wa imma takhafanna min qawmin khiyanatan fa-inbidh ilayhim 'ala sawa'. Inna Allaha la yuhibbu al-kha'inin.*

**Indeed, the worst of living creatures in the sight of Allah are those who disbelieve, so they do not truly believe. They are those with whom you made a treaty, then they break their treaty every time, and they have no taqwa. And if you fear treachery from a people, then throw the treaty back to them on equal terms. Surely Allah does not love the treacherous.**

## Introduction

One of the hardest things to preserve in a broken world is moral clarity without naivety.

Some people betray once and repent. Some fail under fear and return to truth. But there is another kind of person the Qur'an forces us to face: the one who keeps using agreement as a temporary convenience, covenant as a disposable tool, and peace as something to exploit until betrayal becomes more profitable. At that point the issue is no longer simple weakness. Something deeper has gone wrong in the moral structure of the self.

[PAUSE]

These ayahs enter that hard terrain. They speak with unusual severity, calling such people the worst of living creatures in Allah's sight. But the passage is not raw anger. It is morally exact. It names what makes them so degraded: they repeatedly break covenant and do so without taqwa. Then it gives two instructions. If they are encountered in active war after this pattern of betrayal, they may be dealt with in a way that deters those behind them. But if betrayal is only feared, not yet openly enacted in battle, the believer is still forbidden from secret duplicity. The treaty must be cast back to them **'ala sawa'** - on equal, open, known terms.

That is one of the most remarkable ethical moves in the surah. The Qur'an refuses gullibility, but it also refuses mirror-treachery. It permits strength and preparedness without letting the believers become faithless in the process.

These verses matter intensely now because many people assume there are only two options in the face of repeated betrayal: be naive and get used, or become treacherous in return. The Qur'an offers a third way: lucid moral firmness. See the pattern. Stop pretending. Deter when war makes deterrence necessary. But do not betray in the dark. Make the rupture explicit, fair, and equally known.

The hook of the passage is this: **the Qur'an does not ask believers to survive treachery by becoming naive or by becoming treacherous themselves, but by holding onto clear, disciplined justice even in the shadow of repeated betrayal.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Sharra al-dawabbi**: Why Covenant-Breaking Can Degrade a Human Being Below Mere Animal Movement

Verse 55 opens with one of the strongest descriptions in the surah:

**inna sharra al-dawabbi 'inda Allah**.

Indeed, the worst of living creatures in the sight of Allah.

[PAUSE]

The wording is jarring on purpose. **Dawab** are moving creatures, beings that crawl, walk, or move across the earth. Why would a human being be described in that company and then judged the worst of them?

Because the issue here is not intelligence, civilization, or political sophistication. It is moral failure at the level of covenant and receptivity to truth. A beast acts by its nature. But a human being can receive signs, enter agreement, speak commitment, and then repeatedly shatter it. That repeated moral treachery is what makes the judgment so severe.

Then the verse explains:

**alladhina kafaru fa-hum la yu'minun**.

Those who disbelieve, and therefore they do not truly believe.

The point is not mere verbal unbelief. The next ayah immediately shows what this disbelief looks like socially: it cannot keep covenant with taqwa.

### 2. **'Ahadtta ... yanquduna 'ahdahum fi kulli marrah**: The Passage Is About Repeated Breach, Not One Isolated Failure

Verse 56 specifies the type:

**alladhina 'ahadtta minhum**.

Those with whom you made a treaty.

So this is not random hostility from strangers. There was a covenant. Terms were known. A public moral bond had already been entered.

Then:

**thumma yanquduna 'ahdahum fi kulli marrah**.

Then they break their treaty every time.

[PAUSE]

The repeated rhythm matters. **Fi kulli marrah** - in every instance, every round, every recurrence. This is not a single lapse followed by shame and restoration. This is a pattern.

The verb **yanquduna** is strong. To break, undo, unravel, nullify what had been tied. The covenant is treated as something woven and then deliberately taken apart.

And then the final diagnosis:

**wa hum la yattaqun**.

They have no taqwa.

That means the real problem is not only political unreliability. It is spiritual vacancy. There is no inward God-conscious brake keeping them from instrumentalizing every agreement.

### 3. **Tathqafannahum fi al-harb**: If Active War Reveals the Pattern, Firm Deterrence Is Permitted

Verse 57 turns to the wartime situation:

**fa-imma tathqafannahum fi al-harb**.

If you come upon them in war, if you catch up with them in the context of war.

[PAUSE]

This is already a limitation. The verse is not speaking about indiscriminate cruelty. It is speaking about a people already marked by recurring betrayal and encountered in the field of war.

Then:

**fa-sharrid bihim man khalfahum**.

Make of them a scattering warning for those behind them.

The language is severe because the reality is severe. At some point repeated treachery must become publicly consequential, otherwise betrayal trains the next betrayers that covenants are costless things to exploit. The goal, however, is not sadism. The verse itself states the purpose:

**la'allahum yadhdhakkarun**.

So that they may take heed.

Deterrence is meant to produce remembrance, recollection, moral learning.

### 4. **Takhafanna min qawmin khiyanatan**: Fear of Betrayal Does Not Yet License Betrayal

Then the next ayah introduces a subtler scenario:

**wa imma takhafanna min qawmin khiyanah**.

If you fear betrayal from a people.

[PAUSE]

This is not the same as being in active war with them already. It is the stage of credible fear, signs, expectation, the smell of impending treachery.

Now this is exactly where many souls become corrupt. They say: since we suspect betrayal, we may as well betray first in secret. We will be practical. We will strike from the shadows. We will abandon ethical clarity because we imagine ethics belong only to safer times.

The Qur'an refuses that move.

### 5. **Fa-inbidh ilayhim 'ala sawa'**: End the Covenant Openly, Equally, and Without Concealed Duplicity

The command is:

**fa-inbidh ilayhim 'ala sawa'**.

Then cast it back to them on equal terms.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the most morally elegant legal phrases in the surah.

**Inbidh** means to throw away, cast aside, set off from oneself. In context it means: do not remain inside a false pretense of treaty while you privately prepare treachery. Publicly return the pact. Make it known that the previous arrangement is no longer in force.

And the key phrase is:

**'ala sawa'**.

On equal footing. On a level field. In a way equally known to both sides. No hidden dagger inside a still-standing public agreement.

Then the verse closes:

**inna Allaha la yuhibbu al-kha'inin**.

Allah does not love the treacherous.

This means the believer is not permitted to preserve righteousness by violating it. Even when betrayal is feared, the line into khiyanah is still guarded by Allah.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Repeated Betrayal Is Not Only a Social Risk but a Spiritual Deformation

We often talk about treachery in purely strategic terms.

Can this person be trusted?
Will this party keep its word?
Is this agreement stable?

[PAUSE]

The Qur'an goes deeper. It says repeated betrayal changes what a person becomes. It reveals an interior where taqwa is absent and covenants are no longer sacred realities before Allah, only tools. That is why the verse's language is so severe. The degradation is moral before it is political.

That should sober us in our own smaller worlds too. Marriage promises. Business agreements. Friendships. Community trusts. Confidential responsibilities. A person who repeatedly treats trust as disposable is not only causing external damage. Something inside them is being trained away from taqwa.

### Theme 2: Islamic Strength Refuses Both Naivety and Hidden Duplicity

These ayahs also heal a common false binary.

People imagine that moral clarity is for innocence, while realism requires murky methods. Once you enter harder political or communal terrain, they say, you must either be simple-hearted and get deceived, or learn to deceive first.

[PAUSE]

The Qur'an says otherwise. You may recognize the pattern. You may prepare. You may deter. You may openly dissolve a treaty whose betrayal you now credibly fear. What you may not do is remain inside outward covenant while secretly practicing betrayal. Allah does not love the treacherous even when they tell themselves they are only being smart.

That is an enormous lesson. The believer's ethics are not suspended by the bad ethics of others. They are tested there.

## Closing Synthesis

These ayahs move with hard clarity.

They begin by naming repeated covenant-breakers as the worst of moving creatures in Allah's sight, because disbelief has matured in them into a pattern of faithless unraveling without taqwa. Then the surah permits firmness in active war so those behind may take heed. But when the matter is only feared betrayal, not yet open war, the Qur'an draws a luminous moral line: cast the treaty back openly, on equal terms, and do not become treacherous in order to survive treachery.

[PAUSE]

One way to sit with this unit is to ask: where in my life have I started treating trust as something conditional on convenience? When I fear being wronged, do I drift toward hidden duplicity and call it wisdom? Do I know how to combine alertness with fairness? And if a covenant truly must end, can I end it in a way that keeps my own soul from crossing into khiyanah?

[PAUSE]

The architecture of this unit lands here: **the Qur'an condemns repeated betrayal as a sign of deep moral collapse, allows disciplined deterrence when war exposes that pattern, and still insists that even suspected treachery must be answered with open, equal clarity rather than hidden betrayal in return.**
