## INTRODUCTION

[ayah:93:1] وَٱلضُّحَىٰ

[ayah:93:2] وَٱلَّيْلِ إِذَا سَجَىٰ

[ayah:93:3] مَا وَدَّعَكَ رَبُّكَ وَمَا قَلَىٰ

[ayah:93:4] وَلَلْءَاخِرَةُ خَيْرٌ لَّكَ مِنَ ٱلْأُولَىٰ

[ayah:93:5] وَلَسَوْفَ يُعْطِيكَ رَبُّكَ فَتَرْضَىٰٓ

*Wa-ḍ-ḍuḥā. Wa-l-layli idhā sajā. Mā wadda'aka rabbuka wa mā qalā. Wa la-l-ākhiratu khayrun laka mina-l-ūlā. Wa la-sawfa yu'ṭīka rabbuka fa-tarḍā.*

By the morning brightness. And by the night when it stills. Your Lord has not forsaken you, nor has He detested. And the Hereafter is better for you than the present. And your Lord will give you, and you will be satisfied.

---

The revelation stopped.

No one knows exactly how long. The narrations range from twelve days to forty. Some say months. What is agreed upon is this: there was a period — after years of Jibril arriving, after the Prophet ﷺ had become accustomed to the presence of the divine voice, after his entire world had been restructured around receiving and transmitting the words of Allah — when the words stopped coming.

Silence.

Not the silence between surahs, where the next revelation is simply pending. This was the kind of silence that felt like absence. Like someone who used to visit every week suddenly not coming. Not a pause. A void.

And the people of Quraysh noticed. A woman — reported in some narrations as Umm Jamil, the wife of Abu Lahab — said to the Prophet ﷺ: *"It seems your devil has abandoned you."*

Imagine that sentence landing on a man who had already lost nearly everything for the sake of what that voice was telling him. Who had been boycotted, mocked, called a madman and a poet. The one thing that made all of it bearable — the one thing that confirmed he was not delusional, that there was a reality on the other end of the line — was the revelation itself. And it went silent.

Into that silence — into the longest, heaviest pause of his life — this surah arrives.

And the first thing it does is not explain. Not justify the absence. Not give a reason for the silence.

It swears an oath by morning light and by the night when it stills. And then — in a single sentence, with a verb form that every Arab in Makkah would have recognised — it demolishes the hypothesis of abandonment.

Two words in this passage are carrying more weight than they should be able to hold. One is a verb in a form that means something very different from its simpler version. The other is a verb with a *missing* pronoun — a pronoun that was deliberately dropped for a reason that will break you when you see it.

## PART 1: THE LINGUISTIC JOURNEY

### I. وَدَّعَ — The Verb That Means More Than Leaving

In Arabic, when someone leaves — simply departs, walks away, is no longer present — the basic verb is **وَدَعَ** (*wada'a*). Form I. Simple departure. He left.

But that is not the verb Allah used.

Allah said: **مَا وَدَّعَكَ** — with a *shaddah* on the *dāl*. That doubled consonant changes the verb from Form I to **Form II**: *wadda'a*.

Here is what Form II does in Arabic.

Form II takes a simple action and makes it **deliberate, thorough, and intensified.** When you move from Form I (فَعَلَ) to Form II (فَعَّلَ), you are not doing more of the same thing. You are doing a *qualitatively different* version of it. Form I *'alima* (عَلِمَ) means "he knew." Form II *'allama* (عَلَّمَ) means "he taught" — the deliberate, thoroughgoing transmission of knowledge to another. Form I *kasara* (كَسَرَ) means "he broke." Form II *kassara* (كَسَّرَ) means "he shattered into pieces" — the intensified, thorough, exhaustive version of breaking.

Now apply this to the root **و د ع**.

Form I **وَدَعَ** — he left. Simple departure. No ceremony. No intention behind the leaving beyond the leaving itself.

Form II **وَدَّعَ** — he **bade farewell**. He took his leave deliberately. He performed the act of departing with full consciousness, with intention, with finality. This is the farewell you give at the airport when you know you are not coming back. The wave from the departing ship. The hand lifted for the last time.

[PAUSE]

And Allah negated it.

**مَا وَدَّعَكَ رَبُّكَ**

Your Lord has not bade you farewell. Your Lord has not done the deliberate, intentional, final, ceremonial act of leaving you.

Not: "Your Lord has not left." That would be the negation of Form I — a weaker claim. Maybe He drifted. Maybe He got distracted. Maybe He's busy. But the negation of Form II is something far more emphatic: **He did not perform the act of leaving you on purpose.** He did not stand at the door and wave goodbye. He did not decide that the relationship was over and walk away with intention.

The Quraysh said: "Your Lord has abandoned you." The Arabic of the accusation carried the weight of deliberate departure — a God who looked at His prophet and chose to leave. And the Quran's response is surgically precise: the verb you used — the farewell, the intentional departure — **did not happen.** مَا. It is negated. Demolished. Gone.

[PAUSE]

And notice the possessive: **رَبُّكَ** — *your* Lord. Not "Allah" — the universal, cosmic, formal name. Not "We" — the royal plural of sovereignty. **Your Lord.** Your nurturer. Your sustainer. The possessive pronoun كَ turns the divine name into something intimate, relational, personal. He is speaking about Himself in the third person — but with a possessive that makes the distance intimate. *Your* Lord did not farewell *you*.

This is not a press release. This is a private reassurance.

[PAUSE — major]

### II. مَا قَلَىٰ — The Absent Pronoun

Now the second clause.

**وَمَا قَلَىٰ**

"Nor has He detested."

**قَلَى** — root **ق ل ي** — Form I, perfect tense. To detest. To hate. To be thoroughly averse to someone. Not mild dislike. Not disappointment. *Qalā* is the Arabic of deep, visceral aversion — the kind of hatred that doesn't just reject but is *repulsed by*.

And Allah negates it: مَا قَلَىٰ — He did not detest.

But look at what's missing.

In the first clause: مَا وَدَّعَ**كَ** — He did not farewell **you**. The pronoun كَ is present. You are named. The verb is connected to its object — the Prophet ﷺ — directly.

In the second clause: مَا قَلَىٰ. No كَ. No "you." Just: He did not detest. Full stop.

[PAUSE]

Where did the pronoun go?

Arabic could have said مَا قَلَاكَ — "He did not detest *you*." The grammar would be clean. The parallelism with the first clause would be perfect. وَدَّعَكَ / قَلَاكَ — farewell you / detest you. Matched, balanced, symmetrical.

But Allah broke the symmetry. He dropped the pronoun.

The classical scholars of Arabic rhetoric — the *balāghiyyūn* — saw this and understood: Allah refused to place the word "you" next to the word "detest." Even in negation. Even while saying it *didn't* happen. The proximity of "you" and "detest" in the same grammatical structure was too much. The attachment of the Prophet ﷺ as the object of the verb "to hate" — even hypothetically, even as the thing being denied — was something the Quran would not permit.

[PAUSE]

Think about what this means.

There is a difference between someone saying to you: "I don't hate you" — and someone saying: "I don't hate." In the first, the word "hate" and "you" occupy the same sentence. They are linked grammatically. Even in denial, the association exists — your name was in the same breath as hatred. You heard yourself as the hypothetical object of hatred, and even though the hatred was denied, the image was formed.

In the second — "I don't hate" — the pronoun is absent. You were never placed in the sentence. You were never, even for a moment, even in grammar, even as a hypothetical, the object of hatred. The verb was negated in isolation. You were kept out of its vicinity entirely.

That is what Allah did.

He negated the farewell *with* the pronoun: مَا وَدَّعَكَ — because "farewell" is painful but not ugly. The word itself is not an insult. It is a departure that can be spoken aloud in connection with "you."

But He negated the hatred *without* the pronoun: مَا قَلَىٰ — because the word قَلَى is so harsh, so viscerally ugly, that He would not allow it to grammatically touch "you." Not even while saying it never happened.

This is linguistic tenderness at a level that no translation can reach. Every English translation writes: "Your Lord has not forsaken you, nor has He detested you." They add the "you" because English requires an object. But the Arabic *withheld* the object. And the withholding is the meaning.

[PAUSE — major]

### III. فَتَرْضَىٰٓ — The Promise Bounded by You

After the negations — after the demolition of abandonment and the refusal to let hatred touch the Prophet's name — the surah turns forward.

**وَلَسَوْفَ يُعْطِيكَ رَبُّكَ فَتَرْضَىٰٓ**

"And your Lord will give you, and you will be satisfied."

Three elements are working together here.

**First: لَسَوْفَ** — the emphatic *lām* + the future particle *sawfa*. سَوْفَ alone means "will" — future tense. But the لَ preceding it is the emphatic *lām* — the particle of sworn certainty. Together they mean not just "will" but "**will certainly, without any doubt, and at the time He chooses.**" The *sawfa* stretches the promise into the future — not immediately, not on your timetable, but coming. And the *lām* ensures it is sworn.

**Second: يُعْطِيكَ** — Form IV of root **عطو**. Form IV is the causative: "He will *cause giving* to reach you." Not Form I (simple giving) but the form that implies deliberate, active causation. And the object of the giving? **Not specified.** The verb has a recipient (كَ — you) but no named gift. What will He give? The ayah doesn't say. The object is open. The giving is without specification — which means it is without limit.

**Third: فَتَرْضَىٰٓ** — "and so you will be satisfied." Root **ر ض و** — *riḍā* — satisfaction, pleasure, contentment. Form I, imperfect, second person masculine singular. *You* will be satisfied. And the فَ at the beginning marks result — the giving will *result* in your satisfaction.

[PAUSE]

Here is what's extraordinary.

The promise is not: "Your Lord will give you X." If X were named — Paradise, the Kawthar, intercession, victory — the promise would be bounded by the thing promised. You would receive the named gift and that would be the fulfilment.

But the gift is unnamed. The only boundary on the promise is the *result*: **فَتَرْضَىٰٓ** — until *you* are satisfied. Not until *He* decides it's enough. Not until the gift matches some divine quota. Until **you** — the Prophet ﷺ — are pleased.

The scope of the promise is bounded by the recipient's own capacity for satisfaction. And the recipient is the most generous-hearted human being who ever lived — the one who would not be satisfied until his entire ummah was safe. Which means the promise expands to the size of his compassion.

When the scholars reflect on this ayah, many of them weep. Because they understand: the Prophet ﷺ will not be satisfied while a single member of his ummah remains in the Fire. And Allah said فَتَرْضَىٰٓ — you *will* be satisfied. The sworn promise meets the prophetic compassion, and the result is an intercession that has no named limit.

[PAUSE — major]

## PART 2: THE THEMATIC DEPTHS

### Theme I: The Silence Was Not Abandonment

You know this feeling.

Maybe it wasn't revelation that stopped. Maybe it was something quieter. There was a period in your life — maybe you're in it now — when du'a felt like it was hitting the ceiling and falling back. When the Quran didn't open you the way it used to. When prayer felt like recitation and nothing more. When the warmth you once felt in sujood turned cool, and you couldn't identify exactly when the temperature changed.

Spiritual dryness. The therapists call it anhedonia when it's clinical. The mystics call it *qabd* — contraction. The ordinary believer calls it: *I don't feel anything anymore.*

And in that silence, a voice arrives — not always from the Quraysh, sometimes from inside your own head: "Maybe He's done with you. Maybe the closeness you felt was imaginary. Maybe you're not worth the attention anymore."

مَا وَدَّعَكَ رَبُّكَ.

Your Lord did not farewell you. The silence is not a departure. The absence of felt closeness is not proof of actual distance. The revelation paused — but the Revealer did not leave.

[PAUSE]

Notice what the surah does NOT do. It does not explain the silence. It does not say: "The revelation paused because..." There is no reason given. No theological justification. No lesson plan. The silence is not decoded or made meaningful in retrospect.

What the surah does instead is far more radical: it negates the worst interpretation of the silence and replaces it with a promise. مَا وَدَّعَكَ (the worst thing you feared *did not happen*) → وَلَسَوْفَ يُعْطِيكَ (and something better than what you had before is *coming*).

The silence is left unexplained. And this is mercy. Because if Allah explained every silence, every drought, every spiritual winter — if every period of felt absence came with a footnote — then the test of trust would be eliminated. Trust is not trust when you can see the reason. Trust is what remains when the reason is hidden and the only thing you have is: **مَا وَدَّعَكَ.**

He did not leave. That is the only information you need.

And the oath that opens the surah confirms it structurally. وَٱلضُّحَىٰ — by the morning brightness. وَٱلَّيْلِ إِذَا سَجَىٰ — and by the night when it stills. Morning light and deep night. Both are sworn by. Both are sacred. The *ḍuḥā* — the brightness after sunrise, the warmth — is not more beloved to Allah than the *layl idhā sajā* — the night at its most still and dark.

The periods of brightness and the periods of darkness are both part of the oath. Both are evidence. The night is not the absence of God's presence. The night is another one of His signs. And سَجَىٰ — the night when it *stills*, when it reaches full covering, full quietude — that is the moment chosen for the oath. Not the noisy night, not the night of activity. The still one. The silent one.

The silence was an ayah.

[PAUSE — major]

### Theme II: The Promise Without a Ceiling

There is a way to comfort someone that actually diminishes them.

"Don't worry, it'll be fine." "Things will get better." "This too shall pass." These are ceilings. They cap the pain by capping the promise. They say: the future will be *adequate*. The bad thing will stop. Normal will resume.

Now listen to how Allah comforts.

**وَلَلْءَاخِرَةُ خَيْرٌ لَّكَ مِنَ ٱلْأُولَىٰ**

"And the Hereafter is better for you than the present."

Not: "the future will be fine." Not: "it'll get better." The *ākhirah* — the last, the ultimate, the final reality — is **better** for you than the *ūlā* — the first, the present, the immediate. What's coming is not a return to normal. It is an *upgrade*. Every stage that follows will be superior to the stage that preceded it.

And then:

**وَلَسَوْفَ يُعْطِيكَ رَبُّكَ فَتَرْضَىٰٓ**

"And your Lord will certainly give you until you are satisfied."

There is no named gift. Not "He will give you victory" or "He will give you Paradise" or "He will give you patience." He will give you — *period*. The object is absent. And the result clause فَتَرْضَىٰٓ makes *your satisfaction* the only measure.

[PAUSE]

Think about what this means practically.

When a parent tells a crying child, "I'll get you a toy," the promise has a shape. The child can evaluate it: is the toy good enough? When the parent says instead, "I'll give you whatever it takes until you stop crying" — the promise has no shape. It expands to meet the need. The limit is not the giver's willingness. The limit is the receiver's need.

Allah said فَتَرْضَىٰٓ. The limit is the Prophet's satisfaction. And the Prophet ﷺ is the man who said, in an authentic narration: "My ummah, my ummah." The man who will prostrate before Allah on the Day of Judgment and ask for his community. The man whose satisfaction cannot be complete while those he loves are suffering.

So the promise expands. يُعْطِيكَ — He will give you — without limit, without specification, without ceiling — **فَتَرْضَىٰٓ** — until YOU are satisfied. And the scholars look at this and understand: the intercession of the Prophet ﷺ is embedded in this ayah. Not as a theological proposition. As a *sworn promise*. لَسَوْفَ — the emphatic future. Certain. Sworn. Coming.

And what applies to the Prophet ﷺ in its highest form applies to every believer in its proportion. The promise is personal: رَبُّكَ — YOUR Lord. The giving is unspecified. The satisfaction is yours. Whatever you need — whatever it would take for your heart to arrive at rest — that is what is coming. Not what you expect. Not what you planned. What would satisfy you at the deepest level of who you are.

You might not know what that is yet. He does. And He swore.

[PAUSE — major]

## CLOSING SYNTHESIS

### The Architecture — From Oath to Promise

The five ayahs form a single emotional arc:

**Oath (93:1-2):** By the morning brightness / by the night when it stills.
Two poles — light and darkness — both sacred. Both sworn by. The listener is placed between them. You are not in the brightness right now. You are in the سَجَىٰ — the stilled, quiet, dark night. And the oath tells you: this place is also sworn upon. It counts.

**Negation (93:3):** Your Lord did not farewell you. Nor did He detest.
The worst fear — demolished. The Form II farewell — negated. The hatred — negated without even touching "you" to the verb. This is not just comfort. This is the surgical removal of a poisonous thought.

**Elevation (93:4):** The Hereafter is better for you than the present.
Not just "it'll be okay." Better. What's coming is not a return to where you were. It is an ascent.

**Promise (93:5):** Your Lord will give you until you are satisfied.
Open-ended. Unspecified. Bounded only by your own riḍā. The ceiling is not His generosity — it is your capacity to be pleased.

The architecture moves: **darkness → negation of abandonment → elevation → open-ended promise.** It takes you from the worst possible interpretation of the silence (He left) through the demolition of that interpretation, through the reframing (what's coming is better), to the final destination (a giving that will not stop until you say it's enough).

And the movement from 93:3 to 93:5 mirrors the movement from night to morning: from مَا وَدَّعَكَ (the darkest fear negated) to فَتَرْضَىٰٓ (the brightest promise affirmed). The surah *is* the ḍuḥā — the morning light breaking into the long night.

### Questions to Carry

When was the last time the silence felt like abandonment? What would it mean to hear مَا وَدَّعَكَ not as information but as a voice — the Voice — saying it directly to you?

If Allah dropped the pronoun from قَلَىٰ to protect His Prophet from even a grammatical association with hatred — what does that tell you about how He sees you when you feel most unworthy?

What would you need to hear to reach فَتَرْضَىٰٓ — genuine, complete satisfaction? Can you name it? Or is the unnamed quality of the promise — the absence of a specified gift — actually the point?

The next time someone you love is in a silent, dark period, will you explain the silence — or will you simply say the equivalent of مَا وَدَّعَكَ: "you have not been left"?

---

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The silence was never departure — it was the night half of an oath, and what is coming will not stop until you yourself say it is enough.**

---

### Closing Invitation

If you are in the night right now — the stilled, quiet, dark night where سَجَىٰ has settled and everything feels covered — then you are in an ayah. Not outside the story. Inside the oath.

The morning is not just coming. It was sworn by.

And the gift that is approaching — the لَسَوْفَ, the emphatic, certain, stretched-into-the-future giving — is not measured by what you expect. It is measured by what would satisfy you.

رَبُّكَ. Your Lord. Not the God of the universe addressing the cosmos. Your Lord, addressing you. He did not farewell you. He did not even allow the verb "to detest" to share a sentence with your name.

And He will give you.

فَتَرْضَىٰٓ.

### Du'ā

*Allāhumma, You who swore by the morning light and by the night when it covered — we are sometimes in the night, and the silence feels like farewell. Teach us to hear مَا وَدَّعَكَ underneath the quiet. You who dropped the pronoun from قَلَىٰ so that hatred would never touch Your Prophet's name — drop the weight of our worst fears about ourselves the same way. And when the giving begins — the unspecified, open-ended, sworn giving — give us until we reach رِضَا. Not what we expect. What would actually satisfy the deepest part of us. You know what that is before we do. You swore.*

*Āmīn.*
