### Absence flags
- The *muḍṭarr* is **definite** (*al-muḍṭarr*) — not *muḍṭarran* ("any distressed one"). The definite article turns "the one compelled into desperation" into a category. Allah is describing the archetypal condition — the human state of being at the end of every alternative — and promising that whenever a human enters *that* condition, He is the one who responds.
- The verb *yujību* takes no explicit agent outside *man* (the relative pronoun). Whoever enters the *muḍṭarr* state is answered — the identity of the caller is grammatically unspecified. The grammar widens the promise.
- The object of *yakshifu* is *al-sūʾ* — "the harm" (definite). Whatever the specific *sūʾ* is, it is the one currently crushing this muḍṭarr. The definite lets every muḍṭarr read their own harm into the word.
- **What is NOT said:** the ayah does not say "when the righteous call" or "when the believing call" or "when the sinless call." Just — *idhā daʿāhu* — "when he calls Him." The condition of the caller is not specified. The door opens for whoever reaches it.

### Architecture flag — the ayah's position in 27:60-64
This ayah sits in the **middle** of a five-fold rhetorical sequence. Each ayah (60, 61, 62, 63, 64) opens with *am-man* ("or who...") and closes with *a-ilāhun maʿa Allāh?* ("is there a god alongside Allah?"). The five cover:
- **60** — Who created heavens and earth, sends down rain, grows gardens?
- **61** — Who made earth firm, placed rivers, set mountains, put a barrier between the two seas?
- **62** — Who answers the muḍṭarr when he calls, removes harm, makes you successors of the earth?
- **63** — Who guides you in the darkness of land and sea, sends winds as heralds of mercy?
- **64** — Who originates creation then repeats it, and provides for you from sky and earth?

Four of the five are cosmic — creation, geology, meteorology, repetition of creation. Only **one** — the exact middle — is personal. Only one asks about *you, calling Him from inside your distress*. The architecture of the passage places the muḍṭarr at the cosmic center. The ayah is saying: *between heavens and rain, between mountains and seas, between darkness and dawn, between origin and return — there is the one desperate soul on a prayer mat at 3am, and answering that soul is as central to the divine self-disclosure as holding the earth together.*

## INTRODUCTION

### Rebuilding the world

Before we open this single ayah, you have to hear it in its rhythm.

An-Naml is a Meccan surah, and by the time you arrive at verse 60, the surah has already taken you through a guided tour of prophetic dialogue — Sulaymān speaking to the ant, the hoopoe bringing news of Bilqīs, Mūsā at the fire, Ṣāliḥ and his people, Lūṭ and his city. The surah has been showing you prophets speaking, listeners responding, and the cost of refusing to listen.

And then, starting at 27:59, the rhythm changes.

Allah steps forward Himself. The tone shifts from narrative to direct address. And He begins asking questions. Not real questions — **rhetorical** questions, the kind of questions a father asks a child when the child is about to do something foolish and the father wants the child to stop and actually think.

Five times in a row, Allah asks the same grammatical shape. *Am-man* — "or who?" *Am-man yakhluqu...*, *am-man jaʿala...*, *am-man yujību...*, *am-man yahdīkum...*, *am-man yabda'u...* Or who creates? Or who ordered? Or who answers? Or who guides? Or who originates? And five times in a row, each question ends with the same refrain: *a-ilāhun maʿa Allāh?* — **"Is there a god alongside Allah?"** [PAUSE]

Stay with that refrain for a moment. *A-ilāhun maʿa Allāh.* It is not "is there a god *besides* Allah," which would be a question about substitutes. It is *maʿa Allāh* — "is there a god *alongside* Allah," which is a question about **partners**. It is asking, with a kind of weary incredulity: *look at the evidence I am putting in front of you, and tell me — do you see anyone else in this picture with Me?*

And the people being asked this question — the original Meccan audience — are a people whose religious imagination has been trained to imagine Allah as the *supreme* god, yes, but with an entire retinue of lesser gods sitting alongside Him. Al-Lāt, al-ʿUzzā, Manāt, Hubal. Three hundred and sixty idols in the Kaʿba. Their instinct is not to deny Allah. Their instinct is to put others *alongside* Him — to negotiate access through a crowded divine court. And the refrain cuts that impulse at the root every time it arrives. *Is there anyone else in this picture? Look. Is there?*

Four of the five questions are cosmic. They point at creation — at the earth and sky and rain, at mountains and rivers, at the origination of things. They are saying: look at the world itself as evidence that there is no partner.

But one of the five questions is not cosmic. One of the five questions points *at you* — at the specific, personal, private moment when you have been pressed past every alternative and you are calling in the dark. And *that* question sits in the exact middle of the five. The center of the sequence.

### The hook

Here is what should stop us before we begin. The Meccan audience is being told: the same God who set the mountains, who made the rivers run, who placed the barrier between the two seas, who originates creation and will repeat it — *that God* — is the one who answered you the night you thought no one was there. And your own memory of being answered is supposed to function as evidence, **right alongside the mountains**, that He has no partner.

Your 3am prayer is as load-bearing, in Allah's argument for His own oneness, as the architecture of the cosmos.

That is what we are walking into.

## PART 1 — THE LINGUISTIC JOURNEY

### Element One: *al-muḍṭarr* — the Form VIII that folds in on itself

Before we open the word, I want to teach you something about how Arabic builds meaning, because without it this word will pass under the radar and you will read "the distressed" and move on.

In Arabic, every verb has a root — usually three letters — and the root carries a core meaning. Then the language wraps that root in a pattern, what we call a *form*, and the pattern does something *to* the meaning. Form I is the simple action. Form II intensifies it. Form III makes it relational. Form IV causes it.

And then there's **Form VIII**, which is rarer and stranger than the others. Form VIII is built by inserting a *tāʾ* — a *t* sound — **between the first and second letters of the root**. Let me show you what this looks like in a simple example. The root *j-m-ʿ* means "to gather." Form I *jamaʿa* is "he gathered [something]." Form VIII *ijtamaʿa* takes that same root, inserts a *tāʾ* after the *j*, and now the word means "he gathered *himself* — he came together." The action folds back onto the one doing it. What was being done to an object is now being done to — or inside of — the subject. That is the secret of Form VIII: it is the form where **the action turns inward**.

Now come to our word. The root here is *ḍ-r-r*, and it means "to harm." Form I *ḍarr* is just "harm." Someone harms something. Simple. If Allah had wanted to say "the harmed one," He had several options in Form I: *maḍrūr*, "one who has been harmed." It's a perfectly good word. It is in the dictionary. It exists in classical Arabic. It would fit the meter.

But Allah does not say *maḍrūr*. He reaches for Form VIII. He inserts a *tāʾ* into the root — and now *ḍ-r-r* becomes *i-ḍ-ṭ-r-r*, *iḍṭarra*. And the passive participle of *iḍṭarra* is *muḍṭarr*. **The one who has been iḍṭarra'd.**

[PAUSE]

What does Form VIII do to the meaning of "harm"? It turns it inward. It is no longer "someone who was harmed by an external aggressor." It is *"someone who has been driven, by their own circumstances folding in on them, into a state of necessity — a state where the harm itself has become the engine forcing them past every alternative."*

Let me say this more carefully. A *maḍrūr* is someone who was hit. Someone picked up a weapon and harmed them. You can point at the harmer. You can name the injury. It happened *to* them.

A *muḍṭarr* is different. A muḍṭarr is someone whose situation has *closed around them*. Maybe no one did anything to them — or maybe many things did, small things, over many years, that nobody would ever count as a single blow. But now, standing where they are standing, **every door is shut except one**. Every option is gone except one. Every relief they might have reached for has been taken off the table — by circumstance, by illness, by loss, by time, by the accumulation of their own choices coming due. They did not choose this state. They were folded into it. And the grammatical form of the word literally performs the folding — the *tāʾ* is inserted *inside* the root letters, the way the person is inserted *inside* their own closing world.

[PAUSE]

And notice what the classical scholars say. Ibn Kathīr and al-Ṭabarī describe the muḍṭarr not by listing who counts but by describing a condition: *man lā malja'a lahu illā ilayh* — "the one who has no refuge except toward Him." That phrase — *lā malja'a illā ilayh* — "no refuge except toward Him" — is the definition. The muḍṭarr is not identified by what their problem is. They are identified by the fact that the problem has become so total that every other refuge has been eliminated. The muḍṭarr is defined not by their pain but by **the closure of every alternative**.

And here is what is staggering. Allah, the Creator of the heavens and the earth, the one who holds all being in existence, **specifically names this category** — not the "righteous" category, not the "believing" category, not the "worshipping" category, but the *no-options-left* category — and promises that **this** is the category His answer is guaranteed for.

Why? Why is the answer guaranteed for the muḍṭarr specifically, and not for the worshipper whose life is comfortable?

Because only the muḍṭarr calls without a backup plan. Only the muḍṭarr's call is not hedged. Every other caller, in some corner of their heart, is also still counting on their savings, or their influence, or their connections, or their expertise. The call is real, but the call is one option among several. For the muḍṭarr, the call is not *an* option. It is **the only option**. And Allah is telling us: when the call becomes *that* — when every other door has shut and there is nothing left between your voice and Him — that is the call that is answered without fail.

This is almost frightening when you sit with it. Because it means that the *condition* for guaranteed response is the *condition* most of us spend our entire lives trying to avoid. We spend our careers, our savings, our relationships, our plans, *building structures that prevent us from ever having to be a muḍṭarr*. And all the while, the ayah is saying: the door you are running from — the door where every other door has closed — is the door where the response is written into the grammar of the promise.

What this word reveals, in one sentence: **Allah names the category of person whose answer is guaranteed, and that category is defined not by their virtue but by the total closure of every other refuge — the one condition human beings spend their lives engineering against.**

### Element Two: The refrain — *a-ilāhun maʿa Allāh?*

Let's come to the closing words of the ayah, and let's come to them aware that they are not unique to this ayah. They are the refrain that closes all five rhetorical questions in this passage.

*A-ilāhun maʿa Allāh?* — "Is there a god *alongside* Allah?"

I want you to feel the Arabic preposition here, because the standard English translation collapses two different things into the same word.

In English, we say "besides Allah" or "other than Allah." But the Arabic is not *ghayr Allāh* ("other than Allah") and not *min dūn Allāh* ("below / apart from Allah"). It is *maʿa Allāh* — **"with Allah," "alongside Allah," "next to Allah."**

This distinction matters. *Ghayr* and *dūn* are the prepositions you would use to ask about *substitutes* — about people who worship a completely different god instead of Allah. But *maʿa* is the preposition you use to ask about **partners** — about people who acknowledge Allah but place Him in a crowd. *Maʿa* is the preposition of company. Of standing alongside. Of being one among several.

And this is where the ayah gets surgical about its audience. The Meccans were not atheists. They were not worshippers of a god who had replaced Allah. They acknowledged Allah — the Quran itself tells us that if you asked them who created the heavens and the earth, they would say: *Allāh*. They knew. The problem was not denial. The problem was **company**. They placed Allah in the middle of a crowded divine court, surrounded by lesser gods they could negotiate with, each with their own specialty — this one for rain, this one for fertility, this one for protection in travel. Allah was acknowledged. He was just not treated as alone.

And the refrain cuts at *exactly* that. Every time it arrives, it asks: **in the evidence I have just presented you — the heavens, the rivers, the answer to the desperate soul, the guidance in darkness, the origination of all being — do you see anyone else standing *alongside* Me?** Not "instead of Me" — that is a different question. *Alongside* Me. Is there a second hand in this act of creation? A second voice answering the muḍṭarr? A second will steering the winds? Look. Is there?

[PAUSE]

Now feel the pressure this refrain puts on the middle ayah specifically. The cosmic ayahs — 60, 61, 63, 64 — are asking: who made the mountains? who sends the winds? who originates creation? And for each of those, the honest muḍṭarr has no answer except *Allah alone*. Fine.

But 62 is different. 62 is asking: **who answered you when you called in the dark?** And now the refrain arrives: *is there a god alongside Allah in that answer?*

Think about this. Think about the times in your life — the nights you actually remember — when you prayed from a place of no other option. Think about the illness in the family where the doctors had done what they could and all you had left was your forehead on the carpet. Think about the financial panic that kept you awake when every spreadsheet closed and every calculation failed. Think about the moment in the marriage where you had said every word you knew how to say and the only voice left to turn toward was Him. Think about the child who was lost and you could not find them.

And then think about what happened after.

Not — *did* you get the specific outcome you asked for. That is a different question. But — did some opening come? Did some peace arrive that you could not have manufactured yourself? Did some path unfold that was not there a week before? Did some door you could not name quietly move on its hinges?

The ayah is saying: *look at that memory. Look at it carefully. And now tell me — was there anyone alongside Allah in that response? Did you call out to someone else as well, and someone else also answered? Or was the voice that reached back into your muḍṭarr condition — just Him?*

This is the weight of placing *a-ilāhun maʿa Allāh* at the end of the muḍṭarr ayah. Your own memory of being answered is being offered *back to you* as evidence — as personally as the mountains are offered back to a geologist as evidence. The answer you received when you had no other options is not just a private mercy. It is **a piece of cosmic testimony** about the oneness of Allah, and the ayah is asking you to read it that way.

What this element reveals, in one sentence: **The refrain "is there a god alongside Allah" reaches into your own memory of being answered and asks whether — at the moment of answer — there was anyone else in the picture besides Him.**

### Element Three: The middle position — where cosmos and intimacy meet

We have to zoom out now, because the third anchor is not a word. It is a structural position.

Let me lay out the five ayahs as Allah sequenced them. Each begins with *am-man*. Each ends with *a-ilāhun maʿa Allāh.*

**60** — Who created the heavens and the earth and sent down rain from the sky, growing gardens of beauty?
**61** — Who made the earth firm, placed rivers through it, made mountains for it, and set a barrier between the two seas?
**62** — Who answers the muḍṭarr when he calls, and removes the harm, and makes you inheritors of the earth?
**63** — Who guides you in the darkness of land and sea, and sends the winds as heralds of His mercy?
**64** — Who originates creation and then repeats it, and provides for you from the heaven and the earth?

Now look at what that sequence is doing.

Ayah 60 — the widest frame. The heavens and the earth as a whole. Creation at the cosmic scale.

Ayah 61 — zoom in. No longer the heavens-and-earth at large, but the *structure* of the earth itself. Rivers. Mountains. The meeting of two seas. The engineering of a habitable world.

Then — ayah 62 — **zoom in further**. Now we are not looking at rivers or mountains. We are looking at **one human being on their knees**, pressed past every alternative, calling in the dark. The frame has contracted from the cosmos, to the earth's geology, all the way down to a single desperate voice in the middle of the night.

And then ayah 63 pulls back out. Guidance across the *darkness of land and sea* — we are back in large territory.

And ayah 64 goes all the way back to the widest frame: the origin of creation itself, the repetition of creation, provision from the heavens and the earth.

The five ayahs form a **lens**. Wide, narrower, narrowest, narrower, wide. And the narrowest point — the exact focal center — is the muḍṭarr. The one person in their darkest moment.

[PAUSE]

Now hear what this architecture is saying.

The same sequence that is asking you to read the mountains as evidence of Allah's oneness is asking you to read *your own desperation* as evidence of the same thing. The architecture refuses to treat your personal moment of calling as smaller than the cosmos. It places that moment **in the middle of the cosmos** — structurally, grammatically, rhetorically. The muḍṭarr sits at the center of the same argument that begins with heaven and earth and ends with the origination of all being.

Here is what this means, and I want us to really sit with it. **Your 3am prayer is not a small thing that Allah attends to when He has finished with the big things.** It is not peripheral to His cosmic work. It is not a footnote to the administration of the universe. The architecture of this passage is telling you that when Allah lays out the evidence of His oneness — when He brings forward His case — He places the answered call of the desperate soul **in the same paragraph** as the mountains and the origination of existence, and He places it *in the middle*, where the emphasis lands.

The muḍṭarr is not an interruption of the cosmic argument. The muḍṭarr is **the heart of the cosmic argument**.

What this reveals, in one sentence: **Allah structurally places your moment of desperate calling at the exact center of His self-disclosure to the universe — not as an aside, but as the emphatic middle of the argument.**

## PART 2 — THE THEMATIC DEPTHS

### Theme One: The door we spend our lives running from

Let me tell you what most of us are actually doing with our time.

We are building walls against ever becoming the muḍṭarr.

Listen to how this sounds in an ordinary life. We save money — not primarily to enjoy it, but so that we will never have to call out from a place of financial desperation. We build relationships carefully — not primarily to love, but so that we will never be alone when we need someone. We get degrees and certifications and careers — not primarily to serve, but so that we will never be helpless in the marketplace. We take out insurance policies, retirement accounts, emergency funds, backup plans, second opinions, hedged bets. We diversify. We prepare.

And none of this is wrong. Islam asks us to tie our camel. The Prophet ﷺ left instructions for his family. The Quran itself tells us to prepare against poverty, against weakness, against the day when we will need something we do not currently have.

But notice what happens if we are not careful. Over time, every single one of those walls we build has a second effect we did not intend. **Every wall against desperation is also a wall against being a muḍṭarr**. Every backup plan is also a plan to avoid the condition where Allah's answer is guaranteed. Every safety net is also a net that catches us before we hit the floor where only He is waiting.

And so a human being can spend forty years on earth, practicing, praying, fasting, giving zakat, reciting Quran — and yet, because of the walls they have built around themselves, never once in forty years actually touch the floor. Never once be a muḍṭarr. Never once call out with no other option behind them. And then they wonder — in some quiet corner of their heart — why their prayers feel like they are bouncing off a ceiling. Why the closeness the ayahs describe feels theoretical. Why other people seem to have these dramatic stories of being answered, and their own prayer life feels like a monologue.

The ayah gives us a clue. *Al-muḍṭarr idhā daʿāhu*. The one compelled into necessity, **when he calls upon Him**. The condition and the call are paired. The answer is linked to a state that most of us, if we are honest, have organized our lives to avoid.

[PAUSE]

Now I am not saying the path forward is to dismantle your savings or cancel your insurance or throw your plans into the sea. That is not what the ayah is teaching. The ayah is not a prescription for how to live; it is a description of where Allah's answer lands. And the practical implication is subtler than "make yourself poor."

The practical implication is this. **You need to learn to call from the muḍṭarr place even when your outer life is not in a muḍṭarr state.** Because here is the secret: every single one of us, every day, has something in our lives that *has* no other door. You may have savings, but you do not have the ability to guarantee your own faith. You may have insurance, but you do not have the ability to guarantee your children's hearts. You may have a career, but you do not have the ability to protect yourself from your own nafs. You may have relationships, but you do not have the ability to soften a heart that has turned away from you. You may have health, but you do not have the ability to reach into your own soul and untangle the knots there.

**Every life has an area in it where you are already a muḍṭarr — you have just been too distracted by the areas where you are not to notice.**

Find that area. Bring it forward. And when you call — call from *there*. Not from the polite request of "Allah please make my day easy," but from the actual wall-pressed place where you genuinely have no other door. Let the call come out of the real corner of your life where Allah is, right now, your only remaining refuge. And then watch what the ayah promises arrive into *that* call.

This is the practical form. The muḍṭarr is not a person, it is a *position*. Any person can occupy that position in any moment, if they are willing to look honestly at where they actually are.

[PAUSE — major pause]

### Theme Two: Your own memory is evidence

I want to come back to something we touched in the linguistic section because it is the second theme and it is not the same as the first one.

The ayah says: *am-man yujību l-muḍṭarra idhā daʿāhu.* "Or who answers the one compelled to desperation when he calls Him." And then, right after: *a-ilāhun maʿa Allāh?* — "is there a god alongside Allah?"

The refrain is asking the audience — asking you — to bring forward evidence. And the evidence it is asking for is not philosophical. It is not cosmological. It is not derived from study or argument or classical proof. The evidence is **your own memory of being answered**.

Now let me ask you something uncomfortable. Have you ever, in your life, been answered by Allah?

Take the question seriously. Do not answer quickly. Do not answer with the polite, pious "yes of course, Allah answers all our prayers." That is not what I am asking. I am asking: can you name, specifically, a moment in your life when you called from a place of genuine need, and something came back that you know you did not manufacture, that you know did not come from your own effort, that you know was not coincidence?

Most of us, if we pause and think honestly, can name at least one such moment. One time. Maybe more. And these moments, because they were private and because we never talked about them in public, have quietly drifted to the margins of our spiritual life. They sit in some back drawer of the memory, and we do not take them out often. We do not treat them as important.

The ayah is telling us to take them out and look at them. Because they are not private. They are **testimony**. The moment you were answered is not just a mercy to you — it is a piece of the evidence Allah is placing in front of the universe for His own oneness. When the ayah asks *is there a god alongside Allah?*, it is saying: bring forward your data. And your data includes the memory of the night you were answered.

And here is what happens when you start treating those memories as evidence rather than as private mercies. You start to notice a pattern. The moments when the answer came most clearly are almost always the moments when you had the fewest other options. The *closure* of every other door was the *opening* of the door that actually answered. You learn, experientially, that the Form VIII verb is true. *Iḍṭarra*. When the circumstances folded in on you, **that** was the moment the answer was near.

And once you see the pattern in your own memory, the cosmic argument of the ayah stops being an external claim and becomes an internal confirmation. You no longer need to argue your way to tawḥīd through the mountains and the rivers. You can argue your way to tawḥīd through *your own answered prayer history*. Because the ayah is saying the two are the same argument, and they converge on the same refrain: *is there anyone else in this picture? Look at what you remember. Was there?*

The practical form. This week — before you go to sleep tonight, even — make a list. Not a pretty one, not for anyone else to see. Just a list in your own head or on a piece of paper. **The times I was answered.** The specific moments in your life when you called from a place of real need and something came back. Hold that list in front of you. And then ask yourself the refrain. *A-ilāhun maʿa Allāh?* Was there anyone else alongside Him in those answers? And let the list do its work. Let your own memory preach to you.

That is what the refrain is asking for. And it will reach into you in a way that no cosmological argument ever will, because it is evidence *you* already have, and Allah is asking you to treat your own life as the data.

## CLOSING SYNTHESIS

### The architectural reveal

Let's see the full shape now.

Five rhetorical questions. Five refrains. Four cosmic bookends — heavens and earth, rivers and mountains, darkness and wind, origin and provision. And in the exact middle — one human being, folded into their own desperation, calling in the dark.

The center of the passage is not a mountain range.
The center of the passage is **you, at your worst hour, calling the name of Allah**.

And the ayah says: *that* is the answer Allah brings forward when He wants to prove there is no one alongside Him. He does not lead with the cosmos and treat your prayer as an afterthought. He *frames the cosmos around your prayer*. The widest frame opens, and when it reaches the narrowest point — the focal center — what you find is not a scientific marvel, not a geological wonder, not an astronomical fact. What you find is one soul who had run out of every other option, calling — and being answered.

That is the architecture. That is what was waiting for us at the summit of the mountain.

### Bringing it together

The linguistic discoveries and the themes are the same insight approached from two directions.

The Form VIII *muḍṭarr* teaches us that the guaranteed-answer condition is not earned by virtue, it is entered through the total closure of every other refuge. The refrain *a-ilāhun maʿa Allāh* teaches us that our own memory of being answered is evidence in the cosmic argument for Allah's oneness. And the architectural middle position teaches us that this answering is not peripheral to God's self-disclosure — it is *central* to it.

All three point at the same truth: **Allah has placed the answering of your desperate call at the structural, grammatical, and theological center of what He wants the universe to know about Himself. Your answered prayer is not small. Your answered prayer is cosmic-scale evidence, placed in the middle of the paragraph.**

### Questions to carry

1. Where in your life right now are you already a muḍṭarr — a place where every other door has quietly closed and you are just not looking at it honestly? Can you name it?

2. How many of the walls you have built in your life — savings, plans, relationships, safety nets — are also, unintentionally, walls against ever being able to call from the muḍṭarr position?

3. When was the last time you prayed with no backup plan behind the prayer? Not a polite request, but a call from a real corner of your life where He is genuinely your only refuge?

4. What moments in your own life, if you treated them as evidence rather than as private mercies, would stand as cosmic testimony to the oneness of Allah? Have you ever held those moments in front of yourself that way?

5. If *a-ilāhun maʿa Allāh* is the refrain of this ayah, what does the honest answer from your own answered-prayer history sound like? Say it out loud if you have to.

### One-sentence distillation

At the structural center of Allah's argument for His own oneness, He places not a mountain, not a river, not a cosmic fact — but one human being folded into their own desperation, calling in the dark, and He promises that *that call* — from *that place* — meets a God who answers without any partner beside Him.

### The closing invitation

What should shift is this: how we treat our own muḍṭarr moments. Stop running from them so hard. Stop papering over them so quickly. When you find yourself in that position — in the corner where every other door has closed — recognize that you have just stepped into the one place where the ayah's promise is written. Do not rush past it. Call from *there*. Let the call come out of the genuine closure, not out of a rehearsed politeness. And trust that the answer is written into the grammar of the promise, as permanent as the mountains, as central as the origination of creation itself.

### Du'a

Yā Mujīb — O You who answer — when we find ourselves folded into the condition of the muḍṭarr, do not let us be too proud to call from inside it. Place our memory of being answered in front of our eyes as evidence of Your oneness. And when every other door has closed around us, let us recognize the one door that is open — and walk through it without hesitation. Āmīn.
