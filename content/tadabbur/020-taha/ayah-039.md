## The World Before the Words

This is the same story we heard in Al-Qasas 28:7 — but told from a different angle. There, the camera was on the mother. Here, the camera is on Allah. There, Allah was instructing a terrified woman what to do. Here, years later, Allah is explaining to Musa *why* He did what He did.

This is Surah Ta-Ha, and Allah is recounting to Musa — now an adult, now a prophet, now standing at the burning bush — the story of his own infancy. "Let Me tell you what actually happened when you were too small to remember." And what follows is one of the most extraordinary sentences in the Quran, because it reveals not just what happened but *the logic inside the happening*. The divine calculus. The reason behind every terrifying step.

In 28:7, the mother was told: nurse him, cast him into the river, don't fear, don't grieve. That ayah was about her obedience.

This ayah is about His orchestration.

And it begins with a word that is harsher than anything in the 28:7 version.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### I. Four Castings — and the Verb That Gets Gentler

<!-- morphology:20:39:34 root=لقي pos=V -->

In Surah Al-Qasas, when Allah told Musa's mother to put her baby into the river, the verb was **أَلْقِيهِ** — *alqeehi* — from the root ل ق ي, meaning to cast, to throw. It's forceful, but it's the standard word for casting something.

Here in Ta-Ha, the verb is different. It's **harsher**.

**ٱقْذِفِيهِ** — *iqdhifeehi*.

Root: ق ذ ف. This is not "cast." This is **hurl. Fling. Throw with force.** In classical Arabic, *qadhf* is the word used for hurling a stone, for flinging something away from you violently. It appears elsewhere in the Quran for hurling the truth at falsehood (*naqdhifu bil-haqqi* — 21:18). It is a word of force, of impact, of irrevocable momentum.

And listen to the sounds. *Iq-dhi-fee-hi.* The ق erupts from the back of the throat. The ذ grates between the teeth. The ف pushes air out forcefully. The word *sounds* like hurling. You can feel the physical exertion in it.

But notice: this harsher verb appears **twice**.

ٱقْذِفِيهِ فِى ٱلتَّابُوتِ — "Hurl him into the chest."
فَٱقْذِفِيهِ فِى ٱلْيَمِّ — "Then hurl it into the river."

Two hurlings. First into the *taboot* — the chest, the vessel, the ark. Then into the *yamm* — the great river. The baby goes from mother's arms to a box to a current. Each step is a further removal from human protection.

[PAUSE]

Now watch what happens to the verb.

The third casting: **فَلْيُلْقِهِ ٱلْيَمُّ بِٱلسَّاحِلِ** — "Then let the river *yulqihi* on the shore."

The verb changes. It is no longer قذف (hurl). It is **ألقى** — *yulqi* — the gentler word for casting. The same root used in 28:7. And the subject has changed too. The mother is no longer the one doing the casting. **The river is.**

The river that received the baby now becomes the agent of his delivery. And the river does not *hurl* — it *deposits*. It uses the gentler verb. As if the river, this mindless body of water, is being more careful with the baby than the instructions to the mother were.

[PAUSE]

And then the fourth casting:

**وَأَلْقَيْتُ عَلَيْكَ مَحَبَّةً مِّنِّى** — "And **I cast** upon you love from Me."

**أَلْقَيْتُ** — *alqaytu*. Same root as the river's verb. ألقى, not قذف. Allah uses the gentle word, not the harsh one. And the subject is now Allah Himself. First person singular. "**I** cast."

[PAUSE]

Do you see the trajectory?

| Casting | Verb | Agent | Object | Direction |
|---------|------|-------|--------|-----------|
| 1 | **قذف** (hurl) | Mother | Baby → chest | Away from safety |
| 2 | **قذف** (hurl) | Mother | Chest → river | Further from safety |
| 3 | **ألقى** (deposit) | River | Baby → shore | Toward destination |
| 4 | **ألقى** (cast) | Allah | Love → Musa | Onto the beloved |

**The verb gets gentler as the agent gets closer to God.**

The human side of the story uses the harsh word. The mother had to *hurl*. It was violent. It was against every instinct. The divine side of the story uses the gentle word. The river *deposited*. Allah *cast*. And what Allah cast was not a baby into danger — it was **love onto a baby who was already in His hands**.

The same root — ل ق ي — appears in both the river's action and Allah's action. As if Allah is saying: "The river that carried you? That was My arm. The current that moved you? That was My hand. And when you arrived, I didn't just receive you — I *threw love at you*."

### II. Love as a Projectile — The Thrown مَحَبَّة

<!-- morphology:20:39:38 root=حبب pos=N -->

Now stay with this image, because the Arabic is doing something here that translations completely flatten.

**وَأَلْقَيْتُ عَلَيْكَ مَحَبَّةً مِّنِّى**

"And I *cast upon you* love from Me."

The verb is ألقى — to throw, to cast, to hurl. The object is مَحَبَّة — love. Allah did not say "I placed love in your heart." He did not say "I made people love you." He said **I threw love at you.**

Love, in this ayah, is not a feeling that gradually grew. It is not a warmth that slowly developed. It is a **projectile**. Something that was launched from a source (مِنِّى — "from Me") and landed on a target (عَلَيْكَ — "upon you"). It has trajectory. It has force. It has a point of origin and a point of impact.

And the word itself — *mahabbah* — is in the **maf'ala** pattern. In Arabic, the maf'ala pattern can indicate either a single intense instance of something, or the place/manner of something. *Mahabbah* is not *hubb* — which is general, abstract, ambient love. *Mahabbah* is a specific, **bestowed** love. A love that was *given as an act*. Something Allah did TO Musa, not something Musa earned or generated.

[PAUSE]

The classical scholars saw layers in this. Some said it means Allah placed love for Musa in the hearts of all who saw him — so that even Fir'awn's wife, Asiyah, looked at this baby and felt irresistible tenderness. Even the tyrant's household could not bring itself to harm him. Others said it means a love from Allah directly — a divine affection placed upon Musa himself, like a garment, like an aura.

And here is where the composite sketch enriches: both readings are held by the grammar. The mahabbah is *from Me* (مِنِّى — originating in Allah) and *upon you* (عَلَيْكَ — landing on Musa). Whether it then radiates outward from Musa to everyone who sees him, or remains as a private divine embrace — the grammar holds both. And the image is richer for it.

A baby floating in a box on the Nile, heading toward the household of the man who ordered the killing of every baby like him. And on that baby — invisible, unmistakable to anyone who comes near — a mahabbah thrown from Allah Himself. A love so thick, so present, so undeniable that even the enemy's wife will look at him and say: *"[He is] a comfort of the eye for me and for you. Do not kill him"* (28:9).

The love arrived before the baby did. The projectile landed before the vessel docked. Allah didn't wait for Musa to be safe and then love him. He threw the love *while Musa was still on the water*.

### III. Fashioned Under a Single Eye

<!-- morphology:20:39:43 root=صنع pos=V -->
<!-- morphology:20:39:45 root=عين pos=N -->

The final phrase. Six words. An entire theology of divine care.

**وَلِتُصْنَعَ عَلَىٰ عَيْنِىٓ**

"So that you would be *tusna'a* under *'aynee*."

Two words need opening. Both are carrying more than translation can hold.

**تُصْنَعَ** — *tusna'a*. Root: ص ن ع. This is the verb for **crafting, fashioning, making with skill.** Not خلق (*khalaqa* — to create from nothing, the word for cosmic creation). Not جعل (*ja'ala* — to make, to render, the general word). صنع — *sana'a* — is the word for the artisan. The carpenter. The goldsmith. The sculptor. It implies meticulous, skilled, intentional workmanship. When you صنع something, you shape it with care. You attend to the details. You know what you're making, and you bring expertise to the making of it.

And it's in the **passive voice** — تُصْنَعَ. "So that you would be *fashioned*." Who is the craftsman? Not named. Hidden behind the passive. But we know. And the concealment is not vagueness — it is elevation. The Craftsman is too great to name as a simple grammatical agent. He is the subject behind every passive verb in the universe.

**عَيْنِىٓ** — *'aynee*. "My eye." And this is **singular**.

[PAUSE]

In Arabic, the dual would be *'aynayya* — "my two eyes." The plural would be *'uyoonee* — "my eyes." But Allah said عَيْنِى — one eye. Singular. Why?

Because a single eye is the image of **focused, undivided, pinpoint attention**. Think of the master craftsman hunched over a piece of jewelry, one eye to the loupe, the other closed. All focus on the work. No distraction. No divided gaze. No glancing away. One eye, on one thing, with absolute concentration.

"So that you would be **crafted under My eye.**"

Not "so that I could watch over you" — though that is part of it. Not "so that I could protect you" — though that is part of it too. But: **so that I could MAKE you.** The purpose of the divine gaze was not passive observation. It was *active crafting*. Allah was not watching Musa the way a bystander watches a child in a park. He was watching Musa the way a sculptor watches the clay under his hands. Every moment in Fir'awn's palace, every experience in Midian, every year of exile and return — all of it was the crafting. All of it was the صنع. And through all of it, the singular eye never blinked.

[PAUSE]

This reframes the entire story. Every terrifying step — the hurling into the chest, the hurling into the river, the delivery to the enemy's household — was not a series of crises. It was a series of *sculpting movements*. The Craftsman was working. The clay was being shaped. And the eye was on the work the entire time.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Trajectory of Trust

Every qadr that felt like distance was actually delivery.

This is the sentence this ayah wants to plant in your chest. And it is one of the hardest sentences in Islam to believe.

Because when you are inside the experience, it does not feel like delivery. When the baby is in the chest, it feels like a coffin. When the chest is in the river, it feels like drowning. When the river deposits you at the feet of the man who wants to kill you, it feels like the worst possible outcome.

Musa's trajectory looked, from the inside, like a series of disasters. Removed from his mother. Placed in a box. Set adrift on a river. Delivered to the doorstep of a genocidal tyrant. If you were narrating this story without knowing the ending, you would say: everything that could go wrong did.

But look at the verb trajectory again. قذف → قذف → ألقى → ألقى. Hurl, hurl, deposit, cast love. The verbs get gentler. The story gets softer. Not because the circumstances got easier — they didn't — but because the agent behind them was increasingly visible.

The mother hurled. That was the harshest step, and it was human.
The river deposited. That was gentler, and it was natural.
Allah cast love. That was the gentlest, and it was divine.

**The closer you get to the divine side of the story, the gentler the handling becomes.**

[PAUSE]

You have experienced this. Maybe not with a river and a tyrant. But you have been hurled. You have been placed into situations that felt like chests — dark, enclosed, with no visibility. You have been set adrift on currents you could not control. And you have arrived at destinations that looked, on the surface, like the last place you should be.

And the question this ayah asks is: are you looking at the trajectory from inside the chest — where it looks like chaos — or from the perspective of the eye that never blinked?

Because from inside the chest, every step is a crisis. From under the eye, every step is a crafting movement.

The same event. Two perspectives. And the ayah is asking you to adopt the second one. Not because the pain wasn't real — it was. Not because the fear wasn't justified — it was. But because the Craftsman was working the entire time, and what looked like distance from safety was actually delivery to purpose.

[PAUSE]

Here is the practical edge: the next time you find yourself in a taboot — in a dark, enclosed, frightening situation where you cannot see the way out — remember that the box was always on its way to the shore. The river was always heading toward Fir'awn's palace. And the love was already in the air before the box touched the water.

### Theme 2: You Were Not Abandoned — You Were Being Made

Modern life is saturated with a particular kind of narrative: the self-made person. We celebrate the individual who "overcame their circumstances." Who "built themselves from nothing." Who "made something of their life against all odds." The hero of this story is always the person. Their grit. Their vision. Their resilience.

And that narrative has value. Effort is real. Perseverance matters. But this ayah offers a radically different reading of the same story.

**وَلِتُصْنَعَ عَلَىٰ عَيْنِىٓ**

"So that you would be *fashioned* under My eye."

Passive voice. You were not the maker. You were the one being made.

Every experience you thought was shaping your character? It was. But you were not the sculptor. Every hardship you thought was teaching you resilience? It was. But you were not the teacher. Every loss that you survived and thought "I made it through" — you did. But you were being carried through by a Craftsman whose eye was on you the entire time.

This is not a diminishment of human effort. Musa still had to live the life. He still had to endure the palace of Fir'awn, the years in Midian, the walk back to Egypt, the staff and the sea. He was not a puppet. He was the clay — and clay participates in its own shaping. It yields. It resists. It cracks and is reworked. But the hands that shape it are not its own.

[PAUSE]

The word is صنع — not just "make" but **craft**. Which means: there is a design. There is intention. There is expertise. You were not randomly assembled by circumstance. You were *crafted*. The painful experiences were not accidents to be survived — they were strokes of the chisel. The confusing years were not wasted time — they were drying time, curing time, the time the material needed between shapings.

And through all of it: **عَيْنِى**. My eye. Singular. Focused. On you.

Not scanning a crowd. Not monitoring a population. Not dividing attention between a billion projects. *'Aynee.* **My eye.** As if you are the only piece of work on the bench. As if the Craftsman has cleared the table and there is nothing under His gaze except you and whatever He is making of you today.

[PAUSE]

What would shift in your relationship with your own past if you genuinely believed this? Not "I survived a difficult childhood" but "I was being crafted through a difficult childhood." Not "I went through a painful divorce" but "I was being fashioned through a painful divorce." Not "I wasted ten years" but "I was being shaped for ten years, and the eye was on me the whole time."

The passive voice is not weakness. It is the most radical statement of trust a human being can make: **I was not the author. I was the work. And the Author knew what He was doing.**

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture Revealed

This ayah tells two stories simultaneously, and the architecture makes them inseparable.

**The first story is horizontal** — a sequence of events in time. Cast into chest. Cast into river. Deposited on shore. Taken by enemy. It is a story of movement: from here to there, from mother to river to palace.

**The second story is vertical** — a single, unchanging reality operating underneath the horizontal one. Love cast from above. Crafted under a singular eye. It is a story of presence: God was there the whole time, not as a spectator but as a maker.

The horizontal story uses harsh verbs at the start (قذف, قذف) and gentle verbs at the end (ألقى, ألقى). The vertical story uses only gentle language from the beginning: *mahabbah*, *tusna'a*, *'aynee*.

And the hinge between the two stories is the word **وَ** — "and." Not "but." Not "however." Just "and." *"An enemy of Mine and an enemy of his will take him — AND I cast upon you love from Me."* The enemy and the love are not contradictions. They are simultaneous. The enemy was taking Musa at the same moment the love was landing on him. The palace of Fir'awn and the eye of Allah occupied the same space at the same time.

And there is a final, devastating iltifat running through the ayah. It begins in third person — "cast **him**" — instructions about a baby who cannot speak or understand. Then, without warning, it shifts to second person: "I cast upon **you** love from Me, so that **you** would be fashioned under My eye." Suddenly Allah is no longer talking about baby Musa. He is talking **to** Musa. The infant has become the prophet. The object has become the addressee. The one who was being hurled is now the one being spoken to, face to face, at the burning bush.

The whole arc of Musa's life — from basket to bush — is contained in the grammar of this single shift.

### Questions to Carry

What is your *taboot* — your dark, enclosed situation right now — and is it possible that it is not a trap but a vessel?

If you looked at the trajectory of your hardest experiences, would you see the verb getting gentler? Would you see the handling becoming more careful as the story moved from human chaos toward divine purpose?

What have you been narrating as "what happened to me" that might actually be "what was being made of me"?

Is there a mahabbah that landed on you before you arrived — a love that preceded you, that was already on you when you walked into the hardest room of your life — that you only recognized in retrospect?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Every time life hurled Musa further from safety, it was delivering him closer to the Eye that never blinked — and the God who told the mother to throw her baby was the same God who threw love at him on the other side.**

### The Closing Invitation

If you heard the previous reflection on Al-Qasas 28:7 — the one about the mother being told to nurse and then cast — you heard the story from her side. The side of obedience. The side of letting go.

Now you have heard the other side. Allah's side. The side of the eye that was watching the whole time. The Craftsman who was shaping the whole time. The love that was already in the air before the box touched the water.

Both sides are true simultaneously. The letting go was real. The crafting was real. The fear was real. The eye was real. You are both the mother who must release and the Musa who was being fashioned. You live in both stories at once.

And when the verbs of your life feel harsh — when the قذف of circumstance hurls you into boxes and rivers and the households of people who mean you no good — know that the trajectory is not what it looks like from inside the chest.

The verb is getting gentler. The agent is getting closer to God. And there is an ألقيت coming — a love cast from the highest source — that will land on you exactly when you need it.

Because He is not planning to love you. He already threw it.

### Du'a

*Allahumma, You hurled a baby into a river and called it craftsmanship. You delivered him to his enemy and called it love. Ya Rabb, we are in our tabaweet — our boxes, our dark enclosed places — and we cannot see the shore. But You said 'aynee — My eye, singular, focused, on the work. Let us trust the passive voice: we are being fashioned, not abandoned. We are tusna'a, not forgotten. And the mahabbah You threw — let it land on us the way it landed on Musa: before we arrived, before we understood, before we could even ask for it. Amin.*
