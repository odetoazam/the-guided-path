## INTRODUCTION

[ayah:90:4] لَقَدْ خَلَقْنَا ٱلْإِنسَٰنَ فِى كَبَدٍ

*Laqad khalaqnā-l-insāna fī kabad.*

We have certainly created the human being in toil.

---

Five words.

In a Book that dedicates sixty-seven ayahs to the story of Yusuf, twenty-seven to the people of the cave, and an entire surah to the cow — five words. That's all Allah needed for this.

And the five words contain what might be the most psychologically honest sentence ever spoken about the human condition.

Surah Al-Balad opens with an oath — *lā uqsimu bi-hādha-l-balad* — "I swear by this city." The city is Makkah. And the one being addressed — the Prophet ﷺ — is someone who is *ḥill* in this city: present, lawful, resident, and yet persecuted. The surah begins in a place of belonging that has become a place of suffering. The Prophet ﷺ lives in the city he loves, among people who reject him. He is home and homeless at the same time.

Then the oath extends: by a father and what he fathered. Life itself. The chain of human generation. All of it placed under oath.

And then the sworn declaration: **لَقَدْ خَلَقْنَا ٱلْإِنسَٰنَ فِى كَبَدٍ.**

Not "the human being will face struggle." Not "life is sometimes hard." Not "hardship builds character."

We **created** the human being — past tense, finished, done — **inside** struggle.

The word used for struggle is going to redirect everything you thought you knew about why life is hard. Because it doesn't come from the vocabulary of tests, or trials, or discipline. It comes from the vocabulary of the body. From the organ that sits in your abdomen and processes what would otherwise poison you.

---

## PART 1: THE LINGUISTIC JOURNEY

### I. كَبَدٍ — The Liver Word

In English, the emotional centre of the body is the heart. We say "heartbroken." "Follow your heart." "My heart goes out to you." The heart is where love, grief, and depth live in the English imagination.

Arabic does something different.

The heart — *qalb* — is the organ of understanding, turning, and spiritual perception. When the Quran says the heart is sealed, it means the capacity to understand truth has been blocked.

But there is another organ. The **liver** — *kabid* (كَبِد). And in the Arabic body, the liver is where **suffering** lives.

When an Arab says someone is the "flesh of my liver" — *falthat kabidī* — they mean: you are the part of me that suffers most when wounded. The liver is not romantic. It is visceral. It is the deepest possible interior — not the heart that beats visibly, but the organ that sits hidden, heavy, processing what would otherwise destroy you.

The root **ك ب د** carries this family of meanings: the liver, hardship, the middle of something (the liver sits in the middle of the body), severity, toil. The word **كَبَد** (kabad) — the form used here — is a *maṣdar* (verbal noun) meaning toil, hardship, struggle, suffering. But it never loses its connection to the organ. When you hear كَبَد, the liver is always present in the background — the body's hidden processor of difficulty.

[PAUSE]

Now notice what Allah did NOT do with this word.

He did not make it definite. He did not say فِى الكَبَدِ — "in THE toil." If He had, the article would have specified it — pointed to a known, identified, bounded struggle. "The struggle" implies you can name it, outline it, contain it.

Instead: **كَبَدٍ**. Indefinite. With *tanwīn* — that final *-in* sound that marks an indefinite noun in Arabic. And indefiniteness on an abstract noun in Arabic does two things simultaneously: it **magnifies** (this is not small toil — it is vast, uncontainable toil) and it **generalises** (this is not one kind of toil — it is every kind).

كَبَدٍ without the article means: struggle that cannot be specified. Struggle that has no ceiling and no walls. Struggle that is so comprehensive it resists the definite article the way the ocean resists a label. You can't say "the ocean" and feel you've captured it. You can't say "the struggle" and feel you've named what the human being lives inside.

The tanwīn opens the word to include everything: the struggle of birth — the first breath is a scream. The struggle of teething. Of learning to stand, to walk, to speak. Of school. Of work. Of loving someone and watching them change. Of burying your parents. Of aging. Of the body slowly refusing what the mind still wants.

All of it. كَبَدٍ.

Your specific, unnamed, particular struggle — the one you are carrying right now, the one you couldn't explain to someone even if they asked — is inside this tanwīn. The indefiniteness was designed to hold it.

### II. فِى — The Preposition That Changes Everything

Now the preposition.

There are several ways Allah could have connected the human being to struggle in Arabic.

He could have said خَلَقْنَا ٱلْإِنسَٰنَ **لِلْ**كَبَدِ — "We created the human being **for** struggle." The *lām* of purpose. This would mean struggle is the goal — the reason you were made. But Allah didn't say that. Struggle is not the purpose.

He could have said خَلَقْنَا ٱلْإِنسَٰنَ **مَعَ** كَبَدٍ — "We created the human being **with** struggle." The *ma'a* of accompaniment. This would mean struggle walks beside you. A companion. But Allah didn't say that either. Struggle is not your companion — standing next to you, separable, something you could theoretically step away from.

He could have said خَلَقْنَا ٱلْإِنسَٰنَ **عَلَىٰ** كَبَدٍ — "We created the human being **upon** struggle." The *'alā* of surface contact. This would mean struggle is the ground you stand on. But Allah didn't say that. Struggle is not the floor.

He said **فِى**.

**فِى كَبَدٍ** — "in struggle."

فِى is the preposition of **containment**. Inside. Immersed. Surrounded. When something is فِى something else, it is not alongside it, not on top of it, not walking next to it. It is **inside** it. The way a fish is in water. The way a seed is in soil. The way a child is in the womb.

[PAUSE]

The human being was not created beside struggle, or for struggle, or on struggle. The human being was created **inside** struggle.

Struggle is the medium.

Think about what this means. A fish doesn't "experience" water as an event. It doesn't have water days and non-water days. Water is the medium of the fish's entire existence — it breathes through it, moves through it, lives and dies in it. Remove the water and the fish ceases to function.

كَبَد is your water.

You do not have struggle periods and non-struggle periods. You are never outside of it. The moments you think are rest — sleeping, eating, being held by someone who loves you — are struggles too. The body is working. The cells are fighting. The mind is processing. The spirit is negotiating between what it wants and what it has.

This is not pessimism. Stay with me. This is the opposite of pessimism. Because if struggle were an intruder — something that shouldn't be there — then its presence would be evidence of failure. Evidence that something went wrong. Evidence that you, or the world, or God, made an error.

But **لَقَدْ خَلَقْنَا** — We *created* you in it. Past tense. Done. The blueprint. This is not a malfunction. This is the specification.

[PAUSE]

### III. لَقَدْ — The Certainty That Closes the Door

One more piece before we leave the language.

The ayah opens with **لَقَدْ** — two particles combined.

**لَ** — the emphatic *lām*. "Indeed." "Truly." A particle whose sole function is emphasis. It says: what follows is not casual, not speculative, not open to negotiation.

**قَدْ** — the certainty particle. When placed before a perfect (past tense) verb, it adds absolute verification. "It has certainly happened." "It is decidedly the case." Think of it as the Arabic equivalent of underlining, bolding, and italicising simultaneously.

Together: **لَقَدْ**. The strongest affirmative construction Arabic has for a completed action. "Most certainly, without any doubt whatsoever, this has already happened."

And what has certainly happened? **خَلَقْنَا ٱلْإِنسَٰنَ فِى كَبَدٍ** — We created the human being in toil.

This is not a suggestion. Not a philosophical observation. Not one perspective among many. لَقَدْ closes the door on alternatives. There is no version of human existence that exists outside of كَبَد. It is not possible. It was never offered. The creation was done, finished, completed — in the perfect tense — and it was done فِى كَبَدٍ. Inside it. Not as an addition. As the medium.

## PART 2: THE THEMATIC DEPTHS

### Theme I: Struggle Was the Specification

There is an industry — worth billions of dollars — dedicated to one premise: that struggle is a problem to be solved.

Self-help. Productivity. Optimisation. Biohacking. Therapy that aims not at understanding suffering but at eliminating it. Meditation apps that promise calm. Supplements that promise energy. Systems that promise a life without friction.

The operating assumption underneath all of it is: *the natural state of the human being is ease, and struggle is a deviation from that state.*

If you could just find the right routine. If you could just fix the right trauma. If you could just earn enough, sleep enough, optimise enough — then the struggle would end. The friction would dissolve. And you would arrive at the place where life is finally, permanently smooth.

لَقَدْ خَلَقْنَا ٱلْإِنسَٰنَ فِى كَبَدٍ.

No.

The natural state of the human being IS struggle. Not as punishment. Not as correction. Not as consequence of sin. As **design**. The verb is خَلَقْنَا — We *created*. The same verb used for the heavens and the earth, for the alternation of night and day, for the fashioning of the human being from clay. Creation. Intentional. Purposeful. Not accidental.

And the medium of that creation is كَبَد. The human being was not created and then placed into struggle as an afterthought. The creation itself occurred فِى — inside — the struggle. The way a pot is shaped on a wheel — the pressure is not applied to the pot after it's made. The pressure *is* the making. Remove the pressure, and there is no pot. There is only clay.

[PAUSE]

What changes if you believe this?

If struggle is a malfunction, then the right response to difficulty is: something has gone wrong. Fix it. Eliminate it. Get back to the default state of ease.

But if struggle is the medium — the water the fish was designed to swim in — then the right response to difficulty is entirely different. It is not: something has gone wrong. It is: **I am inside what I was made for.** The difficulty is not evidence of failure. It is evidence of being human. And the question is not "how do I get out of this?" but "how do I move *through* this in a way that is worthy of having been created?"

This reframes everything. The sleepless night with a newborn is not a deviation from normal. It is كَبَد. The grief after losing someone is not a malfunction. It is كَبَد. The exhaustion of honest work. The heartbreak of trying and failing. The slow erosion of a body that was never built to last. All of it is كَبَد.

And the One who placed you inside it said لَقَدْ — most certainly. He was not apologising. He was declaring.

[PAUSE — major]

### Theme II: The Indefinite Struggle — Why Your Pain Has No Article

There's a specific kind of loneliness that comes from suffering that no one else can see.

You're sitting in a room full of people and everyone is functioning — smiling, eating, making conversation — and inside your chest there is a weight that has no name. It's not clinical enough for a diagnosis. It's not dramatic enough for a story. It's just... there. The low-grade hum of difficulty that accompanies being alive.

And because it has no name, you can't point to it. You can't explain it. You can't even fully acknowledge it to yourself, because acknowledging it would require a category, and there is no category that fits.

كَبَدٍ was made for this.

The tanwīn — that indefinite ending — is not a grammatical accident. It is a theological statement. Allah did not say "We created the human being in THE struggle" — because THE struggle would need a name. It would need to be identified, categorised, bounded. You would need to point at it and say: *that* is what He meant.

But كَبَدٍ — indefinite, unconstrained, open — holds everything. The struggle that has a diagnosis AND the struggle that doesn't. The grief that people send flowers for AND the grief that no one knows about. The difficulty of poverty AND the difficulty of wealth (and yes — wealth has its own كَبَد, its own liver-deep toil that the wealthy often cannot name to anyone because the world has decided they have no right to suffer).

[PAUSE]

The indefiniteness is mercy.

Because if the ayah had specified the struggle — if it had said "We created the human being in poverty" or "We created the human being in illness" — then the person whose struggle is different would feel excluded. Would feel that their pain doesn't count. Doesn't qualify.

But كَبَدٍ has no article. No specification. No walls. It is the container big enough to hold every unnamed, uncategorised, illegible pain that any human being has ever carried.

Including yours. Right now. The one you might not have words for. The one that doesn't fit the categories. The one that sits in the liver — not the heart where people can see it, but the liver, the hidden organ, the thing that processes what would otherwise poison you.

Allah saw that pain before you could name it. He placed the word for it in a surah, without the definite article, so that no one — in any century, in any condition, in any degree of suffering — would read this ayah and think: *He wasn't talking about me.*

He was.

لَقَدْ. Most certainly. Without any doubt.

[PAUSE — major]

## CLOSING SYNTHESIS

### The Architecture — Five Words, Three Layers

The ayah is built in three concentric rings:

**Outer ring — Certainty:** لَقَدْ. The emphatic frame. Before you hear what was created, you are told that what follows is beyond question. The certainty is established before the content. You are not being invited to consider a possibility. You are being told what is.

**Middle ring — The Act:** خَلَقْنَا ٱلْإِنسَٰنَ. We created the human being. The plural of majesty — the royal "We." The verb in the perfect tense — done, finished, not ongoing. And ٱلْإِنسَٰنَ — THE human being, genus, universal, everyone. The act encompasses all of humanity, performed by Allah, completed.

**Inner ring — The Medium:** فِى كَبَدٍ. In toil. The indefinite, the unspecified, the organ-word. This is the centre of the ayah. Everything moves toward it. The certainty exists to frame it. The creation exists to reveal it. And كَبَدٍ — indefinite, tanwīn'd, liver-deep — sits at the core, waiting for anyone who reads it to recognise their own reflection.

The structure is a zoom: from the certainty of the frame, through the act of creation, to the medium in which the creation exists. Outward in, like the niche in the Light Verse — but instead of light at the centre, there is struggle. And the struggle is not darkness. It is the medium in which the human being becomes what they were created to become.

### The Surah's Response

What comes immediately after? 90:5-7:

*"Does he think that no one has power over him? He says, 'I have spent abundant wealth!' Does he think that no one sees him?"*

The human being who was created IN struggle — immersed in it, surrounded by it, breathing it — looks at his wealth and thinks he has escaped. He thinks the money places him above كَبَد. He thinks no one has power over him. He thinks no one sees him.

But he is still فِى كَبَدٍ. The wealth didn't remove the struggle. It changed its shape. And the arrogance of thinking you've risen above the human condition is itself the deepest form of كَبَد — because it is struggle that has forgotten it is struggling.

---

### Questions to Carry

What struggle in your life have you been treating as a malfunction — something that shouldn't be happening, evidence that something went wrong — that might actually be the medium you were designed to grow in?

Is there a كَبَد you carry that has no name — no diagnosis, no category, no recognised status — and if so, does it change anything to know that the tanwīn was placed there to hold exactly that?

If the fish were to suddenly become aware that it's in water — not as a crisis, but as a recognition — what would change about how it swims? What changes for you when you recognise you are فِى كَبَدٍ?

The next time you feel the weight of difficulty and think "this shouldn't be happening" — can you hear لَقَدْ underneath it? The most certain declaration in Arabic saying: this is exactly what was always happening. You are inside what you were made for.

---

### One-Sentence Distillation

**You were not placed in struggle as a punishment — you were created inside it as a medium, because the only version of you worth becoming is the one formed in كَبَد.**

---

### Closing Invitation

The next time life is hard — not catastrophically hard, just the ordinary, daily, liver-deep hard that comes with being alive — let the five words arrive.

لَقَدْ خَلَقْنَا ٱلْإِنسَٰنَ فِى كَبَدٍ.

Not "this too shall pass." Not "God doesn't give you more than you can handle." Not a platitude. A declaration.

You are inside what you were made for. The struggle is the water. You are the fish. And the One who created you inside it — لَقَدْ, most certainly, without any doubt — also created the gills.

---

### Du'ā

*Allāhumma, You who created us inside struggle and called it creation — not punishment, not correction, creation — give us the wisdom to stop treating كَبَد as a mistake. Let us see the water we swim in and know it was always the plan. When the toil has no name and the liver is heavy and we cannot explain to anyone why — let the tanwīn hold us: the indefiniteness that was placed there so no pain would be excluded, so no struggle would be too small or too strange to count. You said لَقَدْ. Most certainly. We believe You.*

*Āmīn.*
