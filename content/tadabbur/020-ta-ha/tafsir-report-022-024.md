## 20:22-24

### Ibn Kathir (English)

Ibn Kathir treats the hand emerging white as Musa's second sign after the staff. He explains that Musa was commanded to place his hand into the opening of his garment or under his arm, and that it came out shining without disease. He links this passage to 28:32, where the staff and hand are described as two proofs from Musa's Lord to Pharaoh and his chiefs. This supports the reflection's reading that the sign is intimate preparation before public mission, not a personal power detached from Allah.

### al-Tabari (Arabic)

Al-Tabari explains `wa-dmum yadaka ila janahika` as Musa placing his hand beneath his upper arm or against his side, and records reports from Mujahid and others on this meaning. He explains `bayda'a min ghayri su'` as whiteness without leprosy or defect, and says `ayatan ukhra` means another mark and proof besides the staff sign. On 20:23, he reads the purpose as Allah showing Musa from His great evidences of His tremendous authority and power. On 20:24, he explains Pharaoh's `tagha` as exceeding his proper measure and rebelling against his Lord, with the implied command to call him to tawhid, obedience, and release of Bani Isra'il.

### al-Muyassar (Arabic)

Al-Muyassar summarizes 20:22 as Allah commanding Musa to place his hand at his side under the upper arm so it would emerge white like snow, without leprosy, as another sign. It explains 20:23 as Allah showing Musa great proofs of His power, authority, and the truth of Musa's message. It explains 20:24 as the command to go to Pharaoh because he exceeded his measure and rebelled against his Lord, so Musa should call him to Allah's oneness and worship.

### Alignment Notes

The reflection aligns with the tafsir sources on the central points: the hand sign is the second sign after the staff; the hand is drawn to Musa's side or underarm; its whiteness is miraculous brightness without disease or harm; the sign functions as a proof of Musa's truthfulness; and Pharaoh's transgression is rebellion and exceeding the limit before Allah. The thematic extensions about private preparation before public responsibility, visible difference not being damage, and tughyan as overflow beyond the servant's boundary are tadabbur applications grounded in the wording and classical tafsir rather than independent claims.
