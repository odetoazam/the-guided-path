scholarly_note: "The phrase لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِۦ شَىْءٌ has been the subject of extensive scholarly discussion across grammar, theology, and philosophy. The central grammatical question: is the كَ (ka, 'like') in كَمِثْلِهِ emphatic/redundant (zā'ida — added for rhetorical emphasis, making the meaning 'nothing is like Him') or literal (making the meaning 'nothing is like His likeness' — a double negation that negates even the concept of similarity)? Al-Zamakhshari (al-Kashshāf) argued the ka is zā'ida for emphasis, meaning: 'there is nothing like Him.' This is the majority position. Al-Rāzī (Mafātīḥ al-Ghayb) explored both readings but favored the literal reading as deeper: if nothing is like His likeness, then His likeness itself is an impossibility — you cannot even form the concept of a comparison because the comparison has no referent. Ibn Kathir reported both positions and noted that the phrase is the strongest possible negation of tashbīh (anthropomorphism). The Ash'ari and Maturidi schools both use this ayah as the foundational proof-text for tanzīh (divine transcendence beyond comparison). The Mu'tazila used it to argue against the Beatific Vision (ru'yah). The Athari school reads it as a straightforward negation of all likeness while affirming whatever attributes Allah affirmed for Himself. The closing وَهُوَ ٱلسَّمِيعُ ٱلْبَصِيرُ is understood by scholars as the corrective balance: after declaring absolute transcendence, Allah immediately affirms two attributes of intimate proximity — He hears and He sees — preventing the listener from concluding that transcendence means distance. The word فَاطِرُ (Fāṭir, Originator) from root ف-ط-ر carries the primary meaning of splitting/cleaving open. Ibn Abbas reported that he only understood the meaning of fāṭir when two Bedouins came disputing over a well, and one said 'anā faṭartuhā' — 'I split it open / I originated it.' The verb ذَرَأَ (dhara'a) means to scatter, sow, multiply — and the pronoun فِيهِ ('in it' / 'through it') has been debated: does it refer to the pairing system (He multiplies you through pairing) or to the earth? ## Absence Flags
1. The ayah says مِّنْ أَنفُسِكُمْ ("from your own selves") — the mates are not made as a separate creation but FROM you. This is present. What is ABSENT is any specification of what makes a mate a mate. No qualities are listed. No functions. No hierarchy. Just: from your selves, mates. The absence of specification makes the concept irreducible — a mate is not defined by role but by origin.
2. After لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِۦ شَىْءٌ there is NO explanation of what Allah IS like. The ayah only says what is NOT true. This is the most radical absence in the Quran: every other theological statement in the Quran affirms something about Allah. This one ONLY negates. The absence of a positive description after the negation is not a failure to describe — it IS the description. He cannot be described by comparison. Period.
3. The pronoun فِيهِ in يَذْرَؤُكُمْ فِيهِ — "He multiplies you in IT" — the referent of "it" is ambiguous (the pairing system? the earth? the process?). The absence of a clear antecedent forces the listener to supply the meaning, which is itself a statement: the mechanism of your multiplication is so integrated into reality that you can't point to a single thing as "it."

# Nothing Like His Likeness
## The Six Words That Broke Every Comparison
### Ash-Shura 42:11

---

[ayah:42:11] فَاطِرُ ٱلسَّمَـٰوَٰتِ وَٱلْأَرْضِ ۚ جَعَلَ لَكُم مِّنْ أَنفُسِكُمْ أَزْوَٰجًا وَمِنَ ٱلْأَنْعَـٰمِ أَزْوَٰجًا ۖ يَذْرَؤُكُمْ فِيهِ ۚ لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِۦ شَىْءٌ ۖ وَهُوَ ٱلسَّمِيعُ ٱلْبَصِيرُ

*Fāṭiru al-samāwāti wa al-arḍ. Ja'ala lakum min anfusikum azwājan wa mina al-an'āmi azwāja. Yadhraʾukum fīh. Laysa kamithlihi shay'. Wa huwa al-Samī' al-Baṣīr.*

**"Originator of the heavens and the earth. He made for you from your own selves mates, and among the cattle, mates — He multiplies you thereby. There is nothing like His likeness. And He is the All-Hearing, the All-Seeing."**

---

## Rebuilding the World

There is a question that every civilization has tried to answer. Every philosophy. Every religion. Every person lying awake at three in the morning.

**What is God like?**

The entire history of human thought is an attempt to answer this question. We build temples to house an image. We write theology to sharpen a concept. We argue in lecture halls about attributes and essence. We sit in meditation trying to empty the mind of everything except the experience of the divine.

And here — in the middle of a Meccan surah about revelation and the nature of divine communication — Allah answers that question in six Arabic words.

Not with a description.

With a demolition.

**لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِۦ شَىْءٌ** — "There is nothing like His likeness."

Six words. And with them, every image you ever had of God — everything you imagined, conceptualized, pictured, compared Him to — all of it is swept off the table. Not refined. Not corrected. *Dissolved.*

But here's what makes this ayah unlike anything else in the history of theology: it doesn't stop at transcendence. It doesn't leave you with an abstract, unreachable God floating beyond the horizon of thought. The very next breath: **وَهُوَ ٱلسَّمِيعُ ٱلْبَصِيرُ** — "And He is the All-Hearing, the All-Seeing."

The God who is beyond every comparison you could ever construct — is also the God who hears every word you say. Who sees every tear that falls.

Transcendence and intimacy. In one breath.

And that tension — that impossible combination — is what this ayah came to teach.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### Element 1: Fāṭir — The God Who Split Reality Open

The ayah opens with a name. Not *al-Khāliq* (the Creator). Not *al-Bāri'* (the Originator in the sense of 59:24). A different name entirely:

**فَاطِرُ ٱلسَّمَـٰوَٰتِ وَٱلْأَرْضِ**

*Fāṭir* — from the root **ف-ط-ر**.

Let's sit with this root, because it carries an image that *khalaqa* (to create) cannot reach.

In Arabic, **فَطَرَ** means to split open. To cleave. To rupture. When you break bread by tearing it apart with your hands — that is *faṭr*. When the dawn cracks the darkness — that is *faṭr*. When a seed splits the earth — that is *faṭr*. When you break your fast and tear through the seal of hunger — that is *ifṭār*, from the same root.

Ibn Abbas — the companion the Prophet ﷺ prayed would be given deep understanding of the Quran — reportedly said he didn't fully grasp the meaning of *fāṭir* until two Bedouins came before him in a dispute over a well. One of them said: **أَنَا فَطَرْتُهَا** — "I *split it open*. I was the first to dig it. I originated it."

[PAUSE]

Notice what this does to the concept of creation.

*Khalaqa* — the more common Arabic word for creating — carries the image of **measuring, proportioning, designing**. A *khāliq* works with a plan. There is forethought. There is structure.

*Faṭara* carries none of that. *Faṭara* is what happens when there is nothing — no material, no blueprint, no precedent — and then reality *cracks open* and something exists that never existed before. Not because it was designed. Because non-existence itself was *ruptured*.

The sound of the word matches. Listen to it: **فَاطِر**. The **ط** — the emphatic *ṭā'* — is one of the heaviest, most percussive sounds in Arabic. It hits the palate like something cracking. The phonetics of the word sound like something splitting open. The Creator didn't assemble the heavens. He *broke open nothingness* and the heavens poured out.

This is the God who is about to be described as beyond all comparison. And the first thing you learn about Him — before the negation, before the philosophical statement — is that He creates by *rupture*. Not by resembling anything. Not by following a model. By tearing open what never existed before.

The name itself is the preparation for what comes next.

[PAUSE]

### Element 2: From Your Own Selves — The Pairing Before the Scattering

Before the ayah reaches its theological summit, it passes through something astonishingly intimate:

**جَعَلَ لَكُم مِّنْ أَنفُسِكُمْ أَزْوَٰجًا**

"He **made** for you, **from your own selves**, mates."

Three things are happening in this construction that most translations flatten into nothing.

**First: the verb is جَعَلَ, not خَلَقَ.** He didn't *create* mates for you. He *placed* them. He *appointed* them. He *arranged* them. The verb **جعل** carries deliberate placement — an act of intentional arrangement. Your mate was not an accident of biology. Your mate was an appointment. A purposeful arrangement by the One who split open the heavens.

**Second: مِّنْ أَنفُسِكُمْ — from your own selves.** Not "from another species." Not "from a different substance." From *you*. The mate is not external. The mate is drawn from the same essential reality as you. Whatever you are made of — your mate is made of the same thing. You are not two foreign bodies coexisting. You are one reality that was *split* — and there's that root again, the echo of *faṭara* — into two beings who recognize each other because they share an origin.

**Third: the word أَزْوَٰجًا is indefinite.** Not *al-azwāj* (the specific mates) but *azwājan* — mates, unspecified, open. No definition of what a mate does. No role. No function. No hierarchy. Just: *from your own selves, pairs.* The absence of specification is the definition. A mate is defined not by function but by **origin** — from the same substance as you.

Then the ayah extends the pairing beyond the human: **وَمِنَ ٱلْأَنْعَـٰمِ أَزْوَٰجًا** — "and among the cattle, mates." The pairing principle is not exclusively human. It runs through creation. It is a design principle woven into the fabric of existence.

And then the purpose of the pairing is revealed in one extraordinary verb:

**يَذْرَؤُكُمْ فِيهِ**

"He **scatters you** through it."

[PAUSE]

The verb **ذَرَأَ** — *dhara'a* — comes from a root that means to scatter, to sow, to spread seed. It is an *agricultural* verb. Organic. When a farmer takes a handful of grain and throws it across the field — that is *dhar'*. The imperfect tense — **يَذْرَؤُكُمْ** — means He is *still doing it*. Right now. Continuously. Every birth is another handful of seed scattered across the field of existence.

And listen to the sound: **يَذْرَؤُكُمْ** — the **ذ** is soft and flowing, the **ر** rolls, and then the **ء** (hamza) at the end creates a sudden stop. Flow-flow-halt. Like seeds scattering through the air and then landing. The phonetics mirror the image.

The pronoun **فِيهِ** — "in it" or "through it" — is beautifully ambiguous. What is the "it"? The pairing system? The earth? The process of creation itself? The classical scholars debated this, and the ambiguity is the answer: the mechanism by which God multiplies you is so deeply woven into reality itself that you cannot separate it from a single referent. It is *all of these* — the pairing, the earth, the process. You are scattered through it all.

Now notice the movement of the ayah. It has taken you on a journey from the most cosmic scale — **فَاطِرُ ٱلسَّمَـٰوَٰتِ وَٱلْأَرْضِ** (the One who split open the heavens and the earth) — down into the most intimate scale — **مِّنْ أَنفُسِكُمْ** (from your own selves) — and back out to the most universal — **يَذْرَؤُكُمْ** (He scatters you across existence).

Cosmic. Intimate. Cosmic again.

And then — with the listener holding both scales simultaneously — the ayah delivers the statement that breaks everything:

[PAUSE — major pause]

### Element 3: The Six Words — لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِۦ شَىْءٌ

This is it. The theological summit of the Quran. The statement that every school of Islamic theology claims as its proof-text. The six words that broke every comparison a human being has ever attempted to make about God.

**لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِۦ شَىْءٌ**

Let me take this apart so slowly that each word arrives on its own.

**لَيْسَ** — "there is not." The sharpest negation in Arabic. Not *lā* (a simple "no") and not *mā* (a denial). *Laysa* is a verb from the *kāna* family — the family of verbs that deal with states of being. When you use *laysa*, you are not just saying "no." You are saying: **this state of being does not exist.** The negation is ontological. It targets reality itself.

**كَـ** — "like." The smallest word in this phrase. One letter. The Arabic preposition of comparison. It is the word that creates analogies, metaphors, similes. Every time you say "something is *like* something else," this letter is doing the work.

**مِثْلِهِۦ** — "His likeness." From the root **م-ث-ل**, meaning similitude, resemblance, equal. *Mithluhu* is "His equal, His match, something resembling Him."

**شَىْءٌ** — "a thing." Indefinite. Unspecified. Any thing. Everything. Nothing excluded.

Now here is where the Arabic does something that has occupied grammarians, theologians, and philosophers for fourteen centuries.

If Allah wanted to simply say "nothing is like Him," the Arabic would be:

**لَيْسَ مِثْلَهُ شَىْءٌ** — "There is not His likeness — a thing."

That would be sufficient. Clear. Complete.

But that is **not** what He said.

He added one letter. He added the **كَـ** — the preposition of comparison — *before* the word "His likeness."

**لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِۦ شَىْءٌ** — "There is not **like His likeness** — a thing."

[PAUSE]

Stay with me, because this is where the ground shifts under your feet.

The scholars of Arabic grammar identified two possible readings, and what's remarkable is that both readings — rather than contradicting each other — build the same truth from two directions, like two searchlights illuminating the same mountain from opposite sides.

**Reading One: The ka is emphatic (zā'ida).**

This is the position of most grammarians and al-Zamakhshari in al-Kashshāf. The *ka* is added for rhetorical emphasis — a known feature of Arabic rhetoric. The meaning would be: "There is nothing like Him." The emphasis of the extra *ka* intensifies the negation. Not just "nothing resembles Him" but "NOTHING — **nothing** — resembles Him."

This is the reading that draws the boundary. No comparison holds. No analogy reaches. No image, no concept, no thought-experiment, no meditation, no mystical vision — nothing in any domain of human experience or imagination can stand in for Him.

**Reading Two: The ka is literal.**

This is the reading al-Rāzī explored in Mafātīḥ al-Ghayb, and it is breathtaking.

If the *ka* is doing its normal work — its function as a preposition of comparison — then the ayah is saying: "There is nothing **like** His likeness."

Not: "there is nothing like Him."

"There is nothing like **His likeness.**"

The negation has moved one level deeper.

[PAUSE]

Think about what this does. In the first reading, the statement says: you cannot compare anything to God. No comparison holds.

In the second reading, the statement says: you cannot even **form the concept** of something that could be compared to God. The very category of "things that are like God" — that category is *empty*. It has no members. Not because nothing has achieved resemblance to God — but because the resemblance itself is *an impossibility*. You cannot get close enough to God to fail to match Him. You cannot even begin the comparison. The starting line doesn't exist.

It is as if someone asked you: "What is north of the North Pole?" The question is not difficult. The question is *structurally impossible*. There is no direction called "north" once you are standing at the North Pole. The concept collapses.

That is what **لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِۦ شَىْءٌ** does to every comparison ever attempted with God. It doesn't say the comparison fails. It says the comparison **cannot be initiated**. The concept of "like Him" has no referent. It is a phrase that points at nothing.

And here is where both readings converge into the same summit: whether the *ka* is emphatic or literal, the result is **total, impassable, unbridgeable divine uniqueness**. Not uniqueness like a rare jewel is unique — you can still imagine another jewel. Uniqueness that dismantles the very machinery of comparison. The word "unique" itself is not strong enough. Because "unique" implies a category in which there is only one member. What this ayah is saying is that **there is no category**.

[PAUSE — major pause]

Now notice one more thing about the grammar. The word **شَىْءٌ** — "a thing" — is indefinite and comes at the end of the sentence. In Arabic, when an indefinite noun appears in the context of negation, it carries the meaning of **absolute generality**. Not "no particular thing." Not "nothing specific." It means: **nothing whatsoever**. No object. No concept. No quality. No experience. No thought. No sensation. No image generated by the most powerful imagination a human being has ever possessed.

Nothing.

The indefiniteness of *shay'* is the final sweep. Whatever you were going to exempt from the negation — whatever comparison you were going to say "well, but maybe THIS one is close" — the tanwīn on *shay'un* closes that door. The word trails off into the open air of its own indefiniteness, swallowing every possible exception.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The God You Cannot Picture — And Why That's Freedom

We live in an age of images.

We photograph everything. We visualize data. We build mental models. We say "I need to *see* it to understand it." Our entire cognitive architecture is built on representation — on making internal pictures of external realities, on finding patterns, on mapping the unknown onto the known.

This is how we learn language: this word *represents* that object. This is how we navigate space: this mental map *represents* that neighborhood. This is how we think about the future: this scenario *represents* what might happen.

Representation. Resemblance. Comparison. The human mind is a comparison machine. It is how we survive. You see a shadow move and your brain compares it to every threat it has ever catalogued. You meet a new person and your brain compares them to every person you've already known. You face a new problem and your brain compares it to every problem you've already solved.

**And then you turn to God.**

And the comparison machine fires up. It reaches for its library of resemblances. What is God like? A father? A king? A force? Love? Light? The universe itself? Consciousness?

Every religion in human history is, at one level, an answer to the question: what image do we use? What comparison do we reach for?

And this ayah — لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِۦ شَىْءٌ — does something that no other theological statement in any tradition does with such surgical precision.

It doesn't say: "Here's a better image."

It says: **the machine itself doesn't apply.**

[PAUSE]

This is not a correction. It's a category shift.

When someone says "God is not like a king," they're operating within the comparison framework — just eliminating one option. When someone says "God is not like anything you've ever seen, but He's sort of like love, except bigger" — they're still operating within the framework. They're reaching.

This ayah doesn't eliminate options within the framework. It dismantles the framework.

And here is why that is not a loss but a liberation.

Every image you've ever had of God — even the most sophisticated, the most philosophically refined, the most mystically achieved — was *smaller than God*. By definition. Because it was an image. And an image is a thing. And **لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِۦ شَىْءٌ** — no thing is like Him.

As long as you are holding an image of God, you are holding something that is not God. You are relating to your own mental construction. And you are — without meaning to — making your mental construction into the object of your devotion, your fear, your hope.

The ayah sets you free from that. It says: **let go of the picture.** Not because the picture is blasphemous — but because the picture is a cage. It limits what you can receive. It limits what you can ask for. It limits the size of your hope and the depth of your awe.

A God you can picture is a God you can eventually get comfortable with. A God you can imagine is a God whose behavior you will eventually start to predict. A God you can compare is a God you will eventually try to control — because comparison is the first step toward mastery.

**لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِۦ شَىْءٌ** is not taking something away from you. It is removing the ceiling from your worship. It is saying: whatever you thought you were praying to — *He is more than that.* Whatever mercy you thought was possible — *His is more than that.* Whatever forgiveness you thought was available — *His is more than that.*

Because the moment you put a boundary on your concept of God, you put a boundary on what you believe God can do. And this ayah says: **there is no boundary.** Not even a conceptual one.

[PAUSE]

### Theme 2: The Impossible Combination — Transcendence That Hears You Cry

And now the ayah does something that, if you are paying attention, should stop you in your tracks.

After delivering the most absolute statement of divine transcendence in all of scripture — after dismantling every comparison, every image, every concept — the very next words are:

**وَهُوَ ٱلسَّمِيعُ ٱلْبَصِيرُ**

"And **He** is the All-Hearing, the All-Seeing."

[PAUSE]

Notice the pronoun shift. For the entire first half of the ayah, Allah has been addressing *you* directly — لَكُم, أَنفُسِكُمْ, يَذْرَؤُكُمْ — "for you, from your selves, He scatters you." The listener is addressed. Intimacy. Proximity. You are being spoken *to*.

Then comes the negation: لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِۦ شَىْءٌ — and the address disappears. This is a statement about *Him*, in the third person. You are no longer being spoken to. You are hearing a declaration about a Being who has just been placed beyond your reach. The pronoun shift creates distance — grammatical distance that mirrors the theological distance.

And then: **وَهُوَ** — "And He" — still third person, still at a distance — but what follows is not more distance. What follows is the most intimate pair of names Allah possesses:

**ٱلسَّمِيعُ** — the All-Hearing. The One whose essential nature is to hear.

**ٱلْبَصِيرُ** — the All-Seeing. The One whose essential nature is to see.

Both are on the **fa'īl** pattern — the intensive adjective in Arabic that denotes not a momentary act but a permanent attribute. A *sāmi'* (active participle) is someone who is hearing right now. A **samī'** (intensive adjective) is someone whose *nature* is hearing. It is not something He does. It is something He *is*. The hearing never started and it never stops. It is not a response to your calling. It is a permanent state of the divine reality.

The same with **baṣīr**. Not someone who is looking at you right now. Someone whose *essence* is sight. He doesn't watch you. Watching is what He is.

[PAUSE]

Now feel the impossibility of what this ayah is doing.

The God who is beyond every comparison your mind can construct — who is so far beyond your categories that even the *concept* of something resembling Him is empty — this God **hears you.** Not metaphorically. Not in some abstract, philosophical sense where "hearing" means "being aware of vibrations in a cosmic field." He **hears** — in the most intimate, fa'īl, permanent, essential, never-interrupted sense of the word.

This is the paradox at the heart of tawḥīd. And it is not a paradox to be resolved. It is a paradox to be *inhabited*.

Most theological systems fall off this tightrope on one side or the other. Either God is so transcendent that He becomes unreachable — the God of the philosophers, the Unmoved Mover, the abstract principle, the God you can think about but never talk to. Or God is so intimate that He becomes manageable — the God of the anthropomorphists, the God who gets angry like you get angry, who loves like you love, who sits like you sit — a God you can picture and therefore domesticate.

This ayah refuses both. In one breath.

**لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِۦ شَىْءٌ** — you will never reach Him with your concepts.

**وَهُوَ ٱلسَّمِيعُ ٱلْبَصِيرُ** — but He has already reached you with His hearing and His sight.

[PAUSE]

Think about what this means for the person making du'a.

You raise your hands. You open your mouth. You don't know what God looks like. You can't picture Him. You can't conceptualize Him. You have no mental image of who is receiving this prayer.

And that — according to this ayah — is the correct starting position.

Because the One receiving it is beyond your pictures. But His hearing of your prayer is more permanent, more essential, more unchanging than anything you have ever experienced. He was hearing you before you spoke. He will be hearing you after you fall silent. Hearing you is not something He decided to do. It is something He *is*.

The most vulnerable position in the universe — a human being praying to a God they cannot picture — is also, according to this ayah, the most secure position in the universe. Because the God you cannot picture is the God whose very nature is to hear and see *you*.

You cannot reach Him with your mind. He has already reached you with His essential attributes.

That is not a paradox to be figured out. That is a reality to be **lived in.**

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture of the Ayah

Now step back and see the whole design.

The ayah moves through four stages, and the movement itself is the theology:

**Stage 1 — فَاطِرُ ٱلسَّمَـٰوَٰتِ وَٱلْأَرْضِ** — Creation by *rupture*. The most cosmic scale. Non-existence split open, and reality poured out.

**Stage 2 — جَعَلَ لَكُم مِّنْ أَنفُسِكُمْ أَزْوَٰجًا... يَذْرَؤُكُمْ فِيهِ** — The most intimate scale. From your own selves, mates. Through pairing, multiplication. The God who split open the heavens also designed the system by which a child emerges from its mother. The cosmic and the intimate are the same act.

**Stage 3 — لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِۦ شَىْءٌ** — Total transcendence. After showing you His work at the cosmic and intimate scale, He declares: and yet, nothing you saw gives you the right to imagine you know what I am. All of it — the heavens, the pairing, the scattering — all of it is *His work*. None of it is *Him*.

**Stage 4 — وَهُوَ ٱلسَّمِيعُ ٱلْبَصِيرُ** — And yet: He hears you. He sees you. The transcendent God is also the intimate God. Not because transcendence was exaggerated. Not because intimacy was overstated. Because both are simultaneously, permanently, essentially true.

The movement is: **creation → intimacy → transcendence → intimacy again.** The ayah begins with God's power, descends into God's nearness, ascends into God's unknowability, and then returns — not to power but to **presence.**

He is not far. He was never far. He is simply beyond your comparison. And "beyond your comparison" is not a synonym for "far away." It is a synonym for **"too close to conceptualize."**

### Questions to Carry

When you picture God in your mind — and we all do, even unconsciously — what image are you carrying? And what has that image *prevented* you from receiving?

If the God you pray to is beyond every comparison — what would change about the size of what you ask for?

When you feel that no one hears you, no one sees you, no one understands — what does it mean that hearing and seeing are not things God *does* but things God *is*?

What in your life would shift if you stopped trying to understand God and started letting yourself be understood *by* God?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The God who is so far beyond your comprehension that even the concept of resembling Him is empty — is also the God whose permanent, essential, never-interrupted nature is to hear you and see you.**

### The Closing Invitation

You will never fit Him into your mind. That was never the invitation. The invitation was always the other direction: to let Him hear you. To let Him see you. To stop trying to reach up — and to realize that He has been reaching down since before you drew your first breath.

The Originator who split open the heavens from nothing — split your mother open, too, so you could arrive. The same God. The same root. The same *faṭr*.

And He hears you right now.

### Du'a

*Allāhumma, You told us there is nothing like Your likeness — and then You told us You hear and You see. We cannot reach You with our minds, but You have reached us with Your attributes. Free us from every image of You that is smaller than You. And let us rest in the knowledge that the God we cannot picture has always been the God who hears.*
