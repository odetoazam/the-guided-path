# So People Have No Argument Against Allah

## Surah An-Nisa (4:163-165)

[ayah:4:163] ۞ إِنَّآ أَوْحَيْنَآ إِلَيْكَ كَمَآ أَوْحَيْنَآ إِلَىٰ نُوحٍ وَٱلنَّبِيِّـۧنَ مِنۢ بَعْدِهِۦ ۚ وَأَوْحَيْنَآ إِلَىٰٓ إِبْرَٰهِيمَ وَإِسْمَـٰعِيلَ وَإِسْحَـٰقَ وَيَعْقُوبَ وَٱلْأَسْبَاطِ وَعِيسَىٰ وَأَيُّوبَ وَيُونُسَ وَهَـٰرُونَ وَسُلَيْمَـٰنَ ۚ وَءَاتَيْنَا دَاوُۥدَ زَبُورًا

[ayah:4:164] وَرُسُلًا قَدْ قَصَصْنَـٰهُمْ عَلَيْكَ مِن قَبْلُ وَرُسُلًا لَّمْ نَقْصُصْهُمْ عَلَيْكَ ۚ وَكَلَّمَ ٱللَّهُ مُوسَىٰ تَكْلِيمًا

And We revealed to Ibrahim, Isma'il, Ishaq, Ya'qub, and the Tribes, and Isa, and Ayyub, and Yunus, and Harun, and Sulayman. And We gave Dawud the Zabur. And messengers We have related to you before and messengers We have not related to you. And Allah spoke to Musa with direct speech. Messengers as bearers of good tidings and warners so that people would have no argument against Allah after the messengers. And ever is Allah Almighty and Wise.**

## Introduction

After long stretches of argument with denial, covenant-breach, selective belief, and historical distortion, these verses do something widening and stabilizing. They pull our eyes away from one resistant community and place Muhammad inside the full river of revelation.

The effect is immediate. Your messenger is not a novelty. Your Book did not fall into a vacuum. What has come to you belongs to the same divine pattern that touched Nuh, Ibrahim, Isma'il, Ishaq, Ya'qub, the Tribes, Isa, Ayyub, Yunus, Harun, Sulayman, and Dawud. Then the ayah goes even further: there are messengers whose stories you have been told, and messengers you have not been told. Revelation is larger than your narrated map of it.

Then, almost suddenly, the surah gives Musa a uniquely emphatic mention: Allah spoke to Musa with direct speech. And after all these names and all this history, the final purpose is unveiled with absolute clarity: messengers came as bearers of good news and warning so that people would have no argument against Allah after they were sent.

That is a humbling ending. We often treat messengers mainly as sources of inspiration, moral beauty, or identity. They are certainly all of that. But this passage reminds us that they are also heaven's removal of our excuse. The line between glad tidings and warning is not random; it is mercy precise enough to leave no one able to stand before Allah and say, "No one came. Nothing was made clear. I had no real summons."

The hook of the passage is this: **revelation is not a scattered collection of inspiring names, but a single divine campaign of mercy and proof through which Allah repeatedly reaches humanity until no honest case remains for refusing Him.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Inna awhayna ilayka kama awhayna**: The Ayah Opens by Placing Muhammad Inside Continuity, Not Isolation

The first movement of the passage is stunningly direct:

inna awhayna ilayka kama awhayna.

We have revealed to you just as We revealed.

The repeated verb awhayna is the center of gravity here. Revelation to Muhammad is not introduced as an exception to the divine way, but as its continuation. Kama matters. As. Just as. The surah is protecting the believer from two opposite errors at once: imagining this revelation as historically disconnected, or imagining previous revelation as irrelevant now.

[PAUSE]

Then Nuh is named first, followed by the prophets after him. That matters because it stretches the line back to one of the earliest great universal prophetic moments. Muhammad is not being placed at the edge of the story, but inside a vast continuity of divine speech across generations.

Then the verse names prophet after prophet. The list has a spiritual effect even before one analyzes each name. It teaches abundance. Allah has not been silent in history. Human beings were not left to grope in religious darkness with only one isolated intervention. Revelation came again and again, through different servants, places, and peoples, while remaining one in source.

Then comes one elegant variation:

wa atayna Dawuda zabura.

We gave Dawud the Zabur.

The passage shifts from awhayna to atayna, from revelation phrased as inspiration to giving phrased as gift. This enriches the sense of divine generosity. Revelation is not merely command from above. It is also a gift placed into history.

### 2. **Rusulan qad qasasnahum ... wa rusulan lam naqsushum**: The Revealed Story Is Larger Than the Portion We Have Been Told

Verse 164 then says:

wa rusulan qad qasasnahum 'alayka min qablu wa rusulan lam naqsushum 'alayka.

Messengers We have related to you before, and messengers We have not related to you.

Qasasnahum comes from recounting, narrating, laying out a story with deliberate sequence. Some messengers were narrated to the Prophet. But others were not. That sentence protects us from a subtle arrogance: the assumption that what we know exhausts what Allah has done.

[PAUSE]

This is spiritually healthy. The believer is asked to love the named prophets without imagining that named means total. The unseen extends beyond our catalog. Allah's guidance to humanity is broader than our remembered list.

There is humility in that. We stand inside revelation without standing over all its details. We are given enough for obedience and certainty, but not everything for curiosity's domination.

Then comes the singled-out clause:

wa kallama Allahu Musa taklima.

Allah spoke to Musa with direct speech.

The verbal noun taklima after kallama intensifies the statement. It is one of the Qur'an's ways of making the act emphatic: a real speaking, direct speech. The point is not to turn the verse into abstraction, but to mark Musa with a distinction of nearness and address.

The passage has been broad and sweeping; then suddenly it narrows into this intimate, emphatic moment. Revelation is both vast in history and personal in encounter.

### 3. **Mubashshirina wa mundhirina ... li'alla yakuna lil-nasi 'ala Allahi hujjah**: The Purpose of Messengers Is Mercy That Also Ends Excuses

Then verse 165 gives the purpose:

rusulan mubashshirina wa mundhirina.

Messengers as bearers of good tidings and warners.

The order matters. Glad tidings first, warning second. Divine communication does not begin from hostility. It begins from opening hope. But warning is still necessary, because mercy that never clarifies consequence is not complete guidance.

[PAUSE]

Then the reason is unveiled:

li'alla yakuna lil-nasi 'ala Allahi hujjah ba'da al-rusul.

So that people would have no argument against Allah after the messengers.

Hujjah is not a small word. It is an argument, a plea, a case one imagines he can stand on. The verse is telling us that messengers are Allah's answer in advance to every future attempt to say, "I did not know, I was not reached, I was never clearly told."

This should deepen our sense of prophetic mercy. The messenger is not merely a teacher among many. He is the removal of your final defense. Heaven has spoken into your life through him.

Then the verse closes:

wa kana Allahu 'azizan hakima.

Allah is Almighty, Wise.

'Aziz answers any thought that Allah needed messengers because He was somehow unable to judge without them. No. He is fully mighty. Hakim answers the other side: the sending of messengers is not arbitrary. It is wise, measured, and exact. Allah sends them not from need, but from perfect justice and mercy.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: We Often Want Spiritual Beauty From Prophets Without Letting Them Remove Our Excuses

Many believers love prophets emotionally, and that love is good. We love their stories, their patience, their du'as, their tenderness, their wounds, their courage. But sometimes we keep the prophets in the realm of inspiration while quietly resisting their more uncomfortable function: they close off our plausible deniability before Allah.

That is what this passage restores.

[PAUSE]

The messenger is not only there to move you. He is there to confront you. He is not only there to console you. He is there to make the path unmistakable enough that refusal becomes morally owned. The more clearly the messenger stands before you, the less room remains for saying, "I never really had a chance."

This should change how we hear revelation. Every ayah is not merely information. It is part of Allah's hujjah reaching us. Every prophetic word is one more closing of the exits by which the ego wants to flee accountability.

And that is still mercy. It is mercy because being left with excuses is not actually safety. It is only delayed ruin. The messenger comes so that truth reaches you before judgment does.

### Theme 2: The Unnamed Messengers Teach Us Humility Before the Vastness of Allah's Care for Humanity

There is another subtle mercy in these verses: some messengers were told to you, and some were not.

That line should make the believer both grateful and small. Grateful, because what we have been told is already enough to live by. Small, because it reminds us that Allah's care for human beings across history is wider than the piece of it we personally know.

[PAUSE]

This can heal a certain spiritual provincialism. We often imagine the sacred story only through the names familiar to us, as though revelation moved through a narrow corridor. But the Qur'an opens the horizon. Allah sent more messengers than your memory can list. He addressed more peoples than your inherited imagination remembers. His concern for humanity was never thin.

And then, in the middle of that wideness, the direct speech to Musa reminds us that divine care is not generic. Allah deals with humanity broadly and with servants intimately. He sends across centuries and He speaks personally.

So the believer is left in a beautiful posture: humbled by how much lies beyond our narrated map, comforted by how much Allah has already made clear, and sobered by the realization that after the messengers, our excuses grow very thin.

## Closing Synthesis

These verses begin by placing Muhammad inside the same revelatory stream as Nuh and the prophets after him.

They then widen the horizon through a long chain of named prophets, only to widen it further by telling us that many messengers were never narrated to us at all.

Then they single out Musa with the emphatic statement that Allah spoke to him with direct speech.

And finally they reveal the purpose beneath the whole prophetic history: messengers came with glad tidings and warnings so that no human being could retain a hujjah against Allah after heaven had already reached them.

[PAUSE]

That is the architecture: continuity, abundance, intimacy, then accountability.

### Questions to Carry

1. Do I mainly seek inspiration from revelation, or do I let it remove my excuses before Allah?
2. Where in my life am I still quietly living as though the messenger's call has not reached me clearly enough?
3. Does remembering the unnamed messengers make me more humble before the vastness of Allah's care for humanity?
4. What would it look like to receive prophetic guidance not as background beauty, but as Allah's living hujjah in my life?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These verses teach that revelation is one continuous divine mercy through many named and unnamed messengers, sent with hope and warning until human beings are left without any honest argument for refusing Allah after His message has reached them.**

### Du'a

Allahumma make us people who hear the prophets not as distant stories only, but as Your living proof and mercy reaching us. Remove from our hearts the love of excuse after guidance has become clear. Let the glad tidings give us hope, let the warnings give us seriousness, and let the memory of Your many named and unnamed messengers humble us before the wideness of Your care and the precision of Your justice. Ameen.
