# What Would It Have Cost Them?

## Surah An-Nisa (4:37-39)

[ayah:4:37] ٱلَّذِينَ يَبْخَلُونَ وَيَأْمُرُونَ ٱلنَّاسَ بِٱلْبُخْلِ وَيَكْتُمُونَ مَآ ءَاتَىٰهُمُ ٱللَّهُ مِن فَضْلِهِۦ ۗ وَأَعْتَدْنَا لِلْكَـٰفِرِينَ عَذَابًا مُّهِينًا

[ayah:4:38] وَٱلَّذِينَ يُنفِقُونَ أَمْوَٰلَهُمْ رِئَآءَ ٱلنَّاسِ وَلَا يُؤْمِنُونَ بِٱللَّهِ وَلَا بِٱلْيَوْمِ ٱلْـَٔاخِرِ ۗ وَمَن يَكُنِ ٱلشَّيْطَـٰنُ لَهُۥ قَرِينًا فَسَآءَ قَرِينًا

[ayah:4:39] وَمَاذَا عَلَيْهِمْ لَوْ ءَامَنُوا۟ بِٱللَّهِ وَٱلْيَوْمِ ٱلْـَٔاخِرِ وَأَنفَقُوا۟ مِمَّا رَزَقَهُمُ ٱللَّهُ ۚ وَكَانَ ٱللَّهُ بِهِمْ عَلِيمًا

*Alladhina yabkhaluna wa ya'muruna al-nasa bil-bukhli wa yaktumuna ma atahumu Allahu min fadlihi. Wa a'tadna lil-kafirina 'adhaban muhina.*

*Wa alladhina yunfiquna amwalahum ri'a'a al-nasi wa la yu'minuna billahi wa la bil-yawmi al-akhiri. Wa man yakuni al-shaytanu lahu qarinan fa-sa'a qarina.*

*Wa madha 'alayhim law amanu billahi wa al-yawmi al-akhiri wa anfaqu mimma razaqahumu Allah. Wa kana Allahu bihim 'alima.*

**Those who are miserly, who command people to be miserly, and who conceal what Allah has given them of His favor - and We have prepared for the disbelievers a humiliating punishment. And those who spend their wealth to be seen by people and do not believe in Allah or the Last Day. Whoever has Satan as a companion, what an evil companion he is. And what would it have cost them if they had believed in Allah and the Last Day and spent from what Allah had provided for them? And Allah is ever Knowing of them.**

## Introduction

There are people who refuse to give.

And there are people who give, but in a way that is spiritually worse than refusal.

One closes the hand. The other opens the hand while keeping the heart closed. One hoards openly. The other spends theatrically. One withholds the gift Allah placed in their life. The other turns that gift into a stage on which the self can be admired.

These verses stand inside that human ugliness and reveal a hard truth: miserliness and ostentation are not opposites in the way we often imagine. They are twins. Both are forms of captivity to the self. Both corrupt wealth. Both distort the meaning of provision. Both reveal a heart that is not actually living before Allah and the Last Day.

That is why the passage moves the way it does. It begins with those who withhold, then names those who spend only for human eyes, then ends with a question so simple it feels devastating: **what would it really have cost them if they had just believed and spent sincerely from what Allah had given them?**

The hook of the passage is this: **the real problem is not merely whether wealth leaves your hand, but whether your heart can let wealth become an act of faith instead of self-protection or self-display.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Yabkhaluna... wa ya'muruna al-nasa bil-bukhl**: Miserliness Wants Company

The first word that defines these people is **yabkhaluna**.

They are miserly. They withhold.

The root **b-kh-l** carries stinginess, reluctance to part with what should be shared, a tightness of soul that feels loss in giving even when Allah has been generous. Bukhl is not simple budgeting or prudence. It is moral constriction.

Then the verse intensifies the diagnosis:

**wa ya'muruna al-nasa bil-bukhl**.

They command people to be miserly.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the most psychologically sharp details in the passage. Sin rarely wants to remain lonely. A miser often seeks witnesses, justifications, an atmosphere, a culture of withholding that makes his own constriction feel normal. So he begins advising others into the same poverty of spirit.

That happens all the time.

Someone is ungenerous, and soon they become the philosopher of ungenerosity. They tell others not to give too much, not to trust too much, not to share too openly, not to let compassion make them "naive." Their spiritual tightness becomes public counsel.

The Quran sees through this. Bukhl is bad enough in the chest. More dangerous is when it becomes an ethic you preach.

### 2. **Wa yaktumuna ma atahumu Allahu min fadlihi**: The Miser Hides Gift as Well as Refuses Gift

Then the verse adds:

**wa yaktumuna ma atahumu Allahu min fadlihi**.

They conceal what Allah has given them of His favor.

The verb **yaktumuna** from the root **k-t-m** means to conceal, suppress, keep hidden. The verse does not say only that they fail to share. It says they hide the very favor itself.

And what is being hidden? **Ma atahumu Allahu min fadlihi** - what Allah gave them from His favor.

The root **f-d-l** returns here as surplus, bounty, extra generosity. So the miser's problem is not only economic. It is theological. Allah gave, but the person begins behaving as though the gift were self-generated, threatened, or too scarce to circulate.

[PAUSE]

Concealment can take several forms. Sometimes it is literally hiding wealth. Sometimes it is hiding knowledge, opportunity, access, influence, introductions, generosity of time, any favor Allah placed in your life that could have benefited others. A person can be miserly with money, but also with doorways.

The point is that favor is being treated as private possession rather than divine trust.

### 3. **Yunfiquna amwalahum ri'a'a al-nas**: Spending Can Also Be Captive to People

Then the passage turns to the other side:

**wa alladhina yunfiquna amwalahum ri'a'a al-nas**.

Those who spend their wealth for the sake of being seen by people.

The verb **yunfiquna** from the root **n-f-q** means to spend, to let wealth go forth, to disburse. Outwardly, this looks like the opposite of miserliness. Wealth is leaving the hand.

But then comes the phrase that exposes the inner corruption: **ri'a'a al-nas**.

**Ri'a'** from the root connected to seeing is display for the gaze of others, acting so that people become the audience whose sight gives meaning to the deed. The act is no longer oriented upward. It is horizontal theater.

[PAUSE]

This is why the Quran is so unsentimental about visible generosity. A person can spend and still be spiritually trapped. The hand opens, but the heart remains imprisoned by people. Their praise becomes the true destination of the deed.

So the passage has now shown us two versions of the same bondage:

the one who cannot bear to part with the gift,
and the one who can part with it only if the self gets larger in the process.

### 4. **Wa man yakuni al-shaytanu lahu qarinan fa-sa'a qarina**: Bad Companionship Can Become Interior

Then comes one of the hardest lines:

**wa man yakuni al-shaytanu lahu qarinan fa-sa'a qarina**.

Whoever has Satan as a companion - what an evil companion.

The noun **qarin** from the root **q-r-n** is a close companion, something paired beside you, attached to your path. It is more intimate than a passing temptation. It is a fellowship of direction.

[PAUSE]

That is frightening because it suggests that repeated insincerity eventually becomes relational. A person spends for people, thinks for people, fears people, withholds for people, performs for people, and over time the inner company of the heart changes. The whisperer is no longer merely outside at the edge of the act. He becomes qarin - a paired presence in the logic of the self.

This helps explain why riya' and bukhl are so sticky. They are not one-off mistakes only. They become habits of companionship. The soul starts hearing stinginess and performance as reasonable advice.

### 5. **Wa madha 'alayhim law amanu...**: The Passage Ends With a Question That Shrinks the Excuse

Then the Quran does something remarkable. After naming vice, withholding, performance, and bad companionship, it does not only thunder. It asks:

**wa madha 'alayhim law amanu billahi wa al-yawmi al-akhiri wa anfaqu mimma razaqahumu Allah?**

What would be upon them? What burden would they have carried if they had believed in Allah and the Last Day and spent from what Allah provided them?

The question is devastating because it makes the excuse look small.

[PAUSE]

What did sincerity really cost?
What crushing burden did faith impose here?
What impossible loss was attached to honest spending?

The answer is implied: nothing like what the ego imagined.

The verse also changes one word with beautiful precision. Earlier it said they conceal what Allah gave them from His **fadl**. Now it says: what if they spent from what Allah **razaqahum** - provided for them.

The root **r-z-q** emphasizes provision, allotted sustenance, what comes to you from Allah as your share to live by. Once wealth is seen as rizq rather than self-authored possession, spending becomes easier to imagine honestly. You are not being asked to amputate your identity. You are being asked to circulate what was already provisioned to you by Allah.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Miserliness and Ostentation Are Opposite Behaviors Built on the Same Fear

At first glance, the miser and the show-off seem like different species.

One clutches. The other performs.
One hides. The other displays.
One says no too quickly. The other says yes too loudly.

But the verses reveal that the inner engine is often the same: fear of living before Allah without using wealth to secure the self.

[PAUSE]

The miser secures the self by keeping the gift close. If I keep enough, I remain safe. The ostentatious spender secures the self by converting the gift into social capital. If I spend in a visible enough way, I remain admired. In both cases, wealth is not being treated as a trust moving through the servant back toward Allah. It is being used as armor for the ego.

That is why these verses belong together. The disease is deeper than the outward act. Revelation is not only asking whether you spend. It is asking what world your spending serves. Is wealth there to protect a frightened self, enlarge a curated self, or obey Allah?

### Theme 2: Sincerity Usually Costs Less Than the Soul Pretends

The final question of the passage keeps working on the conscience long after the verses end.

**What would it have cost them?**

There is mercy in that question. It does not deny that the ego experiences sincere giving as costly. It simply brings the cost into truthful scale. Most of the time the self exaggerates what obedience will take and minimizes what disobedience is already taking.

[PAUSE]

We tell ourselves:
If I give honestly, I will lose too much.
If I stop performing for people, I will become invisible.
If I let generosity be private, unadvertised, unleveraged, I will get nothing back.

But what have we already lost by living the other way? Tightness of chest. Dependency on praise. Habitual suspicion. Spiritual loneliness. A qarin from shaytan. All of that is expensive. The question of 4:39 exposes that our accounting has been distorted.

Faith and sincere infaq are not costless, but they are rarely as crushing as the nafs claims. Often the heavier burden is the one we are already carrying by refusing them.

## Closing Synthesis

These verses map a whole moral economy of the heart.

First, they show us the one who withholds and even teaches others how to withhold.

Then they show us the one who spends, but only as a spectacle for people.

Then they reveal the dark companionship behind both forms of corruption.

And finally they end not with an argument, but with a question that collapses the excuse.

[PAUSE]

That is the architecture: bukhl, riya', qarin, then the unveiled simplicity of what sincere faith would actually have required.

### Questions to Carry

1. Do I ever turn my own spiritual tightness into advice I give other people?
2. When I spend, how much of my heart is still waiting for human sight to validate the deed?
3. In what ways has shaytan become a qarin in my logic around money, generosity, and recognition?
4. If I am honest, what would sincerity really cost me - and what is insincerity already costing me now?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These verses teach that miserliness and ostentation are twin failures of faith, and that the soul discovers too late that sincere belief and giving would have cost far less than life under the companionship of shaytan.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, purify us from bukhl and from riya'. Do not let us hide the favors You gave or spend them for the eyes of people. Make our wealth a trust, our giving sincere, and our hearts free from the companionship of shaytan in all our calculations. Let us feel how light honesty before You really is. Ameen.
