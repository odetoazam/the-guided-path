# Do They Seek Honor With Them?

## Surah An-Nisa (4:138-141)

[ayah:4:138] بَشِّرِ ٱلْمُنَـٰفِقِينَ بِأَنَّ لَهُمْ عَذَابًا أَلِيمًا

[ayah:4:139] ٱلَّذِينَ يَتَّخِذُونَ ٱلْكَـٰفِرِينَ أَوْلِيَآءَ مِن دُونِ ٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ ۚ أَيَبْتَغُونَ عِندَهُمُ ٱلْعِزَّةَ فَإِنَّ ٱلْعِزَّةَ لِلَّهِ جَمِيعًا

[ayah:4:140] وَقَدْ نَزَّلَ عَلَيْكُمْ فِى ٱلْكِتَـٰبِ أَنْ إِذَا سَمِعْتُمْ ءَايَـٰتِ ٱللَّهِ يُكْفَرُ بِهَا وَيُسْتَهْزَأُ بِهَا فَلَا تَقْعُدُوا۟ مَعَهُمْ حَتَّىٰ يَخُوضُوا۟ فِى حَدِيثٍ غَيْرِهِۦٓ ۚ إِنَّكُمْ إِذًا مِّثْلُهُمْ ۗ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ جَامِعُ ٱلْمُنَـٰفِقِينَ وَٱلْكَـٰفِرِينَ فِى جَهَنَّمَ جَمِيعًا

## Introduction

One of the ugliest things hypocrisy does is not merely hide disbelief. It shopkeepers for dignity in the wrong marketplace.

The hypocrite is not only internally divided. He is strategically divided. He watches power, studies the room, attaches himself to whichever side seems more useful, and keeps his loyalties fluid enough that he can later claim belonging with whoever wins. What he wants, beneath all the adjustments, is one thing: protection, prestige, immunity, status, a share of what feels stronger than truth.

These verses expose that psychology with unusual sharpness.

They begin with what sounds like a gift-word turned inside out: give glad tidings to the hypocrites of painful punishment. Then the reason comes: they take disbelievers as allies instead of believers. Why? Do they seek honor with them? The answer comes immediately: honor belongs to Allah entirely.

Then the surah moves from loyalty to atmosphere. If Allah's verses are denied and mocked, you are not to sit there passively until the gathering changes. Why? Because prolonged seated participation softens moral distinction. If you stay as though nothing is happening, the verse says, you become like them in that moment's complicity.

Finally the passage shows the opportunist in motion. He waits to see who is winning. If the believers gain victory, he says, "Were we not with you?" If the disbelievers gain a share, he says to them, "Did we not contain you and shield you from the believers?" The soul has become a political weather vane.

The hook of the passage is this: **hypocrisy often grows from seeking dignity, safety, and advantage from creation in a way that makes truth negotiable and loyalty reversible.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Bashshir ... 'adhaban alima**: A Glad-Tidings Verb Is Used With Terrible Irony

Verse 138 opens:

bashshir al-munafiqina bi-anna lahum 'adhaban alima.

Give glad tidings to the hypocrites that they have a painful punishment.

The verb bashshir normally belongs to good news. That is what makes the opening so cutting. The form of announcement is associated with joy, but what is being announced is punishment. The rhetoric itself exposes the inversion at the heart of nifaq. These are people who outwardly moved through the symbols of good while inwardly trafficking in something rotten.

[PAUSE]

The phrase al-munafiqin also matters. Nifaq is not only disbelief hidden from others. It is the maintenance of dual channels: one outwardly adjusted to belonging, another inwardly aligned elsewhere. So the punishment named here is not random severity. It corresponds to a self trained in duplicity.

The opening therefore sets the tone: this is a section about moral inversion, where even the language of glad tidings is turned into a vehicle of exposure.

### 2. **Ayabtaghuna 'indahumu al-'izzata**: Seeking Honor in the Wrong Place Produces Crooked Loyalties

Then verse 139 names the engine:

alladhina yattakhidhuna al-kafirina awliya'a min duni al-mu'minin.

Those who take disbelievers as protecting allies instead of the believers.

The verb yattakhidhuna is deliberate taking, not passive drift. Then awliya' carries closeness, alliance, patronage, protective loyalty. The problem is not bare social contact. The problem is chosen allegiance in the place where allegiance reveals what you trust and from whom you seek security.

[PAUSE]

Then comes the penetrating question:

ayabtaghuna 'indahumu al-'izzata?

Do they seek honor with them?

The verb from seeking here is active pursuit. And al-'izzah is not a small comfort. It is dignity, strength, untouchability, a sense of elevated standing. The verse is telling us that beneath many distorted alliances lies a hunger for عزّة gained through proximity to what appears strong.

Then the answer falls like an axe:

fa inna al-'izzata lillahi jami'a.

All honor belongs to Allah entirely.

Not mostly. Not ideally. Entirely. So the hypocrite's whole strategy is built on a theological mistake. He is chasing from creation what creation does not independently own.

### 3. **Fa la taq'udu ma'ahum**: Where You Sit Is a Moral Statement

Verse 140 then moves from allegiance to gathering:

if you hear the verses of Allah denied and mocked, do not sit with them until they move into another conversation.

The command la taq'udu is concrete. Do not remain seated. This is not only an inward disagreement verse. It is a bodily verse. Presence matters. Staying matters. The majlis you continue to occupy shapes you, signals something, and blurs something.

[PAUSE]

Then comes the verb yakhudu, to plunge into talk, to wade around in it. The image is apt. Mockery of revelation is not always a single clean statement. Often it is a social atmosphere, a conversation people keep swimming around in while reverence dies by repetition.

The verse refuses passive neutrality there. If you remain as though nothing is happening, innakum idhan mithluhum - you are then like them. Not because every seat-holder is identical in creed, but because tolerated participation creates a likeness in the moral moment.

This is a devastating correction for the self that excuses itself by saying, "I did not say it." The Qur'an asks: but why did you sit through it?

### 4. **Yatarabbasuna bikum**: The Hypocrite Lives by Waiting, Watching, and Switching

Then verse 141 gives one of the best psychological portraits of hypocrisy in the surah:

alladhina yatarabbasuna bikum.

Those who wait and watch you.

This is not principled patience. It is strategic hovering. The hypocrite lingers near the believers without settling, waiting to see what outcome will let him claim advantage afterward.

[PAUSE]

If there is fath from Allah, he rushes to say: were we not with you? If the disbelievers get a nasib, a share, he turns to them and says: did we not gain leverage over you and protect you from the believers?

Then comes one of the most revealing verbs in the passage: nastahwidh. Did we not gain the upper grip over you? The hypocrite wants credit from every side. He is always retroactively narrating himself as useful to whichever camp might reward him.

This is what nifaq becomes when social ambition governs the soul. Truth is no longer the center. Outcome is. Loyalty is no longer covenantal. It is transactional.

Then the verse closes by tearing the whole performance out of his hands: Allah will judge between you on the Day of Resurrection, and Allah will not give the disbelievers over the believers a way. Final judgment and final scale are not set by the opportunist's positioning.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Many False Alliances Are Really Theological Errors About Where Dignity Comes From

These verses should make us ask a hard question: when we bend toward unhealthy loyalties, what are we actually looking for?

Often the answer is not abstract ideology first. It is عزّة. We want cover, influence, protection from vulnerability, access to the room that feels untouchable, the approval that seems to keep us safe. So we start trimming principles, softening distinction, or attaching our emotional survival to people and systems that do not honor Allah's truth.

[PAUSE]

The Qur'an says this is not merely a strategy problem. It is a creed problem. If honor belongs to Allah entirely, then every attempt to secure lasting dignity through betrayal of truth is built on illusion. You may gain a temporary seat, but your soul will become dependent on what cannot finally carry your worth.

This is one reason hypocrisy feels so exhausting. It has to keep adjusting to preserve borrowed status. Real 'izzah is stable because it comes from the One who does not change. Borrowed 'izzah is anxious because it is tied to rooms, factions, and outcomes that can turn at any moment.

### Theme 2: Complicity Often Begins Long Before Open Agreement

Verse 140 is especially searching because it names a stage many people prefer not to count as moral action: remaining seated.

We imagine compromise begins only when we openly say the blasphemy, join the mockery, or endorse the wrong. But the Qur'an notices the softer beginning. You stay. You let the atmosphere continue around you. You teach yourself to tolerate what should have moved you. Little by little your presence tells your own heart that the boundary was not that serious after all.

[PAUSE]

This matters now as much as ever. Whole moral environments are built on people who did not initiate the degradation but did keep sitting through it. The verse interrupts that early. Move. Break the atmosphere. Refuse the ease of just blending in while revelation is treated as a toy.

And verse 141 extends the same lesson into public loyalties. The opportunist is not merely a liar after the fact; he is someone who never wanted to be morally located at all. He wanted the benefits of every side without the obligations of any side. That is why he waits and watches.

The cure is not paranoia. It is allegiance purified by truth. If 'izzah belongs to Allah, then we no longer need to hover anxiously around whichever camp appears stronger. We can afford to be located.

## Closing Synthesis

These verses expose hypocrisy from the inside out.

They begin with ironic "glad tidings" of punishment, then reveal the motive beneath crooked loyalty: the search for honor with those outside believing allegiance.

They command believers not to remain seated in gatherings where Allah's signs are denied and mocked, because tolerated presence creates likeness.

And they end with the opportunist who waits to see who wins before claiming closeness to that side, only to have Allah reclaim judgment and final authority.

[PAUSE]

That is the architecture: false good news, false loyalty, false sitting, false positioning, then true judgment.

### Questions to Carry

1. Where am I tempted to seek dignity, safety, or legitimacy from people in a way that makes truth negotiable?
2. Have I been remaining in atmospheres that quietly train my heart to tolerate mockery of what Allah made sacred?
3. In what relationships or environments am I trying to keep every side pleased instead of being honestly located with the truth?
4. Do I really believe that all 'izzah belongs to Allah, or do I still live as though it is distributed by rooms and factions?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These verses teach that hypocrisy is driven by seeking dignity and safety from the wrong sources, which turns loyalty into opportunism and tolerated complicity into moral resemblance.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, do not let us seek honor anywhere that costs us truth. Make us people who know where to stand, where to leave, and whom to love for Your sake. Purify our loyalties from opportunism, free us from the hunger for borrowed status, and place real 'izzah in our hearts through nearness to You alone. Ameen.
