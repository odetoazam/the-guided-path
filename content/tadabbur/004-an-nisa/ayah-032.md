# Ask Allah for His Favor

## Surah An-Nisa (4:32)

[ayah:4:32] وَلَا تَتَمَنَّوْا۟ مَا فَضَّلَ ٱللَّهُ بِهِۦ بَعْضَكُمْ عَلَىٰ بَعْضٍ ۚ لِّلرِّجَالِ نَصِيبٌ مِّمَّا ٱكْتَسَبُوا۟ ۖ وَلِلنِّسَآءِ نَصِيبٌ مِّمَّا ٱكْتَسَبْنَ ۚ وَسْـَٔلُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ مِن فَضْلِهِۦٓ ۗ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ كَانَ بِكُلِّ شَىْءٍ عَلِيمًا

*Wa la tatamannaw ma faddala Allahu bihi ba'dakum 'ala ba'd. Lil-rijali nasibun mimma iktasabu wa lil-nisa'i nasibun mimma iktasabna. Was'alu Allaha min fadlihi. Inna Allaha kana bikulli shay'in 'alima.*

**Do not long for what Allah has given some of you over others. For men is a share of what they have earned, and for women is a share of what they have earned. And ask Allah for His favor. Surely Allah is ever Knowing of all things.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

Some sins begin before any action ever appears.

Nothing has yet been stolen. No insult has been spoken. No law has been broken outwardly. The corruption begins in the inward glance that keeps returning to another person's portion and asking, "Why them and not me?" A life, a gift, a position, a form of ease, a kind of influence, a category of advantage. The heart leans toward comparison and then quietly starts building its grief around someone else's allocation.

This ayah enters at exactly that early point.

Surah An-Nisa is a surah of shares, trusts, limits, obligations, gendered realities, and divine apportionment. It speaks often about what belongs to whom and by what right. So it is fitting that the surah eventually turns inward and addresses the disease that makes every just distribution feel intolerable to the ego: covetous wishing.

But the verse does more than forbid envy. It retrains the whole movement of the heart. Do not keep staring sideways at what Allah has favored others with. There is a share tied to what you earn. And if your heart still wants expansion, there is a door open toward Allah Himself: ask Him for His favor.

The hook of the ayah is this: **the cure for corrosive comparison is not pretending differences do not exist, but learning to turn from another person's portion toward your own work and your Lord's generosity.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **La tatamannaw**: The Verse Begins at the Level of Inner Longing

The first prohibition is subtle:

**wa la tatamannaw**.

Do not wish for. Do not long after. Do not keep extending your desire toward what is not your portion.

The verb comes from the root **m-n-y**, associated with wishing, projecting desire, stretching the self toward an imagined possession or state. This is not the language of open theft. It is the language of interior appetite before theft. The Quran starts where the corrosion starts.

[PAUSE]

That is important because many of our outward problems are only late-stage symptoms of inward comparison. Before resentment becomes speech, it becomes fantasy. Before injustice becomes action, it becomes repeated interior rehearsal: if only I had what they have, if only my life had been built like that, if only Allah had distributed differently.

The verse does not tell us to become emotionless. It reaches deeper. It tells us not to settle into the kind of wishing that makes another person's portion the constant mirror by which we read our own worth.

### 2. **Ma faddala Allahu bihi ba'dakum 'ala ba'd**: Difference Exists, But It Is Not Self-Created

What are we told not to covet?

**Ma faddala Allahu bihi ba'dakum 'ala ba'd**.

What Allah has given some of you over others.

The verb **faddala** comes from the root **f-d-l**, excess, surplus, added bounty, فضل. It means to grant increase, to prefer, to bestow an extra measure. The root itself is delicate here. It does not always mean moral superiority. It can simply indicate differential allotment, an added portion in some matter.

Then the verse says **ba'dakum 'ala ba'd** - some of you over others. The repetition of **ba'd** matters. The Quran is not pretending flat sameness where human life is obviously unequal. It acknowledges differential distribution. Some are given one thing, some another. Capacities differ. openings differ. circumstances differ. forms of trial differ.

[PAUSE]

But the verse places the whole matter under Allah's action: **faddala Allahu**.

This is the first spiritual correction. The advantage you are staring at is not self-authored in the way the ego imagines. It is part of Allah's apportionment. That does not erase human striving. The ayah will speak of earning in a moment. But it does mean that comparison can become blasphemously intimate if we are not careful. We start not merely resenting another person, but quietly objecting to the divine distribution beneath them.

This is why envy is so spiritually dangerous. It looks horizontal, but very quickly it becomes vertical.

### 3. **Lil-rijali nasib... wa lil-nisa'i nasib**: The Verse Responds to Comparison With the Language of Share

Then the ayah gives balance:

**lil-rijali nasibun mimma iktasabu wa lil-nisa'i nasibun mimma iktasabna**.

For men is a share of what they have earned, and for women is a share of what they have earned.

The noun **nasib** comes from the root **n-s-b**, an allotted portion, an established share, something assigned rather than stolen from the air. In Surah An-Nisa this word already carries legal and moral weight because the surah keeps teaching us that shares are not for the strongest hand to invent. They are apportioned.

Then comes **iktasabu / iktasabna**, from the root **k-s-b**, to earn, acquire, gain through effort and action. The Form VIII shading suggests acquisition through involvement, pursuit, active taking on of consequence and earning.

[PAUSE]

The structure is powerful. The ayah does not dissolve all difference into vague spirituality. Nor does it tell one side to live in grievance toward the other. It says: there is a **nasib**, a share, connected to what each earns. Your first moral task is not to obsess over another person's field. It is to answer faithfully for your own.

This is liberating because comparison paralyzes while nasib clarifies. Comparison keeps the eye sideways. Nasib turns the soul back toward entrusted portion. Earning turns it toward responsibility.

And the parallel grammar for men and women matters too. In a surah deeply concerned with rights, obligations, and differentiated rulings, this line refuses the reduction of either to spiritual irrelevance. Each has a share. Each has moral consequence. Each stands before Allah with real earning.

### 4. **Was'alu Allaha min fadlihi**: The Verse Redirects Desire Rather Than Pretending Desire Does Not Exist

Then comes the cure:

**was'alu Allaha min fadlihi**.

Ask Allah for His favor.

This is one of the most beautiful turns in the ayah. The verse does not merely say, "Stop wanting." It says: if the heart wants increase, turn the wanting upward.

The verb **is'aluu** from the root **s-'-l** is to ask, request, implore. And what are we to ask for? **Min fadlihi** - from His favor, His surplus generosity, His bounty beyond strict desert.

[PAUSE]

Notice the elegance. Earlier the verse said do not covet what Allah has **faddala** some with. Now it says ask Allah from His **fadl**. The same root returns, but the direction changes completely. Instead of staring at the favor as it appears in someone else's life, you are told to seek favor from the Source Himself.

This is one of the most merciful spiritual re-educations in the Quran. Desire is not denied. It is redirected. The self is not asked to become empty of all longing. It is asked to stop feeding longing through rivalry and start feeding it through du'a.

Comparison says: I want what they have.
The ayah says: ask Allah for what He can open for you.

### 5. **Inna Allaha kana bikulli shay'in 'alima**: The Distribution You Question Is Held by Knowledge You Do Not Possess

The verse closes with divine knowledge:

**inna Allaha kana bikulli shay'in 'alima**.

Surely Allah is Knowing of all things.

The root **'-l-m** is knowledge, but here it comes with the breadth of **bikulli shay'** - of every thing. This ending is not decorative. It answers the hidden protest underneath envy.

Why them?
Why not me?
Why this distribution?

Allah knows.

[PAUSE]

He knows what gift would soften one servant and corrupt another.
He knows what deprivation would break one person and purify another.
He knows what timing protects you, what delay ripens you, what share suits your test, what form of fadl would draw you nearer and what form would make you forget Him.

The envious heart suffers partly because it wants divine distribution without divine knowledge. The ayah closes that door gently but firmly. Ask from Allah's favor, yes. But do so with trust that the One apportioning is not careless. He knows every thing.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Comparison Turns Another Person's Gift Into Your Private Theology Crisis

Envy is rarely only social.

At first it seems interpersonal. You want what another person has: their income, ease, beauty, marriage, voice, knowledge, influence, stability, children, opportunities, freedom from some burden you still carry. But if you sit honestly with the feeling long enough, you discover that it does not stop at the other person. It keeps moving. Eventually it reaches Allah.

[PAUSE]

Why did You give them this?
Why is my road shaped like this?
Why do I have this form of test and not that form of gift?

That is why the ayah is so wise to begin with **faddala Allahu** and end with **Allahu kana bikulli shay'in 'alima**. It reveals the vertical dimension of comparison before we have to learn it through prolonged bitterness. Another person's portion is never just another person's portion. It is also a place where your trust in Allah is being examined.

This is painful but freeing. Because once the real battlefield is named, the cure becomes possible. The answer to envy is not pretending other people's gifts are small or insignificant. The answer is learning to bring your ache before Allah instead of turning it into silent protest against His distribution.

### Theme 2: Du'a Is the Quran's Alternative to Covetousness

The most hopeful part of the verse is that it leaves desire alive.

The Quran does not ask the heart to become stone. It does not say, "Want nothing." It says, "Ask Allah for His favor." That is a completely different spiritual posture.

In envy, desire becomes poisoned by comparison. It says: I want increase, but only if I can read my increase against someone else's decrease or superiority. In du'a, desire becomes servanthood. It says: My Lord, You are the Owner of fadl. Open for me what is good, in the measure that is good, at the time that is good.

[PAUSE]

That shift changes everything.

A covetous heart becomes tight, suspicious, and joyless. It cannot celebrate another person's blessing because every blessing looks like evidence against itself. A praying heart can still feel pain, still feel longing, still ask for more, but it asks from Allah's treasury instead of living off resentment. And Allah's treasury does not shrink because someone else was given something.

That is why this verse is not only about contentment. It is about abundance. Human beings envy partly because we imagine provision as a closed room. The ayah opens a window: ask Allah for His favor. His fadl is not exhausted by what He already gave someone else.

## Closing Synthesis

This verse traces the whole spiritual movement of envy and then reverses it.

It begins with inner wishing.

It names differential favor without embarrassment.

It turns the soul back toward nasib, the allotted share connected to one's own earning.

Then it redirects desire upward through du'a and settles the whole matter in divine knowledge.

[PAUSE]

That is the architecture: do not stare sideways, answer faithfully for your share, and if your heart still longs, ask the Lord of favor rather than resenting the recipients of favor.

### Questions to Carry

1. Where has comparison in my life stopped being social annoyance and become a quiet objection to Allah's apportionment?
2. Do I know my own nasib and field of earning, or do I keep abandoning them to study someone else's?
3. When I feel longing, do I turn it into resentment or into du'a?
4. What would change if I truly believed that Allah's favor is not a closed room and that His knowledge exceeds every comparison I make?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**This ayah teaches that the heart is healed from corrosive comparison not by denying difference, but by trusting Allah's knowledge, answering for one's own share, and asking the Lord of favor directly.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, purify our hearts from the kind of wishing that corrodes trust. Keep us faithful to our own share, bless what we earn in obedience to You, and teach us to ask You for Your favor without resentment toward anyone You have favored. You know what we do not know, so make us content with Your wisdom and hopeful in Your generosity. Ameen.
