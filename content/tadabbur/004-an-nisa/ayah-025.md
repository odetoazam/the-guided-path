# Some of You Are From Others

## Surah An-Nisa (4:25)

[ayah:4:25] وَمَن لَّمْ يَسْتَطِعْ مِنكُمْ طَوْلًا أَن يَنكِحَ ٱلْمُحْصَنَـٰتِ ٱلْمُؤْمِنَـٰتِ فَمِن مَّا مَلَكَتْ أَيْمَـٰنُكُم مِّن فَتَيَـٰتِكُمُ ٱلْمُؤْمِنَـٰتِ ۚ وَٱللَّهُ أَعْلَمُ بِإِيمَـٰنِكُم ۚ بَعْضُكُم مِّنۢ بَعْضٍ ۚ فَٱنكِحُوهُنَّ بِإِذْنِ أَهْلِهِنَّ وَءَاتُوهُنَّ أُجُورَهُنَّ بِٱلْمَعْرُوفِ مُحْصَنَـٰتٍ غَيْرَ مُسَـٰفِحَـٰتٍ وَلَا مُتَّخِذَٰتِ أَخْدَانٍ ۚ فَإِذَآ أُحْصِنَّ فَإِنْ أَتَيْنَ بِفَـٰحِشَةٍ فَعَلَيْهِنَّ نِصْفُ مَا عَلَى ٱلْمُحْصَنَـٰتِ مِنَ ٱلْعَذَابِ ۚ ذَٰلِكَ لِمَنْ خَشِىَ ٱلْعَنَتَ مِنكُمْ ۚ وَأَن تَصْبِرُوا۟ خَيْرٌ لَّكُمْ ۗ وَٱللَّهُ غَفُورٌ رَّحِيمٌ

*Wa man lam yastati' minkum tawlan an yankiha al-muhsanati al-mu'minat fa-min ma malakat aymanukum min fatayatikumu al-mu'minat. Wa Allahu a'lamu bi-imanikum. Ba'dukum min ba'd. Fa-inkihuhunna bi-idhni ahlihinna wa atuhunna ujurahunna bil-ma'ruf, muhsanatin ghayra musafihatin wa la muttakhidhati akhdan. Fa-idha uhsinna fa-in atayna bifahishatin fa-'alayhinna nisfu ma 'ala al-muhsanati min al-'adhab. Dhalika liman khashiya al-'anata minkum. Wa an tasbiru khayrun lakum. Wa Allahu ghafurun rahim.*

**And whoever among you cannot afford to marry free believing women, then marry from those whom your right hands possess among your believing young women. Allah knows your faith best. You are from one another. So marry them with the permission of their people and give them their due compensation in a recognized manner, being chaste, not openly promiscuous and not taking secret lovers. Then when they are married, if they commit an indecency, upon them is half of what is upon the free married women of punishment. That is for whoever among you fears hardship. But to be patient is better for you. And Allah is All-Forgiving, Most Merciful.**

## Introduction

Some rulings enter the world exactly where human beings are most likely to become cruel.

A person has desire, but not means. A society has status hierarchies, but also vulnerable people inside those hierarchies. Law could easily move here in one of two ugly directions: either it ignores weakness and demands a kind of purity that many cannot carry, or it permits concession in a way that strips dignity from those already socially exposed.

This ayah does neither.

Surah An-Nisa has already been teaching us that family law is not only about permission and prohibition. It is about whether revelation can enter the places where power, money, gender, desire, and class all press against one another and still keep human worth intact. Here the surah addresses a believer who does not have the *tawl* to marry from among free believing women. But the ayah does not speak to him as though desperation suspends ethics. Nor does it speak of vulnerable women as though concession makes them lesser in the sight of Allah.

In the middle of a legal ruling, the verse says something morally explosive: **ba'dukum min ba'd**. Some of you are from others.

That is the center of gravity here.

The hook of the ayah is this: **Allah makes room for human difficulty without allowing difficulty to become an excuse for contempt.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Lam yastati' minkum tawlan**: The Ayah Begins by Acknowledging Real Constraint

The verse opens with inability: **wa man lam yastati' minkum tawlan**.

Whoever among you cannot manage the means.

The verb **yastati'** comes from the root **t-w-'**, the field of capacity, ability, what a person can actually bear or accomplish. The Quran is not speaking to an imagined human being with endless control over circumstances. It is speaking to people whose desires, finances, and social possibilities do not line up neatly.

Then comes **tawlan**, from the root **t-w-l**, length, extension, amplitude, scope. In this context it carries the sense of financial and social means, the reach to undertake marriage in the expected way. The root is beautiful here because it lets us feel the physical image behind the abstraction. This is not only "money" in the narrow sense. It is having enough extension in your life, enough room, enough reach, enough capacity to carry the commitment.

[PAUSE]

That matters spiritually.

Sometimes religious discourse speaks as though everyone lives on the same terrain. As though temptation lands equally, provision is equally distributed, and obedience unfolds under identical material conditions. The Quran does not flatten human experience like that. It names the shortage. It names the constraint. It starts from the actual strain of a person who wants chastity but does not possess full social ease.

This is already mercy. Revelation does not become less holy by recognizing difficulty. It becomes more searching, because once difficulty is named honestly, the path Allah gives through it can be trusted.

### 2. **Wallahu a'lamu bi-imanikum, ba'dukum min ba'd**: Faith Cuts Across the Hierarchies We Build

Then the ayah says something that interrupts the legal flow with theology:

**Wallahu a'lamu bi-imanikum. Ba'dukum min ba'd.**

Allah knows your faith best. Some of you are from others.

The phrase **a'lamu bi-imanikum** matters because it relocates the whole ruling under divine knowledge. Human beings look at status, lineage, outward rank, who is free and who is enslaved, whose household is established and whose is precarious. Allah says: He knows the reality of iman.

[PAUSE]

Then comes the line that breaks the pride of the social order: **ba'dukum min ba'd**.

The noun **ba'd** means some, part, one portion in relation to another. The repetition gives the sentence a kind of leveling force. You are from each other. You belong to one human fabric. Whatever legal distinctions exist in the society, they may not be allowed to turn into metaphysical arrogance.

This is one of the most moving interruptions in the ayah. It is as if the verse pauses mid-ruling to remind the believing community: do not let legal categories harden into spiritual disdain. Do not imagine that vulnerability removes shared origin, shared humanity, or shared standing before Allah.

We need this correction badly.

Human beings are always tempted to convert circumstance into worth. Whoever has more money begins to feel more substantial. Whoever has more options begins to feel more entitled. Whoever occupies the safer social location starts acting as though that location reveals a deeper kind of human value. This ayah cuts through all of that in four words.

Some of you are from others.

Not above others in your essence. Not made of a better substance. Not nearer to honor by your social ease. From one another.

### 3. **Bi-idhni ahlihinna wa atuhunna ujurahunna bil-ma'ruf**: Concession Is Still Bound by Consent, Due, and Recognized Good

The ayah then gives the procedure:

**fa-inkihuhunna bi-idhni ahlihinna wa atuhunna ujurahunna bil-ma'ruf**.

Marry them with the permission of their people and give them their due compensation in a recognized manner.

The word **idhn** from the root **a-dh-n** is permission, authorized allowance, a matter not seized but entered rightly. Already the ayah is teaching that need does not justify bypassing moral process.

Then **ujurahunna** appears, from the root **a-j-r**, wages, recompense, due return. In marriage context this is the due bridal compensation, not a favor, not optional generosity, not whatever remains after a stronger party is done negotiating. The verse insists that even where social vulnerability is present, the woman's due is still due.

[PAUSE]

And that due is to be given **bil-ma'ruf**.

We have already seen in An-Nisa how often **ma'ruf** appears when power is most likely to become selfish. From the root **'a-r-f**, it means what is recognized as good, honorable, fitting, morally intelligible. The Quran does not say: do the bare minimum that remains technically legal. It says: govern this relationship through recognized good.

That phrase protects the heart of the ruling from becoming exploitative. A concession can still be abused if the stronger party treats it like access rather than covenant. So the ayah surrounds the permission with a moral atmosphere: permission, due compensation, ma'ruf.

### 4. **Muhsanat... ghayra musafihat wa la muttakhidhati akhdan**: Chastity Is Protected in Public and in Secret

The verse then describes the women as **muhsanat**, chaste women, from the root **h-s-n**, enclosure, fortification, guardedness. The same root gives us the sense of a fortress. Chastity in Quranic language is not only abstention. It is protection, dignity, a life not made cheap.

Then come the two negations:

**ghayra musafihat** and **la muttakhidhati akhdan**.

**Musafihat** comes from the root **s-f-h**, to spill, to pour out. The image is startlingly physical. Something that should have been contained is being spilled openly. The word points to open sexual promiscuity, desire made public and diffused.

**Akhdan** comes from the root **kh-d-n**, an intimate secret companion, a hidden lover, a concealed attachment. So the verse shuts both doors: the public spilling and the private concealment.

[PAUSE]

That duality matters because many people imagine morality only at the level of appearance. If scandal is not public, they assume the matter is spiritually lighter. But the Quran refuses that simplification. Open promiscuity and secret arrangements are both named. Human beings can corrupt chastity noisily or quietly, with spectacle or with privacy, and both forms still matter before Allah.

### 5. **Wa an tasbiru khayrun lakum**: Even a Real Permission Is Not Always the Highest Path

Near the end, after concession has been opened and regulated, the ayah says:

**wa an tasbiru khayrun lakum**.

And that you remain patient is better for you.

The root **s-b-r** carries the sense of binding, restraining, holding oneself together under pressure. The ayah does not erase the permission it just gave. But it also does not let permission automatically become recommendation. There are cases where restraint is better, cleaner, less fraught, more preserving of hearts and futures.

[PAUSE]

This is a subtle but important Quranic pattern. Allah may permit something out of mercy for hardship while still letting us know that, where patience is genuinely possible, patience may be the more beautiful road. The verse therefore refuses two opposite mistakes: harshness that denies concession, and heedlessness that treats concession as spiritually costless.

The final names, **Ghafur** and **Rahim**, tell us why the whole ayah is framed this way. Allah forgives the frailty of His servants, and He is merciful in not legislating for them as though they were angels. But His mercy also teaches them not to injure others while looking for relief.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Hardship Often Reveals Whether We Think Human Worth Depends on Status

One of the ugliest things difficulty can expose in a community is not only desperation, but hierarchy of empathy.

We are often very moved by the hardship of people who look socially recognizable to us, people whose dignity we were already prepared to assume. But when hardship belongs to someone more vulnerable, less protected, less admired, or lower in the social order, our moral imagination can narrow quickly. We start talking about them as cases, exceptions, risks, or problems to be managed.

[PAUSE]

This ayah refuses that collapse. It addresses constrained men, but it also guards the dignity of vulnerable women inside the same breath. It gives a legal opening, but then immediately reminds the whole society that Allah knows faith best and that some of you are from others. The ruling is not only about marriage access. It is about whether a believing community can face unequal conditions without allowing those conditions to become a ladder of human worth.

That remains painfully relevant.

Whenever people are economically cornered, socially exposed, or dependent on stronger institutions, the temptation of the stronger side is to treat permission as leverage. To say, in effect: because you need something, your dignity becomes negotiable. The Quran says no. Need does not cancel due. Vulnerability does not cancel ma'ruf. Social difference does not cancel shared humanity.

This is a severe test for the heart, because pride loves to convert advantage into identity. The one with more means begins to feel like the standard human being, while others become footnotes. But Allah interrupts that lie before it can mature: some of you are from others.

### Theme 2: Not Every Halal Exit From Pressure Is the Same as Inner Ease

There is another tenderness in this ayah. It recognizes that sexual pressure is real, that financial inability is real, and that people sometimes need lawful outlets before hardship breaks them. Yet after opening the outlet, it still says: **to be patient is better for you**.

That sentence feels almost impossible until you sit with real human life.

Many of the things we seek in moments of strain are not only permissions. They are imagined rescues. We tell ourselves: if I can just get an allowed path through this pressure, the heart will finally settle. But often the allowed path still carries complexity, unequal power, emotional difficulty, and responsibilities we were not honest enough about at the start. So revelation gives us law, and then beyond law it gives us wisdom.

[PAUSE]

Patience here is not repression dressed up as piety. It is the sober recognition that not every accessible thing is the most healing thing. Sometimes a person is better served by waiting, building capacity, deepening taqwa, and refusing to let urgency make every near permission look like a final answer.

That is hard counsel, but it is merciful counsel. The ayah sees our weakness and does not shame it. It also sees how weakness can make us overread immediate openings. So Allah legislates with compassion and then whispers a higher discernment into the end of the verse: if you can bear patience, there is goodness in that too.

## Closing Synthesis

This ayah moves through social difficulty without ever surrendering moral clarity.

It begins with inability, opens a concession, surrounds that concession with consent, due compensation, and recognized good, names chastity in both its public and private threats, and then ends by saying that patience may still be better.

And right in the middle of all that law, the ayah inserts a sentence that interprets the whole scene: **ba'dukum min ba'd**.

That is the architecture. The ruling is not built only to manage desire. It is built to protect dignity while managing desire. It is built to make room for hardship without turning hardship into a theology of lesser people.

### Questions to Carry

1. Where do I still quietly convert a person's social vulnerability into a judgment about their worth?
2. Have I ever mistaken lawful access for true readiness?
3. In moments of pressure, do I remember that Allah's concessions are framed by ma'ruf, dignity, and accountability, not only relief?
4. What would change if I really believed that some of us are from others and that Allah knows faith better than status does?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**This ayah teaches that divine concession for hardship must still move inside dignity, due, and chastity, because before Allah the vulnerable are not lesser beings but people from our own human fabric.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, when we feel pressure, do not let us seek relief in ways that injure the dignity of others. Teach us to see iman where social pride sees rank, to honor every due You have made binding, and to choose patience when patience is better. You are Ghafur and Rahim, so forgive our arrogance and make us gentle with the vulnerable. Ameen.
