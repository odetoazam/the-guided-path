# They Want a Way Between That

## Surah An-Nisa (4:150-152)

[ayah:4:150] إِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ يَكْفُرُونَ بِٱللَّهِ وَرُسُلِهِۦ وَيُرِيدُونَ أَن يُفَرِّقُوا۟ بَيْنَ ٱللَّهِ وَرُسُلِهِۦ وَيَقُولُونَ نُؤْمِنُ بِبَعْضٍ وَنَكْفُرُ بِبَعْضٍ وَيُرِيدُونَ أَن يَتَّخِذُوا۟ بَيْنَ ذَٰلِكَ سَبِيلًا

[ayah:4:151] أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ هُمُ ٱلْكَـٰفِرُونَ حَقًّا ۚ وَأَعْتَدْنَا لِلْكَـٰفِرِينَ عَذَابًا مُّهِينًا

[ayah:4:152] وَٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ بِٱللَّهِ وَرُسُلِهِۦ وَلَمْ يُفَرِّقُوا۟ بَيْنَ أَحَدٍ مِّنْهُمْ أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ سَوْفَ يُؤْتِيهِمْ أُجُورَهُمْ ۗ وَكَانَ ٱللَّهُ غَفُورًا رَّحِيمًا

*Inna alladhina yakfuruna billahi wa rusulihi wa yuriduna an yufarriqu bayna Allahi wa rusulihi wa yaquluna nu'minu bi ba'din wa nakfuru bi ba'din wa yuriduna an yattakhidhu bayna dhalika sabila.*

And We have prepared for the disbelievers a humiliating punishment. But those who believe in Allah and His messengers and do not make a distinction between any of them - those, He will give them their rewards. And ever is Allah Forgiving and Merciful.**

## Introduction

There is a kind of disbelief that does not present itself as open rebellion. It presents itself as editing.

A person does not say, "I reject heaven's right to speak." That would sound too exposed. Instead he says something more socially manageable: I accept this part, not that part. This messenger, not that messenger. This revelation when it confirms my inherited loyalties, not when it unsettles them. In this way, the ego tries to preserve the dignity of being religious while reserving the sovereignty of final selection for itself.

These verses cut through that entire performance. They describe people who disbelieve in Allah and His messengers, but then the verse does not leave the matter at blunt rejection. It shows the inner mechanism: they want to separate between Allah and His messengers, and they say, "We believe in some and we disbelieve in some." Then comes the deepest diagnosis: they want to take a way between that.

That middle way is the fantasy. It is the imagined zone where a person gets to keep the language of faith while refusing the total claim of revelation. He wants enough acceptance to avoid being called a rejecter, but enough refusal to remain answerable only to preference. The Qur'an refuses to let that ambiguity survive. Those are truly the disbelievers.

Then the passage turns and offers the opposite image in a single line: those who believe in Allah and His messengers and do not make distinctions between any of them. This is not blind tribalism. It is coherence. If Allah is the sender, then faith cannot become a buffet of messengers chosen by taste.

The hook of the passage is this: **real iman does not reserve for the self the right to curate revelation, because the moment we try to create a respectable middle road between surrender and refusal, we have already made ourselves judge over what only Allah had the right to send.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Yuriduna an yufarriqu**: Disbelief Here Is Described as an Act of Separation

The verse begins by naming disbelief plainly:

inna alladhina yakfuruna billahi wa rusulihi.

Those who disbelieve in Allah and His messengers.

But then it immediately gives us their interior project:

wa yuriduna an yufarriqu bayna Allahi wa rusulihi.

They want to separate between Allah and His messengers.

The repetition of yuriduna matters. The passage is not only describing doctrinal error. It is describing desire. They want this. They are seeking it. There is an appetite beneath the creed.

[PAUSE]

Then comes yufarriqu, Form II from the root of separation. This is not merely noticing difference. It is actively making a division, imposing a split, introducing a distinction that fractures what should remain held together. Allah and His messengers belong to one revelatory relation: He sends, they convey. To honor the sender while selectively rejecting the sent is not reverence. It is rupture disguised as nuance.

That is why the wording is so strong. The verse does not say they distinguish between one messenger and another in harmless preference. It says they seek to make a separation between Allah and His messengers. In other words, they want the authority of God without the binding force of what He actually sent through His chosen carriers.

This is one of the most important spiritual warnings in the passage: disbelief is not always loud denial. Sometimes it is the quieter violence of severing what Allah joined.

### 2. **Nu'minu bi ba'din wa nakfuru bi ba'd**: Selectivity Sounds Reasonable Until the Qur'an Names It

Then the verse lets us hear their own statement:

nu'minu bi ba'din wa nakfuru bi ba'd.

We believe in some and we disbelieve in some.

The symmetry of the line is devastating. Some accepted, some rejected. Some received, some refused. The structure itself sounds balanced, almost moderate. That is exactly what makes it dangerous. The ego loves partiality when partiality can wear the costume of fairness.

[PAUSE]

But revelation is not a negotiation between co-equal parties. The servant does not stand over the message as editor. Once Allah is recognized as Lord and sender, the logic of selective acceptance collapses. To say "some yes, some no" is to relocate authority from heaven to the self.

This is why the passage is not really about information management. It is about ubudiyyah. Who decides? Who evaluates? Who gets final say over which messenger deserves recognition? The statement sounds theological, but the disease beneath it is lordship claimed by the nafs.

And then the verse adds the line that exposes everything:

wa yuriduna an yattakhidhu bayna dhalika sabila.

They want to take a way between that.

Yattakhidhu is Form VIII, to adopt, take for oneself, install as a chosen path. Sabil is not just an opinion. It is a way, a road, a traversed course. So the verse is telling us that this selective theology is not accidental confusion. It is a consciously desired lifestyle of in-betweenness.

### 3. **Ula'ika humu al-kafiruna haqqan** and **wa lam yufarriqu bayna ahadin minhum**: The Qur'an Closes the False Middle, Then Opens the True Contrast

Verse 151 responds with finality:

ula'ika humu al-kafiruna haqqan.

Those are the disbelievers, truly.

Haqqan matters. In truth. Really. Without the camouflage. The Qur'an strips away the prestige of ambiguity here. It does not let the selective rejecter hide behind a half-believing self-description. If he has made himself arbiter over Allah's messengers, then the judgment is not hybrid. It is real kufr.

[PAUSE]

Then comes the consequence:

wa a'tadna lil-kafirina 'adhaban muhina.

We have prepared for the disbelievers a humiliating punishment.

Muhina carries abasement, humiliation, lowering. That is fitting because the person had tried to secure dignity for himself through selective acceptance. He wanted to stand above revelation and still retain the prestige of being near it. The punishment answers the inner posture. The one who refused humble submission meets humiliation.

Then verse 152 gives the pure contrast:

wa alladhina amanu billahi wa rusulihi wa lam yufarriqu bayna ahadin minhum.

Those who believe in Allah and His messengers and do not separate between any of them.

Notice the echo. The same root of separation returns, but negated. Real iman is not merely believing in Allah abstractly. It is refusing to split the line of His messengers according to appetite. And the wording is stronger now: bayna ahadin minhum, between any one of them. No selective cutting, no self-serving exceptions.

Then the promise comes:

ula'ika sawfa yu'tihim ujurahum.

Those, He will give them their rewards.

Not because they mastered every mystery, but because they surrendered where surrender belonged.

And the verse closes not with harshness, but with:

wa kana Allahu ghafuran rahima.

Allah is Forgiving, Merciful.

That ending is beautiful. The passage was severe in doctrine, but it ends by reminding us that whole-hearted belief is met not only with recompense, but with mercy.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Self Wants a Religion It Can Approve Without a Revelation It Must Obey

These verses reveal one of the oldest temptations in religious life: to remain near revelation while still keeping veto power over it.

Most people do not like to think of themselves as rejecters. Rejection feels too exposed, too morally naked. So the nafs prefers a more sophisticated posture. It accepts whatever fits inherited pride, communal identity, political usefulness, emotional comfort, or intellectual vanity, and then it frames the rest as unnecessary, implausible, outdated, or negotiable. The result is a religion that still bears divine vocabulary while being quietly reorganized around the self.

[PAUSE]

That is why the verse's middle-road language is so psychologically exact. A person wants a way between that. He does not want open kufr, because he still wants some of the beauty, dignity, or benefits of faith. But he also does not want total taslim, because taslim dethrones the self. So he tries to build a third place: near enough to revelation to borrow its light, far enough from revelation to avoid full obedience.

The Qur'an says that place does not exist.

This has consequences for us even when we are not dealing with formal rejection of prophets. Any time I reserve the right to honor revelation only where it flatters me, and to suspend it where it costs me, I am rehearsing the same spiritual instinct. The content may differ, but the soul's claim is similar: let me remain believer while still deciding which divine claims I will let through.

### Theme 2: Whole Faith Is Not Credulity; It Is Coherent Surrender

The opposite of this pathology is not mindless sloganism. It is coherence.

The believer in verse 152 believes in Allah and His messengers and does not separate between any of them. That does not mean all prophets brought identical laws in every historical detail. The Qur'an itself knows variation in legislation and context. The point here is deeper: the believer refuses to relate to revelation as a consumer choosing preferred voices from the divine line.

[PAUSE]

Once Allah is the sender, the correct posture is not curation but surrender. I may study, ask, wrestle, and seek understanding. But I do not enthrone myself above the sending. I do not confer legitimacy on one messenger and withdraw it from another based on my taste. That is why this passage is ultimately about adab before Allah more than it is about comparative religion alone.

And there is comfort in this too. The verse ends with reward, forgiveness, and mercy. Whole-hearted faith is not presented as a trap. It is a home. The self that stops editing revelation and learns to receive it from Allah is not crushed by that surrender. It is gathered by it. There is rest in no longer having to play lord over what was never yours to authorize.

That may be one of the quiet mercies of iman: you do not need to invent a middle road anymore. You can be claimed by the truth whole.

## Closing Synthesis

These verses expose a particular form of disbelief: not only rejection, but the attempt to divide between Allah and His messengers.

They quote the selective creed directly: we believe in some and disbelieve in some.

They then reveal the desire beneath it: a wanted path between surrender and refusal.

Then the Qur'an closes that imagined road completely: those are truly the disbelievers.

And finally it opens the real contrast: believers are those who accept Allah and His messengers without making self-serving distinctions, and they are met with reward under Allah's forgiveness and mercy.

[PAUSE]

That is the architecture: separation desired, middle path imagined, truth named, coherence rewarded.

### Questions to Carry

1. Where do I still want a respectable middle road between full surrender and selective obedience?
2. In what ways do I try to keep the beauty of revelation while retaining final editorial power over it?
3. Do I receive divine guidance as servant, or do I quietly place myself in the role of evaluator over what Allah sent?
4. What would it look like for my iman to become more coherent, less curated, and more whole before Allah?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These verses teach that the moment we try to create a respectable middle path between accepting and rejecting Allah's messengers, we have already shifted authority from revelation to the self, while real iman is the coherence of receiving all whom Allah sent.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, do not let us turn revelation into something we curate according to our moods, pride, or fears. Protect us from the false dignity of selective submission and from the illusion that there is a safe road between surrender and refusal. Make our hearts whole in receiving what You sent, and meet our imperfect but sincere faith with Your forgiveness, mercy, and reward. Ameen.
