# Not Even an Atom

## Surah An-Nisa (4:40)

[ayah:4:40] إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ لَا يَظْلِمُ مِثْقَالَ ذَرَّةٍ ۖ وَإِن تَكُ حَسَنَةً يُضَـٰعِفْهَا وَيُؤْتِ مِن لَّدُنْهُ أَجْرًا عَظِيمًا

*Inna Allaha la yazlimu mithqala dharrah. Wa in taku hasanatan yuda'ifha wa yu'ti min ladunhu ajran 'azima.*

**Surely Allah does not wrong even the weight of an atom. And if it is a good deed, He multiplies it and gives from His own presence a tremendous reward.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

One of the deepest fears in the religious life is that something sincere might disappear.

You tried to hold your tongue once. You gave quietly once. You resisted something ugly once. You wept in a room no one saw. You carried a burden without applause. You left a sin before it became public. You made one small turn toward Allah that seemed too slight to matter. Then time passed, life became noisy again, and the heart began asking its old anxious question: did that count?

This ayah enters exactly there.

It comes after verses about miserliness, ostentation, false dealing, shaytan's companionship, and the tragedy of insincere spending. After all of that corruption around wealth and deed, the Quran gives the believer a theological floor to stand on: Allah does not wrong even an atom's weight. Not in punishment. Not in reward. Not in judgment. Not in accounting.

Then the verse does something even more beautiful. It says Allah does not stop at exact justice. If the deed is good, He multiplies it and then gives from Himself an immense reward beyond the multiplication.

The hook of the ayah is this: **with Allah, no sincere good is ever too small to be fully seen, and no sincere good remains only its original size.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **La yazlimu mithqala dharrah**: Divine Justice Reaches the Smallest Measurable Weight

The verse begins with certainty:

**inna Allaha la yazlimu mithqala dharrah**.

Surely Allah does not wrong even the weight of an atom.

The verb **yazlimu** comes from the root **z-l-m**, injustice, misplacement, putting something where it does not belong, withholding a due or assigning a burden wrongly. Zulm in Quranic language is not random harshness only. It is disorder in moral placement.

So when the ayah says Allah does not wrong, it is saying more than "Allah is not cruel." It is saying that Allah never misplaces a deed, a burden, an intention, a consequence, a person. Nothing is assigned to the wrong account with Him.

[PAUSE]

Then comes **mithqal**, from the root **th-q-l**, weight, heaviness, what can be measured by its mass. The verse does not only say "not even a little." It says not even the measurable weight of the smallest thing.

And that smallest thing is **dharrah**.

Across classical usage this can point to the tiniest speck, the minute particle one barely perceives, something almost beneath human notice. The point is not scientific precision in our modern sense. The point is moral precision. If a thing is so small that human attention would likely pass over it, Allah still does not let it vanish from just reckoning.

This is one of the most relieving truths in the Quran. Human beings are forgetful, distracted, biased, and often blind to the scale of one another's hidden efforts. Allah is not.

### 2. **Wa in taku hasanatan yuda'ifha**: Goodness Is Not Only Preserved, But Increased

Then the verse turns from justice to generosity:

**wa in taku hasanatan yuda'ifha**.

And if it is a good deed, He multiplies it.

The word **hasanah** comes from the root **h-s-n**, beauty, goodness, what is fair, excellent, made lovely. A hasanah is not merely a legally acceptable act. It is a good that has beauty in it, rightness in it, something pleasing to Allah.

Then comes **yuda'ifha**, from the root **d-'f**, doubling, multiplying, making something more than one measure of itself. This is stunning because the verse is no longer speaking about bare fairness. Exact justice would have been enough to preserve the act as it was. But Allah goes beyond preservation into increase.

[PAUSE]

That means the believer lives with two reassurances at once.

First: no real good is lost.
Second: no real good stays as small as it first appeared.

This changes how we understand hidden obedience. You may remember the deed by its size, the smallness of the effort, the fragility of the moment. Allah may receive it by its sincerity and multiply it far beyond the form in which you offered it.

### 3. **Wa yu'ti min ladunhu ajran 'azima**: There Is Reward Beyond the Multiplication

The verse still is not finished. After multiplication it says:

**wa yu'ti min ladunhu ajran 'azima**.

And He gives from His own presence a tremendous reward.

The verb **yu'ti** from the root **a-t-y** is to give, grant, bring forth. But what matters most here is the phrase **min ladunhu**.

**Ladun** suggests from directly with Him, from His special presence, from a nearness more intimate than a generic "from Him" would have conveyed. The verse is not only promising reward from a system. It is promising gift from the nearness of Allah's own generosity.

[PAUSE]

Then that gift is called **ajr** - reward, recompense, return for what was done - and it is وصفed as **'azim**, tremendous, immense, weighty in greatness.

This is one of the most beautiful movements in the Quranic moral imagination:

No zulm.
Then multiplication.
Then gift from Allah's own presence.

The servant comes with one small hasanah. Allah preserves it with perfect justice, enlarges it with bounty, and then gives beyond it from Himself.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: We Underestimate the Moral Weight of Small Sincere Things

Human beings are easily dazzled by scale.

Large donations, public sacrifice, visible scholarship, obvious heroism, decisive moments - these attract the eye. But most real religion is built from smaller materials. A private refusal. A quiet apology. A temptation resisted before anyone knew it existed. A prayer dragged through tiredness. A resentment swallowed. A secret charity. A restrained sentence. A patient afternoon with someone difficult.

[PAUSE]

Because these things are small, we often misread them as spiritually light. We think the decisive matters must look dramatic. But this ayah retrains us to think in atoms. Not because the Quran wants us obsessed with micro-accounting, but because it wants us cured of contempt for the hidden and the slight.

Many people leave good undone because they assume it is too small to matter. They tell themselves: this gesture is tiny, this effort is partial, this turn is late, this offering is unimpressive. But the One receiving the act is not measuring only visible size. He measures with a justice that never loses the particle and a generosity that can make the particle immense.

### Theme 2: Allah's Economy Is Better Than Ours Because Justice Is Not the Ceiling

There is another reason this verse steadies the heart. Human systems often make us suspicious of all accounting. We know what it is to be overlooked, misjudged, under-credited, blamed unfairly, or rewarded without proportion. We know what it is to have effort vanish in other people's forgetfulness.

So we can start projecting that anxiety upward.

Will Allah also let things disappear?
Will the hidden effort be too hidden?
Will the small deed be too small?

[PAUSE]

The verse answers not only with justice, but with more than justice. That is crucial. If Allah merely did not wrong, that alone would be enough to calm the conscience. But He also multiplies and gives from His presence. In other words, the divine economy is not a cold courtroom only. It is justice inside generosity.

This changes how we endure obscurity. You may be unseen by people. You may not get back what you gave in the world. You may even look foolish in the short term for having chosen sincerity over display. But with Allah none of that means loss. The whole structure of this verse says that the servant of Allah is never dealing with a miserly Lord.

## Closing Synthesis

This ayah is astonishing in its compactness.

It begins by ruling out the smallest conceivable injustice.

Then it says good is not merely preserved but multiplied.

Then it says that after multiplication, there is still more to be given from the nearness of Allah's own generosity.

[PAUSE]

That is the architecture: exact justice at the smallest scale, then enlarged mercy, then immense reward.

### Questions to Carry

1. Which small acts of sincerity have I been treating as too insignificant to matter with Allah?
2. Do I secretly imagine divine accounting through the lens of human forgetfulness and unfairness?
3. What would change in my daily life if I really believed that no hasanah remains only the size I first offered it?
4. Am I willing to keep doing small hidden good when this verse promises that Allah sees even the atom?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**This ayah teaches that Allah's justice loses nothing, and His generosity does not leave sincere goodness at its original size but multiplies it and adds from His own presence.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, do not let us belittle the small good You love. Make us people of hidden sincerity, trusting that with You nothing is lost and every real hasanah can grow beyond what we imagined. Protect us from despair in our efforts and from contempt for small acts of obedience. Give us from Your own presence an immense reward. Ameen.
