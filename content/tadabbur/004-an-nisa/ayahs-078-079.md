## Introduction

This is the passage that confronts the oldest question in human theology — and gives an answer that most people misread as a contradiction.

The year is roughly 4 AH. The Battle of Uhud is still fresh. The Muslim army had won at Badr — a miraculous, outnumbered victory that everyone attributed to Allah. Then came Uhud — and the archers left their positions, the tide turned, the Prophet ﷺ himself was wounded, and seventy companions were martyred. The same community that had celebrated Badr now had to make sense of Uhud.

And some couldn't. The hypocrites among them — the people who had joined Islam when it was winning — began saying: if Badr was from Allah, then Uhud must be from Muḥammad. Good outcomes prove divine favour. Bad outcomes prove human failure. God gets credit. The Prophet gets blame.

Into this, two ayahs arrive. And they appear to say opposite things.

Ayah 78: **All is from Allah.**
Ayah 79: **Good is from Allah. Evil is from yourself.**

Every reader stumbles here. Is it all from Allah? Or is evil from us? The answer is: **both are true, at different levels** — and the entire Quran's theology of *qadar* (divine decree) hinges on understanding why.

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### I. يُدْرِككُّمُ ٱلْمَوْتُ — Death as the Hunter

Before the theological argument even begins, the ayah opens with mortality. *Aynamā takūnū yudrik-kumu al-mawt* — wherever you are, death will **overtake** you.

The verb *yudrik* is Form IV of the root د-ر-ك (*daraka*) — to reach, to catch up with, to overtake. Form I means simply to arrive. Form IV — with its causative force — means to **pursue and catch**. Death doesn't wait for you. It *hunts* you.

And notice: *wa law kuntum fī burūjin mushayyadah* — even if you were in towers that are *mushayyadah*. This word is a Form II passive participle from the root ش-ي-د — meaning built up, reinforced, fortified with great effort. Form II adds thoroughness: these are not ordinary buildings. They are the **best** humanity can construct. The highest walls. The thickest stone. The most elaborate defences a civilisation can engineer.

And death reaches you anyway.

[PAUSE]

Why does the ayah open with this image? Because the theological debate that follows — who caused the good, who caused the evil — rests on an illusion. The illusion is that human beings control outcomes. That we can engineer our way to safety. That with enough towers, enough planning, enough strategy, we can determine what happens next.

The opening sentence demolishes this before the debate begins. You cannot control whether you live or die. You cannot control when the arrow finds you. So how can you pretend to know which outcomes are from Allah and which are from the Prophet? The attribution error the hypocrites are making — good is from God, evil is from you — rests on the assumption that they can distinguish between the two. But they can't even determine the most basic outcome of their own existence: when they will die.

### II. حَسَنَةٌ / سَيِّئَةٌ — The Indefinite That Universalises

In both ayahs, the words for good and evil carry *tanwīn* — the indefinite marker. *Ḥasanatun*. *Sayyi'atun*. Not *al-ḥasanah* (the specific good thing). Not *al-sayyi'ah* (the specific bad thing). **A** good thing. **A** bad thing. Any. Every.

This grammatical choice universalises the passage beyond Uhud. The hypocrites were talking about one battle. The Quran is talking about **every good and every evil that has ever befallen any human being**. The *tanwīn* lifts the discussion from a specific historical grievance to a universal theological principle.

And the verb: *tuṣibhum* / *aṣābaka* — both from the root ص-و-ب in Form IV (*aṣāba*). The core meaning of this root is an arrow hitting its target. When the Quran says something *aṣāba* you, it means it **found you** — like an arrow that was released and landed precisely where it was aimed.

[PAUSE]

This is not random. The good that befalls you was aimed. The evil that befalls you was aimed. The question is not whether there is a trajectory — the question is **who released the arrow and who positioned the target**.

And that is exactly what these two ayahs answer — at two different levels.

### III. كُلٌّ مِّنْ عِندِ ٱللَّهِ vs فَمِن نَّفْسِكَ — The Two-Level Truth

Here is the heart of the passage. And it requires patience, because the answer is not simple — but it is precise.

**Ayah 78** answers the hypocrites: *qul kullun min 'indi Allāh.* Say: **all is from with Allah.** The word *kullun* — "all" — is indefinite with *tanwīn*, meaning everything without exception. Good. Evil. Badr. Uhud. Your health. Your sickness. Your ease. Your hardship. **All of it** originates from Allah in the sense that nothing exists outside His will. Nothing happens without His permission. Nothing befalls you that He did not create the conditions for.

This is the level of **qadar** — divine decree. And at this level, the statement is absolute. All is from Allah.

**Ayah 79** then addresses the Prophet ﷺ directly — and appears to say something different: *mā aṣābaka min ḥasanatin fa-mina Allāh, wa mā aṣābaka min sayyi'atin fa-min nafsik.* Whatever good befalls you is from Allah. Whatever evil befalls you is **from yourself**.

[PAUSE]

How can both be true?

Because they are answering different questions.

*Kullun min 'indi Allāh* answers: **who created the conditions?** Allah. Everything that exists — good and evil, ease and hardship — exists within the scope of His will and creation. He made a world in which both are possible. He decreed the framework. At the ontological level — the level of existence itself — all is from Him.

*Fa-min nafsik* answers: **who earned this particular evil?** You did. Not because Allah is absent, but because Allah created a world with moral causation — a world where your choices produce consequences. The evil that befalls you comes through the door **your own hands opened**. Allah created the door. You walked through it. Both statements are true.

Think of it this way. A parent builds a house. The house has a stove. The child touches the stove and gets burned. Who built the stove? The parent. Who touched it? The child. Both truths coexist. The parent created the conditions that made the burn possible. The child's action caused the burn. You don't say "the parent burned the child" — that misattributes moral causation. You don't say "the stove had nothing to do with the parent" — that misattributes ontological origin.

[PAUSE]

Notice how the Quran handles this linguistically. In 78, the hypocrites use elaborate phrasing: *min 'indi Allāh* and *min 'indika* — "from the presence of Allah" / "from your presence." The word *'inda* adds specificity: they are pointing fingers, assigning blame, making it theatrical.

In 79, the correction strips away the elaboration. *Fa-mina Allāh.* *Fa-min nafsik.* Just: **from Allah** / **from your self**. No *'inda*. No theatrical pointing. The truth is simpler than the lie. It doesn't need the dramatic phrasing the hypocrites used. It stands bare.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Blame Game That Never Ends

Every human being has a theodicy problem. Not just theologians. Everyone.

When things go wrong — when the business fails, when the marriage breaks, when the diagnosis comes back, when the person you trusted betrays you — the first instinct is attribution. **Who did this to me?**

And the answer you choose shapes your entire spiritual life.

Option one: God did this to me. He is punishing me, testing me, singling me out. In this reading, God gets both the credit and the blame, and you are a passive recipient of whatever He sends. This produces either fatalistic resignation ("it was written") or theological rage ("why me?"). Both are spiritually corrosive, because both eliminate your agency.

Option two: I did this to myself. I made bad choices. I failed. I earned this. In this reading, you carry all the blame, and God is removed from the picture. This produces either crushing guilt or defiant self-reliance — and both are spiritually corrosive, because both eliminate the relationship with Allah.

[PAUSE]

The Quran's answer is neither. It is **both at once**, held in tension.

*Kullun min 'indi Allāh* — yes, Allah created the conditions. You live in a world where suffering is possible. That is His design. Not because He is cruel — because He is building something (a test, a growth, a purification) that requires the possibility of pain. The framework is His.

*Fa-min nafsik* — and yes, your hands opened the door. The evil didn't fall from the sky onto someone who had no part in it. Somewhere in the chain of causation — a choice you made, a boundary you crossed, a trust you betrayed, a preparation you neglected — your nafs contributed. Not everything bad that happens is your fault in the simplistic sense. But the Quran insists that your nafs is never entirely absent from the equation.

The spiritual maturity this demands is staggering. You must simultaneously say: "Allah is in control of everything" AND "my choices have real consequences." If you collapse into only the first, you become a fatalist. If you collapse into only the second, you become an atheist. The Quran asks you to hold both — and the discomfort of holding both is itself the faith.

### Theme 2: The Witness Who Makes the Debate Irrelevant

The passage closes: *wa kafā bi-Llāhi shahīdā.* Sufficient is Allah as a Witness.

*Shahīd* is the *fa'eel* intensive pattern — permanent, constitutive witness. Not someone who happened to be there. Someone whose **nature** is witnessing. Allah didn't observe these events as an outsider. He was present — as Creator, as Sustainer, as the One who sent the arrow and the One who placed the target — and His witnessing is sufficient.

[PAUSE]

Sufficient for what? For ending the debate.

The hypocrites were arguing about attribution. Who caused Uhud? Whose fault is it? The Quran gives the two-level answer — and then closes by making the entire debate irrelevant. Because there is a Witness. And the Witness saw everything. He saw the qadar — the conditions He created. He saw the kasb — the choices you made. He saw both levels simultaneously, because He operates at both levels simultaneously.

You are arguing about who did what. He **was there**. He doesn't need your analysis. He doesn't need your attribution framework. He doesn't need the hypocrites to explain Uhud to Him. He watched it happen. He knows exactly which outcomes were His decree and which were human consequence — because He is the Author of the decree and the Witness of the consequence.

*Wa kafā bi-Llāhi shahīdā.* And that is sufficient.

The practical application: the next time you find yourself in the blame game — "why did God do this to me?" or "this is entirely my fault" — remember that the question has already been answered at two levels, and the Witness who holds both levels is watching. Your job is not to solve the theodicy problem. Your job is to respond to what befell you with the best your nafs can offer — and to trust that the Witness will sort the rest.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture

The two-ayah pair has a mirror structure:

**78 (The Demolition):**
- Death reaches you even in towers → human control is an illusion
- Hypocrites' attribution error: good from God, evil from Muhammad
- Correction: ALL is from Allah (ontological level)
- Rebuke: why can't they understand?

**79 (The Construction):**
- Good from Allah → divine gift
- Evil from your nafs → human moral causation
- Prophetic mission: sent as a messenger to people
- Closure: sufficient is Allah as a Witness

The first ayah demolishes the error. The second constructs the truth. The first operates at the ontological level (all is from Allah). The second operates at the moral-causal level (evil is from your self). Together, they form the Quran's complete theodicy — not in volumes of philosophy, but in two sentences.

### Questions to Carry

- When something goes wrong in your life, which collapse do you tend toward — "God did this to me" (removing your agency) or "this is entirely my fault" (removing Allah from the picture)? Can you hold both levels at once?

- *Fa-min nafsik* — from your self. Is there a hardship in your life right now where, if you're honest, your own choices opened the door — even if the conditions were not in your control?

- The hypocrites' error was selective attribution: God gets credit, the Prophet gets blame. Where in your life are you selectively attributing — thanking Allah for the wins and blaming others for the losses?

- *Wa kafā bi-Llāhi shahīdā* — sufficient is Allah as a Witness. If you truly believed that every outcome you've experienced was witnessed by Someone who saw both the decree and the choice, would that change how you talk about your suffering?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**Everything is from Allah at the level of existence, but evil finds you through your own hands — and the Witness who saw both levels needs no human to explain which is which.**

### The Closing Invitation

The discomfort of this passage is the point. It refuses to let you rest in simple answers. God is in control — AND your choices matter. The framework is His — AND the door you opened is yours. This is not a contradiction. This is the full picture. And the Witness who holds both truths simultaneously is *shahīd* — not a bystander who happened to see, but the One whose very nature is witnessing.

You don't need to solve the theodicy problem. You need to live faithfully inside it. Hold the tension. Trust the Witness. And when the arrow finds you — whether it carries good or harm — know that the One who created the trajectory and the One who witnessed the landing are the same, and He is sufficient.

### Du'ā'

*Allāhumma, You told us that all is from You and that evil is from our own selves — help us hold both truths without collapsing into fatalism or despair. When good reaches us, let us see Your hand and be grateful. When harm reaches us, let us search our own hands and be humble. And in both — let us remember that You are Shahīd, that You were there before we arrived and will remain after we leave, and that Your witnessing is sufficient even when our understanding is not.*
