# The Heart That Would Not Give a Naqir

## Surah An-Nisa (4:53-55)

[ayah:4:53] أَمْ لَهُمْ نَصِيبٌ مِّنَ ٱلْمُلْكِ فَإِذًا لَّا يُؤْتُونَ ٱلنَّاسَ نَقِيرًا

[ayah:4:54] أَمْ يَحْسُدُونَ ٱلنَّاسَ عَلَىٰ مَآ ءَاتَىٰهُمُ ٱللَّهُ مِن فَضْلِهِۦ ۖ فَقَدْ ءَاتَيْنَآ ءَالَ إِبْرَٰهِيمَ ٱلْكِتَـٰبَ وَٱلْحِكْمَةَ وَءَاتَيْنَـٰهُم مُّلْكًا عَظِيمًا

[ayah:4:55] فَمِنْهُم مَّنْ ءَامَنَ بِهِۦ وَمِنْهُم مَّن صَدَّ عَنْهُ ۚ وَكَفَىٰ بِجَهَنَّمَ سَعِيرًا

## Introduction

Some hearts reveal themselves most clearly when they imagine power.

As long as a person lacks control, he can still look restrained, moderate, even principled. He can tell himself a story about fairness because reality has not yet given him much to distribute. But revelation has a way of cutting through that illusion. It asks a devastating question: if this person actually had a share of authority, what kind of world would come out of his hand?

That is where these verses begin.

They come after a passage in which some People of the Book praised the path of disbelief and distorted the scale of guidance itself. Now the Quran digs beneath that public corruption and names two private diseases underneath it: scarcity of soul and envy. First it says that if such people had any share of dominion, they would not give people even a *naqir*, the tiniest imaginable amount. Then it asks if what is really driving them is envy over Allah's favor. Finally it answers that envy by reminding them that Allah already gave the family of Ibrahim revelation, wisdom, and kingdom, and human beings still split before that gift into belief and refusal.

So the passage is not only about what others did long ago. It is about a recurring human pattern. A person envies what Allah gave someone else, imagines himself more worthy of it, and reveals by that very envy that if power were placed in his hand he would become a miser with authority.

The hook of the passage is this: **envy does not only protest another person's blessing; it quietly reveals the poverty of heart from which one would govern if authority were ever given.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Nasib min al-mulk ... naqira**: The Verse Measures the Soul by the Smallest Mark

The first question is almost mocking in its sharpness:

**am lahum nasibun min al-mulk?**

Do they have any share of dominion?

The word **nasib** from the root **n-s-b** is an allotted portion, a share assigned to someone. And **mulk** from **m-l-k** is authority, possession, ruling power, effective dominion. The verse is not granting them grandeur. It is asking whether they even have a portion of it.

Then comes the answer hidden in the consequence:

**fa idhan la yu'tuna al-nasa naqira**.

If that were so, they would not give people even a *naqir*.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the Quran's most precise humiliations of greed. A **naqir** is the tiny speck, pit, or minute mark on a date stone, something almost beneath the threshold of notice. The image is brilliant. Revelation is not only saying they would be stingy in large matters. It says their heart is so contracted that even at the scale of the almost invisible, generosity would fail.

That matters because power exposes measure. Anyone can sound generous in theory. But if the self is governed by envy and scarcity, then once authority arrives, it will not produce justice. It will produce withholding. And the verse proves that by shrinking the imagined gift down to almost nothing. Not even that.

So before the passage ever says "envy," it already teaches us to read the soul politically. The one who resents others' blessings is often telling you what he would do with power if it were his: he would make the world smaller for everyone else.

### 2. **Am yahsuduna al-nasa**: Envy Is Protest Against Allah's Distribution

Then the second question arrives:

**am yahsuduna al-nasa 'ala ma atahumu Allahu min fadlihi?**

Or do they envy people for what Allah gave them from His favor?

The verb **yahsuduna** comes from the root **h-s-d**, envy, resentment at another's gift, a disturbed inability to bear that someone else received what you did not. But the verse is careful with what envy is directed toward. It is not merely "they envy people." It is "they envy people for what Allah gave them."

[PAUSE]

That turns envy into a theological offense, not only a social emotion. The envier is not simply irritated with another person. He is inwardly protesting Allah's apportioning. He is looking at divine distribution and saying, in effect, this should have been otherwise.

Then the gift itself is described as **fadl**.

The root **f-d-l** carries surplus, bounty, extra generosity, what exceeds strict necessity. So the envied thing is not framed as theft or injustice. It is Allah's own bounty. The envier cannot bear the wideness of Allah's giving.

This is why hasad is so spiritually dangerous. It lets the nafs keep a pious surface while inwardly quarreling with divine generosity. It can dress itself as principle, discernment, or concern for standards. But the Quran strips it down. Sometimes the issue is simply that you hate the fact that Allah favored whom He favored.

### 3. **Ala Ibrahima al-kitab wa al-hikmah ... mulkan 'azima**: Revelation and Rule Can Both Be Divine Gifts

Then the verse answers the envy not by soothing it, but by widening the historical frame:

**faqad atayna ala Ibrahima al-kitaba wa al-hikmata wa ataynahum mulkan 'azima**.

We already gave the family of Ibrahim the Book and wisdom, and We gave them a mighty kingdom.

The word **kitab** from **k-t-b** is revelation inscribed, ordered guidance. **Hikmah** from **h-k-m** is wisdom, placement, judgment, ordered understanding that puts things where they belong. Then comes **mulkan 'azima** - mighty dominion, great authority.

[PAUSE]

That sequence is important. The verse is saying that Allah's favor to the line of Ibrahim was never small. Revelation itself was given there. Wisdom was given there. And even kingdom was given there. So what exactly is the envier objecting to? This is not some strange new departure in the divine pattern. Allah has long given both guidance and outward authority where He wills.

The answer to envy, then, is not to shrink Allah's generosity down to something the envious heart can tolerate. The answer is to remind the heart how large divine generosity already has been across sacred history.

And that makes the envy look even smaller. The envier behaves as if the current gift were unbearable, while the Quran replies: look at Ibrahim's family. Allah's giving has always exceeded your resentful imagination.

### 4. **Fa minhum man amana bihi wa minhum man sadda 'anhu**: The Real Division Is Not In the Gift, But In the Human Response

Then the passage lands in one of the most elegant lines:

**fa minhum man amana bihi wa minhum man sadda 'anhu**.

So among them are those who believed in it, and among them are those who turned away from it.

The gift was real. The revelation was real. The wisdom was real. The kingdom was real. Yet human response still split.

One group **amana bihi**. They believed in it. They received it as truth.

Another group **sadda 'anhu**. The verb from **s-d-d** carries turning away, obstructing, averting oneself or others from a thing. So the refusal here is not passive distance only. It has the feel of active deflection.

[PAUSE]

This is a profound correction. The problem is not that Allah's favor was unclear or that His distribution lacked signs. The problem is that human beings respond differently to the same gift. Some surrender. Some resist. Some are healed by revelation. Some are exposed by it.

Then comes the closure:

**wa kafa bi Jahannama sa'ira**.

Hell is enough as a blazing fire.

The noun **sa'ir** from **s-'-r** carries flame set ablaze, kindled burning. The verse ends by saying that when envy hardens into rejection and obstruction, no further proof of consequence is required. Jahannam is enough.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Envy Often Reveals a Fantasy of Better Distribution

Hasad is not only sadness that someone else has something. It often carries a hidden fantasy: if distribution were in my hand, I would arrange things more rightly.

That fantasy is what 4:53 destroys.

The verse asks whether such people even have a share of dominion, and then immediately tells you what kind of dominion their souls would produce. Not justice. Not generosity. Not breadth. They would not give people even a naqir.

[PAUSE]

That means envy is deeply self-exposing. The person thinks he is criticizing Allah's distribution, but in reality he is revealing the poverty of his own imagined rule. His complaint sounds lofty until the Quran tests it with the question of actual giving. Then it becomes clear that the envier's problem was never an excess of justice. It was a shortage of heart.

This is worth carrying into our own lives. When we resent another person's opening, recognition, influence, provision, family, or religious gift, what are we quietly saying? Very often: if I held the keys, I would have distributed this better. But would I? Or would my own scarcity simply make me more withholding, more anxious, more grudging, more incapable of celebrating any gift not routed through me?

The envier imagines himself generous because he imagines himself deprived. Revelation answers by showing what his deprivation has already made of him.

### Theme 2: The Same Gift Softens Some Hearts and Hardens Others

The move into Ibrahim's family and then into the split of responses is one of the passage's deepest consolations and warnings.

Allah's favor can be enormous. Book, wisdom, kingdom. Yet even then, not everyone responds with faith. Some believe. Some turn away. So the presence of divine favor does not force surrender. It reveals the heart.

[PAUSE]

This matters because we often misread rejection. We think: if truth were clearer, if the blessing were larger, if the signs were stronger, people would surely accept. But these verses say the opposite. Allah already gave magnificently, and the human split remained. That means the decisive issue is not only what was given. It is what kind of heart received it.

That is both sobering and liberating. Sobering, because no external greatness can replace inner honesty. Liberating, because if some turn away from what is plainly Allah's favor, their refusal does not reduce the favor itself. The gift remains from Allah even when human envy snarls around it.

So the believer learns two things at once: do not envy what Allah distributed, and do not be shaken when others reject a gift Allah clearly gave. Human beings have always divided before revelation and favor. The straight response is not resentment. It is iman.

## Closing Synthesis

These verses move through the whole anatomy of envious refusal.

First they test the soul with power and reveal that it would not give even the tiniest thing.

Then they name the deeper disease: envy at Allah's favor.

Then they answer that envy by reopening the great precedent of Ibrahim's family, who were given revelation, wisdom, and kingdom.

Finally they remind us that the true division was never in Allah's generosity, but in the human response to it: some believed, and some turned away.

[PAUSE]

That is the architecture of the passage: scarcity, envy, divine bounty, then the split of human hearts before that bounty.

### Questions to Carry

1. Where does my discomfort at another person's blessing expose a fantasy that I would have distributed better than Allah?
2. If authority were truly placed in my hand, would my heart widen or tighten?
3. Do I know how to distinguish grief from envy and righteous concern from quiet protest at Allah's favor?
4. When Allah gives clearly, am I among those who believe in His distribution or among those who turn away from it?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These verses teach that envy protests Allah's favor while quietly revealing a stingy soul, and that the real divide before divine bounty is always between those who believe and those who turn away.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, purify our hearts from hasad. Do not let us resent what You have given, and do not let imagined deprivation turn us into people who would withhold even the smallest good from others. Make us people who recognize Your favor, honor Your distribution, and respond to Your gifts with iman rather than protest. Keep us far from the hearts that turn away from what You have made clear. Ameen.
