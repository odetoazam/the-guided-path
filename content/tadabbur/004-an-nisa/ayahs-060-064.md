# The Claim of Faith and the Place of Return

## Surah An-Nisa (4:60-64)

[ayah:4:60] أَلَمْ تَرَ إِلَى ٱلَّذِينَ يَزْعُمُونَ أَنَّهُمْ ءَامَنُوا۟ بِمَآ أُنزِلَ إِلَيْكَ وَمَآ أُنزِلَ مِن قَبْلِكَ يُرِيدُونَ أَن يَتَحَاكَمُوٓا۟ إِلَى ٱلطَّـٰغُوتِ وَقَدْ أُمِرُوٓا۟ أَن يَكْفُرُوا۟ بِهِۦ وَيُرِيدُ ٱلشَّيْطَـٰنُ أَن يُضِلَّهُمْ ضَلَـٰلًۢا بَعِيدًا

## Introduction

There is a kind of false faith that does not begin by denying revelation. It begins by relocating the place where decisions are really made.

The tongue says, "We believe in what was sent down." The public claim remains intact. The religious vocabulary is still available. But when conflict, grievance, self-interest, or social pressure arrives, the soul reveals where it actually wants judgment to come from. Not from what Allah sent down. Not from the Messenger. It wants another court, another standard, another power, another arrangement that leaves the ego less exposed.

These verses stand inside that exact fracture.

They are among the sharpest Quranic descriptions of nifaq because they do not only name hypocrisy as hidden disbelief. They show its behavior. It claims iman, seeks judgment from taghut, recoils when called back to revelation, then returns only after consequence has landed and tries to rename the whole evasion as good intention. Finally the passage opens the true road back: prophetic authority is not ornamental, and tawbah is not complete when it bypasses the messenger-relationship Allah Himself established.

So the arc is devastatingly precise. First a counterfeit claim. Then a practical choice. Then refusal. Then excuse. Then counsel. Then the real door of return.

The hook of the passage is this: **the deepest test of faith is not whether we can say the right things about revelation, but whether we let revelation and the Messenger decide for us when the ego most wants another authority.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Yaz'umuna**: The Passage Opens by Downgrading Their Claim

Verse 60 does not say, "those who believe."

It says: **alladhina yaz'umuna annahum amanu**.

Those who **claim** that they believe.

The verb **yaz'umuna** from the root **z-'-m** is not the strong Quranic language of verified confession. It carries claiming, alleging, saying something about oneself that the context has not granted full trust. The very first word after the relative clause places distance between their speech and the reality of their state.

[PAUSE]

That is psychologically exact. Nifaq often lives on borrowed language. It survives by keeping the confession verbally intact while hollowing out its practical meaning. So the Quran does not begin by debating their theology abstractly. It begins by letting the wording itself carry suspicion. They say they believe.

Then the verse deepens the exposure by mentioning that they claim belief not only in what came to the Prophet but also in what came before him. In other words, the issue is not lack of information about revelation as a category. They speak the language of continuity and scripture. The problem lies elsewhere.

That elsewhere arrives immediately:

**yuriduna an yatahakamu ila al-taghut**.

They want to seek judgment from taghut.

### 2. **Yatahakamu ila al-taghut**: Hypocrisy Is Revealed by the Court It Really Wants

The verb **yatahakamu** comes from the root **h-k-m**, judgment, arbitration, governance, decisive placement. In Form VI it carries the sense of taking a matter for mutual adjudication, seeking an authority to settle what is disputed.

That is important because the verse is not only about private admiration of falsehood. It is about where disputes are taken. Where do you actually go when something costly must be decided?

And the destination is named:

**al-taghut**.

From the root **t-gh-y**, overstepping, crossing bounds, swelling past rightful limit. Taghut is any false authority that demands a submission or judgment it has no right to hold against Allah's command.

[PAUSE]

That makes the verse unnervingly relevant in every age. Taghut is not only an idol in the crude sense. It is any structure, person, appetite, system, tribal pressure, or self-protective arrangement that becomes the authority you prefer over revelation when revelation becomes inconvenient.

Then the verse says:

**wa qad umiru an yakfuru bihi**.

They had already been commanded to reject it.

So the issue is not ambiguity. The command to disbelieve in taghut was already there. Yet the soul still wanted that court. That is why the verse ends with shaytan wanting to lead them into **dalalan ba'ida** - a far wandering, not a minor slip. Once the place of judgment is moved, the drift is no longer small.

### 3. **Ta'alaw ... yasudduna 'anka sududa**: When Called Upward, They Turn Away With Weight

Verse 61 intensifies the scene:

**wa idha qila lahum ta'alaw ila ma anzala Allahu wa ila al-rasul**.

When it is said to them: come up to what Allah sent down and to the Messenger.

The verb **ta'alaw** comes from the root **'-l-w**, height, rising, elevation. The summons is not neutral motion. It is upward motion, a coming into a higher moral register. Revelation is not only another opinion on the table. It is an ascent.

[PAUSE]

And how do they respond?

**ra'ayta al-munafiqina yasudduna 'anka sududa**.

You see the hypocrites turning away from you with intense aversion.

The root **s-d-d** carries turning aside, obstructing, blocking. Then the verbal noun **sudud** seals the movement with force. It is not a casual hesitation. It is thick refusal, aversion with momentum in it.

That pairing is spiritually revealing. The more clearly revelation is presented as the place to come, the more some hearts show that they do not merely disagree. They recoil. The problem is not lack of access. It is resistance to ascent.

### 4. **Qawlan baligha** and **li-yuta'a**: The Passage Ends by Rebuilding the Right Relationship

After consequence lands and excuses appear, verse 63 says:

**wa qul lahum fi anfusihim qawlan baligha**.

Speak to them, concerning their very selves, a penetrating word.

The noun **qawl** is speech, but **baligh** from **b-l-gh** is reaching, arriving fully, attaining its target. This is speech that reaches where it is meant to reach. Not loud speech. Not merely harsh speech. Speech that lands inside the self.

[PAUSE]

Then verse 64 gives the principle underneath the whole passage:

**wa ma arsalna min rasulin illa li-yuta'a bi-idhni Allah**.

We did not send any messenger except to be obeyed by Allah's permission.

This is the correction to the whole disease. Messengers are not sent as inspirational accessories while real judgment happens elsewhere. They are sent so that obedience to Allah takes concrete form through the one He sent, always **bi-idhni Allah**, by Allah's permission.

Then the verse shows what return should have looked like:

they wronged themselves,
they came,
they sought forgiveness from Allah,
and the Messenger sought forgiveness for them,
and then they would have found Allah **tawwaban rahima**.

The intensive noun **tawwab** matters. Allah is not merely one who can forgive once in the abstract. He is ever-relenting, repeatedly turning toward returning servants. But that mercy is found on the far side of honest movement, not on the near side of self-managed excuses.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: We Reveal Our Real Theology When We Need a Decision That Costs Us

Most people can speak beautifully about divine guidance when nothing immediate is being threatened.

We affirm revelation in the abstract. We honor prophetic language. We say we want Allah's judgment. But then a conflict appears. Our pride is on the line. Our relationship is at stake. Our status, money, grievance, desire, or self-image becomes involved. And suddenly we start searching for a court more favorable to the ego.

[PAUSE]

That is when these verses become painfully close.

The question is not only, "Do I believe revelation is true?" The question is, "Where do I really want adjudication from when truth may expose me?" Some of us want revelation as affirmation but not as verdict. We want Allah's words to comfort us, but not to overrule the arrangement we already prefer.

This is one of the hidden forms of nifaq in religious life. It may not look like open denial. It may even sound pious. But beneath the pious sound is a practical theology: when the matter becomes costly, I trust another authority more than I trust the one Allah established.

The verses expose that without sentimentality. The court you seek tells the truth about the faith you claim.

### Theme 2: Excuses Often Arrive Only After Consequence, but Return Requires Humility Earlier

Verse 62 is painfully human. Disaster lands because of what their own hands sent ahead, and only then do they come swearing, "We intended nothing but good and reconciliation."

How recognizable that is.

We often resist truth while it is still asking for surrender, then run toward explanation only after consequences begin to close around us. We rename our compromise as wisdom. Our evasion as balance. Our refusal as good intentions. We want language that can rescue our self-respect after our choices have already injured us.

[PAUSE]

The Quran does not deny that mixed intentions exist. But this passage shows how easily "we meant well" becomes a shelter the ego builds after refusing the path Allah had already made plain.

That is why verse 64 is so healing. It shows that the way back was never barred. The problem was not that Allah's mercy was scarce. The problem was that pride preferred excuse to approach. Had they come earlier, asked forgiveness earlier, submitted earlier, they would have found Allah waiting as **Tawwab Rahim**.

This teaches us something crucial about tawbah. Real return is not merely feeling bad after the fallout. It is allowing Allah's appointed path of correction to humble you before the fallout hardens into narrative management. It is coming while the ego still resists coming.

## Closing Synthesis

These five verses move with severe order.

First they weaken the claim of faith by calling it a claim.

Then they show the real location of trust: adjudication sought from taghut.

Then they reveal the recoil of hypocrisy when summoned upward to Allah's revelation and the Messenger.

Then consequence lands, excuses arrive, and the Prophet is told to answer with speech that reaches the self.

Finally the principle is stated plainly: messengers are sent to be obeyed, and mercy would have been found through honest return instead of evasive explanation.

[PAUSE]

That is the architecture: claim, false court, recoil, excuse, penetrating counsel, then the true road of obedience and tawbah.

### Questions to Carry

1. When a decision becomes costly to my ego, where do I really want judgment from?
2. Have I ever used "good intentions" to rename an evasion after its consequences became visible?
3. Do I treat the Messenger's authority as real obedience under Allah, or only as language I admire from a safe distance?
4. What would it look like for me to come back through Allah's appointed path of return before my excuses harden any further?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These verses teach that hypocrisy is exposed by the authority we seek when truth becomes costly, and that mercy is found not through self-protective excuses but through obedient, humble return to Allah by the path He established.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, do not let us hide behind claims of faith while our hearts seek other courts. Save us from taghut in all its forms, from excuses that come only after consequence, and from a religion curated by the ego. Give us words that reach our own souls, and make us people who return to You through the path of obedience You established until we find You Tawwaban Rahima. Ameen.
