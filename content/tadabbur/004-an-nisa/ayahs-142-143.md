# They Stand for Prayer Lazily

## Surah An-Nisa (4:142-143)

[ayah:4:142] إِنَّ ٱلْمُنَـٰفِقِينَ يُخَـٰدِعُونَ ٱللَّهَ وَهُوَ خَـٰدِعُهُمْ وَإِذَا قَامُوٓا۟ إِلَى ٱلصَّلَوٰةِ قَامُوا۟ كُسَالَىٰ يُرَآءُونَ ٱلنَّاسَ وَلَا يَذْكُرُونَ ٱللَّهَ إِلَّا قَلِيلًا

[ayah:4:143] مُّذَبْذَبِينَ بَيْنَ ذَٰلِكَ لَآ إِلَىٰ هَـٰٓؤُلَآءِ وَلَآ إِلَىٰ هَـٰٓؤُلَآءِ ۚ وَمَن يُضْلِلِ ٱللَّهُ فَلَن تَجِدَ لَهُۥ سَبِيلًا

*Inna al-munafiqina yukhadi'una Allaha wa huwa khadi'uhum. Wa idha qamu ila al-salati qamu kusala yura'una al-nasa wa la yadhkuruna Allaha illa qalila.*

*Mudhabdhabina bayna dhalik la ila ha'ula'i wa la ila ha'ula'. Wa man yudlili Allahu fa lan tajida lahu sabila.*

**Indeed, the hypocrites think to deceive Allah, but He is deceiving them. And when they stand for prayer, they stand lazily, showing themselves to the people and not remembering Allah except a little, wavering between that, belonging neither to these nor to those. And whoever Allah sends astray - never will you find for him a way.**

## Introduction

There is a kind of worship that exhausts the body because it never reaches the heart.

Outwardly it looks like prayer. The person stands. The motions are there. The appointment is kept in form. But inwardly there is heaviness, reluctance, social calculation, and very little remembrance of Allah. The limbs appear in worship while the heart is still busy managing spectators.

These verses take us into that painful interior.

They begin with the hypocrisy of deception itself: the munafiq imagines he can deceive Allah, while Allah is dealing with him through a fitting reversal. Then the verse gives us one of the most unforgettable portraits in the Qur'an: when they stand for prayer, they stand lazily. They perform before people. They remember Allah only a little.

Then the next verse names the deeper condition beneath that worship: mudhabdhabin. Wavering, dangling, shaken back and forth. Neither fully with these nor fully with those. The prayer was heavy because the self itself was unsettled. There was no real center from which worship could rise.

The hook of the passage is this: **performative worship becomes heavy because nifaq empties prayer of settled belonging, leaving the soul suspended between audiences and far from Allah.**

[PAUSE]

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Yukhadi'una Allaha wa huwa khadi'uhum**: The Attempt to Deceive Allah Is Already a Form of Delusion

The first line is startling:

inna al-munafiqina yukhadi'una Allaha wa huwa khadi'uhum.

The hypocrites seek to deceive Allah, while He is the One dealing with them in turn.

The verb yukhadi'una comes from the field of deception, strategic concealment, trying to make another party receive a false appearance. But to aim that toward Allah is itself a sign of profound unreality. The munafiq has spent so long managing surfaces that he begins to live as though the Lord of the unseen could be handled through performance the way people can.

[PAUSE]

Then comes the divine answer: wa huwa khadi'uhum. The wording is not symmetrical in weakness, but in fitting judgment. The one who lives by false presentation will meet the consequences of that false presentation from Allah. The game of surfaces does not outwit heaven; it returns upon its player.

This opening matters because it frames everything that follows. Lazy, performative prayer is not a random character flaw. It is the worship-form of a deeper life built on managed appearance.

### 2. **Qamu kusala ... yura'una al-nasa**: The Body Stands, but the Soul Is Still Looking Sideways

Then the verse says:

wa idha qamu ila al-salati qamu kusala.

When they stand for prayer, they stand lazily.

The repetition of qamu intensifies the image. They do stand. The outward act happens. But the mode of standing is kusala - sluggish, heavy, reluctant, drained of eager life.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the Qur'an's most searching descriptions of prayer emptied of love. The issue is not only missed prayer. It is prayer as burden because the soul is elsewhere.

Then the verse tells us why:

yura'una al-nasa.

They show themselves to the people.

Riya' is public-facing worship, the redirection of a sacred act toward human eyes. Once people become the effective audience, the act naturally grows heavy. Why? Because the soul was not made to find rest in being watched by creation. That kind of worship is labor without intimacy.

So the laziness is not just temperament. It is theology embodied. Prayer becomes weighty when Allah is no longer the one before whom the heart is most truly standing.

### 3. **Wa la yadhkuruna Allaha illa qalila** and **mudhabdhabin**: Little Dhikr Reveals an Unsettled Center

Then comes the wound beneath the wound:

wa la yadhkuruna Allaha illa qalila.

They do not remember Allah except a little.

The scarcity here matters. Dhikr is not absent absolutely, but it is thin, faint, undernourished. Enough perhaps to preserve form, not enough to generate life.

[PAUSE]

And then verse 143 gives the name of the deeper state:

mudhabdhabin bayna dhalik.

Wavering between that.

Mudhabdhab is a remarkable word. It carries agitation, being shaken back and forth, left without stable settling. The soul is not planted. It is oscillating.

Then the verse makes the instability explicit: neither to these nor to those. Not fully with the believers in truth, not fully with the disbelievers in honest declaration. Just suspended in a calculating in-between.

That explains the thin dhikr and the heavy prayer. A heart that belongs nowhere in truth cannot bring full presence into salah. It is too busy splitting itself.

So the verse ends: whoever Allah lets go astray, you will never find for him a way. Once the self consents to this instability deeply enough, the road out is no longer found through surface correction alone.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Prayer Becomes Heavy When It Is Performed for an Audience Rather Than Offered From Belonging

Many believers know some taste of heaviness in prayer, and that alone is not hypocrisy. Human hearts fluctuate, bodies tire, and khushu' rises and falls. But these verses show a specific kind of heaviness: the heaviness of worship detached from real allegiance and redirected toward spectators.

That is why the verse is so psychologically exact. If the soul stands in prayer still hungry for human approval, then salah will feel like exposure management. You are no longer entering divine shelter; you are maintaining a visible role. Of course that grows tiring.

[PAUSE]

One way this verse helps us is by teaching us not to mistake all prayer-heaviness for the same thing. Sometimes the cure is rest, repentance, or renewed understanding. But sometimes the heaviness is telling the truth about divided intention. It is revealing how much of the act has been handed over to the gaze of others, their judgment, their expectations, their symbolic use of you.

The healing then is not merely "try harder." It is to return prayer to Allah. To let salah become again the place where the self stops performing and starts belonging.

### Theme 2: A Soul That Refuses Honest Location Will Eventually Lose the Ability to Stand Cleanly Anywhere

Verse 143 is one of the great verses of spiritual instability.

There are people who imagine that never fully locating themselves is a form of intelligence. Keep every option open. Never commit too deeply. Be vague enough to survive in every room. Say just enough for each audience to keep access. But the Qur'an shows the spiritual cost of that strategy. The self becomes mudhabdhab - not flexible, but shaken loose from center.

[PAUSE]

Once that happens, not only loyalties but worship itself starts to wobble. You cannot stand cleanly before Allah while still trying to remain undefined before truth. The in-between posture that feels socially useful becomes spiritually corrosive.

This is why the passage is so merciful in its severity. It tells us what instability does before we make a home inside it. It warns that little dhikr, lazy salah, and audience-conscious worship are not isolated symptoms. They may be signs of a deeper refusal to belong honestly with Allah and His people.

The cure is difficult but simple: stop living between audiences. Let the heart be located. Let prayer be for Allah. Let dhikr grow until the self is no longer surviving on scraps of remembrance.

## Closing Synthesis

These verses begin with the delusion of trying to deceive Allah, and answer it with the certainty that the strategy of surfaces only returns upon its صاحب.

They then show us hypocritical prayer: standing outwardly, but with laziness, public performance, and only a little remembrance of Allah.

Then they expose the root condition beneath that worship: a mudhabdhab soul, shaken between camps, unable to belong truthfully anywhere.

[PAUSE]

That is the architecture: deception, heavy worship, thin dhikr, then unstable identity.

### Questions to Carry

1. In what ways has my prayer become heavier because I am still too aware of people while standing before Allah?
2. Is my dhikr abundant enough to give my worship life, or have I been surviving on "a little" remembrance?
3. Where in my life am I still trying to remain undefined rather than belonging honestly with the truth?
4. What would it look like for my salah to become an act of real belonging rather than managed appearance?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These verses teach that hypocritical worship grows heavy because the soul that seeks to manage appearances before people has lost settled belonging with Allah, and therefore stands in prayer with little dhikr and no true center.**

### Du'a

Allahumma purify our salah from performance and our hearts from divided allegiance. Do not let us become people of little dhikr, lazy standing, and shifting loyalties. Plant us firmly with truth, let us belong to You without bargaining, and make our prayer a place of life rather than an audience-managed burden. Ameen.
