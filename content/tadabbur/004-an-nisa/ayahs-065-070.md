# No Haraj, Then Good Company

## Surah An-Nisa (4:65-70)

[ayah:4:65] فَلَا وَرَبِّكَ لَا يُؤْمِنُونَ حَتَّىٰ يُحَكِّمُوكَ فِيمَا شَجَرَ بَيْنَهُمْ ثُمَّ لَا يَجِدُوا۟ فِىٓ أَنفُسِهِمْ حَرَجًا مِّمَّا قَضَيْتَ وَيُسَلِّمُوا۟ تَسْلِيمًا

## Introduction

Many people are willing to obey as long as obedience confirms what they already wanted.

The real test begins when a verdict comes down against the ego. A dispute opens, a judgment is made, a command arrives, and suddenly the question is no longer whether we admire revelation. The question becomes whether the heart can stay soft when revelation rules contrary to its preference.

These verses enter exactly there.

They come right after a passage exposing those who claimed faith while seeking judgment from taghut. Now the Quran states the matter without camouflage. Real iman is not established until the Messenger is made judge in what has tangled between people, until the heart finds no inner tightness against his decision, and until surrender ripens into complete submission. Then the passage does something beautiful: it shows that such obedience is not crushing but strengthening. If they had done what they were exhorted toward, it would have been better for them, more stabilizing, more rewarding, more guiding. And it ends with one of the Quran's sweetest promises: good company with the prophets, siddiqin, shuhada', and salihin.

So the movement is remarkable. It begins with the hardest thing for the nafs to bear, inward surrender after an unwelcome judgment, and ends with the tenderness of blessed companionship.

The hook of the passage is this: **the obedience that first feels like loss to the ego becomes, in Allah's accounting, the very road to firmness, guidance, reward, and the best company imaginable.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Yuhakkimuka ... shajara baynahum**: Real Faith Is Tested in the Tangle, Not the Theory

Verse 65 opens with one of the strongest emphases in the surah:

**fa la wa rabbika la yu'minuna**.

No, by your Lord, they do not truly believe.

Then comes the condition:

**hatta yuhakkimuka fima shajara baynahum**.

Until they make you judge in what has tangled between them.

The verb **yuhakkimuka** comes from the root **h-k-m**, judgment, decisive placement, ruling that settles confusion. The same root appeared earlier in the desire to seek judgment from taghut. Now the right judge is named explicitly.

[PAUSE]

And what is being judged? **Fima shajara baynahum**.

The verb **shajara** from **sh-j-r** is striking. It carries branching, interweaving, something that has grown tangled like branches crossing. Disputes are not always clean lines. They become thorny, emotionally entangled, spread through relationships and interests. The Quran chooses a word that lets you feel the messiness.

That matters because obedience is easiest in clean abstractions. The verse tests it in the most human place: the tangled conflict, the thing that has grown branches between people. Will you let the Messenger judge there too? In the place where grievance is alive and everyone thinks they have a case?

The verse says that is where faith becomes visible.

### 2. **La yajidu fi anfusihim harajan ... wa yusallimu taslima**: The Inward State Matters as Much as the Outer Verdict

Then the verse deepens:

**thumma la yajidu fi anfusihim harajan mimma qadayta**.

Then they find in themselves no constriction concerning what you decided.

The word **haraj** from **h-r-j** is tightness, constriction, inward unease, a chest that narrows under what it does not want to accept. This is one of the most searching criteria in the Quran. Outward compliance alone is not enough. The verse reaches into the inner atmosphere after judgment.

[PAUSE]

Then comes the seal:

**wa yusallimu taslima**.

And they submit in full submission.

The root **s-l-m** carries surrender, handing over, becoming yielded, being made sound through submission. The verbal noun **taslima** intensifies the act. It is not a reluctant minimum. It is complete yielding.

This is why the verse is so demanding and so healing. It refuses the idea that iman can remain only at the level of external deference while the inward self continues to litigate against Allah and His Messenger. The wound in us is not only that we disobey outwardly. It is that we keep private court sessions in the heart after Allah's judgment has already come.

Real submission closes those sessions.

### 3. **Wa ashadda tathbita** and **min ladunna**: Hard Obedience Produces Stability, Not Fragility

Verse 66 then says something the nafs rarely believes:

**wa law annahum fa'alu ma yu'azuna bihi lakana khayran lahum wa ashadda tathbita**.

If they had done what they were admonished with, it would have been better for them and far more strengthening.

The noun **tathbit** from **th-b-t** is firmness, stabilization, being made steady. This is profound because we usually fear that costly obedience will make us smaller, weaker, or less secure. The verse says the opposite. Doing what revelation exhorts toward is what anchors the soul.

[PAUSE]

Then verse 67 adds:

**la ataynahum min ladunna ajran 'azima**.

We would surely have given them from Our own presence a tremendous reward.

The phrase **min ladunna** carries nearness, from directly with Us, from an intimacy more specific than a generic "from Allah." The reward is not merely from a distant system. It is from Allah's own nearness.

So the passage moves beautifully: obeying what first feels hard becomes **khayr**, then **tathbit**, then **ajr 'azim**, and then in verse 68 straight guidance itself.

### 4. **Al-siddiqin ... al-shuhada' ... rafiqa**: The End of Obedience Is Company

Verse 69 is one of the most beloved promises in the Quran:

**wa man yuti'i Allaha wa al-rasula fa ula'ika ma'a alladhina an'ama Allahu 'alayhim**.

Whoever obeys Allah and the Messenger, those will be with the ones Allah favored.

Then the favored company is named.

**Al-nabiyyin** - the prophets.

**Al-siddiqin** - from **s-d-q**, the utterly truthful, those whose truthfulness has become rank and identity.

**Al-shuhada'** - from **sh-h-d**, the witnesses, those whose lives stand in testimony.

**Al-salihin** - from **s-l-h**, the made-right and the right-acting, people in whom goodness has become soundness.

[PAUSE]

Then the verse ends with:

**wa hasuna ula'ika rafiqa**.

Excellent are those as companions.

The noun **rafiq** from **r-f-q** carries companionship marked by gentleness, fitting company, one who travels with you in a way that eases rather than harms. This is not merely being placed in the same location as the righteous. It is being granted their company.

That is extraordinary. The passage began with judgment in tangled disputes and ends with companionship among the most favored servants of Allah. The road is obedience; the atmosphere at the end is suhbah.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Hardest Part of Obedience Is Often the Private Resistance No One Else Sees

Most of us know how to perform outward submission when required.

We can say yes. We can stop arguing. We can comply visibly. But verse 65 searches a place much deeper than social behavior: **la yajidu fi anfusihim harajan**. No inward constriction. No private resentment. No secret sense that Allah's judgment wronged me even if I am too disciplined to say it aloud.

[PAUSE]

That is where many spiritual illnesses hide. We obey outwardly while nursing an inner complaint against what Allah decided for us. We let the body bow while the self still keeps a clenched fist around its own preferred ruling. Sometimes the issue is a legal matter, sometimes a relational wound, sometimes a decree in life itself. Why this loss? Why this command? Why this closing? Why this path and not another?

The verse teaches that faith ripens when the soul stops treating Allah's verdict as a foreign imposition and starts surrendering from within. That does not mean we never feel pain. It means pain does not become litigation against the Lord who knows better than we do.

And that is why this verse is so liberating. It diagnoses the hidden fatigue of the believer who outwardly obeys but inwardly keeps revisiting the verdict with resistance. Submission becomes lighter when the private courtroom closes.

### Theme 2: The Obedience We Fear Is Often the Very Thing That Would Make Us Firm

Verses 66-68 overturn one of the ego's oldest lies. The ego says: if you really obey here, you will lose too much. You will become less free, less safe, less yourself. Revelation answers: if they had done what they were exhorted with, it would have been better for them and more stabilizing.

That is not intuitive to the nafs.

We imagine firmness comes from keeping some protected zone of self-rule untouched. The Quran says firmness comes from the opposite direction. It comes from obeying Allah where obedience costs you. Each surrendered preference becomes a stake driven deeper into the ground of guidance.

[PAUSE]

Then the verses widen the horizon even further. Stability is not the end. Reward from Allah's nearness, straight-path guidance, and the companionship of the most favored servants all unfold from the same obedience the self first resisted.

This should change how we read difficult commands and unwelcome verdicts. Not every hard thing is good, but every command from Allah and His Messenger is better than the nafs believes in the moment it resists it. We often stand before an act of surrender seeing only the immediate loss. Allah sees the later firmness, the greater reward, the clearer path, and the company waiting at the end.

## Closing Synthesis

These verses move with astonishing tenderness through severity.

They begin by swearing that faith is not complete until the Messenger is made judge in tangled disputes.

Then they deepen the requirement: no inward haraj, but full taslim.

Then they answer the fear of obedience by promising khayr, tathbit, immense reward, and guidance.

Finally they reveal that the road of obedience does not end in isolation, but in the companionship of prophets, siddiqin, shuhada', and salihin.

[PAUSE]

That is the architecture: judgment, inward surrender, strengthening, then company.

### Questions to Carry

1. Where do I still obey outwardly while keeping inward haraj against what Allah decided?
2. What command or verdict am I quietly treating as a threat, when Allah says it would actually make me firmer?
3. Do I really believe that costly obedience is better for me, or do I still instinctively trust my resistance more?
4. What kind of person would I become if I pursued good company by way of deeper obedience rather than by mere admiration of the righteous?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These verses teach that true iman appears when the heart surrenders without inward constriction to the Messenger's judgment, and that such obedience becomes the road to firmness, guidance, reward, and the best of company.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, remove haraj from our hearts before Your judgments. Give us real taslim where our egos most resist it, and make the commands You love a source of firmness rather than fear for us. Guide us on a straight path, reward us from Your nearness, and gather us in the company of the prophets, siddiqin, shuhada', and salihin. Ameen.
