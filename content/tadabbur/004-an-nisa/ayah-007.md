## The Ayah

[ayah:4:7] لِّلرِّجَالِ نَصِيبٌ مِّمَّا تَرَكَ ٱلْوَٰلِدَانِ وَٱلْأَقْرَبُونَ وَلِلنِّسَآءِ نَصِيبٌ مِّمَّا تَرَكَ ٱلْوَٰلِدَانِ وَٱلْأَقْرَبُونَ مِمَّا قَلَّ مِنْهُ أَوْ كَثُرَ ۚ نَصِيبًا مَّفْرُوضًا

*Lil-rijāli naṣībun mimmā taraka al-wālidāni wal-aqrabūn. Wa lil-nisā'i naṣībun mimmā taraka al-wālidāni wal-aqrabūn. Mimmā qalla minhu aw kathur. Naṣīban mafrūḍā.*

For men is a share of what the parents and close relatives leave behind. And for women is a share of what the parents and close relatives leave behind — be it little or much — an obligated share.

---

## Introduction

A man has died in Medina. He was a fighter. He had a house, livestock, some trade goods, a modest sum of currency. He had two sons, a daughter, and a wife.

The sons know what happens next. They have watched this scene their whole lives — at every funeral, in every family, for as long as anyone can remember. The adult men of the family gather. The wealth is divided among those who can carry a sword, lead a caravan, defend the family name. The logic is simple: inheritance goes to those who protect and provide. Women and children do not protect and provide. Women and children are protected and provided for. They are dependents. And dependents do not inherit — they are inherited.

In pre-Islamic Arabia, a man's widow could be claimed by his eldest son from another wife — not as a wife to be married but as property to be absorbed into the estate. A man's daughters received nothing. They were fed and clothed by whichever male relative assumed guardianship, and they were married off when the time came, generating a mahr that — as we have already seen in 4:4 — was often paid to the father, not the bride.

This was not cruelty in their own understanding. This was the social contract. Those who fight inherit. Those who are protected are provided for. The system was internally coherent. It had been running for centuries. No one in that room — not the sons, not the widow, not the daughter standing at the edge of it — expected what was about to happen.

[PAUSE]

Into this room, into this system, into this centuries-old logic that no one questioned because no one had a framework for questioning it, this ayah arrives. And it does something that, fourteen hundred years later, still has not been fully absorbed:

It says the same sentence twice.

Not approximately the same. Not summarized. **The same grammatical structure, word for word, once for men and once for women.** And then it seals both with two words that make the arrangement irreversible.

---

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. The Repetition That Is the Revolution

Read the ayah in Arabic and the structure is unmistakable:

**لِلرِّجَالِ نَصِيبٌ مِمَّا تَرَكَ الْوَالِدَانِ وَالْأَقْرَبُونَ**

**وَلِلنِّسَاءِ نَصِيبٌ مِمَّا تَرَكَ الْوَالِدَانِ وَالْأَقْرَبُونَ**

For men — a share — of what — the parents and close relatives — leave behind.
For women — a share — of what — the parents and close relatives — leave behind.

The Quran could have said this in one sentence: "For men and women is a share of what the parents and close relatives leave behind." Arabic has the conjunction *wa* — it could have combined them. It would have been shorter, more efficient, and it would have conveyed the same legal ruling.

But it would not have conveyed the same **revolution**.

[PAUSE]

The repetition is not redundancy. It is insistence. The Quran is speaking to an audience that has never — not once in living memory — heard the sentence "women have a share of the inheritance." The concept does not exist in their world. It has no precedent. It has no framework. Saying it once might be heard as a metaphor, a qualification, a suggestion. The audience might hear "for men is a share" and nod, and then hear "and for women too" and assume it is a lesser share, a symbolic inclusion, a courtesy that will be interpreted away.

So the Quran does not give them that room. It says the **full sentence again**. Not a shortened version. Not "and for women likewise." The entire clause: *lil-nisā'i naṣībun mimmā taraka al-wālidāni wal-aqrabūn*. Every word. The parents. The close relatives. The leaving behind. The share. All of it, repeated with the only change being the subject: *rijāl* becomes *nisā'*.

The grammatical weight given to the women's share is **identical** to the grammatical weight given to the men's share. Not half. Not less. Not subordinated. Identical. The Quran forces the listener to hear the women's inheritance with the same sentence structure, the same rhythm, the same completeness as the men's. Whatever the listener's culture told them about who matters in a room where wealth is divided — the grammar says: the same.

### 2. What a Naṣīb Is — And What It Is Not

The word used for "share" is **نَصِيب** — *naṣīb*. Root ن-ص-ب — *nūn-ṣād-bā'*.

This root carries an image of **setting up, establishing, planting firmly**. A *nuṣb* is a stone marker set in the ground. A *manṣib* is an established position of authority. When something is *naṣb*, it is erected, fixed, visible.

A *naṣīb*, then, is not a handout. It is not a favor. It is not a portion that someone decided to allot you out of generosity. It is a share that is **planted** — established, fixed in place, immovable. Your naṣīb is not something someone gives you. It is something that belongs to you the way a stone belongs in the ground where it was set.

[PAUSE]

Notice that the Quran did not use the word *ḥaqq* (right) here, or *qism* (division), or *juz'* (portion). It used *naṣīb* — the word that carries the image of something planted. The woman's inheritance is not a right granted to her by the community. It is a share set in the ground by Allah. It was there before anyone decided to honor it, and it remains there whether anyone honors it or not. The naṣīb does not require recognition to exist. It exists because it was established.

And the word appears **three times** in this single ayah. Once for men (*naṣībun*). Once for women (*naṣībun*). And once more at the end, in the accusative, sealing both: *naṣīban mafrūḍā* — an obligated share. The repetition of the word itself is doing work: naṣīb, naṣīb, naṣīb. It is being driven into the ground like the stone marker that shares its root.

### 3. The Seal — مَفْرُوضًا

The ayah closes with two words that function as a lock on everything that came before.

**نَصِيبًا مَّفْرُوضًا** — *naṣīban mafrūḍā*.

An obligated share.

*Mafrūḍ* is the passive participle of the root ف-ر-ض — *fā-rā-ḍād*. To decree. To obligate. To make mandatory. This is the root of *farḍ* — the highest category of obligation in Islamic law. A farḍ is not a recommendation. Not a sunnah. Not a good idea. It is a divine command whose abandonment constitutes sin.

And *mafrūḍ* is the passive participle — something that **has been decreed**. The agent is implicit: decreed **by Allah**. The shares are not being presented as a new social policy. They are being presented as a fait accompli — already decreed, already established, already obligated before the listener heard the ayah. The passive voice removes the human from the equation. No scholar decreed this. No council voted on it. No cultural consensus evolved toward it. It was *mafrūḍ* — decreed, passively, by the only One whose decrees cannot be overridden.

[PAUSE]

Now combine all three closing words: *naṣīban mafrūḍā*.

A share — planted, established, fixed — that has been obligated by divine decree.

This is the Quran taking the inheritance right of women and embedding it in the most immovable language available. It is not a suggestion that can be debated. It is not a preference that can be overridden. It is a naṣīb — planted in the ground. And it is mafrūḍ — decreed from above. The word is in the earth and the authority is in the sky, and between them there is no space for a human being to insert a different arrangement.

### 4. "Whether Little or Much" — The Clause That Closes Every Loophole

Between the parallel statements and the closing seal, the Quran inserts six words that might be the most legally consequential in the ayah.

*Mimmā **qalla** minhu **aw kathur**.*

"From whatever is **small** in it **or large**."

*Qalla* — root ق-ل-ل — to be few, small, insignificant.
*Kathura* — root ك-ث-ر — to be many, large, abundant.

The inheritance share applies whether the estate is a single goat or a thousand. Whether the father left a fortune or a debt. Whether the family is wealthy or poor. The principle does not scale with the size of the estate. It is absolute.

[PAUSE]

Why does this matter? Because without this clause, there would be a loophole large enough to drive a camel through. Someone could argue: "Yes, women inherit — but only from large estates. My father left so little that dividing it further would impoverish the family." The clause *mimmā qalla minhu aw kathur* pre-emptively closes that argument. Little or much. No minimum threshold. No exception for poverty. No cultural override.

The share is planted (*naṣīb*). The share is obligated (*mafrūḍ*). And the share applies regardless of size (*qalla aw kathur*). Every possible avenue of evasion has been sealed.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: What It Means to Hear Your Name for the First Time

Imagine you have lived your entire life in a room where decisions are made about you but never to you. Where your future is discussed at tables you are not seated at. Where the words "she gets" or "her share" have never been spoken in connection with wealth, property, or inheritance — because in your world, those words do not apply to people like you.

You are not mistreated, necessarily. You may be loved. You may be well-fed, well-clothed, well-cared-for. But you are cared for in the way a dependent is cared for — as someone who receives what others decide to give, not someone who owns what is rightfully theirs. Your well-being depends on the goodwill of the men in your family. If they are generous, you eat well. If they are not, you do not. Your prosperity is a function of their character, not your right.

[PAUSE]

And then — in a single ayah, in a gathering where the Quran is being recited — you hear your category named. Not as an afterthought. Not as a modifier attached to the men's sentence. You hear a full sentence — subject, verb, object, every grammatical element — dedicated to you.

*Wa lil-nisā'i naṣībun mimmā taraka al-wālidāni wal-aqrabūn.*

For women is a share of what the parents and close relatives leave behind.

Your share. Named. In a sentence that is grammatically identical to the one that named the men's share. With a word — *naṣīb* — that means something planted, established, immovable. And sealed with *mafrūḍā* — decreed by the One whose decrees are not subject to revision.

This is not merely a change in inheritance law. This is the moment a category of human beings was told, for the first time in their cultural memory: **you own something that no one can take away by claiming it was never yours to begin with.**

The revolutionary nature of this ayah is easy to miss from a modern vantage point. We live in a world where female inheritance is — in most legal systems — assumed. But in the world this ayah was spoken into, the idea that a daughter stood in the same grammatical sentence as a son when wealth was being divided was not a reform. It was an upheaval. It overturned not a specific custom but the foundational logic of an entire economic system: the logic that said protection and provision flow only from those who bear arms, and inheritance flows only to them.

### Theme 2: The God Who Decrees Before Anyone Agrees

There is something in the word *mafrūḍā* that reaches beyond inheritance law into the nature of divine legislation itself.

When the Quran wants to describe something as obligatory, it has options. It can use *wājib* — binding, necessary. It can use *lāzim* — attached, incumbent. It can use *ḥatm* — decisive, final. All carry different shades of obligation.

It used *mafrūḍ*. And the passive voice matters: not "a share that Allah has obligated" (active) but "a share that has been obligated" (passive). The agent — Allah — is implicit, not named. The effect is to present the obligation as **pre-existing**, as something that was always already the case, something the ayah is revealing rather than creating.

[PAUSE]

This is how divine legislation works in the Quran. It does not present itself as new policy. It presents itself as uncovered truth. The woman's inheritance was always her right — the jahiliyyah just couldn't see it. The ayah did not create the right. The ayah **removed the veil** that was hiding it.

And this is why the closing word is *mafrūḍā* and not, say, *mu'ṭā* (given) or *mamnūḥ* (granted). Those words would imply that the share was being bestowed — that before this ayah, there was no share, and now there is one. *Mafrūḍ* implies the opposite: the share was always decreed. The decree preceded the revelation. What happened in this ayah was not a new law being enacted but an eternal law being announced.

The implications extend beyond inheritance. If this is how divine law works — not creating rights but uncovering them, not granting shares but revealing shares that were always decreed — then every violation of the law is not just a crime against the victim. It is a crime against reality itself. The man who denies his daughter her inheritance is not just unjust to her. He is **denying a fact** — a fact that was decreed before it was revealed, that existed before it was announced, that is planted in the ground (*naṣīb*) whether he acknowledges it or not.

You can refuse to honor a decree. You cannot make it un-decreed.

## Closing Synthesis

### The Architecture

The ayah has a bilateral symmetry that mirrors its meaning:

**A — Men's share:** *Lil-rijāli naṣībun mimmā taraka al-wālidāni wal-aqrabūn*
**A' — Women's share:** *Wa lil-nisā'i naṣībun mimmā taraka al-wālidāni wal-aqrabūn*
**B — Scope:** *Mimmā qalla minhu aw kathur*
**C — Seal:** *Naṣīban mafrūḍā*

The perfect parallelism of A and A' is the visual embodiment of the principle: the two shares are linguistically equal because they are ontologically equal. Not identical in amount — the Quran will specify different amounts in 4:11-12 — but identical in **status**. Both are naṣīb. Both are mafrūḍ. Both apply to estates little and large. The structure of the sentence IS the argument.

And the movement from A/A' (the parallel shares) to B (the scope) to C (the seal) is a movement from **right** to **universality** to **irrevocability**. You have a share. It applies to everything. It cannot be undone.

[PAUSE]

### Questions to Carry

- The Quran said the same sentence twice — once for men and once for women — when it could have combined them. Where in your life are you combining what should be stated separately, because combining it lets you avoid giving equal weight to both?

- If a naṣīb is something planted in the ground — fixed and established by divine decree — what does it mean that in many Muslim families today, daughters are still pressured to "forgive" their inheritance share? What does it mean to ask someone to uproot what Allah planted?

- The passive voice in *mafrūḍā* presents the obligation as pre-existing — something revealed, not created. What other rights in your life might be pre-existing decrees that your culture has simply not yet uncovered?

- *Mimmā qalla minhu aw kathur.* Whether little or much. Where in your life are you using the smallness of something as an excuse not to honor the principle that governs it?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**The Quran said the women's inheritance in a full sentence — not a clause, not a qualifier, not an appendix to the men's — because some truths need to be stated with a weight the audience cannot reduce.**

### The Closing Invitation

The next time you hear the inheritance verses of the Quran, listen for the repetition. Listen for the moment the sentence resets and begins again — the same words, the same structure, the same grammar — with only the subject changed from men to women. That repetition is not an accident of style. It is the sound of a right being planted in the ground so firmly that fourteen centuries of cultural resistance have not been able to pull it out.

*Naṣīban mafrūḍā.* An obligated share. Decreed before it was announced. Planted before it was honored. Yours before anyone agreed.

### Du'a

*Allahumma, You planted the shares before we heard them and decreed the inheritance before we agreed to it. Help us honor what You planted — for our daughters, our sisters, our mothers — without reduction, without delay, and without the quiet theft of asking them to forgive what was never ours to keep. And when we stand before You on the Day of Accounting, let the naṣīb of every person we were responsible for be fully, completely, irrevocably in their hands. You decreed it, Ya Rabb. Help us not to un-decree what You have already settled.*
