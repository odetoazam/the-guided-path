# Do Not Consume One Another

## Surah An-Nisa (4:29-30)

[ayah:4:29] يَـٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ لَا تَأْكُلُوٓا۟ أَمْوَٰلَكُم بَيْنَكُم بِٱلْبَـٰطِلِ إِلَّآ أَن تَكُونَ تِجَـٰرَةً عَن تَرَاضٍ مِّنكُمْ ۚ وَلَا تَقْتُلُوٓا۟ أَنفُسَكُمْ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ كَانَ بِكُمْ رَحِيمًا

[ayah:4:30] وَمَن يَفْعَلْ ذَٰلِكَ عُدْوَٰنًا وَظُلْمًا فَسَوْفَ نُصْلِيهِ نَارًا ۚ وَكَانَ ذَٰلِكَ عَلَى ٱللَّهِ يَسِيرًا

*Ya ayyuha alladhina amanu la ta'kulu amwalakum baynakum bil-batil illa an takuna tijaratan 'an taradin minkum. Wa la taqtulu anfusakum. Inna Allaha kana bikum rahima.*

*Wa man yaf'al dhalika 'udwanan wa zulman fa-sawfa nuslihi nara. Wa kana dhalika 'ala Allahi yasira.*

**O you who believe, do not consume one another’s wealth among yourselves through falsehood, except that it be trade by mutual consent among you. And do not kill yourselves. Surely Allah is ever Merciful to you. And whoever does that in aggression and wrongdoing, We will soon cast him into a Fire, and that is easy for Allah.**

[PAUSE]

## Introduction

Some kinds of corruption arrive so quietly that they no longer feel violent.

A contract signed under pressure. A profit taken through manipulation. A transaction that is technically neat but morally rotten. A person worn down by a community until their inner life begins collapsing. A social order where people keep taking from one another and then act surprised when despair, rage, and destruction spread through the same body.

These verses descend into that world and name something we often hide from ourselves: exploitation is not only about money, and destruction is not only about blood. A society can consume itself before it notices that it is bleeding.

That is why the structure of the passage is so sharp. It begins with wealth: do not eat your assets among yourselves through falsehood. Then it moves immediately to life itself: do not kill yourselves. The shift is not abrupt. It is interpretive. The ayah is teaching us that when falsehood governs exchange, the damage does not remain in ledgers. It enters the social body.

Then, almost unexpectedly, the verse places mercy at the center: **inna Allaha kana bikum rahima**. Surely Allah has been merciful to you.

The hook of the passage is this: **Allah forbids exploitative taking because He is merciful to the human beings who would otherwise end up consuming one another alive.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **La ta'kulu amwalakum baynakum bil-batil**: The Quran Describes Wrongful Taking as Eating

The verse does not say, "Do not seize wealth," or "Do not acquire wealth," though those would have been possible. It says:

**la ta'kulu amwalakum baynakum bil-batil**.

Do not **eat** your wealth among yourselves through falsehood.

The root **a-k-l** means to eat. The Quran often uses this verb when speaking about wrongful possession, because eating is not only taking. It is taking something into yourself, consuming it, turning it into your own body. The metaphor is morally brilliant. Illicit wealth is not simply held in the hand. It is ingested into the self.

[PAUSE]

That changes the emotional feel of the verse. Fraud is not cleverness. Exploitation is not strategic success. When you take by falsehood, you are feeding yourself from corruption. You are building your inner and outer life out of what was not rightfully yours.

Then the verse says **amwalakum** - your wealth. Not their wealth. Your wealth.

This is striking. The Quran speaks as though the community's property has a shared moral field. One person's exploitation of another is not merely a private transfer between isolated individuals. It is injury inside a collective trust. That is why **baynakum** matters so much as well - among yourselves, between yourselves, in the relational space you inhabit together.

The ayah is not only regulating transactions. It is protecting the tissue of a community.

And the means of corruption is named as **batil**, from the root **b-t-l**, falsehood, nullity, what has no rightful standing, what appears operative but lacks real legitimacy. Batil is one of the most frightening Quranic words because it can still be effective in worldly terms while being empty before Allah. A transaction may go through. A signature may hold. A system may defend it. And still the whole thing can be batil.

### 2. **Illa an takuna tijaratan 'an taradin minkum**: Permissible Exchange Is Built on Mutual Willingness, Not Mere Formality

Then the exception comes:

**illa an takuna tijaratan 'an taradin minkum**.

Except that it be trade arising from mutual consent among you.

The word **tijarah** from the root **t-j-r** is trade, commerce, circulated exchange. Islam does not demonize material life. The verse does not oppose wealth itself, nor trade itself. It opposes batil.

Then comes the phrase that gives lawful exchange its moral soul: **'an taradin**.

**Taradin** comes from the root **r-d-w**, contentment, satisfaction, willing pleasure. And it appears here in a reciprocal form: mutual consent, consent moving in both directions, not one party outwardly signing while inwardly cornered.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the deepest correctives in Quranic commercial ethics. Lawful exchange is not defined only by the absence of theft in a narrow sense. It is defined by the presence of real willingness.

That matters because human beings are experts at manufacturing outward compliance. We can create environments where someone "agrees" because they are intimidated, ashamed, desperate, dependent, or too exhausted to resist. The paperwork exists. The social ritual is complete. But the Quran asks a more searching question: was there *taradin*?

Mutual consent is not decorative softness added to the contract. It is part of what makes the trade morally alive rather than batil.

### 3. **Wa la taqtulu anfusakum**: The Verse Collapses the Distance Between Self-Harm and Social Harm

Then comes the line that startles the reader:

**wa la taqtulu anfusakum**.

And do not kill yourselves.

The root **q-t-l** is direct and severe: to kill, to take life. The noun **anfusakum** from **n-f-s** can mean yourselves, your own selves, but it can also carry the sense of one another within a shared community. The phrasing is powerful precisely because it allows the moral meaning to expand rather than shrink.

[PAUSE]

Do not destroy your own souls.
Do not turn your violence inward.
Do not kill one another, because one another belongs to the same human body of responsibility.

The verse has moved from wealth to life without apology. It is teaching that these are not unrelated domains. False exchange and social destruction belong to one continuum. A community that feeds on batil eventually learns to injure itself more openly too.

This is not only about the final act of killing. It is also about the habits that make life unlivable: exploitation, humiliation, crushing debt, predation, despair-inducing structures, the slow manufacture of conditions under which souls begin collapsing. The Quran names the most severe endpoint so we learn to fear the path that leads there.

### 4. **Inna Allaha kana bikum rahima**: The Prohibition Is Interpreted by Mercy

Right after these prohibitions, the verse says:

**inna Allaha kana bikum rahima**.

Surely Allah has been merciful to you.

Not severe with you in this line. Not merely commanding over you. Merciful to you.

The root **r-h-m** carries mercy, tenderness, protective care, the kind of mercy that guards life and nourishes it. This placement is everything. The verse is telling us how to read the prohibition. Allah is not forbidding false consumption and destruction because He delights in narrowness. He is forbidding them because mercy wants human beings protected from becoming prey to one another and from turning into threats to themselves.

[PAUSE]

Many people misread divine mercy as permission without boundaries. The Quran often shows the opposite. Mercy is sometimes the reason a boundary is placed. A rule can be a shelter. A prohibition can be care.

### 5. **'Udwanan wa zulman... fa-sawfa nuslihi nara**: When the Limit Is Crossed Deliberately, the Consequence Is Named

Ayah 30 gathers the warning into one sentence:

**wa man yaf'al dhalika 'udwanan wa zulman**.

Whoever does that in aggression and wrongdoing.

**'Udwan** from the root **'-d-w** is transgression, crossing past the line, hostility that overruns limits. **Zulm** from the root **z-l-m** is putting things out of place, injustice, misplacement, wronging. Together the pair is forceful. The act is not treated as a minor technical violation. It is a willful crossing and a distortion of moral order.

Then the verse says: **fa-sawfa nuslihi nara**.

We will soon make him enter a Fire.

The verb from the root **s-l-y** carries the sense of being made to enter or be exposed to the Fire. After all the earlier social imagery of consumption, the consequence becomes fittingly bodily. If a person lived by feeding on batil and wounding the social body, the Hereafter unveils the deeper fire already implicit in that life.

[PAUSE]

And the verse closes: **wa kana dhalika 'ala Allahi yasira**.

That is easy for Allah.

The word **yasir** from **y-s-r** means easy, effortless, not difficult. This ending shatters a dangerous human illusion: that because a person escaped accountability in the world, perhaps divine reckoning will also be difficult to actualize. The Quran says no. None of this is hard for Allah.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: A Society Usually Eats Itself Before It Admits It Is Violent

Most people know how to recognize dramatic violence. They can point to blood, bruises, weapons, broken bodies. But there is an earlier violence that often goes unnamed because it hides inside respectability.

It hides in economic arrangements where the strong extract from the weak and call it market logic. It hides in institutions that depend on pressure and then rename that pressure professionalism. It hides in families that use money to control, in businesses that bury coercion beneath paperwork, in communities where shame and scarcity make consent look present when it is actually absent.

[PAUSE]

The Quran calls that world batil. And it describes the taking as eating because exploitation is nourishment stolen from someone else's life. That is why the move to **wa la taqtulu anfusakum** is so devastating. The verse teaches us that once a society becomes comfortable feeding itself through falsehood, the leap to more visible forms of self-destruction is not as large as we pretend.

We often want to isolate our moral failures into separate categories. Financial ethics over here. Mental health over there. Violence somewhere else. The Quran is more unified than we are. It sees that false acquisition, social cruelty, despair, and destruction are often different temperatures of the same spiritual climate.

So these verses do not merely ask, "Was a contract valid?" They ask, "What kind of human world are you building among yourselves?"

### Theme 2: Mercy Sometimes Feels Restrictive Because We Mistake Appetite for Freedom

There is another reason this passage matters. Many people hear moral boundaries and immediately feel deprivation. If I cannot take every profitable path, if I must care about actual consent, if I may not exploit loopholes, if I cannot turn frustration inward or outward destructively, then life feels narrower.

But the ayah insists that the opposite is true: **Allah is merciful to you**.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the hardest spiritual conversions a person can make, to stop reading divine boundaries through the voice of appetite. Appetite says: if it is available, why not take it? Mercy says: not everything you can reach should become part of you. Appetite says: if no one can prove the coercion, the deal counts. Mercy says: Allah sees whether taradin was real. Appetite says: my anger, my despair, my exhaustion are mine to direct however I want. Mercy says: do not kill yourselves.

The more we mature, the more we begin to recognize that many of the things we once called freedom were actually invitations to self-harm. Allah's prohibitions do not shrink the human being. They keep him from being eaten alive by his own unchecked hungers and by the falsehood of others.

## Closing Synthesis

These two verses hold together three realities we often separate.

They speak about wealth, but with the language of eating.

They speak about social exchange, but with the language of mutual consent.

They speak about killing, but in a form that makes us hear both self-destruction and the destruction of one another within one moral body.

Then they interpret the whole frame through mercy and close it through consequence.

[PAUSE]

This is the architecture: false consumption injures the community, true exchange requires reciprocal willingness, mercy places the boundary, and deliberate transgression unveils its fire.

### Questions to Carry

1. Where have I been treating a technically possible gain as though that alone made it morally alive?
2. Do I actually care whether consent is mutual, or only whether compliance is visible?
3. In what ways might falsehood in exchange be harming the social body around me more than I admit?
4. Can I recognize that some divine prohibitions are mercy precisely because they stop me from consuming or destroying myself and others?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These verses teach that unlawful taking is a form of communal self-consumption, and that Allah's mercy places boundaries around wealth and life so we do not quietly destroy one another while calling it normal.**

### Du'a

Allahumma, purify our earnings, our dealings, and our hearts from batil. Make us people of real consent, real justice, and real mercy. Do not let us feed ourselves from what harms others or turn our pain into destruction of ourselves. Guard our communities from hidden forms of violence, and make us fear Your fire before we learn too late what falsehood was doing to us all along. Ameen.
