# Do Not Leave Her Hanging

## Surah An-Nisa (4:129-130)

[ayah:4:129] وَلَن تَسْتَطِيعُوٓا۟ أَن تَعْدِلُوا۟ بَيْنَ ٱلنِّسَآءِ وَلَوْ حَرَصْتُمْ ۖ فَلَا تَمِيلُوا۟ كُلَّ ٱلْمَيْلِ فَتَذَرُوهَا كَٱلْمُعَلَّقَةِ ۚ وَإِن تُصْلِحُوا۟ وَتَتَّقُوا۟ فَإِنَّ ٱللَّهَ كَانَ غَفُورًا رَّحِيمًا

[ayah:4:130] وَإِن يَتَفَرَّقَا يُغْنِ ٱللَّهُ كُلًّا مِّن سَعَتِهِۦ ۚ وَكَانَ ٱللَّهُ وَٰسِعًا حَكِيمًا

*Wa lan tastati'u an ta'dilu bayna al-nisa'i wa law harastum. Fa la tamilu kulla al-mayli fa tadharuha ka al-mu'allaqah. Wa in tuslihu wa tattaqu fa inna Allaha kana ghafuran rahima.*

*Wa in yatafarraqa yughni Allahu kullan min sa'atihi. Wa kana Allahu wasi'an hakima.*

**And you will never be able to be equal between wives, even if you should strive. So do not incline completely and leave one hanging. And if you amend and fear Allah, then indeed Allah is ever Forgiving and Merciful. But if they separate, Allah will enrich each from His abundance. And ever is Allah Encompassing and Wise.**

## Introduction

There are Quranic moments where mercy appears not by pretending a human difficulty does not exist, but by naming it without illusion.

These verses arrive in one of those moments. They do not speak with sentimental vagueness about human attachment, nor do they flatten marriage into dry rule alone. Instead they tell the truth about something people often do not know how to tell the truth about: the heart cannot be engineered into perfect emotional symmetry. But that limitation does not license harm. If inward equality cannot be fully mastered, outward injustice still can and must be restrained.

Then the passage goes further. It names one of the cruelest relational states with a single image: to leave a woman suspended, hanging, neither fully held nor fully released. Anyone who has lived in emotional limbo knows how exact that image is. The pain is not always the clean pain of an ending. Often it is the drawn-out pain of uncertainty, neglect, and partial belonging.

Yet the verses do not end in fatalism. They open two doors under Allah: islah with taqwa, and if that cannot be sustained, then separation without despair because Allah can enrich each from His vastness.

So the passage refuses two false pieties at once. It refuses the lie that says, "Since perfect inward fairness is impossible, harm is inevitable." And it refuses the lie that says, "If the relationship ends, life itself has ended." Between those two lies, the Qur'an places taqwa, repair, and the abundance of Allah.

The hook of the passage is this: **Allah does not demand impossible emotional sameness, but He absolutely forbids turning that difficulty into injustice, neglect, or despair.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Lan tastati'u an ta'dilu**: The Verse Begins With Moral Realism, Not Sentimental Pretending

The first shock in the passage is how direct it is:

wa lan tastati'u an ta'dilu bayna al-nisa' wa law harastum.

You will never be able to achieve full equality between wives, even if you are eager for it.

The verb tastati'u comes from the field of ability and capacity. The verse is naming a limit in what can be fully achieved. Then comes ta'dilu, from justice and balance. And the clause wa law harastum adds the dimension of effort, eagerness, even anxious striving. Even if you tried hard.

[PAUSE]

This is morally important because the Qur'an does not build law on fantasy. It knows that not every interior condition can be leveled into identical emotional measure. That honesty is itself a mercy. It protects the servant from pretending before Allah that the heart can be manufactured by command into perfect sameness.

But the verse does not leave the matter there. Realism is not surrender. The fact that complete inward equivalence cannot be guaranteed does not mean one may abandon justice in conduct. So the next command immediately narrows the field:

fa la tamilu kulla al-mayl.

Do not incline with total inclination.

The repetition of the same field, the verb of leaning and then the noun al-mayl, makes the warning emphatic. Since total balance of feeling is beyond you, do not let your lean become complete enough to turn into visible oppression.

The realism of the verse is therefore disciplined realism. It acknowledges the limit, then guards the boundary.

### 2. **Ka al-mu'allaqah**: One of the Most Painful Human States Is Named in a Single Image

Then comes the image:

fa tadharuha ka al-mu'allaqah.

So that you leave her like one suspended.

Mu'allaqah is a devastating word. It comes from being hung, attached without settled resting place, left suspended between states. Not fully married in the living sense. Not fully released either. No stable belonging, no clean exit, only prolonged uncertainty.

[PAUSE]

The Qur'an could have described the harm analytically. Instead it gives you a picture. That matters because suspension is often how relational injustice feels from the inside. A person is kept waiting, guessing, emotionally starved, partly claimed, partly abandoned. There is enough attachment to prevent closure, but not enough care to provide actual life.

This image should humble anyone who thinks neglect is a softer sin than rupture. Sometimes it is the more exhausting one. A clean wound may scar, but a suspended life keeps wounding in slow motion.

And notice the moral precision: the verse did not condemn a feeling first. It condemned what the unchecked feeling produces in another person's lived reality. That is deeply Quranic. The text moves from internal limit to external ethical consequence.

### 3. **Wa in tuslihu wa tattaqu**: The Door Back Is Not Perfection but Repair and Taqwa

Then the ayah opens mercy:

wa in tuslihu wa tattaqu.

If you make islah and have taqwa.

Islah is repair, setting right, working against damage and fracture. It is a practical word. Not romantic language, not vague regret, but active correction. And tattaqu brings God-conscious guarding: the kind of inward vigilance that prevents self-interest from becoming cruelty.

[PAUSE]

This pairing is beautiful. The verse does not say, "If you feel everything equally, then all will be well." It says: if you repair and if you fear Allah. That is a much more reachable and much more serious path. Feelings may remain uneven, but conduct can still be corrected. The soul can still remember Allah enough to stop translating private lean into public wrong.

Then the divine names come:

fa inna Allaha kana ghafuran rahima.

Allah is Forgiving and Merciful.

That tells us that this domain of intimate failure is not outside mercy. People mishandle closeness. They fall short. They lean. They wound. The answer is not hopelessness. It is islah under taqwa, met by divine forgiveness and mercy.

### 4. **Yughni Allahu kullan min sa'atih**: Even Separation Is Placed Under Allah's Vastness

Then the next verse says:

wa in yatafarraqa yughni Allahu kullan min sa'atih.

If the two separate, Allah will enrich each from His vastness.

The verb yatafarraqa names an actual parting. The Qur'an does not fear saying it. There are times when repair fails or cannot continue. But then comes the promise: yughni Allahu kullan. Allah will enrich each. Not one perhaps salvaged person and one discarded ruin. Each.

[PAUSE]

And the source is min sa'atih, from His spacious abundance, His expansive provision, the wideness of what He can open after a closure.

This is one of the tenderest correctives to relational despair in the Qur'an. We often imagine endings as absolute diminishment. The verse says: do not forget who Allah is after endings. Your future is not chained to the relationship you lost. Allah can widen what you thought had permanently narrowed.

Then the verse closes with Wasi'an Hakima. Vast in scope, wise in measure. So even the hard permission of separation is not random. It lives inside divine vastness and divine wisdom, not chaos.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: The Heart's Limits Do Not Excuse the Self's Injustice

These verses are powerful because they distinguish two things we often confuse: what you cannot fully control and what you are still fully responsible for.

You may not be able to create identical inward feeling. The verse says that plainly. But that does not mean you are free to surrender your choices, your speech, your time, your provision, your attentiveness, or your fairness to the strongest pull of your preference. The heart may lean; the servant is still accountable for what the lean becomes.

[PAUSE]

This matters far beyond the immediate legal context. Many people defend forms of neglect by appealing to inner honesty. "I cannot force feeling." Sometimes that is true. But the Quran's question is sharper: what have you done with that truth? Did you use it to become more careful, more repentant, more disciplined, more just? Or did you use it to authorize half-presence, withholding, and the slow torment of leaving someone suspended?

The verse teaches that moral adulthood begins when we stop hiding external harm behind internal complexity. Human complexity is real. It is not a moral exemption.

### Theme 2: Allah's Vastness Must Be Remembered Both Inside Repair and After Ending

There is another mercy here. The passage gives dignity to both possible endings of pain.

One dignity is islah: the hard work of repair under taqwa. Not performance, not management of appearances, but real correction because Allah sees the one being harmed and hears the one living in suspension.

The other dignity is honest separation when repair is no longer real. That too is not outside Allah. The verse refuses the theology of scarcity that tells people they must remain in emotional wasteland because nothing good could exist beyond it.

[PAUSE]

How many people stay trapped partly because they imagine that if this bond ends, all provision ends with it? Not only financial provision, but emotional dignity, stable life, future companionship, inner peace, even meaning. The Qur'an answers that fear by attaching the future not to the bond but to Allah's sa'ah, His wideness.

That does not trivialize loss. Separation can still ache. Repair can still be demanding. But it does restore the deepest truth: Allah is larger than the crisis we are inside. His abundance is not exhausted by the forms we fear losing.

This is why the passage feels so balanced. It neither romanticizes staying nor romanticizes leaving. It moralizes neither pain nor rupture simplistically. It tells the truth, commands justice, opens mercy, and places the future under Allah's vast wisdom.

## Closing Synthesis

These verses begin with sober realism: complete emotional equality cannot be fully guaranteed even with effort.

Then they immediately guard the ethical boundary: do not lean so completely that you leave a person suspended.

Then they open the path of repair through islah and taqwa under Allah's forgiveness and mercy.

And finally, if separation comes, they place both people under Allah's vastness, promising that He can enrich each from His abundance.

[PAUSE]

That is the architecture: realism, restraint, repair, then spaciousness.

### Questions to Carry

1. Where have I tried to excuse an external wrong by appealing to an internal difficulty?
2. Have I ever left someone emotionally suspended because I lacked the courage either to repair properly or to release honestly?
3. When I think about relational hardship, do I really believe Allah's mercy reaches the space of repair?
4. If an ending comes, do I believe Allah can still enrich and widen life after it?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These verses teach that while the heart may not achieve perfect sameness, the servant is still forbidden from translating emotional limitation into relational injustice, and Allah's mercy remains open both in repair and in honest separation.**

### Du'a

Allahumma do not let the complexity of our hearts become an excuse for ظلم against those entrusted to us. Teach us islah where repair is possible, taqwa where desire leans us toward unfairness, and trust in Your vastness if parting becomes necessary. Do not leave us or anyone we love suspended in harm. Ameen.
