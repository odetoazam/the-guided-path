# They Did Not Kill Him Certainly

## Surah An-Nisa (4:156-159)

## Introduction

There are moments when people do not only reject the truth. They try to rewrite the story so that rejection looks like victory.

That is the atmosphere of this passage. It begins not with a minor wrong, but with a monstrous one: a tremendous slander against Maryam. Then it moves to a boast: "We killed the Messiah, Isa son of Maryam, the messenger of Allah." The claim is not merely informational. It is triumphant. It wants ownership of the outcome. It wants the humiliation of a messenger of Allah to become a human achievement.

The Qur'an answers that boast in a remarkable way. It does not only negate it. It slowly empties it. They did not kill him. They did not crucify him. It was made to appear so to them. Those who differ are in doubt. They have no knowledge except following assumption. They did not kill him certainly. By the time the verse has finished, the human claim that began with swagger has collapsed into ignorance, doubt, and conjecture.

Then comes the divine answer in two words of immense elevation: bal rafa'ahu Allahu ilayhi. Rather, Allah raised him to Himself. The story is taken out of human hands and returned to divine sovereignty. And then the passage does not end in history alone. It ends in testimony. Isa will not disappear into competing narratives. On the Day of Resurrection, he will be a witness against them.

The hook of the passage is this: **when human beings boast that they have mastered the fate of Allah's messengers, the Qur'an answers by stripping their certainty down to guesswork and returning the final meaning of the story to Allah's raising and Allah's judgment.**

## Part 1: The Linguistic Journey

### 1. **Buhtanan 'azima**: The Passage Begins With Shock, Not Debate

Verse 156 is brief, but devastating:

wa qawlihim 'ala Maryama buhtanan 'azima.

Their saying against Maryam a tremendous slander.

Buhtan is not ordinary falsehood. It is a lie so severe it leaves the hearer stunned. A fabrication that shocks by its brazenness. That is fitting here because the attack on Maryam was not casual gossip. It was a violent assault on purity itself, on one of the most honored women in revelation.

[PAUSE]

And the adjective 'azima intensifies it further: tremendous, enormous, immense. The Qur'an is training us to hear the moral scale correctly. Not all speech crimes are equal. Some words are so corrupt that they become historical wounds.

This opening matters because it frames the rest of the passage. The boast about Isa does not come from a neutral audience discussing events with restraint. It comes from a people already named for kufr and for catastrophic dishonesty toward his mother. The moral atmosphere is already compromised before the next claim is even spoken.

### 2. **Wa ma qataluhu wa ma salabuhu ... ma lahum bihi min 'ilm illa ittiba'a al-zann**: The Qur'an Dismantles Boast by Dismantling Certainty

Then verse 157 quotes the claim:

inna qatalna al-masiha 'Isa ibna Maryama rasula Allah.

Indeed we killed the Messiah, Isa son of Maryam, the messenger of Allah.

The line is swollen with human certainty. "We did it." We reached him. We controlled the end of the story. But immediately the Qur'an responds with repeated negation:

wa ma qataluhu wa ma salabuhu.

They did not kill him, nor did they crucify him.

The repetition is powerful. Denial once might have been enough. But the verse closes every imagined door of triumph one after another.

[PAUSE]

Then comes the delicate clause:

wa lakin shubbiha lahum.

Rather, it was made to appear so to them.

The verse does not linger on furnishing curiosity. It does not satisfy every later appetite for narrative detail. It gives us exactly the spiritual point we need: the matter was not what their boast assumed it was. Appearance and reality were not aligned for them.

Then the verse moves from negation into diagnosis:

wa inna alladhina ikhtalafu fihi lafi shakkin minhu.

Those who differed over it are surely in doubt about it.

Ikhtalafu and shakkin form an important pair. Difference here is not rich clarity. It is dispute circling around uncertainty. Then the verse becomes even sharper:

ma lahum bihi min 'ilmin illa ittiba'a al-zann.

They have no knowledge of it except following assumption.

This is one of the most devastating intellectual reversals in the Qur'an. A public boast begins as confidence and ends as zann, mere following of conjecture. The claim that felt victorious is exposed as epistemically empty.

And then the verse seals it:

wa ma qataluhu yaqina.

They did not kill him certainly.

Yaqina closes the space. The certainty belongs not to their boast, but to Allah's negation of it.

### 3. **Bal rafa'ahu Allahu ilayhi ... shahida**: The Story Is Returned to Allah, Then Carried to the Day of Witness

Verse 158 is breathtaking in its brevity:

bal rafa'ahu Allahu ilayhi.

Rather, Allah raised him to Himself.

Bal overturns the whole direction of the previous claim. No, the story does not terminate in human violence. No, the final meaning of Isa is not that his enemies mastered him. Allah raised him.

[PAUSE]

Rafa'a is a strong word. It is not merely rescue language. It is elevation. The verse does not leave Isa in the hands of the narrative built against him. It lifts him beyond it.

Then the divine names come:

wa kana Allahu 'azizan hakima.

Allah is Almighty, Wise.

'Aziz means unconquered, not reached against His will. Hakim means perfectly wise, placing things as they belong. These names answer the human boast directly. No messenger of Allah is ever ultimately at the mercy of a plot outside Allah's dominion. The event belongs inside divine might and wisdom, not chaotic human victory.

Then verse 159 carries us forward:

wa in min ahli al-kitabi illa la yu'minanna bihi qabla mawtihi.

There is none of the People of the Book but will surely believe in him before his death.

Classical tafsir discusses the pronoun and timing here in more than one way, but the verse's force is clear even before those details are sorted: Isa will not remain forever buried under false narration. The truth about him reaches a point of unavoidable recognition.

Then the final line:

wa yawma al-qiyamati yakunu 'alayhim shahida.

On the Day of Resurrection he will be a witness against them.

Shahid here completes the passage beautifully. What began as human speech about Maryam and Isa ends not with human speech, but with Isa himself standing as witness before Allah.

## Part 2: The Thematic Depths

### Theme 1: Human Beings Love Narratives of Triumph Even When They Are Built on Guesswork

One of the most psychologically revealing features of this passage is how it treats boastful certainty.

People often do not cling to false stories because the evidence is overwhelming. They cling because the story serves the ego. It gives them a sense of control, vindication, domination, or release from moral accountability. "We killed him" is not just a historical claim. It is a claim to mastery over a messenger, a claim that truth can be humiliated by force and then narrated as human success.

[PAUSE]

The Qur'an's response is deeply instructive for us. It does not merely say, "You are wrong." It traces how wrongness lives inside the soul. It shows how a claim can be loud but knowledge-empty, triumphant but hollow, socially repeated yet built on nothing more than following assumption. That is a warning far beyond this single story.

How many narratives do human communities hold with that same mixture of confidence and thin evidence? How often do we repeat things because the repetition serves identity, tribe, grievance, or victory more than truth? The passage teaches us to distrust the intoxication of certainty when certainty has not been earned by knowledge.

And it teaches something even harder: sometimes people do not just misunderstand the truth. They need the false version emotionally. It lets them feel they won.

### Theme 2: Allah Protects the True Meaning of His Servants Even When the World Tries to Write Their Ending

The brief line "Allah raised him to Himself" is not only a correction of a claim. It is a theology of protection.

Not protection in the simplistic sense that beloved servants never face hostility, slander, or plots. Maryam was slandered. Isa was targeted. The passage itself makes that plain. But there is a deeper protection here: the enemies of Allah do not get final authorship over the meaning of His chosen servants.

[PAUSE]

That matters to any believer who has ever feared what false speech can do. Slander can feel permanent. Distorted public narratives can feel overpowering. The enemy's version of events can look, for a while, like the only one that remains standing. But this passage reminds us that appearance is not sovereignty. Human narrative control is not final judgment.

Allah can return the entire matter to Himself in a way no public claim can prevent. And in the end, there is witness. Isa becomes shahid against them on the Day of Resurrection. The one spoken about becomes the one who speaks. The one misrepresented becomes the one whose testimony stands.

There is comfort here for the wounded believer. Not always immediate worldly vindication, but the assurance that false stories do not own the final court.

## Closing Synthesis

These verses begin with two speech crimes: a tremendous slander against Maryam and a triumphant boast against Isa.

Then the Qur'an answers the boast not with one correction only, but with a gradual dismantling: they did not kill him, they did not crucify him, it was made to appear so to them, those who differ are in doubt, they have no knowledge except following assumption, and they did not kill him certainly.

Then the matter is returned to Allah: rather, Allah raised him to Himself.

And finally the passage moves beyond disputed history into certain eschatology: Isa will not remain lost in contested speech, and on the Day of Resurrection he will stand as witness.

[PAUSE]

That is the architecture: slander, boast, negation, exposed conjecture, divine raising, final witness.

### Questions to Carry

1. Where have I mistaken loudly repeated narrative for real knowledge?
2. Do I recognize how often pride wants certainty before truth has actually been established?
3. When false speech or distorted stories spread, do I remember that appearance is not final sovereignty?
4. What would it mean for me to live more carefully before Allah with my claims, my retellings, and my confidence?

### One-Sentence Distillation

**These verses teach that human boast can sound certain while being built only on conjecture, but Allah protects the true meaning of His servants by overturning false triumph, raising whom He wills, and reserving final witness for the Day when no narrative can be manipulated.**

### Du'a

Allahumma protect us from speaking about Your servants with slander, pride, or false certainty. Purify our tongues from claims we have not earned through knowledge, and save us from the intoxication of narratives that make us feel victorious while distancing us from truth. As You raised the matter of Isa beyond the reach of false boasting, raise our hearts above conjecture and make us people who wait for Your judgment with reverence and honesty. Ameen.
